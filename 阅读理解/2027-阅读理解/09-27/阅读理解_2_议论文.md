# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Be Encouraged to Keep a Handwritten Diary?**

① In my grandmother's bedroom, there is a small wooden box holding **fourteen thin notebooks**, one for each year of her teenage and early married life. They are covered in small, tidy handwriting; the edges have turned yellow. Last summer, on a quiet afternoon, she opened one from 1967 and read out a short entry from a rainy September day. Sixty years had passed, yet the **words on the page still carried her fourteen-year-old voice**. That afternoon I asked myself a question: in an age of phones, voice messages and short-video diaries, **should middle-school students still be encouraged to keep a handwritten diary?**

② Those in favour argue that a diary is, above all, **a place to think**. The modern teenager's life is loud: notifications buzz, classmates argue, parents worry, homework waits. When a student sits down with a pen and a blank page, the noise naturally slows. Writing by hand is **slower than typing**, and this slowness is actually an advantage: it forces the brain to **choose words carefully**, to arrange ideas, and to see patterns in the day. Many psychologists now believe that ten minutes of handwriting before bed can reduce anxiety about exams and improve sleep — something a 15-second video post does not do.

③ A second argument is about **memory**. Research shows that information we write by hand is remembered **longer and more clearly** than information we only type. A handwritten diary, kept for even one year, becomes a **personal, searchable memory** of the events, people and feelings of that year. Ten years from now, a short paragraph scribbled on a November evening may bring back an entire afternoon that an adult has otherwise forgotten. Voice recordings and videos can capture sound and image, but writing captures something harder to name: the **shape of our thinking on a certain day**.

④ A third argument is about **language and writing skills**. Regular diary-writing is probably the cheapest and most enjoyable way to improve written Chinese — and, if some entries are in English, written English, too. The Grade-9 student who writes 150 – 200 words each evening in his diary will, by the end of the year, have written **around 60 000 words**: easily more than any school assignment can produce. This quiet daily practice often shows up clearly in Zhongkao compositions, though the student is not writing with exams in mind.

⑤ However, some opponents worry that "compulsory" diary-keeping can backfire. If a school treats the diary as a **graded assignment**, students may write not honestly, but **only the safe things** that teachers expect to see. The true power of a diary comes from **privacy**; remove the privacy and you remove much of the benefit. There is also a practical worry: in a busy exam year, a diary may feel like **one more task**, and an already tired student may start to hate pens and paper. Writing should be a friend, not a second homework.

⑥ Another concern is that in 2027, many students simply prefer **digital notes**. Short voice clips, photos with captions, or a note-app diary on the phone can be just as thoughtful, they argue. A forced "only handwritten" rule could feel old-fashioned, and could exclude students with hand pain or visual difficulties for whom typing is easier.

⑦ My own view is that schools and families should **strongly encourage**, but **never force**, a diary practice, and should allow a **flexible form**. For example: a **short handwritten paragraph** four times a week, plus a weekly longer entry that may be in any language or any mixed style. Parents should **never read** a child's diary without permission — even a kind parent's eyes will change the way the child writes. Privacy is the soil in which honest writing grows. Digital diaries are also acceptable, provided the phone is **not** also used for videos in the same session; screens invite distractions that a paper notebook never brings.

⑧ **A practical start:** pick a small, plain notebook this October. Write three lines at the end of each day — what happened, how you felt, one sentence of thanks. No fancy words. No perfect sentences. After three weeks, you will begin to see small patterns: when you feel tired, when you feel strong, which friends lift you, which habits drain you. A diary is not a teacher and not a judge. It is **a quiet mirror** you hold up to yourself every evening.

⑨ Sixty years from now, your own grandchildren will not need to see your likes and shares on a forgotten app; but a thin notebook from Grade 8, left in a drawer, may one day make them look up and ask, "**This was you, at my age?**" — and discover, through the real weight of old paper, that you were once exactly as real, brave, and unsure as they are. That, in the end, is why the handwritten diary still deserves a place in the life of a young student in 2027.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What question does the writer raise in Paragraph 1, after reading her grandmother's old diary?**

A. Whether all old diaries should be thrown away.  
B. Whether middle-school students, in an age of phones and short videos, should still be encouraged to keep a handwritten diary.  
C. Whether grandmothers are still interesting.  
D. Whether rainy September days are good for writing.

**2. According to Paragraph 2, why is handwriting described as having a useful "slowness"?**

A. Because it takes a student an entire week to finish one page.  
B. Because the slower speed forces the brain to choose words carefully, arrange ideas and see patterns; psychologists say it reduces anxiety and helps sleep.  
C. Because only slow students can write diaries.  
D. Because handwriting can replace all sleep.

**3. What does Paragraph 3 say about memory?**

A. Writing by hand is a worse way to remember things.  
B. Information we write by hand is remembered longer and more clearly than what we only type; a handwritten diary becomes a personal, searchable memory of events, people and feelings.  
C. Only video can capture memory.  
D. Memory does not depend on whether we write or type.

**4. What do opponents worry about in Paragraphs 5–6?**

A. That a compulsory, graded diary may make students write only "safe things"; that a diary can feel like one more task in a busy year; that some students prefer digital notes and have hand pain, so a strict "only handwritten" rule could feel old-fashioned or unfair.  
B. That diaries secretly contain spells.  
C. That students will write too little in a year.  
D. That diaries will replace all textbooks.

**5. What is the writer's own view (Paragraphs 7–9)?**

A. Schools must force every student to keep a handwritten diary or fail the year.  
B. Diaries are useless; students should only post videos online.  
C. Schools and families should strongly encourage but never force a diary practice; handwriting, with some flexibility in form and strict privacy, can be "a quiet mirror", and one day a thin notebook may allow a grandchild to understand who you were at their age.  
D. Parents should read every diary entry to check it.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】段落主旨题  
【定位】第①段末 "should middle-school students still be encouraged to keep a handwritten diary?"  
【解析】首段以奶奶日记引出核心议题。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "forces the brain to choose words carefully ... reduce anxiety about exams and improve sleep"  
【解析】手写的慢促进深度思考并缓解焦虑。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "remembered longer and more clearly ... personal, searchable memory"  
【解析】手写内容更易长期记忆。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤⑥段列出担忧  
【解析】A 概括了反方三层担忧。B、C、D 无文中依据。故选 A。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】作者观点题  
【定位】第⑦⑧⑨段 "strongly encourage, but never force ... a quiet mirror ... 'This was you, at my age?'"  
【解析】作者主张鼓励但不强制，允许灵活形式并保证隐私。A、B、D 均偏离。故选 C。
