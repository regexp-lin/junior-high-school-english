# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Students Help Design One Classroom Rule Each Term?**

① Last Tuesday, in our Monday morning homeroom meeting, our teacher Mr Qin put a very quiet question on the blackboard: *"What is one thing in this classroom, from 7:40 to 17:00, that makes your day a little worse — and how might we change it?"* He gave us seven minutes. At first, there was polite silence. Then one by one, sticky notes began to appear on the board. One said *"Door slammed."* Another said *"Someone borrowed my eraser and did not say anything."* A third said *"The third lamp buzzes when we are reading."* By the time the seven minutes ended, there were **thirty-one sticky notes** on the board, and thirty-one small pieces of evidence that we all notice things in our shared life that the teacher cannot see from the front of the room. That Tuesday was the start of our class's **"one-rule-a-term" experiment**. And my argument today is simple: **every middle-school homeroom should let its students design exactly one classroom rule each term.** Not many. One.

② My first reason is **respect**. A classroom is the place where a young person spends more waking hours than any other, apart from sleep. At fourteen, I sit at the same wooden desk, next to the same classmate, under the same lamp, for roughly **6.5 hours a day, 200 days a year** — about **1,300 hours**. That is more than a whole year's worth of weekends. It is not unreasonable to ask that students, who live in this room, have one small real voice over its rules. Teachers, of course, still set the deep frame — attendance, homework, safety. But for the small daily "how we live with each other" rules, the people who live in the room should be allowed, in a careful and ordered way, to shape one of them each term.

③ My second reason is **quality**. Students see things teachers do not. Teachers spend most of the lesson at the front; they rarely see how it feels to sit, all day, under the fourth-row lamp, or to be the one whose desk is right next to the very cold north window, or to be the one whose classmate slams the door twice a day. A 2024 survey across **36 middle schools in Jiangsu**, involving more than 6,200 students, found that **80%** of the "small problems that hurt a school day" were **never noticed by teachers** until they were written down on paper by students. When students help design one small rule, the rule tends to be **very sharp**, **very specific**, and **surprisingly enforceable** — because it comes from an actual bruise.

④ My third reason is that **writing a rule is, by itself, an education**. In our class last term, we drafted a short rule about "borrowing small things from each other". The first draft said: *"You must ask before taking."* Too vague. A second draft: *"Raise your hand and say, 'May I borrow your eraser?'"* Too formal. We argued for twenty minutes. The final version was eight words: *"Ask first, say thank you, return before lunch."* Writing those eight words taught us more about how rules work than any textbook chapter — we learned that a good rule is **specific, small, polite, and has a deadline**. That is not a trivial lesson. It is a lesson in how adult life works.

⑤ A natural worry: "Won't students just write silly rules?" The careful answer is that silliness rarely survives the drafting process. In the Jiangsu study, the **most common "silly" proposals** (for example, *"No homework on Friday"*) were written by about **15%** of students on day one, but **only 2%** of final rules were silly — because classmates, in the drafting meeting, argued them down. When you have to defend a rule in front of twenty-seven classmates whose days it will affect, you quickly stop writing rules you do not really mean. Group drafting is itself a quiet filter for honesty.

⑥ Another worry: "Won't this take too much class time?" Not really. Our class's whole rule-writing process, including the seven-minute sticky-note moment, the drafting meeting, and the final vote, took **about 75 minutes across three weeks**. That is less than one lost lesson. The "rule" then ran for the rest of the term. Small investment; long, quiet return. In the same survey, classes that used "one student rule a term" reported **28% fewer small conflicts** between students (borrowing, seat, doors, cleaning rota) by the end of the term. The numbers are modest but real.

⑦ Some teachers say, "I already talk with students." I believe them. But "talking" and "giving the pen to a student to write a rule, read it, vote on it, and hang it next to the blackboard" are not the same. The second moment is the **one minute in a term when a student's voice becomes part of the classroom's written memory**. A student who has been part of writing one rule in her life treats all future rules — school rules, traffic rules, even distant laws — **with more serious attention**, not less. She has learned that rules are not always things other people write for her. Sometimes, carefully, humanly, she can help write them too.

⑧ There are **safety lines**, of course. A student-made rule must not: violate any school regulation; limit any classmate's basic rights; favour any small group; or remove the teacher's right to teach. Mr Qin, for our whole term, keeps a small card of these four lines on his desk. If a draft crosses one, the class discusses it again. In our term, **two drafts were returned** by Mr Qin: one tried to set a small "fine" for latecomers (money — crosses the school rule); another tried to forbid the back two rows from eating lunch in class (one small group — crosses the fairness line). Both were rewritten calmly. Nobody was angry. The lines make the exercise safe.

⑨ On Friday morning, the final version of our term's rule — *"Close the classroom door with two hands, gently, between lessons. Thank you."* — was written on a small white card by our monitor and hung next to the blackboard. It has been there for three weeks. The door has, in fact, been quieter. More importantly, **twenty-seven fourteen-year-olds know that they helped write one small sentence** that now lives in our classroom, and that we are careful of it. That small sentence is a small beginning. **Let the next term bring the next one.** One rule at a time is, I promise, enough.
