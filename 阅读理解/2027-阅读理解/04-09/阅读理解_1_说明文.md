# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do We Yawn?**

① You are sitting in an afternoon class. The clock ticks. The teacher explains a formula in a soft, steady voice. Suddenly, a student in the front row opens his mouth wide, takes a long breath, and closes his eyes for a moment. Two seconds later, the boy next to him does the same thing. Then a girl three rows back. **Yawning**, like a shy wave, seems to spread across the classroom. Why do we yawn at all, and why does it seem to be **"catching"**?

② For many years, people thought yawning was simply the body's way of **getting more oxygen**. The old idea was: when the room is stuffy, the brain wants more oxygen, so we open our mouths wide and drink in a large breath. This sounds reasonable, but modern research shows it is **not fully correct**. Some studies have tested people in rooms with high oxygen, low oxygen, and normal oxygen. Surprisingly, people yawned about the same number of times in all three.

③ A newer idea is that yawning helps **cool the brain**. Our brain works best at a certain temperature. When it gets a little too warm — for example, after hard thinking, in a stuffy room, or after a big lunch — the body may send a yawn signal. A wide yawn pulls in cool air, stretches the jaw, and helps send cooler blood into the head. In this way, yawning is like a small air conditioner for the tired brain.

④ Yawning is also closely linked to **sleep and tiredness**. When we are about to sleep, or just waking up, we yawn more often. One reason is that our body slows down the flow of certain chemicals near bedtime, and a yawn can gently shake us awake enough to climb into bed. Babies start yawning even before they are born — pictures of unborn babies in the mother's body have clearly shown **tiny mouths opening wide**.

⑤ Then there is the most mysterious part: **yawning is contagious**. When you see someone yawn — or even read about yawning, as you may be doing now — you may feel a soft urge to yawn yourself. Scientists think this is connected to **empathy**, the human ability to share feelings. In studies, people who score higher on empathy tests are more likely to yawn after seeing others yawn. Even dogs, especially friendly ones, yawn after their owners do!

⑥ So the next time you find yourself yawning in a science lesson, do not quickly turn red. A yawn is not always a sign of being lazy. It may mean your brain is hot, your body needs a small reset, or you are simply more caring than you thought. Yawning, this everyday wide breath, is in fact a **small window into how our body and mind work together**.
