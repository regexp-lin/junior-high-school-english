# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do Stars Twinkle?**

① On a clear autumn night, far from the bright city lights, we can see **thousands of stars** painting the sky. Many of them seem to dance a little — now bright, now dim, now bright again. Even the Tang poet Li Bai wrote about this old scene: "**星汉灿烂，若出其里**." Science today gives us a calm, simple answer to the old question: **why do stars twinkle?**

② The surprising truth is that **stars do not twinkle at all**. Most stars are large balls of hot gas, giving out **steady light** for millions of years. An astronaut on the International Space Station, looking through a window, sees stars as small, clean points of light — almost no twinkling at all. So the "dancing" we see on the ground must be caused by something **between us and the stars**.

③ That "something" is our own **atmosphere** — the ocean of air around the earth. Air is not one still body. It has layers of **different temperatures and pressures**. Warm air rises; cold air falls; winds move everywhere. When a straight beam of starlight enters the atmosphere, it passes through these moving layers. Each layer **bends the light a little**, and the direction changes many times before the light reaches our eyes. Because the bending is not the same from moment to moment, the star seems to become brighter and dimmer, shifting quickly. We call this **twinkling**.

④ Interestingly, not everything in the night sky twinkles the same. **Planets usually do not twinkle** as much as stars. Why? A star is so far away that, from earth, it looks like a single point of light. Any little bending of air is enough to shake that point. A planet, such as Venus or Jupiter, is much **closer** to us. Its image is a tiny disk rather than a point. When different parts of that disk are bent at the same time, the bending effects **cancel each other out**, so the planet looks steadier. A quick way to remember: **"stars wink; planets glow."**

⑤ Twinkling also tells us something useful. Astronomers do not like twinkling — it blurs their images. That is one main reason big telescopes are built on **high mountains or even in space**. Higher places have less air above; space has no air at all. Modern telescopes also use **adaptive optics**, a clever mirror system that changes shape many times per second to fix the blurred light. Thanks to these tricks, our pictures of far-away galaxies are now sharper than ever.

⑥ Next time you stand under a night sky, look up and smile. A twinkling star is not a shy star. It is a straight, steady beam of light, playing a small game with our restless air on the way down. Through our eyes, the whole atmosphere is quietly dancing — and the stars, patient as always, are only keeping time.

**1. What is the main question of the article?**

A. Why people sing songs about stars.  
B. Why stars look like they are twinkling from the earth.

**2. What do stars really look like from outside the earth's atmosphere?**

A. They twinkle even more brightly.

D. Planets are always hidden behind the moon.

B. Because the air there is less, so twinkling is weaker.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段末 "why do stars twinkle?"  
【解析】核心为解释星星闪烁之因。A、C、D 非主题。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "small, clean points of light — almost no twinkling"  
【解析】太空中星点稳定。A、C、D 与原文不符。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "different temperatures and pressures... bends the light"  
【解析】大气层使光线弯曲。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "a tiny disk... cancel each other out"  
【解析】行星较近显面，效果抵消。A、C、D 与原文不符。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "Higher places have less air above; space has no air at all"  
【解析】减少大气干扰。A、C、D 与原文无关。故选 B。
