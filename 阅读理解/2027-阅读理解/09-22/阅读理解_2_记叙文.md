# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Old Photo in My Grandmother's Drawer**

① Last Chinese New Year, while I was helping my grandmother tidy her small wooden wardrobe, my hand touched something hard at the very back of her **second drawer**. It was a small square tin, about the size of a pencil case, painted with faded blue flowers. Inside the tin, wrapped carefully in a piece of yellow cloth, was a single **black-and-white photograph** — soft at the corners, thin as a leaf. My grandmother saw me staring, sat down beside me on the warm edge of her bed, and said quietly, "Ah. That one. I haven't opened this tin for twenty years."

② The photo showed a young woman, about eighteen years old, standing by a river. She wore a thin white blouse, long black trousers, and an open-collared coat that looked a little too big for her shoulders. In her hands she held a small wooden flute. Her hair was cut short, just below the ear, like a boy's. She was smiling, but her smile was **tired**, like someone who has just walked a long way. "That is me," said my grandmother, "in the winter of 1964, the year before I left my village."

③ I did not recognise her at first. My grandmother now is a small, gentle lady with grey hair and glasses too big for her face; in the photo, she was tall, thin, and had a face that looked ready to argue with the sky. I asked her, very slowly, where the river was. She did not answer at once. Instead she took out a **second photograph** from under the first, smaller, with a fold across the middle. On it were three people: the same young grandmother, a slim boy about the same age in a blue cap, and a taller man with a serious face and glasses. "This boy," she said, touching the image lightly with her thumb, "was my **older brother**. This man was our **village teacher**."

④ Slowly, that afternoon, a story came out that I had never heard before. My grandmother and her older brother had grown up in a small mountain village far from any city. Their parents had died early. Their teacher, Mr. Gu, had saved them — he paid for their books, taught them at night, and once, when my grandmother was nine and dangerously sick with fever, carried her on his back for **five hours** through heavy snow to the nearest town clinic. "Without him," my grandmother said, "there would have been no school, no middle school, no anything." The photo of the three of them was taken three days before my grandmother was chosen to leave the village to study in the city. "I never saw Mr. Gu again," she said. "He died the next winter, of the same kind of fever I had once had."

⑤ I did not know what to say. My grandmother closed the tin, but gently, as if trying not to wake anyone inside. She then said something I remember very clearly: "For many years I thought the photograph hurt too much to look at. But now I am old, and I think perhaps I was wrong. Forgetting is not the same as healing. Keeping a photo does not make the pain sharper. It only makes the kindness harder to lose." She pressed the tin back into my hand. "You keep it," she said. "When I am no longer here, you will know who to thank — for everything I was later able to give you."

⑥ I protested; I said the tin was hers. But she only smiled the small, firm smile I know so well, and closed my fingers around it. That night, after all the relatives had gone home, I opened the tin again on my desk. In the corner of the second photo, under a small scratch I had not noticed before, I saw a tiny line of **pencil** writing: *"Winter, 1964 — for those who do not forget."* I did not cry — I was too surprised. But for a long time I sat there, looking at three faces from sixty years ago, thinking about the fact that my own life, my school, my books, my pocket-sized English dictionary, all quietly **reached back through a long chain** into the kindness of a mountain teacher I would never meet.

⑦ I am now in Grade 8. The blue-flower tin sits on the top shelf of my bookcase, beside my English textbooks. I take it down once or twice a year. I do not show it to many friends. But whenever I feel tired of a difficult exam or annoyed about a small thing, I look at the young grandmother by the river, and I remember my grandmother's words: **"Forgetting is not the same as healing."** Some gratitude, I am learning, does not shout. It waits quietly in a small blue tin, at the back of a drawer, for a grandchild to find — and then to carry forward.

---

**注释：** wardrobe 衣柜；tin 铁盒；blouse 女式衬衫；clinic 诊所；gratitude 感激；pencil writing 铅笔字

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What did the writer find at the back of her grandmother's second drawer?**

A. An old gold ring.  
B. A small square tin painted with faded blue flowers, which contained a single black-and-white photograph wrapped in yellow cloth.  
C. An English textbook.  
D. A bag of dry herbs.

**2. Who were the three people shown in the second, smaller photograph (Paragraph 3)?**

A. The grandmother, her older brother and their village teacher, Mr. Gu.  
B. The grandmother and her two younger sisters.  
C. Three strangers the grandmother had never met.  
D. The grandmother's three schoolmates in the city.

**3. What did the village teacher, Mr. Gu, do for the grandmother when she was nine years old and dangerously sick?**

A. He brought her some old books.  
B. He carried her on his back for five hours through heavy snow to the nearest town clinic.  
C. He wrote her a long letter.  
D. He sent her to hospital by bus.

**4. Which sentence best expresses what the grandmother learned over the years (Paragraph 5)?**

A. "Old photographs are always dangerous; it is best to burn them."  
B. "Forgetting is not the same as healing. Keeping a photo does not make the pain sharper; it only makes the kindness harder to lose."  
C. "Nothing from the past matters; we should only think about the future."  
D. "Middle-school students should not ask about family history."

**5. Why does the writer now keep the blue-flower tin on the top shelf of her bookcase (Paragraph 7)?**

A. Because it is a valuable antique worth much money.  
B. Because her grandmother is always watching from the photo.  
C. Because it reminds her, silently, of the chain of kindness behind her life, and of her grandmother's words: "Forgetting is not the same as healing."  
D. Because she wants to sell it one day.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "a small square tin ... a single black-and-white photograph wrapped in yellow cloth"  
【解析】蓝色小铁盒内一张黑白照片。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "my older brother ... our village teacher"  
【解析】照片上是奶奶、哥哥和顾老师。B、C、D 均错。故选 A。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "carried her on his back for five hours through heavy snow to the nearest town clinic"  
【解析】顾老师雪中背她五小时就医。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "Forgetting is not the same as healing ... makes the kindness harder to lose"  
【解析】奶奶悟出记忆即保留善意的道理。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨/细节题  
【定位】第⑦段 "Forgetting is not the same as healing ... reached back through a long chain into the kindness of a mountain teacher"  
【解析】铁盒是善意传承的静默提醒。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。
