# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Three Saturdays I Taught My Cousin to Ride a Bicycle**

① On Saturday morning, **4 December 2027, at 09:10**, my aunt brought my six-year-old cousin **Lele** into our courtyard, holding his small red helmet in one hand and a **brand-new pale blue 14-inch bicycle** in the other. The bicycle had two small black training wheels on either side of the back wheel, a white basket in front, and one single brass bell that went *"ding"* when you touched it. Lele looked at the bicycle. He looked at the concrete courtyard, which was about **40 metres long and 12 metres wide**, empty at this hour on a December Saturday. He looked at me. Then he said, in a very small serious voice, *"Yiran-jie, I am very afraid of falling. But today I want to learn."* I was fourteen. I had learnt to ride a bicycle at eight. I had not, in my whole life, taught anyone anything. I crouched down to his height, looked into his careful small eyes, and said, *"Lele, we will go slowly. Three Saturdays. If on the third Saturday you still cannot ride, we will not mind. Is that fair?"* He nodded twice.

② Before the first ride, I did three small careful things. **One:** I helped him put on his helmet and tightened the chin strap until I could slide exactly **two fingers** under it — my father had taught me this when I was eight. **Two:** I checked the bicycle. I pressed both tyres with my thumb — firm, not soft. I squeezed both brakes — the back brake was a bit loose; I tightened the small silver nut with my father's little spanner. I turned the pedals backward twice to check the chain. **Three:** I drew, with a small piece of school chalk, **two parallel white lines** on the courtyard concrete, about **1.5 metres apart**, fifteen metres long. *"Lele,"* I said, *"this is our road. Between the two lines, not outside."* He smiled at this — he loved rules.

③ **Saturday One (4 December).** For the first hour, we did not touch the pedals at all. The training wheels stayed on. I held the back of his seat firmly with my right hand, and he simply sat on the saddle, both feet touching the ground, and **walked the bicycle forward**, one foot at a time, between my two chalk lines. *"Feel the balance,"* I said, *"not with your arms. With your stomach."* At first he gripped the handlebars so hard his knuckles turned white; I asked him to loosen his fingers *"as if you were holding a small sleepy hamster."* He laughed. His fingers relaxed. After twenty minutes, we tried slow pedalling with the training wheels still on. He wobbled once, almost stopped. I jogged along beside him, holding the back of the saddle with only two fingers now. At **10:40**, he rode a full 15 metres without a foot touching the ground. He cheered. I cheered. His mother cheered from the bench. I did not, that day, remove the training wheels. We went inside for a plate of warm dumplings at 11:20.

④ **Saturday Two (11 December).** The courtyard was **colder**: the air was about **3 °C** and a small sharp wind came from the west. I brought an extra thin scarf for Lele. This was the hard day. I sat him down on the small stone bench at the edge of the courtyard and said, very calmly, *"Lele, today I am going to take off the two training wheels. The bicycle will feel different — it will want to fall to one side or the other. But I will not let go of the seat until you are ready. I promise."* He looked at the two small black wheels as I unscrewed them with my father's red spanner. His eyes became very serious. I asked him, *"Do you trust me?"* He said, *"Yes, Yiran-jie."* I had never, in my fourteen years, been trusted quite so seriously before.

⑤ We started again between the two chalk lines. The first time Lele pushed off without training wheels, the bicycle fell strongly to the left — my hand, still on the back of the seat, caught it softly. He did not fall. He was surprised. *"The bicycle is heavier without the little wheels,"* he said. *"Yes,"* I said. *"But your body is stronger than the bicycle. You just have not believed it yet."* We tried again. And again. For thirty long minutes, I walked behind him, bent slightly at the waist, one hand on the seat, jogging in small careful steps. I did not shout. I did not pull. Twice, when he started to wobble badly, I said one quiet small word: *"Pedal."* Movement, I had learnt at eight, is the only real cure for wobbling. At **10:55**, for about **seven metres**, I felt my hand stop carrying any weight. The bicycle, and Lele, were upright on their own. I did not tell him yet. I let my fingers rest, lightly, for three more seconds, just touching the back of the seat. Then, very gently, I let go. He rode for another **four metres**. Then he looked back — and the moment he looked back, he wobbled and gently placed his left foot on the ground.

⑥ He turned. His face was the colour of a small red winter apple. *"Yiran-jie!"* he cried. *"I felt — I felt it! Your hand was not there!"* *"For how long?"* I asked, as calmly as I could. He thought for a careful second. *"Three seconds. No — four."* I smiled. *"Four seconds is the hardest part of learning to ride. The rest is just more of the same four seconds."* He believed me instantly. Children at six believe what older cousins say to them with quiet eyes. We did four more runs. On the last one, he rode **about 12 metres** before he stopped with his own brakes — alone. His mother quietly wiped her eyes. We did not stay long. The cold air was making his cheeks too red. We went up for hot milk at 11:30.

⑦ **Saturday Three (18 December, today).** This morning, the sky was blue and surprisingly windless. At 09:05, Lele arrived in our courtyard riding his own bicycle **all by himself**, holding the handlebars loosely, his mother walking far behind him. I had not expected this. He rode up to me, braked cleanly, put one foot down, and said, with a small businesslike face, *"Yiran-jie, I can ride now. Today I want to learn to **turn**."* I laughed until I cried. His mother laughed. The old grandfather doing tai-chi at the far end of the courtyard laughed.

⑧ So today, I drew with chalk a large **gentle circle** in the middle of the courtyard — **5 metres across** — and taught him the three simple rules of a slow turn: *"Look where you want to go. Lean very slightly into the turn. Do not brake in the middle of a curve."* He circled the chalk ring at first carefully, then smoothly, then — after twenty-five minutes — with a small confident grin on his face that I had not seen in any of the previous three weeks. Toward the end, he rode the circle with one hand off the handlebars, waving at me. I shouted, *"Two hands, please!"* He laughed and put both hands back.

⑨ At **11:40**, as we walked upstairs for lunch, Lele held my hand tightly. *"Yiran-jie,"* he said, *"you did not shout once in three Saturdays."* I was quiet for a moment. I had not really noticed. I had, simply, remembered something: my own father, the December I was eight, had not shouted either. He had jogged behind me in the cold for three Saturdays and had said, exactly twice, one quiet small word: *"Pedal."* That was all. *"Lele,"* I said, squeezing his small hand back, *"the best teachers in a family do not shout. They just jog behind you for three cold Saturdays."* He thought about this for the whole elevator ride. Then, with the small serious face of a six-year-old who had become, in three cold weeks, one small honest centimetre taller, he said, *"When I have a younger cousin, I will also not shout."* I did not answer. I only held his hand, which was still, beautifully, the hand of a boy who had learnt to ride a bicycle — and one small thing more.

**1. According to Paragraph ②, what three small careful things did the writer do BEFORE Lele's first ride?**

After thirty long minutes with the training wheels removed — the writer walking behind in small careful steps, never shouting or pulling, and saying only one quiet small word, "Pedal", whenever he wobbled badly — at about 10:55, for about seven metres, the writer felt her hand stop carrying any weight; she let her fingers rest, lightly, for three more seconds, then gently let go, and Lele rode another four metres on his own before looking back, wobbling, and placing his left foot on the ground; when he turned, his face the colour of a small red winter apple, he cried "Yiran-jie! **5. What is the main lesson the writer learns from her own father at the end of the passage?**

A. Shouting is the best way to teach children.  
B. Only buying expensive bicycles teaches a child to ride.  
C. At 11:40, as they walked upstairs for lunch holding hands, Lele told the writer "You did not shout once in three Saturdays"; the writer realised she had, in fact, simply remembered that her own father the December she was eight had not shouted either, but had jogged behind her in the cold for three Saturdays and said, exactly twice, one quiet small word "Pedal" — leading her to tell Lele that "the best teachers in a family do not shout; they just jog behind you for three cold Saturdays," and Lele, thinking about this on the elevator ride, promised that when he had a younger cousin he too would not shout.
