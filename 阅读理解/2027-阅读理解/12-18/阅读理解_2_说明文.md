# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Does a Simple Compass Find North?**

① On a cold December afternoon, if you place a small round magnetic compass on a flat library table in your school, close your eyes, spin the compass gently three times, and then open your eyes, **the red end of the tiny needle will always — after a few quiet seconds of trembling — settle, pointing in the same direction**. You have not plugged it in. You have not opened a map app. You have not connected to any satellite. And yet the needle, a small piece of magnetised metal no bigger than a grain of rice, knows, quietly, exactly where north is. How? The answer turns out to be one of the most beautiful small facts of our planet: **you are standing, right now, inside an enormous invisible magnet**, and the compass is simply a tiny polite visitor who nods to it.

② **Finding one: Earth itself is a giant magnet.** Deep inside the Earth, at a depth between about **2,900 km and 5,150 km**, there is a layer called the **outer core** — an ocean of molten iron and nickel, about **2,300 km thick**, at temperatures of roughly **4,000 °C to 5,000 °C**. This hot molten metal is not sitting still. It moves in slow, vast, spiral currents, driven by the rotation of the Earth and the small temperature difference between the solid inner core and the cooler mantle above. Moving liquid metal is, in the language of physics, an **electric current** — and every electric current, as the 19th-century Danish scientist **Hans Christian Ørsted** discovered in 1820, creates a magnetic field around it. The result of billions of such moving spirals of iron, over the whole outer core, is a single enormous **planetary magnetic field** that stretches from the centre of the Earth outward, past the surface, past the Moon's orbit, to about **65,000 km into space** on the side facing the Sun (and much further on the opposite side). We live inside this field. We cannot see it, feel it, or taste it. But a small compass needle can feel it perfectly.

③ **Finding two: a compass needle is just a small, free-to-turn magnet.** A compass needle is not magic. It is a tiny strip of steel (or similar metal) that has been **magnetised** in a factory — meaning its own tiny iron atoms have been lined up so that their small internal magnetic fields all point, together, in the same direction along the needle. One end of the needle therefore has a magnetic **north pole**, and the other end has a magnetic **south pole**. The needle is then placed on a sharp thin pivot (a small upright pin, usually made of brass), with a tiny drop of oil or a jewel bearing so it can rotate very freely. Now a simple physics rule takes over: **any free-to-turn magnet, placed inside an outside magnetic field, will slowly rotate until its own north pole lines up with the outside field.** Earth's field points (roughly) from the **Earth's geographic south** toward the **Earth's geographic north**. So the north end of any compass needle, after spinning, settles pointing (roughly) toward geographic north. That is the whole story.

④ **Why "roughly" north — not exactly north?** Because of one small beautiful complication. The **geographic North Pole** — the top of the Earth's spinning axis — is **not** the same point as the **magnetic North Pole** — the place where Earth's magnetic field actually points straight down into the ground. Currently (2024 measurements, World Magnetic Model, published by the British Geological Survey and NOAA), the magnetic North Pole sits in the **Arctic Ocean, around 86° N, 139° W**, about **400 km** from the geographic North Pole, and — this is the beautiful part — **it moves**. It has moved from northern Canada in 1900, across the Arctic, toward Siberia, at a current speed of about **55 km per year**. The difference between where your compass points and true geographic north is called the **magnetic declination**. In Beijing today (2025), declination is about **-6°** (the compass points 6° west of true north). Careful hikers, sailors, and pilots learn to correct for this.

⑤ **Finding three: the Earth's magnetic poles have flipped many times.** Deep in the rocks of the Atlantic Ocean floor, when scientists in the 1960s measured the direction of small fossilised magnetism in the lava stripes, they found that the Earth's magnetic north and south have **swapped places** many times — at least **184 times** over the last **83 million years**. The last full reversal, called the **Brunhes–Matuyama reversal**, happened about **780,000 years ago**. During such a flip, which takes roughly **1,000 to 10,000 years** to complete, the magnetic field does not simply turn upside down instantly; it weakens, becomes briefly multipolar, and then reorganises around a new stable north. A school compass during a future flip will, slowly, start pointing in a very different direction than today. There is no reason to worry — such flips have happened many times during human history (well, pre-human history) without harming life.

⑥ **What disturbs a compass?** A few small quiet things.

| Disturber | How it affects the compass | How to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| **Iron and steel objects** (radiators, metal chairs, your pencil-case clip) | Local metal has its own small magnetic moments that distort the nearby field | Hold the compass away from large metal objects, at least 50 cm |
| **Electric wires carrying current** (in walls, behind a TV) | Moving charge creates a local magnetic field that can shift the needle | Avoid using a compass right against a wall socket or a laptop |
| **Strong permanent magnets** (fridge magnets, some earphones, some iPad covers with magnetic clasps) | A close strong magnet completely overpowers Earth's gentle field | Keep such magnets more than 30 cm away |
| **Solar storms** (large bursts from the Sun) | Waves of charged particles briefly distort the upper parts of Earth's field | Rare; barely noticeable on small school compasses |
| **Magnetic rocks** (iron-rich mountains) | Local stones with strong magnetisation can pull the needle | Mountaineers learn to cross-check with a second compass |

⑦ **A 3,000-year-old idea.** The compass, quietly, is not a recent invention. Around the 2nd century BCE, **Chinese scholars in the Han dynasty** first discovered that a natural stone called **lodestone** (a naturally magnetised lump of magnetite) would swing, when suspended, toward the south. By the **11th century (Song dynasty)**, Chinese sailors were using small iron-needle compasses floating on water bowls to cross the South China Sea at night — a full two centuries before Europeans adopted the same tool. The needle you spin today in a school library, therefore, is a direct quiet descendant of a Chinese fisherman's bowl of water, one thousand years ago.

⑧ **Why does the compass still matter in the age of GPS?** A GPS phone tells you, with great accuracy, **where** you are — but it does not tell you **which way you are facing**, unless you start walking. A compass tells you which way you are facing while you are standing still, without batteries, without a satellite, without electricity. In a power cut, in a deep forest, in a boat adrift, on a winter mountain when the phone is cold-dead, the small needle still quietly points. The **Himalayan mountain-rescue services** (Nepal, 2022 data) still require **two non-electronic compasses per rescue team**, by protocol. GPS is newer; the compass is older; both, wisely used, keep humans unlost.

⑨ **The quiet lesson.** Put a cheap compass on your winter desk. Watch the red end tremble, search, and settle. You are looking at one of the shortest, cleanest demonstrations of deep physics available in any living room: a small lined-up magnet rotating, in perfect silence, inside the enormous invisible magnetic field of the planet below you. No electricity. No battery. No code. Just a small piece of metal, quietly nodding to the four thousand-degree ocean of iron at the centre of the Earth — and telling you, kindly, which way is home.
