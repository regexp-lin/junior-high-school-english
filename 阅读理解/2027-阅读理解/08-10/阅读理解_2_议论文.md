# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle Schools Teach Basic Money Skills?**

① Open any teenager's school bag, and you will find a pencil case, a water bottle, and — almost always — a phone or a small wallet. Most middle-school students buy snacks, bus tickets, and birthday presents with their own money. Yet most of them have never taken a single class on how money works. Should **middle schools teach basic money skills** — how to budget, how to save, how to read a simple bill, how to avoid being cheated online? In my opinion, **yes, they should**, because money is now a part of our daily lives, because some mistakes can be painful, and because understanding money is also a way of understanding the world.

② The first reason is that **money has become part of daily teen life**. Twenty years ago, pocket money was given in coins once a week, and most spending happened in a small shop on the corner. Today, a middle-school student may buy online games, order food, watch paid videos, and tip a streamer — all with one click. Each small click is a small financial decision. If nobody teaches us how to think about money, we learn only from ads and friends, whose advice is not always wise. A short, clear class could prevent many quiet problems: the game that keeps charging every month, the "free" app with hidden fees, the "lucky pack" that takes more than it gives.

③ The second reason is **safety**. Every year, we read news stories about teenagers who lose family money through online scams, or give away their parents' bank card for a few virtual fish. Sometimes the sum is small; sometimes it is the family's savings for half a year. Scammers are professional — they speak kindly, they sound official, they use time pressure. A young student with no training is exactly their target. A simple lesson on common scams, safe passwords, and when to say "let me check with my parents" is a cheap but powerful kind of protection.

④ The third reason is **long-term thinking**. Money skills are not only about not being cheated; they are about building good habits. If a student learns at 13 to write down a simple monthly plan — "so much for snacks, so much for books, so much to save" — the habit is likely to stay for life. Starting at 13 is much easier than starting at 30, when bills, rents, and loans suddenly arrive. A small amount of training now can quietly change the shape of a future adult life.

⑤ Those who are against money classes at middle school usually say three things. First, "Middle-school students should focus on their studies and leave money to adults." Second, "Money topics are vulgar and not suitable for a school." Third, "Parents should teach money at home."

⑥ These views sound reasonable, but they are not strong enough. Separating "study" from "real life" is a false idea; real learning includes the life we actually live. Middle-school students already use money every day, and refusing to teach them does not stop them from using it — it only stops them from using it well. Saying money is "vulgar" is an old idea. Calmly planning a family budget is not vulgar; losing 5,000 yuan to a fake app is far less dignified. And about parents: many parents would like to teach their children about money, but they are busy, and some of them never learned themselves. School is exactly the place to make sure **every** child, rich or not, gets at least the basics.

⑦ Some teachers worry that a money class would become a "selling class" or push children to think like little businessmen. This worry is fair, but it describes a bad teacher, not a bad subject. A good money class is not about "how to get rich quickly" — it is about **how not to lose what you have**, how to give thoughtful gifts, how to support a charity, how a water bill is paid, how a school trip is priced. It is, in a way, a **citizen class**: understanding the boring but important machinery behind daily life.

⑧ A possible plan is simple. One class every two weeks, not more; 20 minutes at the end of a Friday life-skills lesson; topics such as "the difference between needs and wants", "reading a mobile bill", "three common online scams", "how to save for a goal". No homework, no test score — just short conversations. Schools in some cities have tried this kind of lesson and the students often say it is their most useful class of the week.

⑨ So, should middle schools teach basic money skills? My answer is yes. Not because we want students to love money more, but because we want them to understand it more clearly — so that money serves them, and not the other way round. A school that teaches us the rules of language, the laws of physics, and the dates of history should also teach us the simple, silent rules of the money we already hold in our hands.

**1. What is the writer's main view?**

D. Parents alone should always teach money, and schools should stay out of it.
