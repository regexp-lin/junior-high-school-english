# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Be Allowed to Give Feedback on Their Teachers' Lessons?**

① In many universities around the world, it is perfectly normal for students to **fill in a feedback form** at the end of every term and quietly score their teachers on things like clarity, fairness and classroom atmosphere. In Chinese middle schools, however, this idea is still unusual. Most 13-year-olds never formally tell their teacher what is working in class and what is not. But in 2027, a small number of Chinese schools have begun an experiment: a **student feedback form**, filled in twice a term, to help teachers improve. This raises a sharp question: **should middle-school students be allowed to give feedback on their teachers' lessons?**

② Those in favour answer "yes" for three clear reasons. First, **nobody knows the classroom better than the students**. A teacher sees the lesson from the front; students see it from the chairs. A student can tell you whether the board is hard to read from the back row, whether the teacher speaks too fast in English, whether a worked example was skipped too quickly. These are real details that the teacher, alone at the front, may never notice. Thoughtful student feedback is, in this sense, a kind of **free lesson planning for the teacher**.

③ Second, giving feedback teaches students **how to think carefully and kindly about others' work** — a skill they will need all their lives. Writing a feedback form trains a student to say "In today's lesson, I understood the first example, but I felt lost at the second step; could we try it again?" That is a very different sentence from "Bad class today!" One sentence is useful; the other is noise. Learning to **give polite, specific feedback** is itself an education.

④ Third, a feedback system can **rebuild trust**. Students often feel that school decisions are made "to them" rather than "with them". When a school invites students to speak honestly — and then, more importantly, **acts** on some of the useful points — the relationship between classroom and office changes. Students feel seen; teachers feel supported; the school culture becomes more open.

⑤ However, opponents raise real worries. First, **teenagers can be unfair**. A strict teacher who gives hard homework may be more useful than a popular teacher who tells jokes; but a quick feedback form may reward the fun teacher and punish the strict one. Second, **feedback can be misused**. In some countries, student evaluations have been shown to contain biases against female teachers, older teachers, and teachers from minority backgrounds. If a Chinese school copies the system without care, the same thing could happen here. Third, **feedback may hurt relationships**. A shy Grade-7 student who secretly gave a low score may feel strangely guilty every time she sees her teacher in the hallway afterwards. Is that cost too high?

⑥ There is also a **privacy** worry. If students sign their names on the forms, some will hide their real opinions, afraid of small revenge. If forms are fully anonymous, some students may write cruel or silly things under the cover of secrecy. A feedback system must solve both problems at once, which is not easy.

⑦ My own view is that careful feedback should be **allowed and welcomed**, but the design matters as much as the idea. A sensible model would include at least **four rules**. First, feedback is **never used alone** to decide a teacher's bonus or job; it is only one of several sources (along with lesson observation, exam results and parent views). Second, forms are **anonymous**, but each form must include **at least one concrete example** and **one suggestion** — pure complaining is discarded. Third, students are given a **short class** before their first feedback form on **how to write fair, specific feedback**. Fourth, teachers receive the summary **privately**, and are invited, if they wish, to share one change they plan to make with the class.

⑧ **Students also have a duty.** The right to give feedback comes with the duty to give it **honestly and kindly**. A hard teacher is not a bad teacher; a funny teacher is not automatically a good one. Before writing a low score because "the lesson was boring", a thoughtful student should ask himself: *Did I try to listen? Did I do the homework? Have I told the teacher what I did not understand?* Feedback that begins in self-honesty is worth ten times more than feedback given in a rush on the last day of term.

⑨ In the end, letting middle-school students give feedback on their teachers is **not about giving power over adults**. It is about learning to speak clearly, listen humbly, and share the work of making a classroom better. A school that teaches students this — gently, carefully, and with fair rules — is not weaker; it is quietly stronger. And the students leaving that school may just carry this habit into their future offices, families and communities, where people of every age must learn to say, politely and usefully, **"Here is what I see, and here is what I suggest."**

**1. What is the main question of the essay?**

A. Whether middle-school students should be allowed to choose their teachers.  
B. Whether middle-school students should be allowed to give feedback on their teachers' lessons.  
C. Whether middle schools should cancel exams.  
D. Whether teachers should grade each other.

**2. According to Paragraph 2, why do supporters say students should give feedback?**

A. Because students can punish teachers they dislike.  
B. Because nobody knows the classroom better than the students themselves, who notice things like board visibility, speaking speed, or unclear steps — thoughtful feedback is "a kind of free lesson planning for the teacher".  
C. Because students want higher marks.  
D. Because teachers want less work.

**3. Which concern is NOT mentioned by the opponents (Paragraphs 5–6)?**

A. Teenagers can be unfair and may reward fun teachers over strict but useful ones.  
B. Feedback can contain biases against female, older, or minority teachers if copied without care.  
C. A feedback system may hurt relationships and raise difficult privacy issues — signed forms may silence honest opinions; anonymous forms may cover cruelty.  
D. Feedback will force students to repeat all their homework.

**4. What does the writer suggest in Paragraph 7 as a sensible design?**

A. Use the feedback alone to decide a teacher's job or bonus.  
B. Use signed forms where every student writes their full name and ID.  
C. Use anonymous forms, each of which must include at least one concrete example and one suggestion; provide a short class on how to give fair specific feedback; never use the forms alone to judge a teacher; deliver the summary privately.  
D. Ban feedback altogether.

**5. What is the main message of the last paragraph?**

A. Letting students give feedback is not about power over adults, but about learning to speak clearly, listen humbly, and share the work of making a classroom better; a school that teaches this is quietly stronger.  
B. Students should run the school completely on their own.  
C. Teachers should stop teaching and only listen to students.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】段落主旨题  
【定位】第①段末 "should middle-school students be allowed to give feedback on their teachers' lessons?"  
【解析】核心议题明确。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "nobody knows the classroom better than the students ... free lesson planning for the teacher"  
【解析】学生视角独特，反馈有价值。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节判断题  
【定位】第⑤⑥段列出三大担忧  
【解析】"强迫重做作业"未提。A、B、C 均为原文担忧。故选 D。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】作者观点题  
【定位】第⑦段四条规则  
【解析】C 最符合作者方案。A、B、D 均被作者否定。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】主旨/观点题  
【定位】第⑨段 "not about giving power over adults ... share the work of making a classroom better"  
【解析】末段升华反馈的教育价值。B、C、D 均偏离。故选 A。
