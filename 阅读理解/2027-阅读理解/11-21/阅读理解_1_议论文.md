# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle Schools Teach a Short Course on Everyday Money?**

① On a cold Monday morning our maths teacher set the class a small puzzle: "You have 200 yuan in your bank account from last year's lucky-money; you earn 10 yuan each week for small house chores; one hoodie costs 149 yuan; a second-hand bicycle costs 380 yuan. How long do you need to wait before you can buy the bicycle, if you want to keep at least 50 yuan for an emergency?" Twenty seconds of silence. Then hands began to rise — slowly. Half of the class could not name **what a bank account actually is**. Two students admitted, quietly, that they had never held a 100-yuan note in their own hand. This small morning gave me one clear idea: **middle schools should teach a short, practical course on everyday money — just twelve lessons a year, carefully designed**.

② The first argument is simple: **we will meet money long before we meet most of our other school subjects in real life**. We probably will not use quadratic equations tomorrow, and many of us will not use advanced biology at university. But every student, almost from Grade 7 onwards, **handles money every week** — school lunch cards, mobile-phone recharges, small online gifts on app stores, paper envelopes at Chinese New Year, sometimes even helping a parent scan a QR code at the market. If a skill is this close to daily life, a school that ignores it is pretending the world does not exist.

③ The second argument is that **money is where small mistakes become big ones**. A student who does not understand how a "free trial" can **quietly become a monthly fee** on a parent's phone, can unknowingly waste 200 yuan in a year. A student who does not understand how **online gifts in game streams** work, can send a month of pocket money in a single evening. A student who does not understand the simple rule **"Interest is money that works for you — or against you"** can, five years later as a young worker, find herself paying the same debt twice. A twelve-lesson course cannot prevent every mistake; but it can make the most common ones much less likely.

④ Some people say, "Money is the parents' job." I respect this, but I disagree. In 2024, a city-wide poll of 1,800 middle-school families found that **only 37%** of parents had ever had a structured talk with their child about money — most families just mentioned it when something went wrong ("You should not have bought that"). Parents often want to teach, but feel unsure themselves. A clear, fair, **school course is not a replacement for family talks; it is a warm invitation to start them**. After each lesson, a small "home talk task" — such as "ask an adult family member about their first salary" — brings the school and the dinner table together.

⑤ Others worry, "A money course will turn children into small calculators; they will care too much about money." I understand this worry, but I think it misreads the course. A good everyday-money course is not about **getting more money**; it is about understanding **time, fairness, and care**. Its lessons include: *"A careful budget is a form of kindness to your future self."* *"A charity donation is also a money decision."* *"A loan from a friend still carries an invisible weight."* These are moral lessons with numbers; they do not narrow the heart — they widen it.

⑥ What should a good twelve-lesson course include? Here is my own modest draft: (1) **What is money, really** (barter, coins, notes, digital); (2) **Budget a school week** (a small paper spreadsheet, 45 min); (3) **The real cost of "free"** (trials, ads, attention); (4) **Wants vs needs** (with real photos from classmates, anonymous); (5) **Saving for a small goal** (four-month project, a real item under 400 yuan); (6) **Simple interest, simple inflation** (numbers kept small); (7) **Loans between friends** (the invisible weight); (8) **Online-shopping pauses** (the 24-hour rule); (9) **Scams that look friendly**; (10) **Giving well** (charity, family gifts, red envelopes); (11) **First part-time job, safely**; (12) **Talk with an adult you trust**. Twelve lessons. No hard exam. One simple **personal-budget diary** kept for two months.

⑦ A thoughtful worry remains: **"Will a money course make poorer students feel ashamed?"** This is a serious question. The answer lies in the teacher's design. No real family amount is ever asked aloud. All classroom examples use **fictional, clearly labelled** numbers ("imagine a family with 5,000 yuan a month"). Anonymous cards, not hands, are used for any survey. A good money course should make every student stronger; it should never make any family smaller. Done with care, it is one of the most equal lessons a school can give — because it gives the same tools to all, regardless of whose home the student goes back to in the evening.

⑧ Tomorrow, after the morning flag ceremony, I will bring the class's small proposal to the school office: *twelve lessons a year, no heavy exam, one teacher volunteering.* Even if the answer is a cautious "we will think about it," the conversation is already worth starting. Because — and this is the quiet point of the whole argument — **a school that sends us out into a world full of prices, choices, and quiet money decisions, and yet never spends twelve lessons on any of it, is like a school that teaches swimming only on dry land**. The pool is already here; we are already in it. The only honest question is whether the school will give us the small, simple tools to swim well.
