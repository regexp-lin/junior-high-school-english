# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Is the Sea Salty?**

① Most of us have at some time swallowed a mouthful of seawater at the beach. The taste is not pleasant — a strong, heavy **salt** that stings the throat. On average, one litre of seawater contains about **35 grams of salt**, roughly seven normal teaspoons. That means the sea is about **three times saltier than soup**. Where does all this salt come from? And — a question children often ask — **why is the water in lakes and rivers not salty, then?**

② The answer is a slow, patient story that is still happening under our feet today. It begins with **rain**. When raindrops fall to earth, they are almost pure water at the top of the cloud, but on the way down they pick up a small amount of **carbon dioxide** from the air. This makes the rain **very slightly acidic**, weaker than lemon juice but stronger than pure water. Over millions of years, this slightly acidic rain hits the rocks of mountains and fields.

③ The rocks contain many different **minerals**: sodium, magnesium, calcium, potassium. Bit by bit, the rain washes these minerals off the rocks — the way a careful wash slowly removes colour from a paper bag. The tiny amounts dissolve into streams, streams flow into rivers, and rivers flow into the sea. Every year, all the world's rivers together carry **about 4 billion tonnes of dissolved minerals** down to the sea — enough to fill a thousand large football stadiums, invisible inside the flowing water.

④ So the first surprising truth is this: **river water also contains a little salt**. It is simply too thin for our tongue to notice — about a hundred times thinner than seawater. Over **hundreds of millions of years**, however, these tiny amounts add up.

⑤ "But rivers enter the sea all the time," a careful reader might say, "so why does the sea not get freshened?" Here is the second part of the answer: the sea **has no final exit**. The sun heats the ocean surface; water **evaporates** back into the sky as clouds; but evaporation only lifts the **water** — the salt **stays behind**. When the water rises up, it is pure again; when it falls as rain, it starts a new round, and the small salts it brings back to the sea slowly pile up, year after year, like dust in an untidy corner.

⑥ There is a third source, quieter than the first two. Deep under the ocean floor, hot rocks release **minerals through underwater vents**. These openings, discovered only in the 1970s, shoot out dark clouds of mineral-rich fluid. The hot sea there can reach 400 °C without boiling, because of the pressure. The minerals slowly spread into the great body of the ocean, adding to its flavour.

⑦ People sometimes ask: **is the sea getting saltier every year?** The honest answer is that the sea is **almost in balance**. New salts arrive from rivers and vents; salts leave the sea in several ways — for example, marine animals build their shells out of calcium, dead matter drops to the floor and stays there, and in some quiet bays salt **crystallises out** when the water is very hot and shallow. For at least the last **500 million years**, the world's sea has stayed at roughly the same salt level. Our oceans are like a patient old kettle: full of salty water, receiving a slow inflow, losing it again through quiet outflows, and neither getting saltier nor sweeter.

⑧ So, the simple beach mouthful that once seemed pointless is actually telling a long, long story: of rain cutting rocks, of rivers carrying minerals, of a sun that lifts only water, and of deep vents adding quiet pinches of mineral. **Next time you taste the sea, you are tasting hundreds of millions of years of Earth's patient chemistry — served in a single drop.**

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. About how much salt is in one litre of seawater, according to Paragraph ①?**

A. 3 grams.  
B. About 35 grams, roughly seven teaspoons.  
C. 350 grams.  
D. No salt at all.

**2. Why does rain water slowly wash minerals off rocks?**

A. Because the rocks are hot.  
B. Because rain picks up carbon dioxide in the air and becomes slightly acidic, which dissolves small amounts of minerals from rocks over a long time.  
C. Because rain is heavy.  
D. Because rocks are made of sugar.

**3. Why doesn't the sea become freshened even though rivers keep pouring into it?**

A. Because rivers contain more salt than the sea.  
B. Because the sea has no final exit; the sun evaporates only the water, but the salt stays behind, so salts slowly pile up.  
C. Because rain only falls on land, not on the sea.  
D. Because the rivers are blocked.

**4. What "third quiet source" of salt does the passage mention?**

A. Fish tears.  
B. Beach sand.  
C. Underwater vents on the ocean floor, which release mineral-rich fluid discovered only in the 1970s.  
D. Modern factories on the coast.

**5. Which of the following best explains why the sea stays at roughly the same salt level?**

A. The sea is always losing all its salt at night.  
B. New salts arrive from rivers and vents, while salts leave the sea through animal shells, dead matter on the floor, and crystallisation in hot, shallow bays, keeping it almost in balance.  
C. Humans add fresh water to the sea every day.  
D. The sea exchanges water with rivers freely.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "about 35 grams of salt, roughly seven normal teaspoons"  
【解析】直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "picks up carbon dioxide ... very slightly acidic ... hits the rocks"  
【解析】微酸雨水长年作用溶解矿物。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "sea has no final exit ... evaporation only lifts the water ... salt stays behind"  
【解析】无出口 + 只蒸发纯水，盐分累积。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 "underwater vents ... mineral-rich fluid ... discovered only in the 1970s"  
【解析】深海热泉为第三来源。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段 "New salts arrive ... salts leave ... almost in balance"  
【解析】进出平衡三途。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。
