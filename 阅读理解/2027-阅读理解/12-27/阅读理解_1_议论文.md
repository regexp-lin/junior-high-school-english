# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle School Keep One "Slow-Walk Hour" Each Week?**

① On **Wednesday afternoons at 15:30**, after the last regular class, something unusual has been happening at Lihua Middle School for eleven weeks now. From **15:30 to 16:30** — one whole hour — the school opens its two small back gardens, the east running track, and the west willow path, and **invites any student who wishes** to walk — **slowly**, in silence or in quiet pairs, with no phones, no running, no loud groups, no homework in hand. A small chalk sign by the east garden door, written by Mr Qin in his slow teacher handwriting, says simply, *"Walk at the pace of a grandmother. Think at the pace of a cloud."* We call it our **"Slow-Walk Hour"** (慢步一小时). It was started on 13 October 2027 as a small, quiet experiment for Grade-8 Class B only, but by early December, **two Grade-7 classes and one Grade-9 class had quietly joined in**. **I believe every middle school in China should keep one Slow-Walk Hour each week — 60 minutes, one afternoon, outdoors, optional, silent or quiet, starting this spring term.**

② **The first reason is about eyes.** A Grade-8 student in a typical Chinese city spends, by conservative estimate, **more than 11 hours a day looking at things less than 60 centimetres from her face** — textbooks, exercise books, notebooks, phones, tablets, test papers, whiteboard writing, and canteen trays. The eye, scientists now agree, was not made for this much **close-distance focusing**. Every few days, the eye muscle needs long moments of **far-distance looking** — pine tree tops, hill outlines, cloud edges, street-corner rooftops 300 metres away — to relax the tiny muscle fibres that control the lens. **A Slow-Walk Hour is, quite simply, sixty minutes of honest far-away looking**, and that alone is a small gift to every pair of middle-school eyes in China. Our school's nurse, Ms Fang, told me in the school clinic last week that in the eleven weeks since the Slow-Walk Hour began, the average number of weekly eye-strain headache complaints in 8-B has dropped from 6 per week to 2. **That is a real number, built on nothing more than quiet walking.**

③ **The second reason is about thinking.** Many of us, myself included, used to believe that "thinking well" means **sitting still at a desk with a sharp pencil in hand**. This is only half true. There is another kind of thinking — the kind the poet Ba Jin described when he said the best sentences he ever wrote came to him on slow afternoon walks along the Huangpu River — that only happens when **the body is moving gently and the mind is not being forced**. Teachers at Lihua have begun to notice that students return from Slow-Walk Hour with small quiet ideas about problems they had been stuck on all week: a geometry puzzle suddenly becomes clear; a difficult English composition opens up; a small argument with a friend is quietly forgiven. The body's slow rhythm, it seems, gives the brain **permission** to rearrange the furniture of the mind. **And a brain that can rearrange itself, quietly, once a week, is a calmer and sharper brain.**

④ **The third reason is about classmates.** Our school has 27 students in one class; we sit together for 6 hours a day, but how often do two Grade-8 girls who do not share a homework table really talk? Almost never. In a noisy classroom, quiet friendships are slow to grow; on a slow garden path, with no phones and no shouting, a gentle sentence between two students carries much further. In the past eleven weeks, I have spoken, for the first proper time, with Lin Ruoxi (the quietest girl in 8-B), Shen Kexin (the train-signal engineer's daughter), and Shi Yifan (whose mother is a sanitation worker). Three real conversations in eleven weeks. That is, for a thirteen-year-old, a small personal miracle. **Slow-Walk Hour is a place where, without any teacher forcing "team-building games", quiet classmates quietly meet each other — and that may be the warmest outcome of all.**

⑤ **Some readers may worry that 60 minutes of walking is wasted study time.** I respectfully disagree. A middle-school week already contains **between 38 and 42 classroom hours** of study. One outdoor walking hour is less than **3% of that week**. If those 60 minutes reduce one eye-strain headache, unlock one stuck homework problem, and begin one quiet friendship, the 60 minutes have repaid themselves many times over. **Good learning is never only the hours at the desk; it is also the hours during which the brain is allowed, gently, not to be at a desk.**

⑥ **Some readers may worry that unsupervised walking is unsafe.** At Lihua, two homeroom teachers are already at the garden gate during every Slow-Walk Hour, **quietly walking too**, not shouting orders. Running is politely discouraged (a soft sign reads, *"Please walk. Running is for the track on Fridays."*). Phones are left in classroom lockers. Three simple rules, eleven weeks, **zero incidents**. Safety, it turns out, grows stronger — not weaker — when a school trusts its students to walk without supervision noise.

⑦ Across China, schools spend millions of yuan each year on complicated health programs, eye-care software, stress-reduction apps, and mindfulness workshops. **A Slow-Walk Hour costs nothing but 60 minutes of a calm willow path and a small handwritten chalk sign.** It does not require a new building, a new subject, a new textbook, or a new test. It requires only the small quiet choice of one teacher, one Wednesday afternoon, to open a garden gate and trust the students to walk slowly and think slowly for one humble hour a week. I believe that quiet choice is available to every middle school in China — from a 27-student class in a small county in Yunnan to a 52-class campus in central Beijing. **Open the gate. Slow the pace. Trust the young. Wait for the hour.** That, I think, is one of the smallest and wisest hours a Chinese middle school can give its students this spring.

**注释：** Slow-Walk Hour 慢步一小时；eye strain 视疲劳；lens 晶状体；mindfulness 正念；supervision 监督
