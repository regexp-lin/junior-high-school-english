# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Seat I Offered on the Long-Distance Bus**

① The bus from our small town back to the city takes about two hours. Every other weekend I sit in seat **12C** and watch the yellow autumn fields slide past the window. Seat 12C is my favourite: by the window, not too close to the door, and exactly above a **warm heating vent** in winter. Last Sunday afternoon, in late November, the vent was the only warm spot on the whole bus; the air outside was two degrees, and the driver had not yet turned the main heating on. I was wearing my grey hoodie, holding a small red apple, and looking forward to ninety quiet minutes with my book.

② At the third stop, an old man climbed onto the bus — slowly, one careful step at a time. His right hand held a thin wooden stick; his left hand held a **pale blue cloth bag** of oranges. He was probably in his late seventies. His coat was clean but very thin for November. He paid the driver with small coins, looked at the full bus, and began to walk toward the back. By the time he reached my row, he was already a little short of breath.

③ I stood up. I said, perhaps too loudly, "Grandfather, please take my seat. It is warm here." I smiled, proud of my small politeness. I had seen adults do this before; I had wanted to be the kind of person who does this. I stepped into the aisle, ready to receive his thank-you.

④ But the old man shook his head. Gently, very gently. "No, child. Thank you. Please sit back down." His voice was soft, not angry, not cold. I did not know what to do. I stood there in the aisle, my small red apple in my hand, hot with a sudden, silly kind of shame. Had I done something wrong? Had I been rude by calling him "Grandfather" too loudly? An older woman in the seat in front of me turned and smiled, not unkindly. "Child, sit down. You meant well. But don't stand."

⑤ I sat back in seat 12C, my face still warm. The old man walked three more rows, reached seat 15B on the other side — a single aisle seat near the back — and sat down slowly, placing the cloth bag on his knees. He closed his eyes. The bus began to move. I felt foolish. My idea of "the right thing" had just been, without any warning, politely returned.

⑥ Ten minutes later, the old man opened his eyes. He leaned forward and, through the gap between the seats, tapped my shoulder. His voice was still soft. "Child, may I tell you why I said no? I do not want you to feel bad. It was not because of anything you did wrong." I nodded. He spoke carefully. "I had a small heart operation two months ago. My doctor told me that, when I sit on a warm, narrow heating vent for a long time, my blood pressure can rise and I feel dizzy. Seat 15B has no vent. That is why, these days, I ask the driver for a back seat. **Your kindness was real. My body simply needed a different answer today.**"

⑦ I did not know what to say. I only said, "Thank you for telling me, Grandfather. I did not know." He smiled. "How could you know? A kind thought is not wrong just because it has a wrong shape. Next time, if an old person smiles and says no, **do not feel ashamed**. Trust that they know something about their own body that you cannot see." He paused. "If you really want to help today, you can pass me one small thing."

⑧ I looked at him, puzzled. He said, "The small red bag in your hand — the apple. If you can spare it, an apple is good for a dry throat on a cold bus." I laughed, a little embarrassed, a little relieved. I walked three rows back and handed him the apple with both hands. He held it carefully, looked at it, and said, "Thank you. Now you have given me exactly the help I actually needed." I walked back to 12C, my seat still warm, my face no longer hot with shame, only with something calmer.

⑨ The fields outside turned from yellow to a pale, thin orange. For the rest of the trip I did not open my book. I thought about how **kindness is not only giving; it is also listening**. The first lesson of helping someone, I realised, is not to decide in advance what they need, but to watch and ask. When the bus arrived at the city station, the old man stood up slowly and walked past me. He did not say goodbye; he only touched my shoulder once, very lightly. That small touch, more than the whole trip, is the part I will keep. Since that Sunday, whenever I offer my seat, I try to ask quietly first — *"Would you like this seat?"* — and then, most important of all, I listen carefully to the answer, no matter what it is.

**注释：** aisle 过道；vent 通风口；ashamed 羞愧；politeness 礼貌；operation 手术

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Why did the writer like seat 12C so much?**

A. Because it was near the door.  
B. Because it was by the window, not too close to the door, and exactly above a warm heating vent in winter — and on that November afternoon it was the only warm spot on the whole bus.  
C. Because it was the cheapest seat.  
D. Because it was next to the driver.

**2. What did the old man do when the writer offered her seat?**

A. He sat down at once.  
B. He shook his head gently, said "No, child. Thank you. Please sit back down," and walked on to sit in seat 15B near the back.  
C. He shouted at her.  
D. He took the seat and thanked her loudly.

**3. Why did the old man refuse to take seat 12C, according to his own explanation?**

A. He did not like the window.  
B. He had had a small heart operation two months ago, and his doctor told him that sitting on a warm, narrow heating vent for a long time could raise his blood pressure and make him feel dizzy; seat 15B had no vent.  
C. He did not trust the writer.  
D. He wanted to sit with friends.

**4. What did the old man ask the writer to do, as real help?**

A. Open the window.  
B. Give him her small red apple, saying an apple is good for a dry throat on a cold bus; he then told her, "Now you have given me exactly the help I actually needed."  
C. Call the driver.  
D. Buy him medicine.

**5. What is the writer's main lesson from this experience?**

A. Old people are hard to please.  
B. Kindness is not only giving; it is also listening — the first lesson of helping someone is not to decide in advance what they need, but to watch and ask; now she asks quietly "Would you like this seat?" and listens carefully to the answer.  
C. Never offer a seat to strangers.  
D. Apples are better than seats.

【答案】B

【定位】第④—⑤段  
【解析】温和拒绝 + 15B 直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

【答案】B

【答案】B

【答案】B
