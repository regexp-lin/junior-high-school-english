# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle Schools Have PE Class Every Day?**

① Ask any Chinese middle-school student how many PE classes they have each week, and the answer is usually **"two — sometimes three"**. In many schools, the rest of the week is filled with Chinese, English, maths and science. In 2025, the Ministry of Education asked all middle schools to keep **at least one hour of daily physical activity**, and some pilot schools have gone further: one PE class **every single day**. Parents are now asking a question that sounds simple but is not: **should middle schools have PE class every day?**

② Those in favour point first to a **serious health problem**. A national survey in 2024 showed that almost **60 %** of Chinese middle-school students had **short-sightedness**, and about **20 %** were **overweight**. These numbers are, doctors warn, the results of **too many hours sitting** indoors — in classrooms, at desks, in front of screens. Daily PE would give every student at least one clear break from the chair: a chance to run, jump, and breathe. In countries such as Japan and Finland, where daily exercise has long been a school habit, the rates of short-sightedness and teenage obesity are much lower.

③ A second argument is about **learning itself**. It may sound strange, but more exercise often means **better exam results**, not worse. Scientists have found that even **20 minutes** of movement can raise blood flow to the brain, sharpen focus, and improve memory for **up to two hours**. Several Chinese middle schools that added a **30-minute morning run** before first period reported fewer sleepy students, fewer discipline problems, and, in some cases, a rise in average maths and English scores. The old saying "A sound mind in a sound body" is, it turns out, supported by modern science.

④ However, opponents of daily PE raise three real worries. First, **time**. A Chinese middle-school student's day is already tight. Taking one 40-minute slot every day for PE means either **cutting 40 minutes** from another subject or **lengthening the school day** by 40 minutes. With Zhongkao approaching, many parents fear that the cut will come from English or science. Second, **weather**. China has many regions with **very cold winters, very hot summers, or polluted spring air**; forcing outdoor PE every day may do more harm than good. Third, **space and teachers**. A large school with only one small playground cannot give every class 40 daily minutes without traffic jams, and many schools already lack **qualified PE teachers**.

⑤ A more careful look shows that the debate is often about **what "daily PE" really means**. It does not have to mean the same 40-minute class every day. A sensible version might include a **longer PE class twice a week**, a **short 15-minute morning run** three days a week, and a **20-minute active break** in the middle of the afternoon, when students are most tired. Together, these add up to a **daily dose** of movement without pushing other subjects out of the timetable.

⑥ My own view is that **daily movement should become a school habit**, but the form should be flexible. Schools in the north can bring most winter activity **indoors** — skipping ropes in the corridor, a short dance routine in the classroom, simple strength movements, table tennis in the gym. Schools in smoggy areas can use air-quality apps to decide each morning whether to run outside or do **indoor exercises** instead. PE teachers should be **added, not cut**, and rainy-day plans should be written clearly in advance. The phrase should not be **"one PE class a day"** but **"one hour of safe, suitable movement a day"**.

⑦ **Parents and students**, again, have a part to play. Even on a busy school day, a family can walk together for twenty minutes after dinner, instead of all four members scrolling phones on the sofa. A student who cycles twenty minutes to school already meets much of the "one hour" target. A small change on Monday, repeated every day, is worth more than a dramatic big plan that lasts only one week.

⑧ Education is **not a race to fill every minute with textbook pages**. A student who can do long division but cannot run 800 metres without pain, who can read an English passage but cannot sleep well at night because she has moved so little all day, is not really being well prepared for life. Making room for daily movement is, in the end, not a sacrifice to academics — **it is one of the quiet things that makes good academics possible**. That is why, with careful design, the answer to the question "daily PE?" should, in my view, lean towards **yes**.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What health problem is highlighted in Paragraph 2?**

A. Most students are too thin.  
B. Almost 60 % of Chinese middle-school students have short-sightedness and about 20 % are overweight, partly because of too many hours sitting indoors.

D. Middle-school students have no time to sit down.

**2. What surprising finding about exercise and studying is mentioned in Paragraph 3?**

A. Too much exercise always lowers exam scores.  
B. Even 20 minutes of movement can raise blood flow to the brain, sharpen focus and improve memory for up to two hours; some schools with a morning run saw better grades.  
C. Only top athletes can benefit from running.  
D. A sound body has nothing to do with a sound mind.

**3. Which of these is NOT a worry raised by opponents of daily PE (Paragraph 4)?**

B. Bad weather — very cold winters, very hot summers, polluted air — can make outdoor daily PE unsafe.  
C. Many schools lack playground space and qualified PE teachers.  
D. PE makes students lose their memory for Chinese characters.

**4. What is the writer's suggested design for daily PE (Paragraphs 5–6)?**

A. Exactly the same 40-minute class every day, outdoors, no matter the weather.

D. Students should teach PE to each other while teachers rest.

**5. What is the writer's final message in Paragraph 8?**

A. Exam results are the only thing that matters; sport must be dropped.

【答案】B
