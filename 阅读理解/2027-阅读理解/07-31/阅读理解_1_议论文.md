# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Term Have a "Tech-Free Week" at Middle School?**

① Walk into any middle-school classroom during the ten-minute break, and you will see a scene that was almost unknown twenty years ago: most heads bent over a small bright screen. The phone feeds us videos, games, chats, news, and sometimes — in honest moments — nothing at all, just a quiet endless scroll. Some schools in our country have begun to try an interesting experiment: one full week each term, called a **"Tech-Free Week"**, during which students agree to put away all personal phones, tablets, and game devices, both at school and at home. Many parents and students ask: is this just a silly old-fashioned idea, or could it really help? In my view, **a well-designed Tech-Free Week is one of the best gifts a middle school can give its students, and it should become a regular part of every term.**

② The first reason is **attention**. A 2026 study followed 2,400 middle-school students in four cities for two years. Those who took part in one Tech-Free Week per term performed, on average, **9%** better on end-of-term reading tests than students in schools without such weeks — even though both groups studied the same textbook. The difference was not in the content learned but in the ability to sit with a long page, follow a long idea, and finish a long task. Phones are built to catch attention. A week without them gently teaches our brains to hold attention again.

③ The second reason is **real friendship**. During a Tech-Free Week, the corridor is louder. Students look into each other's faces at lunch. They tell full jokes instead of forwarding short ones. In a 2025 survey by Nanjing Normal University, 78% of students said their face-to-face friendships "felt closer" during the week. It is harder to feel lonely when the person in front of you is actually looking at you.

④ The third reason is **sleep**. A simple screen often becomes a sleep thief: just one more video, just one more message, and suddenly it is 23:45. During a Tech-Free Week, students go to bed earlier — not because they are more disciplined, but because the temptation is no longer in their pocket. Headteachers report that absences for headache and tiredness fall noticeably in the days right after such a week.

⑤ Of course, many adults (and quite a few students) will push back. "We need phones," some parents will say, "to reach our children in an emergency." This worry is reasonable and must be answered carefully. A good Tech-Free Week does **not** mean "no communication". Students still use a simple classroom phone or a borrowed teacher phone for real emergencies. Parents can reach the head teacher during school hours, and at home a family landline or simple dumb-phone can be used. The aim is to remove the casual, constant scroll, not the true safety call.

⑥ Another worry is about **study materials**. Many modern lessons use apps, online dictionaries, or short videos. "Are we turning back the clock?" some teachers ask. No — just slowing it for seven days. For one week, paper dictionaries replace apps; school Wi-Fi computers (used under a teacher's eye) replace personal phones; printed reading materials replace e-books. Some teachers report that students who had relied heavily on quick app answers begin to write longer, more thoughtful notes during the week. The slow path, briefly taken, strengthens the fast path we take the rest of the year.

⑦ A third worry is about **fairness**. "What about students who use helpful apps for their learning needs — a reading app, a spelling app for students with certain difficulties?" This is fair. A careful Tech-Free Week always has a list of **approved educational tools**, and the school uses these openly in class for any student who truly needs them. The week is about removing casual temptation, not useful help.

⑧ Finally, a small objection says, "One week will not change anything." It may not change everything — but it changes more than we expect. Like a short hike, a Tech-Free Week shows us how heavy our daily backpack has become. After the week, most students do not throw their phones away. But many reset their habits: they put the phone in another room at night, they delete one app they rarely use, they read a few more pages before sleep. Small changes, multiplied by four weeks a year over three years of middle school, add up to a very different life.

⑨ We are not the first generation to face a powerful new tool. Adults too are learning, quietly and slowly. A Tech-Free Week says, kindly, **that the phone is ours, not our master**. For middle-school students, the earlier they feel that truth, the longer it will protect them.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main view?**

A. All phones should be banned from middle schools forever.  
B. A well-designed Tech-Free Week should become a regular part of every term.  
C. Tech-Free Week is old-fashioned and useless.  
D. Only primary school students need Tech-Free Weeks.

**2. What did the 2026 study find about students who took part in Tech-Free Weeks?**

A. They forgot most of the textbook.  
B. They performed, on average, 9% better on end-of-term reading tests than students in schools without such weeks.  
C. They became unhappy at school.  
D. Their eyes got worse faster.

**3. How does the writer answer the worry that parents need phones for emergencies?**

A. Students can use a simple classroom phone or a teacher's phone in real emergencies; parents can reach the head teacher in school hours; the aim is to remove casual scrolling, not true safety calls.  
B. Emergencies are not important.  
C. Parents should not contact children at all during the week.  
D. All students should buy a second phone.

**4. What does the writer say about students who rely on educational apps for special learning needs?**

A. They should be forbidden from joining the school that week.  
B. The school has a list of approved educational tools and uses them openly for anyone who truly needs them.  
C. They should fail all their subjects.  
D. They must use a parent's phone in secret.

**5. What does "the phone is ours, not our master" most likely mean, in paragraph ⑨?**

A. Students should sell their phones immediately.  
B. Middle-school students should pay their own phone bills.  
C. The phone is a tool that should serve us; we should control it, not be controlled by it.  
D. Adults never had this problem.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】首段尾句  
【解析】每学期固定的 Tech-Free Week。A/C/D 均偏。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "9% better on end-of-term reading tests..."  
【解析】阅读测试高 9%。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段  
【解析】应急手段仍在，但去除日常 scrolling。B/C/D 均错。故选 A。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段  
【解析】学校有批准工具清单供有需要者使用。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑨段末句  
【解析】手机是工具，不该主宰我们。A/B/D 均偏。故选 C。
