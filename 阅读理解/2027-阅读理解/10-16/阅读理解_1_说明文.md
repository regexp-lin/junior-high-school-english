# 阅读理解（说明文） **The White Lines in the Sky: What Are Contrails?**

① On a clear autumn afternoon, look up. You will often see **long white lines** stretching across the blue sky, slowly drawn by a passing plane. Children sometimes ask: "Is that smoke? Is the plane broken?" The answer is much more interesting. Those white lines are called **contrails**, short for "condensation trails".

② A jet plane usually flies at about **10,000 metres** above the ground. Up there, the air is very cold — often around **–50°C**. At the same time, the plane's engines burn **jet fuel**, and one of the products of this burning is **water vapour**, together with hot exhaust gases. When this warm, wet exhaust meets the freezing air outside, the water vapour quickly **turns into tiny ice crystals** — the same change that turns your breath into a little white cloud on a winter morning. Millions of these ice crystals together form the white line we see.

③ Not every flight leaves a contrail. Whether a contrail appears depends mainly on **two things: temperature and humidity**. If the upper air is warm, the water vapour stays as gas and no white line forms. If the upper air is very cold but quite dry, the contrail will form, but it will quickly disappear as the ice crystals change back into invisible vapour. This is called a "**short-lived**" contrail, and it fades in a few seconds or minutes. If the upper air is very cold **and** humid, the ice crystals can last much longer and slowly spread, becoming a thin, wide cloud. This is called a "**persistent**" contrail.

④ Scientists pay attention to contrails for two reasons. First, contrails are a simple, natural sign of what is happening high in the sky. Weather scientists can read a contrail the way a farmer reads a leaf: a long, spreading contrail usually means the upper air is moist, which often brings weather changes **within 12 to 24 hours**. Second, on a global scale, persistent contrails slowly join other clouds in the sky and can slightly change how much sunlight reaches the ground — a problem now studied carefully by climate scientists.

⑤ Some people worry that contrails are poison. They are not. The main body of a contrail is simply **ice**, the same material as a cloud; and the extra gases from the engine quickly spread into the huge air around the plane. Of course, heavy flight traffic still brings real pollution near airports; that is a separate question, dealt with by cleaner fuels and better engines.

⑥ Next time you see a plane drawing a long white line above you, try this small experiment: watch the line for one minute. If it stays and slowly widens, the weather may change tomorrow. If it fades in seconds, the next day is likely to be dry and clear. **The sky is writing a simple weather report — all we have to do is learn to read it.**

**1. What are contrails made of, according to the passage?**

A. Smoke and oil.  
B. Tiny ice crystals formed when warm water vapour from the engine meets very cold high-altitude air.  
C. Coloured dust.  
D. Gas from passengers.

**2. At what height does a jet plane that makes a contrail usually fly?**

A. About 1,000 metres.  
B. About 3,000 metres.  
C. About 10,000 metres.  
D. About 50 metres.

**3. According to Paragraph ③, why may a contrail disappear in only a few seconds?**

A. The plane is flying slowly.  
B. The upper air is very cold but quite dry, so the ice crystals change back into invisible vapour.  
C. The sky is too bright.  
D. There are too many birds.

**4. What useful information can a long, spreading contrail give weather watchers?**

A. It shows that the plane will soon land.  
B. It shows that the upper air is moist, which often brings weather changes within 12–24 hours.  
C. It shows that a storm has already ended.  
D. It shows that the plane is empty.

**5. Which of the following best expresses the writer's attitude toward contrails?**

A. Contrails are dangerous poisons above cities.  
B. Contrails are useless and should be hidden.  
C. Contrails are not poison; they are a simple, readable sign of high-altitude weather that the sky writes for us.  
D. Contrails are only made by broken planes.
