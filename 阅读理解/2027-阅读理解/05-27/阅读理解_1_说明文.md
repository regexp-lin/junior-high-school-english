# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Does GPS Know Where You Are?**

① When you open a map app and a small blue dot shows your exact position on a screen, you are using something very modern and very clever: the **Global Positioning System**, usually shortened to **GPS**. You do not need to type your address. You simply open your phone, and within a few seconds the phone "knows" it is standing at the gate of the school, at a bus stop, or on the top of a hill. How does such a small device solve this puzzle? The answer starts far above our heads.

② More than 20,000 kilometres above the Earth, a group of about 30 **satellites** is travelling in big circles around the planet. Each satellite is about the size of a small car. They are carefully placed so that, from any point on Earth, at least four satellites are usually in the sky at the same time. These satellites carry very accurate clocks and keep sending out weak radio signals day and night.

③ A signal from a satellite is simple: it says, "I am satellite A. I am at this position in space. The time on my clock is 10:24:05.123". The signal travels to Earth at the speed of light, which is very fast — about 300,000 kilometres per second. But because the satellite is so far away, the signal still needs about one-tenth of a second to reach the ground.

④ Your phone also has a small clock. When the signal arrives, the phone compares the two clocks and calculates: **"How long did this signal travel?"** From the travel time, the phone works out the distance between itself and satellite A. Now imagine a ball in space: your phone must be somewhere on the surface of a giant ball around satellite A, with that distance as the ball's radius.

⑤ One ball is not enough. Your phone then listens to satellites B, C, and D. From each satellite, it gets another ball. Mathematically, three balls meet at only two points, and four balls usually meet at just one. That one meeting point **is exactly where your phone is standing**. The map app then uses this point to show the little blue dot on the screen.

⑥ Of course, GPS is not perfect. Tall buildings, thick clouds or deep valleys can block or weaken signals. That is why your blue dot sometimes "jumps" on city streets. Scientists now also use signals from Chinese **Beidou** satellites and European **Galileo** satellites to make the result more accurate. Next time you find your way in a new city with the help of a little dot, remember: you are being quietly guided by a group of far-away clocks and mathematics in the sky.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】全文围绕 GPS 原理  
【解析】文章解释 GPS 如何定位。A/C/D 均非主题。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "More than 20,000 kilometres above the Earth..."  
【解析】高度超两万千米。A/B/D 均错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "I am satellite A. I am at this position in space. The time on my clock is..."  
【解析】卫星名、位置、时间。A/C/D 均无据。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "three balls meet at only two points, and four balls usually meet at just one."  
【解析】多星定位方可确定位置。B/C/D 均无据。故选 A。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 "Tall buildings, thick clouds or deep valleys can block or weaken signals."  
【解析】信号被阻挡造成偏差。A/C/D 均无据。故选 B。
