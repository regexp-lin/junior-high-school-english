# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Old Shoes I Almost Threw Away**

① Last March, my mother and I were cleaning the shoe cupboard for spring. Near the back, behind a pair of winter boots and three dusty shoe boxes, I found a pair of small, worn-out blue running shoes. The sides were cracked, the laces were yellow, and the right one had a small hole in the front where my big toe used to push through. I held them up and laughed. "Mum, why do we still keep these? Let me throw them away." My mother, however, stopped wiping the shelf and looked at the shoes for a long time.

② "Wait," she said softly. "Do you know what those are?" I did not understand the question. "They're my old running shoes," I said. She sat down on the low wooden stool by the door, and I knew, from her face, that there was going to be a story.

③ She asked me if I remembered the 800-metre race in Grade 5. I did. Before that race, I had been ill for two weeks with a long fever, and I had missed almost a month of PE classes. When the sports day list was posted, my name was there anyway — because every student of our class had to enter at least one event. I remembered how angry I was. I had told my mother I would pretend to hurt my ankle and drop out. But on the night before the race, she had come quietly into my room and placed a new pair of blue running shoes on my desk. I had not asked for them. She had simply said, "You don't have to win. You only have to finish."

④ I had not thought about those shoes in years. Now, in the corner of our apartment, I saw the same pair in my hands — old, tired, almost dead. "That's why I kept them," my mother said. "That race changed you a little. Do you remember how you felt at the end?"

⑤ I remembered. On the day of the race, the first four runners flew away from the starting line like birds, and I found myself in the last group, running slowly, my face red, my lungs burning. Halfway, I thought of stopping. But then I saw my mother — not in the parents' area, but near the far corner of the track, standing quietly, her arms crossed, her face calm. She did not shout. She did not cheer loudly like other parents. She only lifted her hand, once, to say: *keep going*. I kept going. I finished 13th out of 15 runners. Nobody took a photo. Nobody gave me a medal. But as I crossed the line, my mother stepped forward, pulled me into a short, strong hug, and said the only four words I still remember: **"You finished. Well done."**

⑥ Back in the living room, holding the old shoes, I felt something warm behind my eyes. "Mum, why didn't you tell me before that you kept them?" She smiled. "Because I wanted you to find them yourself. Some things are better to meet again by accident."

⑦ I was about to say thank you, but then I saw something else. Inside the right shoe, stuck against the inner sole, was a small folded note. I pulled it out. My mother's handwriting, six years older than today: "*For Xin. May 2021. Remember: finishing is the first kind of winning.*" I had never seen that note. I had worn the shoes for many months and never lifted the inner sole.

⑧ I looked up. My mother's eyes were wet too, but she was smiling the same calm smile I had seen at the corner of the running track. "You didn't see it then," she said, "but the words were already there."

⑨ That afternoon, we did not throw the shoes away. I took them to my room, put them on the top shelf above my desk, the note folded back inside. They are not beautiful; they do not fit me any more. But sometimes, when I am afraid of an exam, a speech, or a new first day, I look up at that shelf, and I hear her four quiet words.

⑩ In senior high school, I know I will run many other "races" — tests, competitions, interviews. I may win some, and I may finish far from the front in others. But my mother has already taught me the most useful lesson a runner needs: **finishing is itself a kind of winning**. And some notes are placed inside our lives long before we are ready to read them.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Why were the old blue running shoes kept in the cupboard for so many years?**

A. Because the writer wore them every day.  
B. Because the mother kept them on purpose, as a memory of a race that had quietly changed her son.  
C. Because they were too expensive to throw away.  
D. Because the writer hid them from his mother.

**2. Why did the writer have to run the 800-metre race in Grade 5?**

A. He loved running and trained every day.  
B. He had been ill and missed PE classes, but every student of his class had to enter at least one event, so his name was still on the list.  
C. His mother forced him to become a champion.  
D. His teacher did not like him.

**3. What did the writer's mother do during the race?**

A. She shouted loudly for the writer from the parents' area.  
B. She went home to wait for the result.  
C. She stood quietly near the far corner of the track and lifted her hand once to tell him to keep going.  
D. She took many photos and sent them to relatives.

**4. What was the small folded note inside the right shoe?**

A. A shopping list the mother had forgotten.  
B. A short message from the mother, written in May 2021: "For Xin. May 2021. Remember: finishing is the first kind of winning."  
C. The writer's final result from the race.  
D. A ticket to a sports event.

**5. What does the writer mainly want to tell us at the end of the story?**

A. Old shoes should always be kept forever.  
B. Finishing is itself a kind of winning; and some quiet lessons from our parents are placed in our lives long before we can read them.  
C. Winning first place is the only way to be successful.  
D. Children should never clean their home.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段  
【解析】母亲有意保留作为纪念。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段  
【解析】病后每生必报一项。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段  
【解析】在远角处抬手示意继续。A/B/D 均错。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段  
【解析】原文直接引用那张便条。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第⑩段末句  
【解析】完成本身=胜利；父母的安静教诲提前埋在我们生命里。A/C/D 均偏。故选 B。
