# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do We Dream While We Sleep?**

Every night, after we close our eyes, something quiet and rich begins inside our heads. Rivers appear that we have never seen. Familiar faces say strange words. Old friends, forgotten for years, walk back in as if they had never left. When morning comes, some pictures are still bright; most fade in a few minutes. We call this experience a **dream**. Almost everyone has them, but why do we dream at all?

To answer this, scientists first had to understand sleep itself. Sleep is not one single state; it has several **stages**. The most important one for dreams is called **REM** (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep. During REM, our body's muscles are mostly locked in place, our breathing becomes light, but our brain is very active — almost as busy as when we are awake. Our eyes move quickly behind closed lids. Our heart rate rises a little. If someone wakes us during REM, we usually remember a dream.

So much for "when". Now comes the bigger question: why? There is not one simple answer. Modern research points to at least three main ideas, and each probably captures a different part of the truth.

The first idea is **memory sorting**. During the day, we take in far more information than we can keep. At night, the brain seems to replay bits of the day, weed out what is useless, and store what is valuable in long-term memory. In a famous experiment, students studied a complex map. Those who slept later — and especially those who entered REM sleep — remembered the map much better than classmates who stayed awake. Dreams may be the visible side of this midnight cleaning.

The second idea is **emotional processing**. Middle-school life is full of small and big feelings: an embarrassing classroom moment, a fight with a best friend, a worry before a test. These feelings do not simply disappear when we fall asleep. They visit us again, mixed into strange stories. During dreams, the brain seems to give these emotions a safer stage to play on. By morning, the feeling is often lighter, even if we do not remember why. In this way, a dream can work like a **therapist** who sends the bill through our pillow.

The third idea is **problem solving and creativity**. Many writers, scientists, and musicians have said that important ideas appeared to them in a dream. The brain during REM is less bound by strict logic. It can link things that do not usually connect — a mountain shape and a math formula, a pop song and a history lesson. Such unusual mixtures can lead to new **insights**. Of course, not every dream is creative; most, we must admit, are just odd and messy. But the very same freedom that creates the mess can also produce a **spark**.

Are there also physical reasons? Yes. Babies sleep with very long REM periods, and their small brains are rapidly building connections. Older adults who sleep poorly often report problems with mood and memory. When medicines reduce REM for months, some people become anxious and forgetful. All of this suggests that dreaming is not just a pretty decoration on top of sleep — it is a core part of how our brain works.

Can we do anything to "dream better"? Scientists recommend simple habits. First, keep a regular **sleep schedule**, because REM tends to be richer in the second half of the night, which we lose if we sleep late. Second, reduce heavy screens and bright light in the hour before bed. Third, keep a small paper by the pillow and write one line about a dream when you wake. Even this small act trains the brain to hold the images a little longer.

So, when you next wake from a strange dream, do not shake it off too quickly. That small river, those familiar faces, those odd words — they are tiny evidence of a busy factory at work, quietly sorting your memories, softening your feelings, and sometimes, if you are lucky, handing you a little idea to carry into the day.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. During which stage of sleep do most dreams take place?**

A. Just after falling asleep.  
B. REM sleep, when the eyes move quickly and the brain is very active.  
C. Only in the early morning.  
D. In short naps in the afternoon.

**2. What does the "memory sorting" idea suggest?**

A. We should forget everything.  
B. The brain replays information during sleep, keeping useful things and clearing useless ones.  
C. Memory only grows when we are awake.  
D. Studying at night is useless.

**3. According to the passage, how can dreams help our emotions?**

A. They give strong feelings a safer stage to play on; by morning, the feeling often becomes lighter.  
B. They make our emotions disappear forever.  
C. They teach us to shout louder the next day.  
D. They replace real conversations with parents.

**4. Why might dreams sometimes help creativity?**

A. Because REM sleep is less bound by strict logic, so the brain can link unusual things.  
B. Because dreams always give full solutions on paper.  
C. Because dreams copy our textbooks directly.  
D. Because dreams are never strange.

**5. What is NOT suggested as a way to "dream better"?**

A. Keep a regular sleep schedule.  
B. Reduce heavy screen and bright light before bed.  
C. Write one line about a dream when you wake.  
D. Drink three cups of coffee before going to sleep.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"REM... eyes move quickly... brain is very active"  
【解析】REM 阶段。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"replay bits of the day, weed out what is useless..."  
【解析】大脑整理记忆。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"a safer stage... the feeling is often lighter"  
【解析】情绪被安全加工。B/C/D 均错。故选 A。

**4.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】因果推理题  
【定位】"less bound by strict logic... link things that do not usually connect"  
【解析】REM 不被死板逻辑束缚。B/C/D 均错。故选 A。

**5.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节理解题（反向）  
【定位】"simple habits"  
【解析】文中未建议睡前喝咖啡。A/B/C 均提及。故选 D。
