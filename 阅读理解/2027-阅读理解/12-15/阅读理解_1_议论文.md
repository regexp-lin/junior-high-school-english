# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle School Have a "Cross-Grade Reading Partner System"?**

① On a Friday afternoon in early December, I was returning a library book at the small Grade-8 book corner when I saw, by pure chance, something quietly moving. A **Grade-9 girl**, whose name I did not know, was sitting on a small wooden stool with a **Grade-7 boy** on another stool next to her. Between them, open on the low table, was a battered English novel — *Charlotte's Web*. The Grade-9 girl was reading the first paragraph slowly, in a clear careful voice; the Grade-7 boy was following with his finger. When the girl came to a hard word, she would stop, not correct him, only ask gently, *"What do you think this word could mean?"* The boy would guess. She would nod, write the word in her small notebook, and read on. I stood still, pretending to look at the shelf, watching this for **four quiet minutes**. Nobody was watching them but me. Nobody was grading them. Nobody was taking a photo. And yet, in the small yellow light of the afternoon library, something was clearly, quietly, **working**. **This is why I think every middle school in China should set up a "Cross-Grade Reading Partner System": a voluntary pairing in which one older student (Grade 9 or senior 8) meets one younger student (Grade 7 or junior 8) for forty minutes, once a week, to read an English or Chinese book together in a quiet school corner.** Once a week. Forty minutes. One book. That is enough.

② Some people will immediately say: *"Is this just free tutoring? Should younger students not pay a proper tutor?"* I respectfully disagree. A Cross-Grade Reading Partner is **not a tutor**. A tutor teaches; a partner reads. The difference is not small. In **2023**, a Beijing Normal University education study compared **288 Grade-7 students** in 18 Beijing schools: **96 had a paid tutor**, **96 had a Grade-9 reading partner** (no pay, no grade), **96 had neither**. After one semester, the **paid-tutor group** improved their test scores by **4.8%**. The **reading-partner group** improved test scores by **5.1%** — and, importantly, their **self-reported interest in reading** rose by **31%**, compared to only **8%** in the tutor group. The partner did not, officially, teach any grammar. But she read kindly, for forty minutes, once a week, and the younger student quietly wanted to keep reading on her own. That is not tutoring. That is something smaller, quieter, and in the end more lasting.

③ Second, **it is a small gift to the Grade-9 student too**. A common worry among Grade-9 parents is that their children, deep in exam preparation, have no time for anything "warm". But the same 2023 study found that the **Grade-9 partner students** themselves reported lower **exam anxiety scores** (down 18% on a standard Zarit-scale questionnaire) and higher "school belonging" scores, compared to matched Grade-9 students who did not partner. The reason is simple and human: for forty minutes a week, a Grade-9 student who spends most of her life being judged, tested, and pushed, quietly becomes the **calm older person** in the room. She is, for a short hour, not the person with a problem, but the person with a small kindness to give. **Giving, even a small gift of forty minutes, quietly steadies the heart of the giver.** Grade-9 preparation is not hurt by this. It is helped.

④ Third, **it solves a small, specific, real problem**. Every middle school I know has two quiet problems. One: **Grade-7 students**, especially shy ones, often feel lost in the huge new middle-school building in their first months. They are scared of Grade-9 students, whom they see in the corridor as "big". Two: **Grade-9 students**, in their final year, often feel isolated from the rest of the school. They rarely speak to younger students. A simple weekly pairing solves both problems **at the same time**, with no new classroom, no new curriculum, no new teacher. The Grade-7 student discovers that a "big" Grade-9 is, in truth, a kind person in a school uniform who remembers being small. The Grade-9 student discovers that she is still part of a whole school, not just a Grade-9 exam-cell. One small weekly pairing. Two quiet problems, gently softened.

⑤ Some will worry: *"What if the pairing doesn't work? What if the Grade-9 student is unkind, or the Grade-7 student is too shy?"* A fair concern. My small proposal: **the first three weekly meetings are a "trial period".** Either student may, through a simple paper form handed to the homeroom teacher in private, politely ask to be **re-paired**, with no reason required. After the trial period, if both sign a small "yes, continue" line, the pairing is fixed for the semester. In the **2024 Shanghai pilot** of this system across 14 schools, **91% of the pairs continued past the trial**; **9% asked for re-pairing**; none of the re-paired students reported problems afterwards. The system has a gentle exit built in.

⑥ Others will say: *"Where is the book chosen from?"* From a carefully prepared **school booklist of 60 titles**: 30 in Chinese, 30 in English, all at Grade-7 reading level, with most priced under ¥35. The pair chooses one book together in the first meeting. If the pair runs through the book in fewer than eight weeks, they may choose a second. **There is no quiz at the end. There is no book report.** The only "output" is the partners' small shared notebook, which stays with the Grade-7 student.

⑦ Finally, let me be honest about the cost. The school will spend: about **¥45 per pair** (two small notebooks, one shared pen, one copy of each shared book, added to the school library); **one 30-minute orientation** for all partners at the start of the semester; and **one quiet corner room** free for forty minutes every Friday afternoon. That is the total price. In return, the school gives, per semester, about **150 Grade-7 students** (one per Grade-9 partner) a small kind older reader in their life, and **150 Grade-9 students** a small weekly hour of meaning inside their hardest year.

⑧ **To sum up:** I am not asking for a new subject. I am not asking for new homework. I am asking, simply, for **forty minutes, one older student, one younger student, one shared book, one quiet school corner, once a week**. A Cross-Grade Reading Partner System costs the school almost nothing; it harms no one's exam preparation; it quietly helps two students at once, in two different years, in a single small quiet way. The Grade-9 girl and the Grade-7 boy I saw that Friday afternoon were not doing anything grand. They were simply reading, kindly, together, for four minutes that I happened to see. **We should quietly turn those four minutes into a weekly forty minutes for every pair of students in our school.** The rest, I believe, will take care of itself.

**注释：** pair / pairing 配对；orientation 说明会；tutor 家教；curriculum 课程；isolate 使孤立；Zarit-scale Zarit 量表
