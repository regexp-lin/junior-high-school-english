# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Wear School Uniforms Every Day?**

① On Monday morning outside our school gate, you can see a river of the same blue-and-white uniforms flowing in. Some students walk cheerfully; others tug at the collar, complain about the shape, or quietly envy a friend who has just bought new sneakers. Around the world, many middle schools still require uniforms, while others let students choose their clothes freely. Should middle-school students wear school uniforms **every day**? In my view, the answer is **yes, most of the time**, because uniforms protect fairness, save time and attention, and build a shared sense of school identity. But I also believe schools should leave **some space** for students to express themselves.

② The first reason for uniforms is **fairness**. Not every family has the same income. If students come to school in their own clothes, expensive brands quickly become new "status symbols". Classmates may be quietly judged by price tags, and a child whose parents cannot afford the latest hoodie may feel ashamed or left out. When everyone wears the same uniform, this kind of silent competition disappears. In the classroom, a student is simply a student — not a walking advertisement for a brand. Especially in middle school, where friendships are still forming, reducing this pressure matters.

③ The second reason is **attention and time**. Teenagers are at an age when they naturally care about how they look. Without uniforms, choosing clothes can easily take fifteen or twenty minutes every morning. Once at school, students may keep thinking about their outfit: "Do I look OK? Is this too childish?" This small but constant worry pulls attention away from lessons. A uniform turns clothing into a non-issue. You put it on without thinking, and your mind is free for maths, English, and friends.

④ The third reason is **school identity**. When students from the same school go on a trip, play in a match, or volunteer in a park, the uniform tells the world: "We belong together." It reminds every student that they are part of something bigger than their private taste. Many people in later life still remember their school uniform with warm feelings, because it is tied to shared memories of classmates, teachers, and youth. That sense of belonging is hard to replace.

⑤ Those who are against daily uniforms usually raise two concerns. First, they argue that uniforms **kill personality**. Second, they say uniforms can be uncomfortable or badly designed. These points should be taken seriously, but they don't mean the uniform rule itself is wrong — they mean the uniforms should be **better**. Schools can invite students to help design or update their uniforms. They can choose cotton over hot, heavy fabric, and allow different styles for different seasons. A good uniform should be comfortable enough to run, jump, and sit for six hours in.

⑥ Also, to keep a balance, schools can keep **a few free-dress days** each term — for birthdays, sports festivals, charity events, or the last day before a holiday. On these days, students can wear their favourite colours, prints, and styles. This small freedom acts as a pressure valve. It tells students: "We respect your taste, but not at the cost of fairness in the normal school day." Many schools in Shanghai and Beijing already use this balanced model, and students generally like it.

⑦ Critics sometimes add that "real personality does not come from clothes anyway". In a sense, they are right. A kind word, a creative project, a thoughtful question — these show who you truly are, far more than a new jacket. If we free students from competing with clothes, they may actually notice each other's real qualities more clearly.

⑧ So, should middle-school students wear school uniforms every day? Yes — for fairness, for focus, for a shared sense of school. But this should come with well-designed uniforms and a few free-dress days for self-expression. A good uniform should not feel like a cage; it should feel like **a team shirt** that quietly says: "We're in this together."

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main view on school uniforms?**

A. Uniforms should be removed completely.  
B. Students should wear uniforms every day, without any free-dress days.  
C. Students should wear uniforms most of the time, but schools should allow some free-dress days.  
D. Only teachers should wear uniforms.

**2. According to the passage, how do uniforms help fairness?**

A. They make all students study the same subjects.  
B. They stop students being quietly judged by the price of their clothes.  
C. They make rich students poorer.  
D. They force everyone to walk at the same speed.

**3. Why can uniforms help students focus on schoolwork?**

A. Because uniforms are printed with exam tips.  
B. Because students no longer spend much time choosing clothes or worrying about how they look.  
C. Because uniforms hurt the eyes so you can't look down.  
D. Because uniforms stop all kinds of thinking.

**4. How does the writer answer people who say "uniforms kill personality"?**

A. He agrees completely and says the uniform rule is wrong.  
B. He says these worries are real but mean uniforms should be better, not that uniforms should be removed; and he supports a few free-dress days.  
C. He says personality does not exist at all.  
D. He says only adults have real personalities.

**5. What does the writer mean by calling a good uniform "a team shirt" at the end?**

A. A uniform should be used only in football matches.  
B. A uniform should feel like something that quietly says "we're in this together" — a sign of shared belonging, not a cage.  
C. Every uniform should be red.  
D. Schools should buy uniforms from football clubs.

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题

【答案】B

【答案】B

【解析】作者承认关切但主张"改进校服+自由日"，而非废除。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B
