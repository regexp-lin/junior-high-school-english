# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle-School Student Take One "Family-History Interview Day" Per Term?**

① Last Saturday evening, after a normal winter dinner of tomato-egg noodles, I asked my grandmother, who is **seventy-three** and has lived in our small city since 1981, one single careful question: *"Nainai, what was the first thing you cooked, completely alone, when you were my age?"* She stopped washing the bowl in her hand and looked up, her eyes becoming suddenly younger. For the next **forty-one minutes**, without stopping, she told me about a winter kitchen in **1967**, a small iron stove, her mother's handwritten recipe for steamed bread, and a snowy afternoon when she carried a warm bun three kilometres to an aunt who was ill. I had known my grandmother for fourteen years. I had never heard this story. And it is this evening that makes me write today: **I think every middle school in China should plan, once per term, a "Family-History Interview Day" — a single afternoon on which each student sits with a parent or grandparent for forty minutes and records, in careful writing, one small piece of family history that might otherwise quietly disappear.** Once per term. Forty minutes. One story. That is enough.

② Some people will say: *"This is a family matter. Why should the school be involved?"* I understand the question. But the honest answer is: **without the school's gentle push, most of us never ask.** We are busy with maths, English, PE, after-school classes, and small arguments with our siblings. Our parents and grandparents are also busy — working, cooking, worrying about prices. The small kitchen conversation, which seems so natural, almost never happens on its own. A 2024 Peking University family-communication study of **2,108 Chinese middle-school students** found that **only 7%** had, in the last twelve months, asked a parent or grandparent a serious question about their childhood. Seven per cent. The school cannot force a good conversation; but the school can, quietly, **create the 40 minutes** in which a good conversation becomes possible.

③ Second, **family history is real history**. In our textbooks we read about the **Reform and Opening-Up of 1978**, the building of the first high-speed railway, the move from bicycles to subways, the green mountains that were once bare. These are big words on big pages. But in every family there is a small, living version: a grandfather who remembers the first time he saw a car in his village (he was nine); a grandmother who queued for two hours in 1983 to buy one kilo of pork; a mother who, in 2003, took her first plane alone to Beijing for university and was afraid of the seatbelt. **A student who records one of these small memories has learned history in a way no textbook can teach.** She has put a real human face on a date, and the date stops being just a date.

④ Third, **it is a small gift of dignity to the elderly in our family**. Many grandparents, after retiring, feel quietly invisible. Nobody asks them what they think; nobody asks what they did. A careful forty-minute interview, with a notebook and a pen, tells them something simple: *"Your life is interesting to us. Your story matters."* I have seen it with my own grandmother: after our forty-one minutes, for three days she was in a small, warm mood, humming songs in the kitchen. The interview did nothing practical for her — it changed no bill, cured no illness — but it changed, visibly, the **colour** of her week.

⑤ Some will worry: *"Will this add homework pressure?"* It will not, if it is designed gently. My small proposal: one weekday afternoon per term (perhaps the last Saturday before winter break and summer break), the school gives each student a simple **Interview Kit**: a blank A5 notebook, a short list of **ten optional questions** ("What did you eat most often at fourteen?", "What is one thing you wanted and did not get?", "Who was your best friend at sixteen?"), and a small folder to keep the answers safe. The student chooses any one family member — parent, grandparent, uncle, aunt — sits with them for forty minutes, and writes down **two or three stories** in her own handwriting. **There is no grade. There is no class presentation unless the student volunteers.** The only output is the notebook, which the student keeps at home.

⑥ Others will say: *"My grandparents are far away."* A fair point. For students whose elder family members live in another city, the interview can be done **by a single video call** of forty minutes, scheduled at a quiet weekend hour. The school can offer the mini-app's video room, if the family has no home call software. For a very small number of students with no living elder family member, the school should gently offer **a substitute kind elder** — a retired teacher who has agreed, with joy, to be "interviewed" by a young student for an afternoon. No student is left without an elder to listen to.

⑦ Some will ask: *"Why does it have to be once per term?"* Because the **once-per-term frequency** is the only way this habit quietly lasts. Once a term, over three middle-school years, is **six interviews** — six small stories from a family, written in a student's own young handwriting. By Grade 9, a student has, in her A5 notebook, a small honest history of her family — the kind of object a person keeps for fifty years. A single one-time event would be moving but thin. Six small interviews, careful and gentle, become a small book that belongs to her forever.

⑧ Finally, let me be honest about the cost. The school will spend: about **¥6 per student per term** (notebook, pen, folder); **one and a half hours** of teacher time in homeroom to explain the kit; and **one weekday afternoon** (four lost class periods, twice a year). That is the total price. In exchange, the school gives 3,000 students, over three years, **18,000 small family stories** — stories that would otherwise be lost inside the quiet rooms of a busy country.

⑨ **To sum up:** I am not asking the school to change its timetable. I am not asking parents to change their jobs. I am asking, quietly, for one small afternoon per term, one blank notebook, one old family member, and forty minutes of careful listening. My grandmother's snowy walk with a warm bun in 1967 will never, now, be forgotten; it is written in my own handwriting in a small green notebook on my shelf. **Every student in our country deserves a small green notebook of her own.** And six small notebooks, once per term, is not a heavy price for a family history saved.
