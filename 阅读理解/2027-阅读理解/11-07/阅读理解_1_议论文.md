# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Shops Near Schools Stop Selling Energy Drinks to Middle-School Students?**

① Every Monday afternoon at 16:35, the small shop at the corner of our school gate becomes the busiest place on the street. Students in blue uniforms crowd in, and on the way out most of them hold not a bottle of water, not a box of milk, but a tall, bright can of **energy drink**. In the last two years the number of such cans sold near middle-school gates in our city has, according to a short report by the Municipal Health Bureau (November 2027), **doubled**. The question is honest and not loud: **should our city simply stop these shops from selling energy drinks to students under 15?** After weeks of reading and listening, I believe the answer is yes.

② The first reason is **health**. A standard can of energy drink contains about **80 to 120 milligrams** of caffeine, which is close to the amount in a strong coffee. Doctors generally agree that children under 12 should have **almost no caffeine**, and that those aged 13 to 15 should have no more than about 100 mg a day — not at a single burst. A can in the late afternoon keeps the young heart beating faster for 4–6 hours, and the quiet sleep at night becomes lighter, shorter, and — above all — less repairing. Over months, poor sleep is a far larger enemy to young people than a difficult maths paper.

③ The second reason is **addiction in small steps**. Energy drinks are sold with bright colours, sports stars, and short, exciting words on the can: "fire", "storm", "unlimited". A student who drinks one to stay awake on Tuesday night may, by December, feel he "cannot" study without one. Unlike a cup of tea that the family shares at home, these cans are private, portable, and quietly expensive. A small habit of 10 yuan a day, over a year, is more than **3,500 yuan** — and a tired body.

④ The third reason is **fairness at school**. In every class, a few students are shy about their own tired mornings. When one student shows up loud and over-sharp after a can at 07:10, and another is sleepy after a long bus ride from the outer district, the classroom is **no longer a fair playground**. One teacher in Hangzhou said quietly to a newspaper: "I can now guess, by the first five minutes of each lesson, which student has drunk one today." That is a sad sentence about a school.

⑤ Some voices argue that we should not make such rules at all. "Students have the right to choose their own drinks," they say. "Parents should decide." This sounds respectful. But the same sentence could once have been used to sell cigarettes or alcohol to minors — and most cities long ago agreed that certain drinks, for **developing bodies**, should simply not be sold to children. The right to choose is real, but a 13-year-old's body is **growing** in ways that demand some protection from the market.

⑥ Others say, "We cannot stop students from buying them near the school — they will just walk two more streets." This is a real worry, but not a reason to do nothing. A quiet law passed in Shenzhen in 2025 — "**no energy drinks to be sold within 200 metres of a primary or middle school**" — has, according to city figures, cut student purchase of such drinks by **54%** in the first year. Not every student stops. But more than half does; and more than half, in public health, is a huge victory.

⑦ So what should the city do? Three small steps. First, ask all **shops inside the 200-metre school circle** to sign a simple agreement not to sell energy drinks to students in school uniform. Second, print a **short leaflet** together with the school nurse about caffeine, sleep and growing hearts — more facts, fewer slogans, in language that a Grade 7 student can understand. Third, ask the soft-drinks industry to provide **bright, sugar-free sparkling waters** at a lower price on shop shelves — so that the student who still wants a "cool" drink after school has a real, cheap, harmless option. A sharper rule without a better choice is only half a solution.

⑧ I love the small shop at our corner. The aunty who runs it has known my name since Grade 1. I am not asking her to lose a single yuan — many cities make up part of such small lost income through a local tax break. I am asking our city to protect a part of us that **cannot yet protect itself**: the quiet inside of a thirteen-year-old heart. We are more than the bright can we hold in the late afternoon. **Take the can away, and we still have the evening. Keep the can, and we may, very slowly, lose the evening for many years to come.**

---

**注释：** energy drink 能量饮料；caffeine 咖啡因；addiction 成瘾；minor 未成年人；leaflet 小册子

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion?**

A. Shops should sell more energy drinks to students.  
B. Our city should stop shops from selling energy drinks to students under 15.  
C. Students should buy energy drinks only on Mondays.  
D. Only teachers should drink energy drinks.

**2. According to Paragraph ②, why are energy drinks especially harmful for young people?**

A. Because they make students feel very hungry.  
B. Because a can contains 80–120 mg of caffeine, close to a strong coffee; doctors say 13–15 year-olds should have no more than about 100 mg a day, not in one burst, and poor sleep is a big enemy to young people.  
C. Because they are very cold.  
D. Because they contain too much sugar only at night.

**3. How does Paragraph ④ describe the "fairness" problem in class?**

A. All students are equal because they all drink the same thing.  
B. A Hangzhou teacher could guess in five minutes which student had drunk a can, because of the difference in sharpness between those who had and those who had not — the classroom is no longer a fair playground.  
C. Energy drinks make all students quieter.  
D. Only rich students drink them.

**4. Which of the following is NOT one of the writer's three steps in Paragraph ⑦?**

A. Ask shops within 200 metres of a school to sign a simple no-sell agreement.  
B. Print a short clear leaflet on caffeine, sleep and growing hearts.  
C. Ask the industry to offer bright, sugar-free sparkling waters at a lower price as a harmless option.  
D. Fine every student who drinks one.

**5. What does the writer mean by "Take the can away, and we still have the evening. Keep the can, and we may, very slowly, lose the evening for many years to come"?**

A. Students should not go out in the evening.  
B. The evening stands for the quiet, repairing sleep of young people; removing the can keeps that sleep; keeping the can slowly damages it over years.  
C. Only Grade 9 students should drink energy drinks.  
D. Shops should stay open all evening.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段末 "our city simply stop these shops from selling energy drinks to students under 15"  
【解析】立论直引。A、C、D 均偏或相反。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "80 to 120 milligrams ... no more than about 100 mg a day — not at a single burst ... poor sleep is a far larger enemy"  
【解析】咖啡因剂量与睡眠影响。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段教师引言与"no longer a fair playground"  
【解析】课堂公平问题直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节理解题（否定）  
【定位】第⑦段三条措施  
【解析】A、B、C 均在文中；D"罚款学生"作者未建议。故选 D。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑧段结尾  
【解析】evening 暗喻睡眠与健康。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
