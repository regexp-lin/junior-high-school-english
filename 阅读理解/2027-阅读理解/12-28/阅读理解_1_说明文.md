# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Does GPS Know Exactly Where You Are?**

① Every morning, when your parents open the map app on their phone to check the traffic, when a delivery rider finds your small compound gate without asking, when a high-speed train running across the frozen north-eastern plain knows its speed within one kilometre per hour, when a group of lost hikers in the mountains of Yunnan sends out their precise coordinates to the rescue team — all of these small and big moments share the same quiet technology: **GPS**, which stands for **Global Positioning System**. Most middle school students use it every day without ever asking the simple question: **how does a small phone in your pocket know, within three metres, exactly where you are standing on the round Earth?** This short article explains, in simple words, the **four small steps** GPS uses.

② **Step 1 — The satellites above your head.** Roughly **20,200 kilometres above the Earth**, about 31 small satellites, each about the size of a family car, circle our planet twice a day. They do not belong to any single country's mobile phone company; they were built and launched by the United States Department of Defense, and since the year 2000 they have been freely available for **civilian use** anywhere on Earth. Each satellite carries something almost unbelievably precise: an **atomic clock**, accurate to **one second in three million years**. At all times, every satellite is whispering one short radio message to the Earth below: *"I am Satellite No. X, and my atomic clock now reads precisely 15:32:04.87320."* These whispers travel at the speed of light (about **300,000 kilometres per second**) and take only about **67 milliseconds** to reach your phone on the ground.

③ **Step 2 — Your phone secretly listens to four of them.** Inside every ordinary smartphone today is a small, low-power GPS receiver chip, about the size of a rice grain. This chip does **not** send any signal to the satellites — GPS is entirely a **listening** technology, not a speaking one. When you open a map app, your phone's GPS chip silently listens to as many satellites as it can clearly hear — usually between **five and twelve** at once — and chooses the **four with the clearest signal**. **Why four?** Because three satellites can locate your flat position on Earth (latitude and longitude), and the **fourth satellite** is needed to calculate your **altitude** (how high above sea level you are), and also to correct tiny clock errors inside your phone. Without the fourth, GPS would think you are on the floor even if you were on the 18th storey.

④ **Step 3 — The distance from time.** Here is the most elegant idea in the whole system. When your phone reads the message *"I am Satellite No. X, time 15:32:04.87320,"* it compares that satellite's time stamp with the current time inside the phone itself, and calculates the **tiny time delay** between them — perhaps 67.42 milliseconds. Since radio signals travel at a fixed known speed (the speed of light), **time delay can be turned into distance**: 67.42 ms × 300,000 km/s = about **20,226 kilometres** of distance from that satellite. Your phone does this calculation **four times**, once for each satellite, giving it four precise distances to four known positions in the sky. If you imagine drawing four invisible spheres — one around each satellite, each with its own radius — the **single small point** where all four spheres overlap is exactly where your phone is standing on Earth. This geometric trick is called **trilateration** (多点定位), and the whole calculation is done inside your phone in **less than a second**.

⑤ **Step 4 — Small corrections.** A raw GPS reading would only be accurate to about **10 to 15 metres**, because the atmosphere slightly slows down the radio signals as they pass through its upper layers, and tall buildings can bounce signals around. To fix this, two small corrections are added. First, every modern phone compares the GPS reading with another quiet helper called **A-GPS** (Assisted GPS), which uses the nearby mobile-phone tower to correct small atmospheric errors. Second, in many countries including China, phones also quietly listen to a second satellite family called **BeiDou** (北斗), China's own satellite navigation system with more than 30 satellites. With both systems listening at the same time, most modern phones in a Chinese city now reach an accuracy of about **three metres**, which is why your map app can tell the difference between the north and south exits of a subway station.

⑥ Three small side facts worth remembering:

> **A.** GPS does not use any of your phone's mobile data or Wi-Fi. It works in the deep mountains, on a fishing boat, and inside a desert tent — anywhere under the open sky.  
> **B.** GPS does **not** work well indoors under thick concrete ceilings; the weak radio signal cannot pass through many layers of steel and stone. That is why your map app often says *"signal searching"* inside a subway tunnel.  
> **C.** GPS is **one-way**. The satellites never know that you are listening, and they never know your name, your age, or what you are doing. Whatever private information is shared on your phone is shared only by the **apps using the GPS reading**, not by the GPS system itself.

⑦ So the next time your parent's map app gently whispers, *"In 200 metres, turn left,"* you can remember, quietly, the small miracle behind that voice: four tiny satellite whispers from 20,000 kilometres up, each delayed by about 67 thousandths of a second, all arriving at the rice-grain chip in your phone, which in less than one second draws four invisible spheres in the sky and finds the single small point where they all meet — and that point is the one, exact, three-metre patch of Earth where you happen to be standing. **GPS is one of the most elegant examples of maths and physics quietly helping ordinary people find their way home.**
