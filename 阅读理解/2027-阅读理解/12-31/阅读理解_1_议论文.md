# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle School Class Write One Anonymous Letter to Their 2028 Classmates?**

① On the last morning of this year, **Friday 31 December 2027, at 07:50 sharp**, something almost silent happened in our 8-B classroom at Lihua Middle School. Mr Qin, without any announcement on WeChat the night before, walked in carrying a small wooden box, a bundle of **27 blank cream-white cards**, and **27 plain envelopes**. He placed the box and the cards on the front desk, turned to us, and said, very quietly, *"Good morning, 8-B. In twelve hours this year ends. Before we go, we are going to write one small letter each — not to each other, not to a parent, not to a teacher — but to **one classmate of 2028 whose name you do not know and will never know**. You will not sign your own name. You will not write the recipient's name. You will simply write one short, honest, kind letter of three to ten sentences. When you finish, fold it into the envelope, seal the envelope, and drop it quietly into this small wooden box. In the first week of 2028, the box will travel to our sister class 9-A, and their homeroom teacher Ms Wu will randomly hand one envelope to each of her 27 students. They will read yours. You will read theirs. No one will ever know which letter came from whom."* The class became, in about five seconds, the quietest it had been all winter. That single 40-minute lesson, I believe, quietly proved a small idea I want to propose today. **Every middle school class in China, on the last school day of each year, should spend one lesson writing anonymous letters to their next year's classmates. One lesson. One envelope. No names. One calm gift, sent quietly across the winter.**

② **The first reason is about honesty.** Middle school students in China, I gently observe, often live with a small quiet fear: that whatever small difficulty they are going through — a bad maths test, a lonely lunch break, a worry about a parent's illness, a first small crush, a small misunderstanding with a friend — will, if said aloud, be **overheard, remembered, and quietly held against them** by somebody in the long years to come. This fear is not irrational; it has kept some of our most thoughtful classmates silent. But an **anonymous letter has no author**. Without a name at the bottom, what you write cannot be traced back to you; it is not your reputation, your mark, or your future that carries any risk. And that tiny lifting of risk, I noticed in our 8-B lesson on 31 December, let many of my classmates finally write sentences they would never have said at a normal class discussion: *"When I feel afraid before a physics exam, I cry in the bathroom for three minutes and then I come back."* *"I was the person who, in October, quietly broke the porcelain cup in the reading corner, and I was too embarrassed to say."* *"My Mum and my Dad have been speaking less and less in October, and I do not know what to do, so I am telling a future stranger."* The honesty was not new; only the **freedom to say it** was new.

③ **The second reason is about kindness across time.** Each of our 27 letters will be read, next month, by a Grade-9 stranger who will not know our names — but she will, for ten full minutes of her Grade-9 January, hold the paper we wrote and feel the small, genuine warmth of one 8-B student thinking gently about her. And that kindness, once received, tends to move forward: a Grade-9 girl who receives such a letter in January 2028 is, I believe, slightly more likely to write a kind note to somebody else in March, and that somebody else is slightly more likely to speak gently to a younger student in May. One single 40-minute lesson can quietly send out **small waves of kindness** that touch classmates we ourselves will never meet. Very few 40-minute lessons on a Chinese school timetable can honestly claim that.

④ **The third reason is about closure for our own year.** A school year is, in truth, a long strange object — ten and a half months of early bells, canteen trays, friendships, small quarrels, unfinished homework, small achievements, and small regrets. At the end of it, students often feel a **vague heavy tiredness** they cannot quite name. Writing one small anonymous letter on the last morning is, I believe, a way of **gently saying goodbye to the year** — a way of lifting a small weight off our own shoulders and passing on, not the weight itself, but the quiet wisdom the weight taught us. When I folded my letter into its envelope at 08:23 on 31 December and dropped it into the small wooden box, I felt something I had not felt all year: **a clean, soft closing**. Not loud, not festive, but honestly done.

⑤ **Some readers may worry that anonymity could hide unkindness.** I respectfully disagree, but with one small safeguard. At Lihua, Mr Qin opened every envelope briefly (with no other student watching) before sealing them again, and returned to any student — in private, with no class knowing — any single letter whose tone felt unkind, asking that student to rewrite. In 8-B, **not one letter needed rewriting**. When students know that their letter will travel to a stranger and never come back, the tone chosen is almost always **gentle**. Anonymity tends to bring out, in a quiet 14-year-old, not the worst self but a softer better self — the self we usually wish our family or friends would see.

⑥ **Some readers may worry that this is, in the end, a small sentimental gesture with no real learning value.** I disagree here too. **Writing anonymously in English or Chinese, in 27 minutes, in a short honest form, is one of the most concentrated language exercises a middle-schooler can do.** Every word must matter, because you cannot come back to explain what you meant. Every sentence must be clear, because the reader does not know you. Every kindness must be real, because kindness written to a stranger is harder than kindness written to a friend. Our 8-B English marks, Mr Qin quietly told me afterwards, have been slowly rising since the letters. I do not think that is a coincidence.

⑦ In short, one 40-minute anonymous-letter lesson, on the last school day of each year, is very nearly **free** for any school to adopt. It requires only 27 pieces of paper, 27 plain envelopes, one small wooden box, and a teacher willing to walk quietly to the sister class in early January. In return, it gives every student an honest chance to write without fear, an unseen Grade-9 stranger a small warm gift in a cold January, our whole class a clean honest closing to a long year, and a real language exercise dressed in the form of a kindness. **One lesson. One envelope. No name. One quiet wave of kindness sent forward into 2028.** I believe every middle school in China can, very easily, afford this small gift this spring.

**注释：** anonymous letter 匿名信；reputation 名誉；closure 心理上的了结；sentimental 感伤的；safeguard 安全保障；concentrated 高度集中的
