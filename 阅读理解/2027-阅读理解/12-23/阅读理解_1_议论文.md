# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle School Host a Monthly "Silent-Reading Morning"?**

① On the first Friday of this school term, our homeroom teacher Mr Qin did an unusual thing. From **07:45 to 08:20** — a 35-minute window normally used for small class chatter and the tail of breakfast — he closed the classroom door, turned off the speaker, and placed, on each of our twenty-seven desks, one small handwritten card that said, simply, *"Read. Anything. Quietly."* Then he sat down at the teacher's desk with his own book, a thin worn copy of *Journey to the West*, and began, silently, to read. For thirty-five minutes, no one spoke; no one checked a phone; no one did homework. Twenty-seven Grade-8 students read twenty-seven different books, each at her own slow pace, in a warm classroom with yellow winter light coming through the window. When the bell rang at **08:20**, something strange happened — we did not want to stop. Two students quietly finished the paragraph they were in before closing their books. One student, Lin Ruoxi, who is usually the quickest to pack up, sat for a full minute longer, staring at the page, before she moved. It was the quietest half-hour our 8-B classroom had experienced in a full year. **This is why I think every middle school in China should host a monthly "Silent-Reading Morning" — 35 minutes on one Friday of each month, devoted wholly and silently to free reading, in every homeroom, for every student, with teachers reading beside the students.** Once a month. Thirty-five minutes. Every student. Every teacher. One simple rule.

② Some will immediately say: *"Students should be reading at home. Why use school time?"* I respectfully disagree. The truth is that **most of us do not read at home, even when we want to**. A 2024 China Children's Reading Index study of **12,480 middle-school students** across six provinces found that **61%** read zero pages of free-choice books (outside homework) in the past school week; another **24%** read only one or two pages; only **15%** read 10 pages or more. Reasons given: *"no time"*, *"too tired"*, *"my phone is next to me"*. Home is, for most of us, a place of tired screens. The school is the only regular place in a modern child's week where a **protected, quiet, phone-free 35 minutes** can be created. The school cannot force a love of reading, but it can — quietly — protect the conditions under which love of reading is possible.

③ Second, **35 minutes is long enough to do real work for a reading brain**. A 2023 University of Sussex study (UK) and a 2022 East China Normal University study both found that **30–35 minutes** is the approximate time the reading brain takes to enter a mode researchers call **"deep immersion"** — the point at which the reader forgets her surroundings, her reading speed stabilises, and comprehension increases by about 18%. Shorter reading bursts (say, 10 minutes) rarely reach this mode. Homework-related reading almost never reaches it either — there is always a question on the side. A monthly protected 35-minute block is, in short, the **minimum length** at which reading is more than a performance; below that, the brain stays in shallow scan mode. Once per month is little; but that "little" matters more than ten unprotected home attempts.

④ Third, **the teacher-reads-too rule is the quiet heart of the habit**. I have seen, over the last school term, several classmates' faces change gently when they looked up and saw Mr Qin, their strict maths teacher, reading his small worn *Journey to the West* at his own desk. Without saying a word, he was telling them: *"Adults read too. Reading is not only a homework task for children. It is a small human pleasure I myself still practise at 49."* A **2023 Peking University education study** of **41 homeroom teachers and their 1,218 students** found that in classrooms where the teacher read during silent-reading sessions, **students' self-reported reading at home** rose by **22%** over one semester, compared to only **5%** in classrooms where the teacher corrected homework silently at the desk. **Modelling, quietly, is the most powerful teacher.** A teacher who reads is worth a thousand posters about reading.

⑤ Some will say: *"Students will just pretend to read."* A fair concern, especially for Grade 7 or students with reading difficulty. But the small evidence is reassuring. In the **2024 Wuxi pilot** of Silent-Reading Morning across **18 schools and 6,800 students**, **86%** of students, over three months, reported "I did real reading, at least most of the time"; **14%** reported they had pretended to read sometimes, but **76% of even these pretenders** said the session *"felt calmer and kinder than normal homeroom"*. Pretending to read quietly, as it turns out, is still better for the classroom air than not being quiet at all. The 35-minute room, by its very quietness, **is its own small soft teacher**.

⑥ Others will worry: *"What about students who cannot read well — new transfer students, students with dyslexia, students who struggle?"* My small proposal: these students are offered, quietly and privately, **three gentle options**: (a) a book at a simpler reading level, chosen in advance with the school librarian; (b) an audio-book played through single-ear headphones at low volume, with the written text open on the desk; (c) a simple comic or picture-book from the Art Department's rotating shelf. **No student is asked to announce which option she chose.** Reading difficulty is private; the classroom is silent; the options remove any quiet shame.

⑦ A deeper concern: *"Will this lead to assigned book reports?"* **No.** Silent-Reading Morning should have zero measurement attached to it. No book report. No student sharing. No teacher check-in questions afterwards. **The entire point of the ritual is that, for 35 minutes a month, reading is released from the job of being judged.** Any time reading is measured, it stops being reading and starts being performance. The **2024 Hangzhou pilot** of 13 schools showed that in classrooms that added a simple optional "what did you read" sharing, voluntary home reading dropped by 11% within three months. Measurement, quietly, kills reading. Silent-Reading Morning protects reading from measurement.

⑧ Finally, let me be honest about the cost. The school will spend: about **¥18 per homeroom per semester** (one shared bookbasket of 50 rotating library titles, replaced every two months); **10 minutes** of homeroom orientation at the start of term; and **one 35-minute morning per month** (a very small calendar sacrifice for a nine-month school year). In return, every student gets **nine protected reading mornings a year**, **twenty-seven across three middle-school years** — and every teacher gets, quietly, nine mornings in the year in which she reads beside her students as a fellow reader. There are few cheaper gifts a school can give both sides of the classroom.

⑨ **To sum up:** I am not asking for a new subject. I am not asking for homework changes. I am not asking for ranks, prizes, or book reports. I am asking, quietly, for **one 35-minute Friday morning per month, in every homeroom, in which every student reads freely and every teacher reads alongside**. The silent half-hour our class experienced that first Friday — twenty-seven students reading twenty-seven books in warm yellow light — ended with a small strange reluctance to close the books. **That small reluctance is the entire point.** A school that gives its students twenty-seven such reluctances across three years has given them the beginning of a lifelong reading habit, for a cost of less than an hour a month. **Silent-Reading Morning is the cheapest large gift a middle school can give.**
