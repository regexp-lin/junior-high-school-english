# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Do Honey Bees Find Their Way Home?**

① A honey bee is only about **one centimetre long**, and her brain is smaller than a grain of rice. Yet every summer morning, she flies **up to five kilometres** from her hive to find flowers, drinks their sweet nectar, carries back a small yellow basket of pollen on her legs — and then lands at the **exact hole** on the side of her own wooden box. How does such a tiny creature do what many lost hikers cannot?

② Scientists have studied bees for more than a hundred years. They now believe that bees use **at least three natural "maps"**, and they switch between them depending on the weather.

③ The first map is the **sun**. A bee's eyes can see the direction of the sun even when the sky is only partly blue. The bee remembers the angle between her flight path and the sun when she leaves the hive. On the way back, she simply reverses the angle, adjusting for how the sun has moved across the sky — which, to a bee, feels like a giant, slow clock. If the sky is totally covered by clouds, this map stops working, and the bee has to change to the second map.

④ The second map is a **kind of light that humans cannot see**. Even on a cloudy day, a little **polarized light** passes through the clouds, forming invisible lines across the sky. The bee's eyes can read these lines like a network of faint roads. So, as long as a small patch of sky remains, the bee can still find north, south, east and west.

⑤ The third map is the **landscape**. Bees remember groups of clear ground features — a tall pine, a red roof, a bend in a small river — and link them to the direction of home. That is why a bee raised in a forest behaves strangely if we move her hive to a flat city park: her "familiar paintings" are gone, and she must spend several days making a new map of windows, chimneys and street trees.

⑥ Bees share their maps with each other through a famous **"waggle dance"**. When a successful bee returns with good news about a flower patch, she dances on the wall of the hive. The **direction** of her dance shows where the flowers are (compared to the sun), and the **length** of the dance shows how far away they are. Young bees watch, remember, and then fly out in the right direction — without ever seeing the flowers before.

⑦ Modern life is making these maps harder to read. Bright city lights change what bees see at dusk; tall new buildings hide the sun and old landmarks; **some common garden chemicals** can even damage the part of a bee's brain that stores memory. Scientists say that bees in some cities now forget the way home far more often than their country cousins. The result is not just a sad bee, but a weaker hive and, in the long run, **fewer apples on our tables**, because one out of every three bites of our food depends on bees for pollination.

⑧ We are only beginning to understand how a small insect can do such big mathematics in the sky. Protecting bees is not only about their happiness; it is about keeping the **living map** they have been drawing for millions of years — a map that, in the end, also leads back to us.

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. How far can a honey bee fly from her hive to find flowers?**

A. Up to 50 metres.  
B. Up to 500 metres.  
C. Up to 5 kilometres.  
D. Up to 50 kilometres.

**2. According to the passage, how does a bee use the sun to find her way?**

A. She flies directly towards the sun.  
B. She only flies at noon.  
C. She remembers the angle between her flight path and the sun, and corrects for how the sun has moved during the day.  
D. She avoids the sun.

**3. Why is the "second map" important?**

A. It works only in winter.  
B. It lets the bee navigate on cloudy days through polarized light that humans cannot see.  
C. It is used for counting flowers.  
D. It is used only for defending the hive.

**4. What do the direction and length of a bee's "waggle dance" show?**

A. The colour and smell of the flower.  
B. The direction to the flower patch (compared to the sun) and how far away the flowers are.  
C. The age of the dancing bee.  
D. The queen's favourite food.

**5. What is the writer's main concern in the last two paragraphs?**

A. Bees are too loud in the city.  
B. Modern life (bright lights, tall buildings, garden chemicals) damages bees' maps and weakens hives, which will finally affect human food.  
C. Bees should be sent to space.  
D. We should stop growing apples.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "flies up to five kilometres from her hive"  
【解析】直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "remembers the angle ... adjusting for how the sun has moved"  
【解析】以日为参照调整角度。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "polarized light ... humans cannot see ... as long as a small patch of sky remains"  
【解析】多云天靠偏振光。A、C、D 无依据。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 "direction ... compared to the sun ... length ... how far away"  
【解析】两项指标直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】推理判断题（作者态度 / 主旨）  
【定位】第⑦⑧段 "bright city lights ... chemicals ... fewer apples on our tables ... the living map"  
【解析】担忧现代生活破坏蜜蜂导航及人类食物。A、C、D 偏离。故选 B。
