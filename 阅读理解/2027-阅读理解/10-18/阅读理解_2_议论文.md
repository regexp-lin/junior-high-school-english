# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Our School Have a "No-Homework Day" Once a Month?**

① Last month, on our class parent-meeting evening, a mother stood up and made a bold suggestion: "Could our school try a **'No-Homework Day' once a month** — one Saturday with no written homework, only reading, exercise or family time?" The classroom went quiet, then started to buzz. Some teachers frowned; some parents nodded hard. Since then, the question has travelled along our corridors and WeChat groups. After listening to both sides, I believe the answer should be **a careful "yes"**.

② Those who support the idea usually start with **health**. Middle-school students in our city already spend about **9 hours a day** on schoolwork on weekends. Shoulders ache, eyes grow tired, and bedtime slips past midnight. One single "light" day each month will not spoil three years of study, but it may save many pairs of eyes. A bow that is pulled all the time will one day break; even the tightest bow needs to be **laid down for a moment**.

③ The second reason is **deeper learning**. Not all learning is written. When we visit a grandparent, walk in a park, repair a bicycle with Dad, or help Mum cook a meal, we quietly learn **how the world works**. These "soft lessons" — patience, courage, kindness, cooperation — rarely appear on a test paper, but they turn up everywhere in adult life. A "No-Homework Day" simply leaves a **blank page** in our notebook, and invites us to write it with real life.

④ Of course, the other side is not making empty noise. Teachers warn that middle school is a **race of minutes**. The Senior High School Entrance Exam is only three years away, and many weekends are already packed with review. If one day is lost, the missing pages must be squeezed into other days, which may cause even more stress. Some parents also worry that a "No-Homework Day" will simply turn into a "Longer-Video Day", because, without a clear plan, children naturally reach for their phones.

⑤ Both worries are fair, yet they can be answered. If the school sets the "No-Homework Day" **well in advance** (for example, the last Saturday of each month, marked in the year calendar), teachers can plan the whole month's assignments more wisely, not simply push papers across days. And if parents prepare **a simple family plan** — a short hike, a museum visit, a charity visit to a nursing home, a cooking day — then the screen becomes only one small option among many richer ones. The day is "no homework", not "no learning".

⑥ Some students worry that rich classmates will still get **private tutoring** on that day and race ahead. That is a real risk, but it is a risk that exists every weekend already. What the school can do is **model the day well**: teachers share free reading lists, teachers and students walk together in a local park, or a few teachers open a voluntary "maker afternoon" — pure fun, no marks.

⑦ One day in thirty is not a revolution. It is a small act of trust: trust in a student's ability to choose, trust in a family's ability to teach at home, trust in **rest** as a real part of growth. If we can spend 29 days pushing forwards with all our strength, surely we can spend the 30th breathing deeply, looking up, and remembering **why** we are running.

---

**注释：** bow 弓；soft lesson 软技能课；in advance 提前；tutoring 课外辅导；rest 休息

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Who first suggested the "No-Homework Day"?**

A. The head teacher.  
B. A mother at a class parent-meeting evening.  
C. The writer herself.  
D. A famous professor.

**2. What reason does Paragraph ② give for supporting the idea?**

A. Students will earn more money.  
B. Health: students already do about 9 hours of schoolwork a day on weekends, and one light day a month may protect their body and eyes.  
C. Students will eat more vegetables.  
D. Students will become tall.

**3. What do critics worry about, according to Paragraph ④?**

A. Middle school is a "race of minutes" before the Senior High School Entrance Exam, and a lost day may have to be squeezed into other days, causing more stress; the day may also turn into a "Longer-Video Day".  
B. Students will stop reading forever.  
C. Parents will quit their jobs.  
D. Teachers will go on holiday.

**4. How does the writer suggest reducing the risk of the day becoming a "Longer-Video Day"?**

A. By banning all videos.  
B. By keeping students at school.  
C. By announcing the day well in advance, planning monthly homework wisely, and encouraging families to prepare a simple plan (hike, museum, charity visit, cooking day).  
D. By giving extra homework.

**5. What is the writer's final attitude?**

A. Strongly against the "No-Homework Day".  
B. A careful "yes" — the day is a small act of trust in students, families, and the value of rest as part of growth.  
C. Completely neutral, with no opinion.  
D. The day should be every week.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "a mother stood up and made a bold suggestion"  
【解析】提案者为家长。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "about 9 hours a day ... save many pairs of eyes"  
【解析】健康负担 + 身体保护。A、C、D 与文无关。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "a race of minutes ... missing pages must be squeezed ... Longer-Video Day"  
【解析】两点担忧直引。B、C、D 均错。故选 A。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "well in advance ... a simple family plan ... hike, museum, charity visit, cooking day"  
【解析】提前宣告 + 家庭计划。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题／作者观点题  
【定位】第①段 "a careful 'yes'" + 第⑦段结尾  
【解析】作者明确持"谨慎支持"立场。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
