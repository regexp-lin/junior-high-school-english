# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Parents Read Their Middle-School Child's Chat Records?**

① Last Saturday my cousin Yiyan came to our flat in tears. Her mother had opened her phone the evening before and read, one by one, **three weeks of chat messages** between Yiyan and her best friend Meier. "I was only worried, darling," her mother had said. "I needed to know what kind of people you talked to." Yiyan, thirteen years old, sat on my sofa that afternoon without eating, without speaking, without taking out her phone at all. Her face asked a question that many of us middle-school students have asked: **Is it right for parents to read our chat records?** After thinking for a week, I believe the answer is this: **in most cases, no — unless there is clear, serious reason.**

② Some parents argue that "young people chat too much and too dangerously", so reading their records is simply a **form of care**. It is true that the internet has **real risks**: strangers who pretend to be kind, rumours that spread fast, words that push a lonely teenager into darker thoughts. These dangers deserve to be taken seriously. And it is also true that parents, in the end, are **legally and morally responsible** for their child's safety.

③ However, **care is not the same as control**, and not every worry deserves a key to the bedroom door. If parents read every message — from the silly midnight joke to the shy confession about a classmate — **three quiet harms** follow. First, **trust breaks**. Once a child knows her chat has been read secretly, even the most loving mother becomes, in the child's eyes, a small hidden camera. Rebuilding trust from there takes years, sometimes longer.

④ Second, **real problems go underground**. If the chat is read, the child simply moves the real conversations somewhere else — to a different app, to a second phone, to a language code understood only by classmates. The parent will then read a chat **stage** carefully prepared for her eyes, while the real life of the child runs elsewhere, invisibly. In this way, over-checking often makes a home less safe, not more safe.

⑤ Third, a child's **inner room** — the place where she thinks aloud to a friend about her body, her crush, her fear of the entrance exam — is a room she must learn to build alone. If it is forced open too often, the child learns to keep **everything** inside. Years later, she finds it hard to tell even her partner or her doctor what she truly feels. A little privacy, from the age of twelve or thirteen, is one of the real vitamins of growing up.

⑥ What, then, about real dangers? Here is where many parents and I agree: there are cases where reading a child's messages **is** right. For example, if a child has suddenly stopped sleeping, stopped eating, spoken about **ending her own life**, or refused school for many days, the quiet protection of privacy becomes less important than the urgent duty of protecting a life. The same is true if a stranger has sent frightening messages, if money has disappeared, or if the school nurse and psychologist have **both** raised serious concern. These are the "clear, serious" lines. They should be few, and they should be honest.

⑦ So, what is the healthiest middle path? Three small rules can help. First, **ask, do not open**. If a parent is worried, she can say, "Something seems heavy lately. Would you like to show me one chat, or tell me the story in your words?" Second, **build a shared rule early** — for example, "We don't read each other's phones; but if anyone feels scared of a friend, a stranger, or herself, we talk to a named adult (parent, teacher, or 12355 helpline)." Third, **when opening is truly needed**, the parent says so **before** — not after. A sentence like "I am worried enough that I am going to read your chat this evening; we will talk together afterwards" keeps trust alive even in a hard moment.

⑧ Parents, please remember: our phones are not our whole lives, but they are the small, messy rooms where we first practise friendship, love, kindness, and anger. Respect that room, and we will grow up honest. Break the door, and we will learn to build **better locks** — not stronger hearts. **Good care asks; it does not read secretly.** In the year 2027, this is perhaps the quiet lesson our families must learn together.

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion about parents reading their middle-school child's chat records?**

A. Parents should always read the records to keep children safe.  
B. In most cases parents should not, unless there is a clear, serious reason.  
C. Children should always give their phones to parents.  
D. Parents should never care about children's friends.

**2. According to Paragraph ③, what is the FIRST harm of reading every message?**

A. Children use the phone less.  
B. Children become better at school.  
C. Trust breaks; the loving mother becomes, in the child's eyes, a small hidden camera.  
D. Parents lose their phones.

**3. Why, in Paragraph ④, does over-checking often make a home LESS safe?**

A. Because real problems go underground — the child moves real conversations to another app or code, and the parent only reads a prepared "stage".  
B. Because phones become very expensive.  
C. Because schools do not allow it.  
D. Because the child stops chatting completely.

**4. Which of the following is NOT one of the "clear, serious lines" in Paragraph ⑥?**

A. The child has spoken about ending her own life.  
B. A stranger has sent frightening messages.  
C. The child enjoyed a film her mother did not like.  
D. Money has suddenly disappeared.

**5. What is the writer's "healthiest middle path" in Paragraph ⑦?**

A. Never talk about the phone.  
B. Ask rather than secretly open; build a shared rule early; and, when reading is truly needed, tell the child before — not after.  
C. Put all phones in the parents' safe every night.  
D. Let students throw phones away.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段末 "in most cases, no — unless there is clear, serious reason"  
【解析】立场明确：原则反对 + 特例同意。A、C、D 均偏极端或偏离。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "trust breaks ... becomes ... a small hidden camera"  
【解析】首项危害直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "real problems go underground ... a chat stage carefully prepared for her eyes"  
【解析】监控反作用。B、C、D 均错。故选 A。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题（否定）  
【定位】第⑥段列举的严重情境  
【解析】A、B、D 均为示例。C 为日常喜好差异，显然不是严重线。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段三条建议  
【解析】询问 + 共同规则 + 事前告知。A、C、D 均错或极端。故选 B。
