# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle Schools Teach Students How to Manage Pocket Money?**

Ask a middle-school student, "How much pocket money do you get each month?" and the answers can surprise you. Some get fifty yuan. Some get five hundred. Some get a mysterious "as much as I need", handed out by grandparents with a smile. Yet very few students can clearly tell you how they spent their money last month. In my view, **schools should teach simple money management from middle school, because it is a life skill that every student will need — and many families are not teaching it enough**.

The first reason is **awareness**. Money is no longer bills in a wallet; it is numbers on a phone screen. A small tap pays for a drink; another pays for a game item. If we do not see money, we easily forget that it was once somebody's work. A 2026 survey of 1,500 middle-school students in our city reported that **58%** could not tell where more than half of last month's money had gone. When money becomes invisible, it also becomes unlimited — in our minds, at least. A short weekly lesson would return a clear picture: *this is how much I had, this is how much is left, this is the gap*.

The second reason is **habit**. Habits formed at age 13 often follow us for decades. A student who tries, just once, to **budget** a month's pocket money is very likely to budget a first salary years later. Schools are especially suitable for this kind of learning, because a classroom gives both rules and examples: one teacher for forty students, a shared notebook, shared mistakes, shared lessons. A parent cannot easily achieve the same effect alone, because pocket money at home always feels a little "free".

Some people disagree. They say, "Money is a family matter; schools should focus on math and Chinese." This point is partly fair — we do not want schools to tell children **exactly** what to buy. But money management is not about what to buy; it is about **how to think**. A class could teach three simple ideas: *earn*, *plan*, *save*. Students could practise by dividing imaginary income into groups (daily, savings, gifts for others). These skills are as useful as algebra or grammar, and they belong to mathematics-of-real-life, not to a single family.

Another worry: "Will this teach students to love money?" I do not think so. In fact, good money lessons often do the opposite. When a student sees that her parents work **forty** hours a week for a certain sum, she starts to respect their tiredness. When she compares the price of a bubble-tea to the price of a library book, she starts to think about what each yuan buys for her life. Money classes, done well, grow **gratitude** rather than greed.

Of course, not every lesson needs to be heavy. A small weekly activity is enough. For example: each week, students write three lines — *this week I earned / received___, I spent it on___, I saved___*. After one term, students look back and see their own story. Slowly, a sense of **responsibility** grows. Parents often notice it first — usually with a surprised smile.

In short, teaching money management in middle school is not about making small businessmen. It is about helping every child meet the adult world with open eyes. If schools can teach us how to balance an equation, they can surely teach us how to balance a small budget. And the skill will stay long after the last bell has rung.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion?**

A. Students should not be given any pocket money at all.  
B. Schools should teach simple money management from middle school.  
C. Only rich families need to discuss money.  
D. Pocket money should be banned by law.

**2. What did the 2026 survey find?**

A. 58% of students used money very wisely last month.  
B. 58% of students could not tell where more than half of last month's money had gone.  
C. All students got the same amount of money.  
D. 5% of students had no pocket money at all.

**3. Why are schools suitable places to teach money habits?**

A. Because schools sell all kinds of products.  
B. Because schools can give both rules and examples: shared mistakes and shared lessons.  
C. Because schools keep students away from parents.  
D. Because schools pay the money for students.

**4. What is the writer's reply to "This will teach students to love money"?**

A. Money lessons, done well, grow gratitude rather than greed — for example, students start to respect parents' long working hours.  
B. Students should love money, just like they love pop stars.  
C. Teachers should not be paid for teaching.  
D. Families should hide all the money from children.

**5. What small weekly activity does the writer suggest?**

A. Buying snacks for the whole class.  
B. Writing three lines about what was earned, spent, and saved each week.  
C. Selling old books every Friday.  
D. Checking each classmate's wallet.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】首段尾句  
【解析】应在初中教理财。A/C/D 均偏离。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"58% could not tell where more than half..."  
【解析】58%无法说清消费去向。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"rules and examples... shared mistakes..."  
【解析】规则+共享经验。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】"grow gratitude rather than greed"  
【解析】培养感恩，不生贪念。B/C/D 均错。故选 A。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"three lines — this week I earned / received___..."  
【解析】三行账本周记法。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。
