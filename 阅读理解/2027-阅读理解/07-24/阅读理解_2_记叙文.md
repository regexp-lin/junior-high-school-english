# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Classmate Who Taught Me to Draw a Circle**

① For most of Grade 7, I believed I was "hopeless at art". My drawings of houses looked like boxes falling over, my trees looked like green clouds on sticks, and worst of all, I could not draw a circle. Every time I tried, the pencil betrayed me — my circles ended up egg-shaped, pear-shaped, or like a very sad potato. When our art teacher asked us to draw a simple sun at the top corner of a sketch, I quietly wrote the word "sun" instead. My classmates laughed kindly, and I pretended to laugh along.

② Then, in March, a new boy moved into our class. His name was Ming. He was quiet, a little shy, and sat near the window. During his first art lesson, I noticed something odd: he finished his circles in a single smooth movement, and they were round, clean, and perfect. I could not stop staring. After class, I gathered my courage and asked him: "How do you do that?"

③ Ming looked surprised, as if no one had ever asked him anything before. "It's not a secret," he said softly. "Come to the library at lunch. I'll show you." I was not sure if he really meant it, but at noon, he was waiting at the same table, with a blank notebook and two pencils.

④ "The problem is not your hand," he said kindly. "It's your eye." He showed me his own circle-drawing method. First, he pressed four tiny dots around the paper — top, bottom, left, right — making a square shape in his mind. Then, instead of watching the pencil point, he **watched the far dot** his pencil was moving towards. "Your hand follows your eye," he said. "Trust the next dot, not the line you've already drawn."

⑤ I tried. My first try was still a potato. My second was a pear. On the third try, something strange happened: my pencil seemed to move by itself, and a small, quiet O appeared on the page — not perfect, but real. I looked up. Ming did not clap, but he gave a small, honest smile that stayed with me all afternoon.

⑥ For the rest of the term, Ming and I met at the library every Tuesday and Thursday. He taught me how to shade a sphere with six lines, how to draw a straight line by moving my whole arm, not just my fingers, and how to "see big shapes first, small details later". He never said "good" or "bad". He only said, "Try again — this time slower," or, "Your eye was too close; step back." Slowly, my trees began to look like trees, and my houses stopped falling over.

⑦ But the real lesson was not about art. One Thursday I asked him, "How did you become so good at this?" He was quiet for a long time. Then he said, "When I moved cities last year, I knew no one. I didn't know how to start a conversation. So I drew. I drew the bus driver, the shop owner, my new neighbour. Some of them saw the picture, and they started talking to me. Drawing was how I made friends." He looked at his hands. "I didn't teach myself. They did."

⑧ That day, I finally understood why Ming had been willing to help me from the very first lunch. A skill, he seemed to believe, was never really only yours. It came from kind strangers, patient teachers, and quiet afternoons. Passing it on was simply paying it back.

⑨ Two years have gone by. Ming moved again last summer, and we still send each other small drawings by post. I am not yet a great artist, but I can draw a circle, a tree, a house, even a sun at the top corner of a sketch. And whenever I do, I remember what he said on that first lunch: **trust the next dot, not the line you've already drawn**. It turns out this rule works not only on paper — but on many difficult days, in many parts of life.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Why did the writer once write the word "sun" instead of drawing one?**

A. Because he had no pencil that day.  
B. Because he believed he was hopeless at art and could not draw a good circle.  
C. Because the teacher forbade students to draw suns.  
D. Because the sun was behind a cloud that day.

**2. According to Ming, what was the real problem when the writer could not draw a circle?**

A. His pencil was too hard.  
B. His hand, not his eye, was broken.  
C. The problem was not the hand but the eye; he had to watch the far dot he was moving towards.  
D. His paper was too small.

**3. What did Ming do during their regular meetings in the library?**

A. He did the writer's homework.  
B. He taught the writer to shade a sphere, draw with the whole arm, and see big shapes first.  
C. He sold drawing books.  
D. He talked only about his old school and nothing else.

**4. Why did Ming start drawing people when he moved to a new city?**

A. Because his parents forced him to.  
B. Because drawing helped him start conversations and make friends.  
C. Because he wanted to become rich.  
D. Because he hated speaking any language.

**5. What does the writer mean by "trust the next dot, not the line you've already drawn" in the last paragraph?**

A. This art rule also works in life: focus on the next step instead of worrying about what is already done.  
B. Students should never look back at their work.  
C. Ming will send the writer more dots by post.  
D. Lines are more important than dots.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "I believed I was 'hopeless at art'... I could not draw a circle."  
【解析】画不好圆所以写字代替。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "'It's not your hand...it's your eye.'"  
【解析】问题在眼不在手，要盯着下一个点。A/B/D 均错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 "how to shade a sphere... whole arm... big shapes first..."  
【解析】阴影、运臂、整体观察。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段 "Drawing was how I made friends."  
【解析】以画结识新朋友。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑨段 "not only on paper — but on many difficult days..."  
【解析】画画道理推广到生活：关注下一步而非过去。B/C/D 均错。故选 A。
