# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Second Notebook Teacher Ma Quietly Kept for Me**

① I was never a good student of Chinese writing. In Grade 6, my essays often had a small red line under them and a single word at the bottom in the teacher's red pen: "**Rewrite.**" My mother sighed; my father shook his head; I started to believe, quietly, that I was simply "not a writing kind of person". Then, on the first day of Grade 7, a thin old man with slightly grey hair walked into our classroom, coughed softly, and said, "My name is Ma Fengshan. I will be your Chinese teacher for three years. And yes, I still use a fountain pen." That morning began something I did not fully understand until the Teacher's Day of Grade 9.

② Teacher Ma did not shout. He did not give out gold stars. He simply made us **write every week** — one small piece, 300 to 500 characters, on any topic we liked. My first three essays came back with red circles, red wavy lines, and long, patient notes in the margin. Not "Rewrite", but: "**This sentence is honest. Keep it.**" Or: "**Don't hide. Tell me what you really saw.**" I read his notes several times, and felt, for the first time, that writing was not a test I was failing — it was a **conversation** that someone was listening to.

③ Halfway through Grade 7, Teacher Ma handed me a second, smaller exercise book, with a pale blue cover. "This is not for class," he said. "This is only for you and me. You write to me when you want to. I will write back. No marks. No grades." I was surprised. That night I wrote my first private entry, barely five lines: "Teacher, I don't know why I want to write. My friend laughs at long sentences. My father says writers are poor. But sometimes at night I still open a notebook." Two days later, the book came back with a line below mine: "**Keep opening it. Poor is not the word. Quiet is.**"

④ Through Grade 7 and Grade 8, the blue book went between our desks like a small slow letter. I wrote about my grandmother's kitchen, about a classmate who moved away, about being afraid of maths tests, about a grey cat that I secretly fed near the back door. Teacher Ma wrote short careful replies, sometimes only two sentences. Once, when I told him I wanted to give up, he wrote: "**Everyone who writes has wanted to give up. You are not special in that. What is special is whether you pick up the pen again the next evening.**" I picked it up that evening.

⑤ In Grade 9, Teacher Ma's voice grew a little weaker. He still came to class every morning, still carried his black bag, but he sometimes paused in the middle of a sentence and took a slow sip of warm water. Our class monitor told us quietly that he had been sick for two years, longer than any of us knew. He had asked the school to let him finish our three years. The school had said yes.

⑥ On **Teacher's Day**, September 10th of my Grade 9, I wanted to give him something. Most classmates bought small cards, a box of tea, or a cake. I had none of those. So I did what he had taught me: I **wrote**. One 600-word piece on a single A4 page, titled "**The Blue Notebook**", about two years of silent letters that no one else in the school knew about. I folded it neatly, put it in an envelope, and placed it, together with a small bunch of late summer flowers, on his desk before morning class.

⑦ That afternoon, after the bell, Teacher Ma asked me to stay behind. He sat down slowly, opened a drawer of his old wooden desk, and pulled out — I could not believe it — **two identical blue notebooks**. "Each year," he said, "I pick one student whose writing I believe in, and I keep a second copy of our letters, in case one day I cannot answer anymore. You are this year's student, Xiao Chen. These are yours now." He pushed the two blue books toward me, gently, the way someone would push a small boat into safe water.

⑧ I could not speak at first. I opened one. It was my side of the letters, rewritten in his neat, slightly shaking handwriting. The other was his replies, also copied by him, sentence by sentence. At the end of the last page, he had written one new line, just for that day: "**If one day you want to stop writing, remember that an old, tired teacher once believed you shouldn't. And then, please, decide for yourself.**"

⑨ Teacher Ma retired at the end of that term. I never saw him teach another class, but his two blue books sit on the top shelf of my desk to this day. Before every important essay — for class, for an English speech, for anything at all — I take them down, open any page at random, and read a few lines. The old red pen, the patient notes, the steady shaking handwriting. Every time, I find the courage to open my own notebook and begin one more honest sentence. For me, every September 10th is, and will always be, **Teacher Ma's Day**.

---

**注释：** fountain pen 钢笔；margin 页边；sentence 句子；envelope 信封

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. How did the writer feel about Chinese writing before meeting Teacher Ma in Grade 7?**

A. She was the best writer in her school and full of confidence.  
B. She quietly believed she was "not a writing kind of person" and felt she had been failing.  
C. She never cared about writing at all.  
D. She was paid to help other students write their essays.

**2. What did Teacher Ma do that was different from the writer's old Chinese teacher?**

A. He wrote only "Rewrite" at the bottom of her essays.  
B. He gave her red stars every week.  
C. He left patient notes in the margin like "This sentence is honest. Keep it." and asked her not to hide.  
D. He refused to read her essays at all.

**3. What was special about the **blue notebook** Teacher Ma gave her in Grade 7?**

A. It was only for homework, with many marks and grades.  
B. It was a private notebook between only her and Teacher Ma, with no grades, where she could write and he would reply.  
C. It was for all the students in the class to share.  
D. It was a school prize for being the top student.

**4. On Teacher's Day in Grade 9, what did Teacher Ma show and give the writer after class?**

A. A photo of his family.  
B. A new pair of glasses.  
C. **Two identical blue notebooks**: one copy of the writer's letters and one of his own replies, both rewritten in his careful handwriting.  
D. A letter from the head teacher.

**5. What does the writer mean by saying, "every September 10th is, and will always be, Teacher Ma's Day"?**

A. That she has officially renamed Teacher's Day.  
B. That Teacher Ma is the only teacher anyone should respect.  
C. That for her, Teacher's Day will always carry a special, personal memory of Teacher Ma's patient trust in her writing.  
D. That she will celebrate her own birthday instead of Teacher's Day.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "I started to believe, quietly, that I was simply 'not a writing kind of person'"  
【解析】相遇前"我"自认不适合写作，屡屡受挫。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "'This sentence is honest. Keep it.' Or: 'Don't hide. Tell me what you really saw.'"  
【解析】马老师用耐心旁注代替冷漠批语。A、B、D 均相反或错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "'This is not for class. This is only for you and me. You write to me when you want to. I will write back. No marks. No grades.'"  
【解析】蓝本子是师生间无评分的私人通信。A、C、D 均与原文相悖。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段 "he pulled out — I could not believe it — two identical blue notebooks ... I keep a second copy of our letters ... These are yours now"  
【解析】马老师把师生通信的誊抄本郑重相赠。A、B、D 均无依据。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨/写作意图题  
【定位】第⑨段 "every September 10th is, and will always be, Teacher Ma's Day"  
【解析】该句承载作者对马老师深切的个人纪念。A、B、D 均为字面或极端误读。故选 C。
