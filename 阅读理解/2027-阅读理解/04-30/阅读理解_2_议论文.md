# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Traditional Handwriting Still Be Practised in the Age of Typing?**

① On most Chinese middle-school mornings, students open their laptops, tap keyboards, and see neat black characters appear on the screen. Homework is typed. Essays are printed. Test answers on many school tablets are uploaded with one finger. In such a **digital world**, some people ask: **should we still spend time practising traditional handwriting, like Chinese calligraphy or tidy English cursive?** Is it just a beautiful but **old-fashioned** skill, or is it still important today?

② Supporters of typing say they are not against beauty, but they argue that **time is short**. A Grade 9 student already has mathematics, science, English, and many other subjects to prepare for. Teaching careful handwriting may take **hundreds of extra hours**. In the future, most people will write with voice input, AI, and touchscreens; why, then, make students practise something they may rarely use? Typing is **faster, clearer**, and can be shared across the world in one click.

③ However, people who defend handwriting have strong arguments, too. First, handwriting is not only a tool; it is also a **cultural art**. **Chinese calligraphy** has been praised for more than two thousand years, from **Wang Xizhi** to modern masters. A handwritten poem on a red Spring Festival scroll, a thank-you card in a teacher's drawer — these are warm pieces of our culture. If no one in our generation can write by hand, who will continue these treasures?

④ Second, science supports handwriting. Several studies, including research at universities in Norway and the United States, have found that **writing by hand activates more parts of the brain** than typing. Students who take notes by hand often remember ideas **better**, because hand movement forces the brain to choose, summarise, and re-shape information. Typing, by contrast, can become a kind of "copy machine": fast, but shallow.

⑤ Third, careful handwriting helps build **patience and calm**. Pressing a pen against paper, waiting for ink to dry, and slowing down one stroke at a time — these small actions train a young mind to sit still. In a world full of pop-up notifications, the handwriting table is a quiet room where thinking can grow. Some schools now use **20 minutes of daily handwriting practice** not to replace typing, but to balance it, like exercise balances sitting.

⑥ Of course, being realistic also matters. We should not demand that students write every paragraph by hand, nor that every homework must be in perfect calligraphy. A sensible path is to **use both**. Long reports, emails, and data tables are best typed. Diaries, thank-you letters, poems, and personal reflections are best **written by hand**. Schools can offer **one weekly hour** of calligraphy or tidy English cursive as an enjoyable class, not a punishment.

⑦ Typing is a bridge to the world; handwriting is a path into ourselves. A fast bridge without a quiet path may lead our generation to places where ideas travel quickly, but feelings are thin. In the age of smooth keyboards and soft-glowing screens, a carefully shaped character in black ink is not old-fashioned — it is, in fact, one of the youngest and bravest things we can still do with a pen.
