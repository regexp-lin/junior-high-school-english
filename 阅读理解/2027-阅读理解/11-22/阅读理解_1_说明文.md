# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do Distant Mountains Look Blue?**

① On a clear afternoon last October our class took a school bus to the hills north of the city. The hills closest to us — about two kilometres away — were the rich green of late-summer leaves. The hills five kilometres further were **softer green, almost grey-green**. But the farthest row, maybe twenty kilometres away at the edge of the sky, looked unmistakably **blue** — a thin, misty, cool blue that did not belong to the leaves of any tree. A classmate raised a strange but beautiful question: *"Mountains are green. So why do far mountains look blue?"* It is a question painters and scientists have both loved for centuries, and the short answer lives in three letters and one long Greek name: **Rayleigh scattering**.

② To understand the answer, we have to remember one fact about sunlight. The white light from the sun is not really white; it is a mixture of **all the colours of the rainbow** — from red, orange, and yellow to green, blue, and violet. Each colour is a **wave**, and each wave has a different length. Red light waves are long, lazy, and heavy; blue light waves are short, quick, and bouncy. Violet is even shorter than blue. This difference in length is the whole secret.

③ When sunlight travels through air, it meets millions of tiny molecules — mostly **nitrogen (N₂)** and **oxygen (O₂)**. Most of the light passes through without much trouble; but the **short, bouncy blue and violet waves** are a much better match in size for these tiny molecules, and so they are **scattered in every direction**, like small balls hitting a field of tiny pins. This is the **Rayleigh scattering** named after the British scientist **Lord Rayleigh** (1871). It is the same reason the sky above us looks blue in the middle of the day: blue light is scattered everywhere, and our eyes finally see it coming from every corner of the sky.

④ Now picture a mountain. The green light from the green leaves of the mountain has to travel a long, long way through the air before it reaches your eyes. On that long trip, **most of the green light gets through**, but much of it is **absorbed or weakened** by water, dust, and humidity in the air. Meanwhile, **new blue light is constantly being scattered into your line of sight** from all the blue sky between you and the mountain — a thick, soft **column of blue air** stretching across several kilometres. The result is simple: the further the mountain, the more the eye sees **fresh blue light added, and the mountain's own green subtracted**. The green fades; the blue grows stronger. Distance turns green into blue.

⑤ This is why old Chinese poems, 1,200 years ago, often call far mountains **"qing"** (青) — a word that sits between blue, green, and grey. The poets had no laboratories and did not know about nitrogen molecules. But they had sharp, patient eyes. They noticed the colour of distance before they had a word for it.

⑥ Several small tests, which you can do yourself, show that the phenomenon is real. **Test 1**: in the early morning, before fog settles, look at a mountain ten kilometres away; it looks greenish-grey. By noon, when the air column between you and the mountain is warmer and thicker with tiny water droplets, the same mountain is clearly bluer. **Test 2**: after a heavy rain, when the air is cleaner, even the same distant row of hills looks **more green and less blue** — because less dust and less water mean less scattering. **Test 3**: look at the same range from a plane, above most of the air; mountains there look **much greener** than they do from the ground, because much of the scattering air is now below you.

⑦ There is also a small related puzzle: **if blue light is scattered, why not violet?** Violet is scattered even more. The short honest answer has two parts. First, the sun actually produces **less violet light** than blue. Second, our eyes are **much less sensitive to violet** than to blue. So even though the sky is technically scattering a little violet too, our eyes simply collect and read most of the scattered light as **blue**. It is one of those places where physics meets the human eye, and the eye has the last word.

⑧ So the next time you stand on a high balcony at sunset and see the farthest range turn from grey-green to a soft, misty blue — and finally to a pale violet-pink — you will know: you are looking at **a thick layer of air** between you and the mountain, and that thick layer is quietly painting the view with the short waves of the sunlight that happens to be the best match for its molecules. The mountain has not changed colour. **The air in between has taken up a brush** and, without asking anyone's permission, has turned green into blue. This is one of the small, quiet miracles of looking at the world from where we are, and it is free, every clear afternoon, to anyone who bothers to look.

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the main scientific name behind the answer to "why far mountains look blue"?**

A. Light reflection.  
B. Rayleigh scattering, named after the British scientist Lord Rayleigh (1871).  
C. Newton's third law.  
D. Water evaporation.

**2. Why are blue and violet light scattered more than red light?**

A. Because blue is the sun's favourite colour.  
B. Because sunlight is a mixture of all rainbow colours of different wave lengths; blue and violet waves are short, quick and bouncy, and their length is a much better match for the tiny nitrogen and oxygen molecules in the air, so they are scattered in every direction — like small balls hitting a field of tiny pins.  
C. Because green leaves block them.  
D. Because red light is too weak.

**3. Why does a mountain's green fade with distance, while blue grows stronger?**

A. Because mountains change colour at night.  
B. Because the green light of leaves must travel a long way through the air and is absorbed or weakened by water, dust and humidity; meanwhile the thick column of blue sky-air between us and the mountain constantly scatters new blue light into our line of sight — so the further the mountain, the more blue is added and green subtracted.  
C. Because the sun sets quickly.  
D. Because trees die far away.

**4. Which of the following small tests supports the Rayleigh-scattering explanation?**

A. After heavy rain, distant hills look bluer than usual.  
B. After heavy rain, when the air is cleaner, the same distant hills look more green and less blue; from a plane, above most of the air, mountains look much greener than from the ground, because most of the scattering air is below you.  
C. At night, mountains always look red.  
D. On cloudy days, mountains look yellow.

**5. Why do we see blue and not mostly violet, even though violet is scattered even more?**

A. Because violet is not a real colour.  
B. Because the sun actually produces less violet light, and human eyes are much less sensitive to violet than to blue, so our eyes collect and read most of the scattered light as blue — a place where physics meets the human eye and the eye has the last word.  
C. Because the sky blocks violet.  
D. Because violet light is absorbed by mountains.
