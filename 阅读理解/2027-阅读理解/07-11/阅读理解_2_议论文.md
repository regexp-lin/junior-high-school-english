# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Learn a Second Foreign Language?**

In our country, English is already a compulsory subject from primary school. But now the question is raised more and more often in parent groups and school meetings: *Should middle-school students learn a second foreign language too — for example, Japanese, French, Spanish, or German?* Some parents feel strongly in favour; others say middle school is already too busy. In my view, **starting a second foreign language in middle school is a wise choice, as long as students learn it in a light, friendly, long-term way**.

The first reason is about the **brain**. Language learning is not only about remembering words; it shapes the way we think. Studies from several countries show that students who learn a second foreign language usually do better in problem solving, memory, and even mathematics. The reason is simple: switching between two language systems forces the brain to choose, to compare, and to organise — all skills that transfer to other subjects. A small effort in French class may come back as clearer thinking in geometry class.

The second reason is **opportunity**. Our world is more connected than ever. Business, tourism, medicine, and technology now cross borders every day. A student who speaks English plus one more language will have **doors** opened to more universities, more scholarships, and more jobs. Many top schools in Asia and Europe give extra points in their entrance test for students with a second foreign language. Even if we never leave our country, reading the news in two languages gives us two windows on the same world.

Some classmates worry: "Middle school is already heavy — adding a language will break me." This worry is serious and deserves a real answer. The key word is **design**. A second language does not need to be studied for three hours every day; fifteen to twenty minutes is enough, if done regularly. A short song on the way to school, a small chapter before bed, a ten-minute conversation with a friend — these build real skill over months, without stealing time from math or science. In fact, many schools in Shanghai now offer second-language classes *after* the main timetable, and students who join those classes report that they feel **refreshed** rather than tired.

Those who oppose a second language sometimes say: "Focus on English first; do not spread the mind thin." This sounds reasonable, but there is a quiet answer. Research shows that starting a second foreign language does not weaken the first. Our brain has no fixed "language box" that fills up. On the contrary, students who meet another language often **notice** English grammar more clearly, because they have something to compare with. "Thinner" learning is not what happens — "deeper" learning is.

Of course, we must also mention **culture**. Every language carries a culture behind it. A boy who learns Japanese may one day read a poem written 800 years ago. A girl who learns Spanish may meet the warm rhythm of Latin-American music. A student who learns French may, on a winter evening, read a short story about a quiet baker in Paris. These moments will not appear on any exam, but they build a richer human being.

In short, a second foreign language is not just an extra subject. It is a new way of thinking, a new set of doors, a new cultural window. If we choose it wisely, keep it light, and stay with it patiently, middle school will not be harder — it will simply become wider.

**1. What is the writer's main opinion?**

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】首段尾句  
【解析】应在轻松、长期的方式下学习第二外语。A/B/D 均偏离。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"forces the brain to choose, to compare..."  
【解析】切换语言锻炼大脑。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"fifteen to twenty minutes is enough"  
【解析】每天 15–20 分钟。A/B/D 均错。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】"no fixed language box... notice English grammar more clearly"  
【解析】双语反而加强英语意识。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】最后一段  
【解析】文化+诗+音乐+故事。B/C/D 均错。故选 A。
