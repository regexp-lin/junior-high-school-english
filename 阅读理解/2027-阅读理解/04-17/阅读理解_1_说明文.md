# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Paper Was Invented in Ancient China**

① Long before laptops and printers, people wrote on whatever they could find. In ancient Egypt, they used a plant called **papyrus**; in Europe, sheepskin was cut into "parchment"; and in early China, people carved **Chinese characters** onto **tortoise shells**, **bamboo strips**, and **pieces of silk**. However, tortoise shells were rare, bamboo strips were heavy, and silk was expensive. There had to be a better way, and the answer came from a small Chinese official named **Cai Lun**.

② Cai Lun worked in the palace of the Han Dynasty around the year **AD 105**. He noticed that people wasted rags, old fishing nets, and plant bark. One day, he asked a simple question: "Could these useless things become something useful for writing?" After many tries, he made **paper**, a thin and smooth sheet that could be rolled, cut, and folded. His method became famous quickly, and his paper was called **Cai Lun paper**.

③ How did he do it? Cai Lun's method had **four main steps**. First, collect soft materials such as **tree bark, old cloth, and used fishing nets**. Second, put them into a big pot of water and **boil** them until they become soft and mushy. Third, beat the soft pulp with wooden sticks until the tiny fibres break apart. Fourth, spread the pulp thinly on a **bamboo screen**, let the water drain, and press and dry the wet sheet under sunlight. What was left behind was a clean, light, and strong piece of paper.

④ Compared with bamboo strips or silk, paper had **three big advantages**. It was **light**, so one person could carry a whole "library" of paper books. It was **cheap**, because the materials were everyday things. It was **easy to store**, since sheets could be cut, stacked, and bound. Because of paper, knowledge could now travel far more easily from teacher to student, and from parent to child.

⑤ Paper slowly made the long journey out of China. After the year AD 751, paper-making moved westwards, reaching Arab lands, North Africa, Spain, and finally the rest of Europe. For more than a thousand years, every book, every school exercise, and every letter of love or trade was carried on these thin sheets. When you write on paper today, you are holding a tiny piece of a very long story — a story that began in a quiet palace room in China almost **two thousand years** ago.

⑥ Of course, paper has a **price**. Making lots of paper uses wood and water. In our own time, we should use every sheet carefully, **write on both sides**, recycle old paper, and choose digital notes when they really do the job better. Cai Lun would probably be proud if he knew that his invention travelled so far; but he might also remind us: every sheet of paper still carries the same hard work of human hands.
