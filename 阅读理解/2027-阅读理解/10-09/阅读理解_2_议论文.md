# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Families Try a Weekly "No-Screen Sunday"?**

① On the last page of a parenting magazine my mother reads, a simple idea has been travelling through several Chinese cities: **No-Screen Sunday**. For one day a week, every member of the family — parents, students, and even grandparents — puts down all phones, tablets, laptops and TVs, and spends the day without screens. Some families have loved it; others say it is impossible. Should middle-school families really try this? I believe **a carefully planned weekly No-Screen Sunday is a good idea** — not because screens are evil, but because a healthy diet needs some days without dessert.

② First, one screen-free day **gives the brain a real rest**. Middle-school students today spend six to seven hours a day on screens — phones, classroom projectors, school tablets, short videos, group chats. Recent studies in several countries have shown that too much screen time is linked to more tired eyes, worse sleep, shorter attention, and even weaker memory of long texts. A single day off every week will not cure everything, but it is a gentle reset — like drinking water after a salty meal.

③ Second, No-Screen Sunday **returns time to the family**. In many homes, when parents, grandparents and children sit together on the sofa, each person is in fact alone: one is watching a soap, one is scrolling short videos, one is on a work chat. Take away the screens for one day, and something old comes back — boardgames, a walk to the market, stories told at the dinner table. My own family tried this twice. On the first Sunday, we were a little bored. On the second, my younger sister began to teach my grandmother how to make paper cranes, and the afternoon disappeared into quiet paper folding and quiet laughter.

④ Third, a screen-free day **shows students that life is interesting without a phone**. A 14-year-old who has known only a phone-filled world often truly believes that free time *means* scrolling. A weekly Sunday without screens quietly proves the opposite. One rainy afternoon, my classmate Li Ming discovered that he could draw a bird that actually looked like a bird. He would never have picked up a pencil if his phone had been in his hand. A single discovery like that can start a lifelong interest.

⑤ **Critics raise reasonable worries**. One is **schoolwork**: many middle-school assignments are now sent through a school app. If a student does no homework all Sunday, Monday could become a disaster. This is a fair point, but the answer is simple: do school-app homework **on Saturday**, and keep Sunday for people and paper. Students can also print key materials on Saturday night. Planning, not screens, is the real work.

⑥ A second worry is **family communication**. Parents say they need messenger apps to stay in touch with work, with the grandparents who live far away, or with the child's teacher. This is true. A perfect "no-pixels-all-day" rule is too hard. A better rule is **a clear small window**: say, 7:30 to 8:00 a.m. and 8:00 to 8:30 p.m. — thirty minutes, twice, just for urgent messages. Outside these windows, phones are put in a small box on a shelf. A rule with a window is a rule the family can actually keep.

⑦ A third worry is **boredom**. "My son will drive me crazy," one father said, "he does not know how to play outside." The truth behind this worry is that the skill of "enjoying real life" is itself something learned. The first No-Screen Sunday will be awkward. The fifth will feel natural. Skills grow only from practice; the same is true for the skill of a slow afternoon.

⑧ There is also a deeper point. A person's relationship with their phone is a small picture of their relationship with **freedom**. A student who cannot stop looking at the screen, even when he wants to, has quietly given the phone the power to decide how he spends his time. One Sunday a week, without screens, is a small but steady way to take that power back.

⑨ My conclusion is balanced. Middle-school families should try **one "No-Screen Sunday" a week** — with a short messaging window and an honest plan for homework on Saturday. Nobody is asking us to return to the 1990s. We are asking only to spend one day out of seven with our own eyes looking up, at the people we live with and the world we live in. If, after three months, this one day turns out to be the day the family remembers most at year's end, perhaps that was the point all along.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main view?**

A. Screens are evil and should be banned from all homes forever.  
B. Middle-school families should try a carefully planned weekly No-Screen Sunday, with sensible rules.  
C. Students should never use phones for schoolwork.  
D. Parents alone, not children, should stop using phones.

**2. In Paragraph 3, what does the writer mainly want to show?**

A. Screens make families closer.  
B. Without screens, the grandparents feel lonely.  
C. A screen-free day can bring families back into real contact — boardgames, walks, stories, even teaching grandma paper cranes.  
D. Sundays should always be boring.

**3. How does the writer reply to the worry that homework requires a school app?**

A. He says homework should be cancelled.  
B. He says students should never use school apps.  
C. He suggests finishing school-app homework on Saturday and printing key materials, so that Sunday stays screen-free.  
D. He says students should do homework on their phones all Sunday.

**4. Why does the writer propose "a short messaging window" twice a day (Paragraph 6)?**

A. Because he wants to sell messaging software.  
B. Because some messages (work, distant grandparents, teachers) really are important, and a rule with a small window is one the family can actually keep.  
C. Because he thinks parents should be punished.  
D. Because windows are cheap.

**5. What does the writer mean when he says our relationship with our phone is "a small picture of our relationship with freedom" (Paragraph 8)?**

A. Phones are free, so everyone should buy one.  
B. A student who cannot stop looking at the screen has given the phone the power to decide how he spends his time; taking one screen-free day back is a small act of regaining that freedom.  
C. Phones are more important than freedom.  
D. Freedom and phones are exactly the same thing.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨/观点题  
【定位】第①段 "a carefully planned weekly No-Screen Sunday is a good idea"；第⑨段 "try one 'No-Screen Sunday' a week ... with a short messaging window"  
【解析】作者支持每周一次、有规划的无屏日。A 极端、C、D 均偏离原文。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "take away the screens for one day, and something old comes back — boardgames, a walk to the market, stories told at the dinner table"  
【解析】没屏幕才能真正回到家人互动。A 与原文相反、B 片面、D 荒谬。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "do school-app homework on Saturday ... keep Sunday for people and paper"  
【解析】作者建议周六做完 app 作业。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑥段 "A better rule is a clear small window ... A rule with a window is a rule the family can actually keep."  
【解析】现实主义设计：保证紧急通讯，又守住原则。A、C、D 均非作者真实理由。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】词义猜测/推理判断题  
【定位】第⑧段 "has quietly given the phone the power to decide how he spends his time. One Sunday a week ... is a small but steady way to take that power back."  
【解析】作者用"自由"隐喻控制自己的时间。A、C、D 均误读。故选 B。
