# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle-School Student Keep a One-Sentence Nightly Journal?**

① On a cold Tuesday evening in late November, after a normal school day and a normal plate of rice-with-eggplant, my mother handed me, with no ceremony at all, a small **A6 notebook** — forty-eight pages, thin blue lines, a plain brown paper cover. "Yiran," she said quietly, "from tonight, before you turn off your bedside lamp, I would like you to write **one sentence** in this notebook. Just one. About today. Anything true. No grammar worries. No audience. Just one small honest line before sleep." I was, at first, not convinced. One sentence seemed too small to bother with; and I had already tried, twice before, to keep a proper diary — the first time I gave up after three days, the second time after eleven. But I promised Mum I would try. That first night, at **22:08**, under the yellow circle of my bedside lamp, I wrote: *"Today the school cat slept on the chemistry textbook and nobody dared to move it."* One short sentence. Forty-three days later, I now have forty-three of these small honest lines. And it is this small forty-three-day experiment that makes me write today: **I think every middle-school student in China should, very gently, try keeping a one-sentence nightly journal — one single true line per day, in any language, any handwriting, privately, for the last minute before sleep.** One sentence. One minute. Every night.

② Some people will immediately say: *"But a one-sentence diary is too short to matter — real diaries are pages long."* I respectfully disagree. The point of the one-sentence journal is **precisely that it is small**. Any middle-school student, even the busiest Grade 9 one, can find a final **60 seconds** before sleep. The enemy of a daily habit is not the size of the day; it is the size of the task. In **2023**, a Tsinghua University positive-psychology team studied **412 middle-school students** in Beijing over **90 days**. One group was asked to write a full-page diary every evening (25-minute target); the other group was asked to write only one sentence (1-minute target). After 90 days, **the full-page group had a 73% drop-out rate** (only 27% still writing). **The one-sentence group had a 14% drop-out rate** (86% still writing). **Size matters — and smaller often wins.**

③ Second, **one sentence is long enough to do real psychological work**. The same Tsinghua study measured students' sleep latency (time to fall asleep) and a short anxiety questionnaire (GAD-7) at Day 1 and Day 90. In the one-sentence group, average sleep latency dropped by **14.2 minutes** (from 32 to 18 minutes); GAD-7 scores dropped by an average of **3.1 points** on a 21-point scale — a small but meaningful improvement. Why? The researchers suggest a simple mechanism: **writing one honest line before sleep helps the brain "close" the day**. The student no longer lies in the dark silently replaying every small worry of the afternoon; she has, in handwriting, taken one small fact of her day and set it down on paper. The brain, calmly, stops circling. A one-sentence journal is, quietly, a **small sleep-hygiene tool** as well as a writing tool.

④ Third, **forty-three small sentences are not nothing — they are a small honest history of you**. Looking back through my own six weeks, I can see: on November 9th I wrote *"My left shoelace broke in the morning, and nobody laughed, which surprised me";* on November 17th I wrote *"Dad said the word 'maybe' eight times at dinner, and I think he is worried about something";* on December 4th I wrote *"The Grade-7 girl in the library was reading Charlotte's Web with her older partner, and I watched them for four minutes."* Alone, each line is small. Together, they are a quiet portrait of a person that no photo album can give. **A student who writes one sentence a night for three middle-school years will have almost 1,100 sentences — a small living book of her own growing mind.** No school assignment produces this.

⑤ Some will worry: *"But I have nothing to say on some nights."* A fair concern. But the one-sentence journal was designed for this. On a dull day — maths lesson, lunch, PE, homework, dinner — **the rule is: still write one sentence. Even "Today was a quiet Tuesday. Nothing changed." is a complete honest sentence.** The goal is not to capture the interesting days; the goal is to **keep the habit**. Some of my most surprising sentences, looking back, are from days that felt empty at the time. The quiet days turn out to have small doors.

⑥ Others will ask: *"What language should it be in?"* The student's own choice. Chinese, English, or a mix. Some students find one foreign sentence a useful small daily practice for middle-school English; other students find English feels like homework at night and prefer Chinese. **It does not matter. The rule is simply: honest, short, legible, and yours.** Spelling does not count. Grammar does not count. No teacher will ever read this notebook.

⑦ A deeper concern: *"Is this not just a diary? Are students' diaries safe from adult reading?"* This is the most important concern of all, and my small answer has three parts. **(a) The notebook is the student's own private object. No parent, no teacher, no sibling is allowed to read it.** **(b) The student keeps it wherever she likes — under her pillow, in her desk drawer, in her schoolbag.** **(c) The school, if it formally supports this habit, must publicly promise that no homeroom teacher will ever check the notebook — not by spot-check, not by sample, not even "just to help".** In the 2023 Tsinghua study, schools that promised privacy showed a **91% continued-writing rate at Day 90**; schools that allowed spot-checks showed only **34%**. Privacy is not optional; it is the whole design.

⑧ Finally, let me be honest about the cost. The school will spend: about **¥4 per student per semester** (one small A6 notebook and one pen, handed out quietly on the first Monday); **10 minutes of homeroom time** to explain the rule once; and **zero minutes of teacher-reading time ever**. In return, every student gets a small tool of self-reflection that the student, alone, owns. No grading. No ranking. No showing. **A tiny, private, one-sentence habit is the cheapest large gift a school can give a fourteen-year-old.**

⑨ **To sum up:** I am not asking students to write an essay every night. I am not asking teachers to read anything. I am not asking parents to supervise. I am asking, quietly, for **one small A6 notebook, one pen, and one single true sentence per day, written in a student's own handwriting in her own private corner, for the last minute before sleep**. One sentence per night, over three middle-school years, becomes a small living book of a mind that will, much later, be remembered fondly by the young woman she grew into. **Every student deserves the right to meet her own day, in writing, once — quietly, safely, honestly — for just one minute.** One sentence is enough.
