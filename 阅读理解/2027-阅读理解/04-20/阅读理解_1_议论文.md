# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Parents Read Their Children's Diaries?**

① A diary is often the **quietest friend** of a middle school student. Between maths tests and English vocabulary lists, many teens use a small notebook to record secret thoughts — crushes, worries, friendships, and dreams. For some parents, however, a closed diary is like a closed door: they worry, they wonder, and some of them, one way or another, choose to **open it and read**. Should they? This is not a new question, but in our family group chats and home evenings, it is still a loud one.

② Some parents strongly believe the answer is **yes**. They say their only goal is to **protect** their child. If a diary shows signs of bullying, heavy sadness, or unsafe online friendships, a parent can act before things become serious. In a world where teenagers can meet 500 strangers in one afternoon online, a silent diary may be the only early **warning bell**. "It is not about distrust," one mother told a TV reporter, "it is about not waiting until it is too late."

③ However, many teachers, doctors, and teens disagree. They say reading a child's diary in secret is a form of **breaking trust**. When a young person finds out that the diary has been read, he may feel **deeply hurt**, as if someone has walked into his bedroom without knocking. The child may then stop writing honestly, or even stop talking openly at home. A parent who wants to "see the truth" may end up seeing **nothing real** again.

④ There is also the question of **growth**. A diary is one of the few safe places where a teen can practise thinking, feeling, and changing without fear. On Monday he may hate a classmate; on Wednesday he may be best friends again. If a parent reads only one page, without seeing the full week, wrong alarms can ring, and small problems can grow into big ones through misunderstanding.

⑤ Supporters of privacy are not saying that parents should do nothing. Instead, they suggest **better tools**: calm weekly chats about school and friends; open doors at home where teens can ask hard questions without punishment; and learning to read **signs in daily life** — changed eating habits, quieter voices, fewer smiles — rather than secret pages. If real warning signs appear, parents may ask to see the diary **together with the child**, not behind his back.

⑥ My own view is that a middle-ground path is wisest. Children, like small plants, need both the **rain of care** and the **sun of privacy**. Parents should promise their children that diaries are safe, and children should promise their parents that they will speak up when life becomes truly dark. Trust is not built by a single rule, but by hundreds of careful choices over many years. A diary, kept closed by love and opened only by a child's own hand, may be one of the most important choices we can make as a family.
