# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do We Laugh When We Are Tickled?**

① One of the small funny mysteries in a child's life is this: a five-year-old knows, ahead of time, that her father is going to tickle her under the arm — she may even beg him, laughing, not to do it. Yet the moment his fingers touch her side, she is absolutely helpless with laughter. She cannot stop. She does not find it really funny (the joke hasn't even been told), and yet her whole body behaves as if it has just heard the best joke in the world. Why? Tickling, which looks like a small ordinary human joke, turns out to be one of the most carefully studied small reflexes in neuroscience. Behind the laugh are **three surprising findings** that even many adults have not heard before.

② **Finding one: there are actually two kinds of tickling.** Scientists carefully separate them. **Knismesis** is the light, feather-like tickle — the kind that makes you want to brush away an invisible insect. It does not, usually, produce laughter; it produces small, careful alertness. **Gargalesis** is the deeper tickle — pressure on certain sensitive spots of the body, which produces the big, uncontrollable laughter. Humans, great apes, and — surprisingly — **rats** are the only animals we know of that show something like gargalesis. (Rats, when tickled gently on the belly by a kind researcher, produce ultrasonic "giggle" sounds at about **50 kilohertz**, clearly audible only with special equipment. Research by psychologist Jaak Panksepp at Washington State University, 1998–2012.)

③ **Finding two: you cannot tickle yourself.** Try it. Run your own fingers under your own arm, against your own side. Nothing happens. Your body stays calm. The reason: the **cerebellum** — a small structure at the back of your brain — **predicts in real time the sensory results of your own movements** and sends a signal ahead to "cancel" them in your conscious experience. When you lift your hand, the cerebellum tells the rest of the brain: *"Expect a touch under the arm in 0.2 seconds — it's just us, not a surprise."* A 1998 University College London study (using fMRI scans of 24 adults) showed the brain region that normally activates in response to tickle-sensation (the **somatosensory cortex**) activates **far less** when the person tickles herself than when another person tickles her, even at the same physical force. Tickling needs **surprise** to work — and your own body always knows what you are about to do.

④ **Finding three: the laugh is not really "fun"; it is a social signal.** Modern neuroscience has spent decades looking inside tickle-laughter, and the honest answer is: the laugh is **not** the same as the laugh we make at a funny joke. **Joke-laughter** and **tickle-laughter** activate different regions of the brain: joke-laughter mainly the **frontal cortex** (humour understanding), tickle-laughter mainly the **hypothalamus + anterior cingulate cortex** (a social bonding area). A 2014 study at Tübingen University, Germany, scanned 40 volunteers: the tickle-laugh overlapped 37% with pain regions in the brain — unlike the clean joke-laugh, which did not. In other words, **tickle-laughter is not a smaller cousin of joke-laughter**. It is an ancient, almost involuntary response that, some scientists suggest, evolved as a **kind of friendly "mock-surrender"** — a small signal used between close family members to say, *"you have physically overpowered me, but this is play; we are safe."*

⑤ **What makes a spot ticklish?** Research on 1,068 adults at the University of Bristol (2019) mapped the most ticklish spots. The top five, in order, were: **under the arms, ribs, belly, the soles of the feet, the back of the neck**. What links them? Each is an area that is **(a) sensitive (many nerve endings in a small skin area),** **(b) close to vital organs** (heart, lungs, stomach, spinal cord), and **(c) relatively hard to defend** in a fight. Evolutionary biologists suggest the reflex may have once protected us: a surprise touch on a vital area **alerts the body quickly, draws attention to the threat, and, in play, teaches children how to protect these spots while laughing it off**. Tickling between older and younger siblings, or parent and child, may, some researchers argue, be a playful version of "defence training."

⑥ **What about people who "hate" being tickled?** They are not imagining the discomfort. A small but real minority of humans — around **6% according to the 2019 Bristol study** — find even light tickling deeply unpleasant. Their tickle-laugh does not contain the usual friendly-bonding marker, and cortisol (a stress hormone) rises rather than falls. Respect people who do not like it. The laugh, in their case, really is a small signal of distress, not a hidden joy.

⑦ **Why does tickling lose its power as we grow older?** Adults are less easily tickled than children, and adults being tickled by strangers laugh less than adults being tickled by close family. Two reasons. First, the cerebellum becomes more skilled at anticipating: adults predict the touch pattern better, reducing the surprise. Second, adult tickle-laughter requires **psychological safety** — the same 2014 Tübingen study found that the laugh sharply reduced when the scanner-subject was in an unfamiliar or stressful room. The reflex is, in a deep sense, a **safety signal between people who already trust each other**. When safety is low, no laugh comes.

⑧ **Is tickling a "real" form of communication?** The answer seems to be yes, in a quietly surprising way. In households where parents tickle children in a safe, short, playful way (never too long, never after a "stop"), children show slightly higher warmth scores toward parents in adolescence — a weak but real effect in a 2023 Zhejiang University longitudinal study of 824 families over 9 years. Tickling, well-used, is a small piece of the long language of family warmth. Ill-used (too long, ignoring a "stop"), it becomes the opposite: a breach of trust.

⑨ **The quiet rule.** Stop when the person says stop. Look at their face; a stressed laugh is not the same as a happy laugh. A tickle should last **seconds, not minutes**. When the rule is kept, tickling is what it has been for five or six million years of human family life: a small, ancient, nearly-wordless way of saying, *"We are safe; we are close; we play."* And the helpless giggling of a five-year-old with her father's fingers light under her arm is not only a laugh — it is **a tiny living piece of human biology**, already watching the adults who will love her, already learning that some of the best messages we give each other do not, in the end, need any words.
