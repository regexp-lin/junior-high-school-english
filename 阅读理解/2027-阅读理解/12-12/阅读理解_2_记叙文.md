# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Afternoon Grandpa and I Fixed the Old Radio Together**

① Grandpa is **seventy-six** this winter. On an ordinary Sunday afternoon in December, he called me over to his small bedroom and said, in his gentle northern voice, *"Yiran, today we are going to fix something. Are you free for two hours?"* I said I was. He walked slowly to his wardrobe, reached up to the highest shelf, and carefully brought down a **small, old, wood-sided radio**. The front grille was light brown cloth; the two black knobs were worn smooth; across the top, in faded gold paint, were four characters: **红旗 · 一九七四 (Red Flag · 1974)**. He set it down on his round tea table, placed a small clean cloth beside it, and said, *"This radio listened to my news every evening from 1974 to 1999. Then it stopped. For twenty-eight years it has lived in a wardrobe. Today, we will listen to the shape of it again."*

② I was fourteen. I had never opened a radio. Grandpa had been an **electric-engine technician** at the old textile factory for thirty-one years before retirement; he knew his way around small machines the way our Chinese teacher knew her way around Tang poems. He did not hurry. He sat across from me at the tea table, under the warm yellow light of his reading lamp, and laid out the tools carefully, one by one: a small flat screwdriver, a small cross screwdriver, a short-handled multimeter, a small soft brush, two cotton swabs, one tiny bottle of contact cleaner, and a narrow strip of soft rosin-core solder. The order of the tools — small to the right, longer to the left — looked, I realised, almost like **calligraphy**.

③ *"Step one,"* he said, *"we look at the outside."* We sat for five minutes, just looking. He pointed out three things: a small scratch on the top left (1981, a metal lunchbox had fallen on it, he remembered); a tiny faded red sticker inside a crack (the factory's serial label 02-7814); and the faint ring of dust around the volume knob, showing the **exact position he had set the volume for twenty-five years**. *"Before you open anything,"* Grandpa said softly, *"look at it carefully. Everything leaves a history. You want to respect the history before you change it."* I nodded. I had not known, until that sentence, that a small radio could have a quiet biography on its own skin.

④ *"Step two, open it."* He unscrewed four small screws from the back. The wooden cover slid off, revealing a surprising small world: **green circuit board, copper coils, two fat capacitors, a single large vacuum-tube-shaped component, and a forest of tiny wires**. Grandpa handed me the soft brush. *"Yiran, we sweep first, as gently as your grandmother dusts her reading glasses."* I swept. Twenty-eight years of thin grey dust came off in small patches. Under the dust, the **inside of the radio was almost new**, beautiful, careful, clean. Grandpa smiled at my face. *"The inside is always cleaner than you think,"* he said. I could feel that this was a sentence he was also, quietly, saying about other things.

⑤ *"Step three, test."* He turned on the multimeter. We checked the power cable first — 220 V input still good. Then the small fuse — intact. Then the main transformer — healthy. One by one, under his quiet guidance, I used the two thin red and black probes, touched them to small metal points, and read numbers aloud as he wrote them down in a small notebook. *"Three point one volts — good. One point five ohms — good. Twenty-seven microfarads — slightly low."* Grandpa looked up. *"There,"* he said, *"is where twenty-eight years have been."* The main capacitor, he explained, had dried out. Electronics, like people, get thirsty over long quiet decades.

⑥ *"Step four, replace."* He opened a small tin box in his top drawer, full of **tiny bags labelled with dates in his own old careful handwriting**. He found one marked *"22 μF capacitor, 1997 spare"* — he had, I realised, kept a spare part for this exact future moment for thirty years. *"Engineers are patient people,"* he said, half-smiling. He showed me how to desolder the old part: a small bead of heated solder, the old capacitor lifted carefully out, the two small leads of the new one inserted, one tiny drop of fresh solder, one sharp gentle cut of the long end. I tried the second joint, under his hand on my wrist for balance. The joint, when we looked under his small magnifying glass, was — I will say it honestly — slightly less beautiful than his, but clean, real, and **my first soldering joint in fourteen years of life**. Grandpa looked at it, and then at me, and said, *"That is a real one."* I almost cried; I did not.

⑦ *"Step five, listen."* We put the wooden cover back. Grandpa pushed the power switch slowly. There was the soft **click** that old switches make — the kind of click, he later said, that belongs to an age before digital buttons. For two seconds, nothing. Then, through the small brown grille, a thin whisper of sound grew, found its strength, and became: a **clear old Chinese Central People's Radio announcement voice**, reading the afternoon news of December 12th, 2027, in the exact warm dignified tone I had only heard in old films. The voice said, slowly, in Mandarin: *"...今天是十二月十二日,星期日..."* — "*Today is December the 12th, Sunday...*" — and for a small, impossible second, the difference between 1974 and 2027 shrank to almost nothing.

⑧ Grandpa listened quietly for twenty seconds, then gently reached for the little black knob, turned it to **exactly the position of the dust ring** I had noticed earlier, and closed his eyes. I understood, without any explanation, that this was the volume level at which his father had also once listened to the evening news in a small northern kitchen in 1976, and that through our small afternoon of careful work, a voice that had been silent in a wardrobe for twenty-eight years had come back into a warm room with two new listeners. I did not speak. I wrote down, in the small notebook, one line: *"Radio working. Volume at the old dust-ring. Radio Beijing, December 12, 2027, 16:32."*

⑨ After the news, we drank warm water with a slice of ginger. Grandpa closed the notebook and said softly, *"Yiran, do you know what today's lesson really was?"* I thought. I said, slowly, *"Maybe... that small machines deserve respect, like people."* He smiled. *"That too. But more: **every old thing has a patient person inside it who kept the spare part**. If you learn to keep spare parts — a part of your memory, a part of your kindness, a part of your skill — for people and things that might come back one day, you will have, across your life, a kinder collection of small afternoons like this one."* I nodded carefully. I was not sure, in that moment, whether he was talking about radios, or about himself, or about me — and I realised, quietly, that the best small lessons are the ones that somehow mean all three at once.

⑩ At 17:40, as the light outside went from pale blue to dark blue, I carried the small radio back to its old wooden shelf in the sitting room — **not** to the high wardrobe. Grandpa did not object. He only said, *"Leave it out. It worked today. Let it listen to us for a while."* I left it out. Walking home through the cold, sharp, almost-silver evening air, with the taste of ginger still warm in my mouth, I thought about capacitors that dry out, about small paper bags labelled in 1997, about a thin brown grille at volume ring of dust, and about a grandfather's patient hand on a fourteen-year-old's wrist. And I realised that this afternoon was, somehow, the kind of memory that, if it were a capacitor, would **not** dry out.

**1. Why did Grandpa call the writer over on Sunday afternoon, and what did he bring down from the high shelf?**
