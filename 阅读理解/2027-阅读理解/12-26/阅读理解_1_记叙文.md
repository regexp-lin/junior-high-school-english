# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The First Time I Took Care of Grandpa Alone During His 38.9-°C Fever**

① On **Sunday 26 December 2027**, at exactly **14:20 in the afternoon**, I became, for the first time in my thirteen years, the **only person in the small flat taking care of Grandpa**. Mum had been called away to the hospital at 11:40 because Auntie Lin had slipped on the icy steps of Shengli Road and broken a thin bone in her left wrist; Dad was driving a work delivery on the ring road and could not return until 19:30 at the earliest. Grandpa, who is 74 and who normally walks one gentle kilometre every morning, had come home from his Saturday walk **shaking a little**, and by Sunday noon his forehead had been hot under my palm. When Mum left, she had told me, quickly, at the door, *"Yiran, thermometer is in the bathroom drawer, warm water every hour, do not give any medicine without phoning me, call me at 13 minutes past any hour — that is when I will be free for 3 minutes."* Then the door closed, and the flat became very quiet.

② I wrote down what Mum had said on the back of a maths-test paper, with the small gold pen Granny gave me. **Thermometer · warm water · no medicine alone · :13 call.** Then I went into Grandpa's room. He was lying on the wooden bed under the old blue quilt, his eyes half-closed, his forehead shining with a thin layer of sweat. I put my palm on his forehead, and I could feel — even without the thermometer — that it was hotter than my own forehead, which was quite cold from the winter air outside. My hands began to tremble a little, but I remembered something Ms Qin said once in a class: *"When a small person is afraid, the first thing to do is the smallest possible useful thing."* I walked to the bathroom and picked up the thermometer. The smallest possible useful thing, I decided, was **to know the real number**.

③ At **14:28**, I shook the old mercury thermometer carefully down below 35°C (Grandpa had taught me this three years ago, before I was even brave enough to hold it), placed it under his armpit, and waited a full **ten minutes** with my phone open to the stopwatch. I did not speak. Grandpa did not speak. Outside the window, a single winter sparrow hopped on the gingko branch. When the ten minutes were over, I took the thermometer out and walked — not ran — to the lamp. The thin silver line had stopped at **38.9°C**. Not a small fever. Not a huge fever. A **middle fever**, as Mum would say. I sat down on the small footstool beside the bed and closed my eyes for five long seconds. Then, because it was 14:38 and not 14:13, I did the next smallest possible useful thing: I boiled water.

④ While the water was cooling to drinking temperature, I did the things I had watched Mum do a hundred times without ever really seeing them: I placed a cool — not cold — damp towel, folded into four, on Grandpa's forehead; I opened the bedroom window for exactly three minutes to let in the dry cold air, then closed it again; I brought a small enamel basin of luke-warm water and, with a clean face towel, gently wiped Grandpa's hands from wrist to fingertips, and then his feet from ankle to toes. Grandpa opened his eyes halfway and said, in his thin voice, *"Yiran… this is warmer than I expected."* Then he closed his eyes again. I almost cried, but I remembered the second useful thing Ms Qin had said: *"If you cry now, you cannot see the thermometer later. Cry tonight, after Grandpa is sleeping."*

⑤ At exactly **15:13**, I phoned Mum. She picked up on the second ring. I said, in a very steady voice that surprised me, *"Grandpa 38.9°C at 14:38. Cool towel on. Feet wiped. Three minutes window. Warm water drunk 80 ml. No medicine. He spoke one sentence — he said the water was warmer than he expected."* There was a tiny pause on Mum's side, and then she said, *"Yiran, you did everything right. Do not give any pill. If it goes above 39.0°C, phone me immediately, not at :13. If it stays between 38.5 and 39.0 for two hours, I will come home at 17:40."* I said *yes*, and I wrote on the maths-test paper: **Upper alarm 39.0°C. Mum back 17:40 if steady.**

⑥ Between **15:13 and 17:38**, I took Grandpa's temperature **four more times** — 38.8, 38.6, 38.5, 38.4 — dropping very slowly but steadily. I gave him 80 ml of warm water at the top of each hour. I changed the forehead towel three times. I wiped his hands and feet twice more. At 16:40, he asked for a small piece of plain cold rice porridge Mum had left on the stove, and he ate seven small spoons of it. At 17:02, he fell asleep properly for the first time all afternoon, and his breathing became **softer, and a tiny bit slower**. I sat on the small footstool and did not move. I did not read. I did not touch my phone. I just watched the quilt rise and fall. For **thirty-eight minutes**, I was the quietest thirteen-year-old girl in the whole city of ours.

⑦ At **17:40**, the door opened. Mum came in, still smelling faintly of the hospital's white soap. She walked straight to the bed, felt Grandpa's forehead, picked up the thermometer log I had written, and read my numbers in silence. Then she turned to me, put one hand on my shoulder, and said softly, *"Yiran. You looked after your grandfather well today. You used your brain, your hands, and your breathing, all at once. I am, this afternoon, more grateful to you than I can say."* I did not speak. I just walked into my little bedroom, closed the door quietly, and **cried for about four minutes, just as Ms Qin had said — only after Grandpa was safely asleep.** When I came back out, Mum had already started a small pan of light ginger soup on the stove. She did not ask why my eyes were red. She simply handed me a bowl of the soup and said, *"Drink slowly. Tomorrow Grandpa will be much better. And tonight, we will both sleep a little earlier."* That night at **21:40**, as I lay in bed, I understood, for the first real time in my life, a small quiet sentence: **to love a family member is, sometimes, to stay very calm and do the next smallest possible useful thing, one after another, for three hours and twenty minutes, without making any noise.**

**注释：** fever 发烧；thermometer 体温计；mercury 水银；armpit 腋下；porridge 粥；ring road 环路
