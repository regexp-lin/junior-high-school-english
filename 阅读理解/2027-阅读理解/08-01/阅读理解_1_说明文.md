# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Do Clouds Form in the Sky?**

① Look up on a clear summer afternoon and you may see them slowly appear: white, cotton-like clouds forming over the fields, or long grey sheets spreading across a blue sky. By evening, some clouds may have disappeared; others may have grown into towers and brought sudden rain. Clouds seem light and gentle, but each one can carry hundreds of thousands of tonnes of water. So how does all this water get into the sky, and how does it gather into the shapes we know? The answer lies in a short, quiet dance between **warm air, water, and tiny bits of dust**.

② The story of every cloud begins with **evaporation**. When the sun warms a lake, a river, a wet leaf, or even a damp pavement, some of the water turns into a gas we call **water vapour**. Water vapour is completely invisible. It is already in the air around you right now. Warm air can hold a lot of it, but cool air can hold only a little. This simple rule is the key to understanding clouds.

③ As warm, wet air rises — pushed up by the heat from the ground, or lifted when wind meets a mountain — it moves into higher parts of the sky, where the air is cooler. The higher the warm air goes, the cooler it becomes. At some point, the air becomes too cool to hold all its water vapour. The "extra" water must come out. This change — from invisible gas back into tiny liquid drops — is called **condensation**.

④ Condensation, however, does not usually happen in empty space. Water vapour needs something small to land on, almost like snowflakes landing on a coat. In the air are tiny floating pieces of dust, pollen, sea salt, and even smoke — so small that they are invisible to our eyes. These are called **condensation nuclei**. When cool, wet air meets these small pieces, water vapour turns into very small drops of water around each one. Billions of these drops, floating together, look white from a distance: a cloud is born.

⑤ Clouds come in many shapes because the air in the sky moves in different ways. On a calm, warm day, small bubbles of rising air form small puffy **cumulus** clouds — the ones that look like cotton. When air rises very high and fast, cumulus clouds grow into tall towers called **cumulonimbus**, which often bring thunderstorms. On cool, quiet days, a thin sheet of rising air can spread into flat grey **stratus** clouds that cover the whole sky. High up, where the air is cold, water vapour turns into tiny ice crystals and forms light, feather-like **cirrus** clouds.

⑥ A cloud is not still; it is constantly changing. Tiny water drops inside it bump into one another and join together into bigger drops. When a drop becomes heavy enough that the upward air can no longer hold it, it falls. If the drop is big, we call it **rain**. If the drop freezes on the way down, we get **snow** or **hail**. A cloud that has given up most of its water fades into the sky, while somewhere else, warm, wet air is already rising to form a new one. In this way, the sky's water is borrowed, used, and returned — again and again, all over the Earth.

⑦ Clouds also play a quiet but powerful role in **Earth's temperature**. During the day, white clouds reflect some sunlight back into space, so the ground stays a little cooler. At night, clouds act like a soft blanket, keeping warm air near the ground, so the earth does not cool down so fast. A cloudy day in July may feel cool, but a cloudy night in January often feels warmer than a clear one.

⑧ Finally, scientists study clouds to understand the weather we feel and the climate of the whole Earth. Small changes in pollution, in ocean temperatures, or in the tiny dust carried by wind can change how and where clouds form. A cloud may look like a simple white shape above us, but inside it, water, air, and dust are quietly telling an important story about the whole planet — and writing the sky we see each morning.
