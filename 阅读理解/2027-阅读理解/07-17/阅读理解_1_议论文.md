# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Students Be Encouraged to Travel Without Parents During Summer?**

When summer comes, a quiet question often rises in many families: *Is our child old enough to travel without us?* For years, parents in our country have said the same thing — "Wait until you are a college student." But recently, more middle-school students have joined organised trips with classmates and teachers, usually for three to seven days, without their parents. Is this a good idea? In my view, **carefully organised trips without parents are a wonderful chance for middle-school students to grow — and schools should design more of them, not fewer**.

The first reason is **independence**. A middle-school student is old enough to pack a bag, buy a bottle of water, and ask a stranger for directions. Yet many of us have never really done these simple things alone, because a parent is always one step ahead. A short trip removes that safety net. Students must read train timetables, count change, help a classmate who has lost her ticket, decide what to eat for dinner. Each small task is a tiny seed of **self-confidence**. By the end of a five-day trip, students often return with a new quality: they look each other in the eye and say what they think — without first looking at a parent for approval.

The second reason is **friendship**. Classrooms are noisy but thin. We see our classmates for thousands of hours, but usually only at their "school-ready" moments — sitting straight, answering questions, wearing uniforms. On a trip, we see our classmates at breakfast, on a long bus ride, when they are tired, scared, or excited. A boy who is always quiet may turn out to be great at reading maps. A girl who is always perfect in math may cry over a lost hat. Real friendship is built from such small moments. Many adults still keep their closest friends from middle-school trips — a fact that tells us something about the power of shared experience.

Of course, many parents worry, and their worries are fair. "Is it safe?" "Who will look after my child?" "What if he falls ill?" These are real questions. But the answer is not to stay home; the answer is to **design the trip well**. A good trip has at least five features: a clear **itinerary** given to parents in advance, a ratio of one teacher to every eight students, a hotel chosen for safety and cleanliness, a local emergency contact, and a daily short phone call home. When all five are in place, the risk is low. We do not refuse to cross roads because roads have cars; we learn the rules and walk carefully.

A second worry: "Students will waste time and come back empty-handed." Research suggests the opposite. A study in 2025 compared two groups of Grade 8 students. Group A stayed home and attended summer classes; Group B went on a six-day history trip. After one month, Group A scored slightly higher on a history quiz, but Group B showed stronger results in **teamwork**, **speaking confidence**, and even in the quality of their Chinese writing. The trip was not empty time — it was time spent on skills that a classroom cannot build the same way.

Some may say, "If the trip is educational, then parents could go too." This sounds reasonable, but it misses a quiet point. When parents are present, children still depend on them for small decisions. When parents are absent, children become the adults, if only for a few days. That tiny shift of responsibility is the real gift of the trip. A parent who truly wants the child to grow must sometimes do the hardest thing — step back, watch the bus drive away, and trust.

Of course, not every student is ready. The trip should be **optional**. Some students may prefer to go later. That is fine. What matters is that the chance is there — that schools, teachers, and families believe middle-school children can travel, help, decide, and return stronger. If we do not give that chance at thirteen or fourteen, the leap to eighteen can feel like a cliff.

In short, summer trips without parents, when well-organised, bring independence, friendship, and steady growth. They are not a break from learning; they are a deeper form of learning — one that happens in trains, in shared dinners, and in the long, slow, quiet conversation with ourselves when the lights are out in a strange room.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion?**

A. Middle-school students must never travel without parents.  
B. Carefully organised trips without parents are a good chance for middle-school students to grow.  
C. Parents should also travel with students on every trip.  
D. Only Grade 9 students are old enough to travel.

**2. How does a short trip help with independence?**

A. Students spend more money on food.  
B. Students must read timetables, ask directions, help each other, and decide small things by themselves.  
C. Students stop talking to adults forever.  
D. Students cannot use phones at all.

**3. What five features make a trip safe and well-designed?**

A. A clear itinerary in advance, a ratio of one teacher to every eight students, a safe hotel, a local emergency contact, and a daily short phone call home.  
B. Free gifts, unlimited snacks, no rules, no teachers, no homework.  
C. Only sons can join; daughters stay home.  
D. A new phone for every student.

**4. What did the 2025 study show about Group B (students who went on the trip)?**

A. They scored worse in every subject than Group A.  
B. They showed stronger teamwork, speaking confidence and Chinese writing quality.  
C. They all became tour guides.  
D. They refused to go back to school.

**5. According to the last two paragraphs, how does the writer deal with the worry "Students are not ready"?**

A. The trip should be compulsory for everyone.  
B. No student should ever travel until age 18.  
C. The trip should be optional; those who are ready grow, and others can go later.  
D. Only students with high scores should travel.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】首段尾句  
【解析】精心组织的无父母旅行有益成长。A/C/D 均偏离。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"read train timetables, count change..."  
【解析】多项小任务培养独立。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"five features"  
【解析】行程+师生比+酒店+紧急联络+每日电话。B/C/D 均错。故选 A。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"stronger results in teamwork, speaking confidence..."  
【解析】团队、表达、语文写作更强。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】"The trip should be optional..."  
【解析】自愿参加，他日再出发。A/B/D 均错。故选 C。
