# 阅读理解（议论文） **Is Learning a Traditional Handicraft Still Worth the Time?**

① On a recent Saturday, our school invited an 80-year-old paper-cut master, Grandpa Liu, to give a short class in the art room. He sat on a low stool, held a pair of small black scissors, and turned a simple red square into a graceful flying bird in less than three minutes. Some students watched closely and took photos; others looked at their phones and asked quietly, "Is this really useful today? Couldn't a laser printer make the same bird in one second?" Their question is fair, and it is a common question in 2027: **is learning a traditional handicraft — paper-cutting, embroidery, kite-making, bamboo weaving, calligraphy — still worth the time of a busy middle-school student?** I believe the answer is **yes, as long as we study it for the right reasons**.

② The first reason is **the kind of mind a handicraft builds**. Making a paper-cut, a small bamboo basket, or a hand-sewn pouch requires hours of small, careful movements. You cannot rush; you cannot copy-paste; you cannot click "undo". Each cut, each stitch, must be careful, because the material does not forgive easily. This kind of slow, focused work is increasingly rare in modern life. Most of our day is full of short tasks and quick swipes. A handicraft class gently trains a different muscle of the mind — **patience**. And patience, unlike a printer, is a skill we need for real friendships, for real study, and for real problems.

③ The second reason is **connection with our own culture**. Every traditional handicraft has a long story. The patterns of embroidery in a minority village, the knots of a Chinese friendship cord, the shapes of a Yangzhou paper-cut, all grew slowly from the lives of real people over many centuries. When we learn one of these skills, even just a little, we begin to **touch a line of history** that passes through our grandparents and further back. A printer can copy the pattern, but cannot copy the story inside it. A student who has tried to hold the scissors himself is very unlikely to see a paper-cut as "only decoration" again.

④ The third reason is **balance** in our overcharged lives. Modern middle-school students live in front of many screens — TV, phone, tablet, computer. Our eyes are tired; our fingers move only in tiny patterns; our whole body often sits too long. A handicraft class asks us to do something different: hold a real object, smell the paper or the bamboo, feel the small weight of a tool. This change of channel is good for the body and for the mind. Many students say that after a long handicraft class, they sleep better that night.

⑤ The fourth reason — and perhaps the most surprising — is **a sense of deep pride in your own hand**. The first time I made a simple paper kite by myself in Grade 7 and saw it climb the cold February sky, I felt a happiness I do not get from even the best mobile game. Something that did not exist in the world now existed, and my hands had made it. In an age when most things are bought ready-made, making anything yourself is a small, quiet way of saying, "I am not just a user. I am also a maker."

⑥ Critics raise fair worries. Some say, "We have too much homework already; a handicraft class is a luxury." Others say, "Machines can now do almost everything; these skills will soon be useless in the job market." Both points deserve a calm answer.

⑦ It is true that we are busy, but the issue is which activities are worth slowing down for. **One** short handicraft session each week is not the main source of pressure; it is often a relief from it. Many of my classmates come out of paper-cutting lessons looking **less** tired, not more. As for the job market: although machines can print and weave, they cannot tell a child the story behind a knot, cannot design in a way that reflects a local community, and cannot feel why a handmade gift moves a grandmother to tears. Careful, thoughtful hand-making is now a rare skill, and in any rare skill, real value stays.

⑧ This does not mean we must all become traditional craftsmen. Few of us will. But trying a handicraft for one term or one summer camp is enough to give us a different way of seeing, and perhaps one new small skill we can offer our family. Not every lesson has to prepare us for an exam. Some lessons prepare us for life.

⑨ So yes, I think learning a traditional handicraft is still worth the time — for patience, for culture, for balance, and for the quiet pride of the hand. In a world that moves very fast, sitting down for an hour with a pair of small scissors is not "going backwards". It may be one of the most modern things a middle-school student can do.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main view?**

A. Middle-school students should completely give up all handicrafts.  
B. Middle-school students should learn a traditional handicraft only for exam marks.  
C. Learning a traditional handicraft is still worth the time, if we study it for the right reasons — patience, culture, balance, and the pride of the hand.  
D. Only village children should learn traditional handicrafts.

**2. According to the passage, why does handicraft-making build "patience"?**

A. Because the teacher scolds students who hurry.  
B. Because each cut or stitch must be careful, the material does not forgive easily, and it cannot be "undone" by clicking a button.  
C. Because the classroom is very cold.  
D. Because it is paid by the hour.

**3. What does the writer mean by "touch a line of history" in the third paragraph?**

A. Students should touch old paintings in museums.  
B. Learning a traditional handicraft connects a student with a long cultural story that goes back through grandparents and earlier.  
C. Every handicraft has exactly the same story.  
D. History is the only subject in school that matters.

**4. How does the writer reply to the worry that "machines can now do almost everything, so these skills will soon be useless"?**

A. The writer agrees and tells students to stop learning handicrafts.  
B. The writer says machines cannot tell stories behind knots, cannot design in a way that reflects a local community, and cannot feel why a handmade gift moves a grandmother to tears; in rare skills, real value stays.  
C. The writer says machines will replace all teachers soon.  
D. The writer says handicraft students will earn only low wages.

**5. What does the writer mean by saying, "I am not just a user. I am also a maker"?**

A. The writer has stopped using any machine.  
B. Handmade things give us a sense of pride, because we produce something in a world where most things are bought ready-made.  
C. All students should sell their handmade objects for high prices.  
D. Being a user is always better than being a maker.

【答案】B

【答案】B

【答案】B
