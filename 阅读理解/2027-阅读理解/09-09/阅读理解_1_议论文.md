# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Be Allowed to Choose Their Own Seats?**

① On the first morning of the new term, nearly every classroom across the country repeats the same small ceremony. The head teacher stands at the door with a piece of paper and calls out names and seat numbers. Some students smile; others groan quietly. Most accept their fate. Then, at the end of the week, a familiar voice rises from the back row: "Teacher, can we **choose our own seats**?" This small question is actually a very old debate between freedom and order in our schools. After thinking it over, I believe the best answer is **"partly yes"** — students should be given some freedom of choice, but never total freedom.

② On one side, teachers worry about **class order**. If every student simply picks any chair, close friends may sit together and talk through lessons; shy students may end up alone at the back; tall students may block the view of shorter students in front of them. A teacher's job is difficult enough. If the seating plan makes attention harder, learning suffers. This worry is fair and cannot be laughed away.

③ On the other side, students worry about **fairness and dignity**. A fixed, teacher-made plan can feel unfair, especially when the same student always sits far from the board, or when two students who simply do not like each other are glued into one desk for a whole term. At middle-school age, we are also learning that our preferences matter. Saying "I prefer this seat" is not only about a chair; it is about beginning to make small, real choices of our own. A classroom that denies every preference for three whole years quietly teaches a dangerous lesson: **"what you want does not matter."**

④ The best way forward, I believe, is a **mixed system**. Here is what I would suggest. **First**, the teacher creates a **basic frame**: tall students near the back, shorter students near the front; students with eye problems closer to the board; students who struggle to focus spread out. **Second**, within this frame, **each student is given two or three possible seats**. **Third**, students write down their **top two choices** in private, and the teacher combines the lists, matching as many preferences as possible, in the order received.

⑤ This plan answers both worries. Teachers keep the final word, so class order and visibility are protected. Students get a real, though limited, voice. The teacher has the right to say, "Xiao Li and Xiao Wang cannot sit together in maths — they talk too much," and students will usually accept this, because their other preferences are often granted. A classroom becomes less a prison of chairs and more a small **shared agreement** between teacher and students.

⑥ There are also good reasons to **rotate seats** every few weeks. Research in several schools has shown that long periods of looking at the board from exactly the same angle may tire one eye more than the other. Rotation also means that every student sits close to the front at least once every term — a chance that many shy students would never ask for directly, but quietly enjoy when it comes. At my school, a simple rule — "everyone moves one column left every two weeks" — has worked well for several classes.

⑦ Special seats deserve special thought. Students with **hearing problems**, **eye problems**, or **special medical needs** must always sit where they need to. Asking them to take a "lucky draw" for a back-row seat would be both unfair and unsafe. A wise seating plan places these students first, then builds around them. Real fairness is not "everyone gets the same chair"; it is "**everyone sits where they can truly learn**."

⑧ Some may ask, "Won't students cheat the system and trade seats secretly?" Perhaps, sometimes. But in a class where preferences are already heard, there is little need to cheat. Most "seat tricks" happen precisely in classes where students feel they have **no voice at all**. Giving a small real voice usually reduces — not increases — small hidden tricks.

⑨ So the old opening-day ceremony can quietly change. The teacher can still stand at the door with a printed plan, but the plan now includes, in a small box below the names, the sentence: "Based on your written choices this morning." A classroom built on this simple sentence becomes more than a room with rows of desks. It becomes a small, early training field where order and voice learn to sit, finally, at the same table.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion about students choosing their own seats?**

A. Students should have absolute freedom to pick any seat, any time.  
B. Teachers should always decide every seat with no student input.  
C. Students should be given some freedom of choice, but not total freedom — the best answer is "partly yes".  
D. Seating plans do not matter at all in middle school.

**2. Which is **NOT** one of the teachers' worries listed in paragraph 2?**

A. Close friends may sit together and talk through lessons.  
B. Shy students may end up alone at the back.  
C. Tall students may block the view of shorter students.  
D. Students may refuse to bring pencils to class.

**3. In the writer's "mixed system", which step comes **first**?**

A. Each student is given a list of the entire class's private preferences.  
B. The teacher creates a basic frame based on height, eyes, and focus.  
C. Students vote to remove the teacher from the classroom.  
D. Students change seats every single day.

**4. What is one reason the writer gives for **rotating** seats every few weeks?**

A. It makes the classroom dirtier, so students have more chores.  
B. Long periods of looking at the board from exactly the same angle may tire one eye more than the other.  
C. It helps only the top students in every class.  
D. It allows students to escape from the lesson.

**5. Which sentence best expresses the writer's final view on a "fair" classroom?**

A. "Everyone gets the same chair, no matter what their needs are."  
B. "Everyone sits where they can truly learn."  
C. "Only teachers' opinions count."  
D. "Seats should be sold to the highest-bidding students."

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段 "the best answer is 'partly yes' — students should be given some freedom of choice, but never total freedom"  
【解析】作者主张部分自由。A、B、D 均极端或无据。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节判断题（否定型）  
【定位】第②段列出三条老师担忧  
【解析】作者未提及"拒带铅笔"。A、B、C 均为老师担忧。故选 D。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "First, the teacher creates a basic frame: tall students near the back ..."  
【解析】第一步即老师按身高、视力等搭出基础框架。A、C、D 均与顺序或事实不符。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 "long periods of looking at the board from exactly the same angle may tire one eye more than the other"  
【解析】长期同一视角看黑板会使某只眼更累。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨/写作意图题  
【定位】第⑦段 "Real fairness is not 'everyone gets the same chair'; it is 'everyone sits where they can truly learn.'"  
【解析】真正的公平是让每人都能切实学习。A、C、D 均违背作者主张。故选 B。
