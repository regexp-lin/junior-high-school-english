# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Be Required to Do Volunteer Work Every Term?**

① At the start of this term, our head teacher showed us a new school idea: **every middle-school student must complete at least ten hours of volunteer work** in the local community each term. Some classmates smiled; others frowned and started whispering: "Ten hours? What about our homework?" The plan is now being discussed in many other schools too. Should such volunteer work really be **required** of every middle-school student every term? I believe the honest answer is **yes, but only if schools do it thoughtfully**.

② The strongest argument for required volunteer work is that it teaches lessons that **classrooms alone cannot teach**. In a classroom, I can read about "the elderly", "clean rivers" or "children left behind in villages". But until I actually sit with a ninety-year-old woman and listen to her slow, careful sentences, or pick up plastic rubbish beside a small stream, or read an English bedtime story to a five-year-old at a nearby community centre, these groups of people remain words in a book. Volunteer work turns words into **faces**.

③ A second reason is that **regular, small service** shapes character more than a single "big" activity. We have all seen a school plant trees once a year and post photos online. That is fine, but it is not the same as visiting an old-age home for two hours every third Saturday for three years. Small and repeated service builds a quiet habit. Over time, even shy or self-centred students — myself included — begin to notice others more, and speak to strangers without first looking at their phones.

④ Some of my classmates worry that **required** volunteering is no longer true volunteering. If you are forced, they say, it is just another homework. This point is real, but I think it confuses two different things. An activity starts as something you do because you must — piano lessons, maths problems, cleaning your room — and sometimes slowly becomes something you do because you want to. The first lesson at an old-age home may feel awkward; the tenth may feel meaningful; the twentieth may feel like an important part of who you are. School can create the first door; the student decides whether to walk through the tenth.

⑤ Another worry is **time**. Middle-school study is already heavy, and ten hours a term, my classmates said, "could be an exam point". But ten hours across a whole term is **about 45 minutes per week** — less than one episode of a short video series. If a student truly cannot afford 45 minutes a week for another human being, then that student has a problem no extra test point will solve. We are not building robots that score well and feel nothing; we are building young citizens of a real community.

⑥ There are, however, **real warnings**. First, volunteer work should **not be fake**. A school that asks students to take photos, post them online, and call that "volunteering" will damage both its own trust and the students' respect for the idea of service. Hours should be counted by the host organisation — the library, the old-age home, the primary school — not by how nice the photos look.

⑦ Second, the required hours should be **flexible and safe**. Students with long travel times or heavy family duties should be allowed to volunteer closer to home: helping a younger child in the neighbourhood with English, cooking one weekly meal for an elderly neighbour, or organising books at the community reading corner. Volunteering must never be dangerous: no lifting heavy furniture, no cleaning tall outdoor windows, no tasks that adults would refuse.

⑧ Third, schools should offer a **quiet "reflection" time** after volunteering — not a report with marks, but a simple fifteen-minute class where students share, in their own language, what they saw, felt, or were surprised by. Without this reflection, hours remain only hours. With it, hours become a personal story students will remember for years.

⑨ Ten hours a term is, in the end, a very small number. It is nothing compared with the hours we give to short videos, private tutoring, and weekend shopping malls. If used carefully, these ten hours can give middle-school students something increasingly rare in modern life: the knowledge, from real experience, that **our ordinary, unknown neighbours are also part of our world**. For that reason alone, I believe required volunteer work — done honestly, safely, and with reflection — deserves a yes.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion on required volunteer work for middle-school students every term?**

A. Required volunteer work is always a bad idea.  
B. It should only be done by Grade 9 students.  
C. Yes, it should be required, but only if schools do it thoughtfully.  
D. Volunteer work is a waste of study time.

**2. Why does the writer believe volunteer work teaches lessons that classrooms alone cannot teach?**

A. Because schools have the wrong textbooks.  
B. Because meeting real people (the elderly, left-behind children, etc.) turns abstract words in a book into real faces.  
C. Because volunteer work gives students more money.  
D. Because it makes teachers feel proud.

**3. How does the writer answer classmates who worry that **forced** volunteer work is no longer "true" volunteering?**

A. She agrees that required volunteering is useless and should be cancelled.  
B. She argues that activities may start because we must, and slowly become something we want to do — the first door can be opened by the school, while the student decides whether to walk through the tenth.  
C. She says students must never be forced to do anything at all.  
D. She says only piano lessons should ever be required.

**4. Which of the following does the writer see as a **real warning** that schools must pay attention to?**

A. Volunteer work should be fake, using only nice photos.  
B. Volunteer hours should be counted by how many photos are posted online.  
C. Volunteer work should be honest, flexible, safe, and include a short reflection class.  
D. Volunteer work should force students to do dangerous tasks.

**5. According to the writer, how much does ten hours a term come to per week?**

A. Over ten hours a week.  
B. About 45 minutes a week.  
C. Exactly five hours a day.  
D. Less than one minute a week.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段 "yes, but only if schools do it thoughtfully"  
【解析】作者有条件地支持。A、B、D 均极端或错。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "Volunteer work turns words into faces"  
【解析】接触真实人群使抽象概念变得具体。A、C、D 均不合。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "An activity starts as something you do because you must ... School can create the first door; the student decides whether to walk through the tenth"  
【解析】作者承认"强制"疑虑但强调"入门"与"选择"的关系。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥—⑧段三条警告：真实、灵活安全、反思  
【解析】作者支持诚实、灵活安全并含反思的志愿服务。A、B、D 均恰与要求相反。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "ten hours across a whole term is about 45 minutes per week"  
【解析】每学期十小时 ≈ 每周 45 分钟。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。
