# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Is Ice So Slippery?**

① Every winter, millions of people around the world slip on ice. Children fall on the playground; old people break their wrists on the pavement; even professional ice-skaters sometimes spin across the rink almost without trying. But strangely, most of us cannot clearly answer a very simple question: **why exactly is ice slippery?** A solid stone is not slippery; a solid block of wood is not slippery. Why should a solid block of frozen water be so different? For more than one hundred and fifty years, this question has interested famous scientists, and the answer turns out to be **more complicated and more beautiful** than one might expect.

② For a long time, the most common explanation was **"pressure melting"**. In the 1850s, the British scientist **Lord Kelvin** and his brother suggested that the weight of a skater's thin blade pressed so hard on the ice that it briefly **melted a thin layer of water** under the blade. That thin layer of water, they said, was what made skating possible: the skater was actually **gliding on water**, not on ice. For nearly a century, this idea was taught in physics textbooks around the world.

③ However, by the 1930s, careful measurements showed that pressure alone **cannot** explain slippery ice. A heavy skater on a thin blade does press hard, but the **temperature drop** caused by that pressure is too small — it can melt ice only at temperatures just below 0 °C. Yet ice remains very slippery even at **−20 °C** or **−30 °C**, where Kelvin's pressure theory clearly fails. So scientists knew that another explanation was needed.

④ A second idea, proposed in the 1930s by British scientist **F. P. Bowden**, is called **"friction melting"**. When a skate blade moves across ice, the **rubbing** between the metal and the ice produces a small amount of heat. That heat melts a very thin layer of water, the blade slides on this water, and the ice feels slippery. This theory works well for moving objects, but it does not quite explain why **standing still on ice** is also slippery — after all, there is no rubbing when you are not moving, yet the ice is still dangerous under your feet.

⑤ The modern answer, supported by experiments since the 1990s, points to something **truly surprising**: even when ice is perfectly still, perfectly cold, and no-one is walking on it, its **top layer** is not quite solid. Very thin and delicate, this layer is often called a **"quasi-liquid layer"** (a "nearly-liquid" layer). In it, the water molecules are loosely held and can slide past each other — much like water can. This thin liquid-like film is **only a few molecules thick** at very low temperatures, but it is enough to make the ice act like a floor lightly covered with oil.

⑥ Why does ice naturally form this strange quasi-liquid layer, while a solid stone does not? The answer lies in the **shape of water molecules**. Water molecules in ice form a neat, open framework, like a delicate fence of small houses. But at the very top of the ice, there are **no more molecules above** to hold the surface molecules in place, so they are not locked as tightly as those in the middle. Even below 0 °C, these top molecules keep **wiggling**. The lower the temperature, the thinner this quasi-liquid layer becomes; but it never fully disappears until temperatures fall close to **−250 °C**, colder than any place on Earth.

⑦ The three explanations — pressure melting, friction melting, and the quasi-liquid layer — are **not really enemies**. Today, scientists believe all three play a role, but in different conditions. The quasi-liquid layer explains why ice is **always** slippery. Friction melting adds extra water when you **move** quickly across it. Pressure melting may play a **small** role only when very sharp blades cut into the ice at temperatures just below 0 °C.

⑧ From this story we can take two small lessons. First, **a simple question is not always simple**. "Why is ice slippery?" sounds like a childish question, but answering it fully takes solid state physics, chemistry, and many years of patient experiments. Second, **old answers are not always wrong**; they are sometimes only incomplete. Lord Kelvin's idea was not a mistake — it was a first step towards a larger truth. Science, at its best, is not about tearing down earlier thinkers, but about **standing on their shoulders and seeing a little further**.
