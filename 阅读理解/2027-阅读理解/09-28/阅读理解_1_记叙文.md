# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Violin Solo I Almost Ran Away From**

① Our school's **2026 winter concert** was the biggest event of my Grade 7 year. Every December, the main hall would fill with parents, teachers, and four hundred students in dark-blue uniforms. That year, I had been chosen to play a **two-minute violin solo** — a short, gentle piece called *Evening Wind*. I had practised it for two months. My violin teacher, Ms. Qin, said my version was "quietly beautiful". My mother had bought a new black velvet ribbon for my hair. Everything, on paper, was ready.

② But on the morning of the concert, something inside me was **not** ready. The hall would hold about **four hundred people**, and the idea of those four hundred pairs of eyes all turning to look at one small girl in the middle of the stage, holding a **thin wooden violin**, suddenly felt impossible. At breakfast, my hands shook so badly that I spilled milk on my jumper. At lunch, I could hardly eat. By two o'clock, thirty minutes before the concert, I made a decision that surprises me now: I **walked out of the dressing room, down the back stairs, and hid in the school garden behind the small pond**.

③ The garden was cold and empty. Dry leaves lay on the paths. I sat on a stone bench, hugging my violin case, telling myself that if I did not go back, the concert would simply carry on without me. There were eight other performances; no one would really miss a short violin solo. I was not brave enough to return; I was not brave enough to truly run away either. I only sat there, frozen between two kinds of fear.

④ After about ten minutes, someone walked slowly down the stone path. It was **Mr. Hong**, our old caretaker. He was about sixty years old, wore thick glasses, and always spoke in a soft, slow voice. He sat down on the other end of the bench without a word. For a long moment, neither of us spoke. Then, looking at the far pine tree rather than at me, he said, "Little Lu, is it the stage, or the people?" I did not answer, but I started crying quietly. He did not try to stop me. After a while, he gently added, "Perhaps it helps to know: **the hall is full of hands, not faces**. Hands that will clap for every player, not judge one. I have watched thirty years of concerts here. Most of the audience are not thinking about us at all. They are thinking about their own dinner."

⑤ I wiped my eyes with my sleeve. Somehow, his slow, unimpressive sentence went past the wall of my fear and landed somewhere calmer. "Mr. Hong," I whispered, "I don't think I can go back." He nodded, as if this were a completely normal thing to say. Then he stood up, reached out his old, rough hand, and said, "Then walk with me. Just to the hall door. After that, if you still want to run, run." I took his hand. We walked, very slowly, down the cold path.

⑥ At the door of the hall, I could hear the second performance beginning inside — a cheerful piano piece by a Grade 8 boy. Mr. Hong stopped and turned to me. He whispered something I will always remember: "Little Lu, the hardest part is already over. You are already **here**. Please play for me. I have swept this stage for thirty years. Not one student has ever played for the old man who cleans the floor. Be the first." Then he opened the side door, nodded once, and quietly disappeared into the wing.

⑦ I do not remember how I reached my seat backstage. I do not remember Ms. Qin whispering last-minute reminders about my bow hand. I do remember, very clearly, the moment I walked onto the stage. The lights were warm and too bright. The hall was full of soft shadows. For a second the sea of faces almost pulled me down — and then, in the middle of the second row, I saw **Mr. Hong**, in his dark work jacket, sitting stiffly and alone, holding an old grey cap against his chest. I looked at him, breathed once, and began to play.

⑧ The two minutes of *Evening Wind* did not feel like a performance that day. They felt more like **speaking to a single old friend**. When my bow lifted at the end, the clapping came only after a small gap — the kind of quiet gap that told me the hall had actually listened. I walked off the stage on shaking legs, and my mother, waiting in the corridor, hugged me without a word. I looked over her shoulder into the hall. Mr. Hong was no longer in the second row. He was quietly sweeping a wrapper near the exit, the way he had done for thirty winters.

⑨ I played at two more school concerts in the years that followed. I still get nervous every time. But whenever the hall looks too big, I remember Mr. Hong's words on that cold stone bench: "**The hall is full of hands, not faces.**" And I remember the old man in the second row who was, for two minutes on a December afternoon, the only audience I really needed. **Some fears do not shrink because we become fearless**; they shrink because, in a small moment, someone we did not expect quietly sits beside us, walks with us to the door, and gives us one steady pair of eyes to play for.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Why did the writer hide in the school garden about thirty minutes before the concert (Paragraph 2)?**

A. Because she wanted to watch the birds quietly.  
B. Because she was suddenly overwhelmed by stage fear — the idea of four hundred pairs of eyes looking at her on stage felt impossible.  
C. Because she had lost her violin.  
D. Because her mother had told her to wait there.

**2. What did Mr. Hong, the old caretaker, say to help the writer (Paragraph 4)?**

A. "You will be famous one day."  
B. "The hall is full of hands, not faces. Hands that will clap for every player, not judge one."  
C. "You should stop playing violin forever."  
D. "Ms. Qin is watching you."

**3. What did Mr. Hong ask the writer at the door of the hall (Paragraph 6)?**

A. That she play for him — saying she would be the first student to play for the old man who had cleaned the floor for thirty years.  
B. That she run away as fast as she could.  
C. That she switch her solo to a piano piece.  
D. That she call her mother first.

**4. What did the writer see in the middle of the second row during her performance (Paragraph 7)?**

A. Her violin teacher Ms. Qin.  
B. Her mother in a red dress.  
C. Mr. Hong in his dark work jacket, holding an old grey cap against his chest.  
D. An empty chair.

**5. Which sentence best expresses the main idea of the last paragraph?**

A. "Some fears do not shrink because we become fearless; they shrink because, in a small moment, someone we did not expect sits beside us and gives us one steady pair of eyes to play for."  
B. "All fears disappear forever after the first performance."  
C. "Only violin players feel stage fear."  
D. "Old caretakers should not speak to students."

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "four hundred pairs of eyes ... suddenly felt impossible"  
【解析】她因突发的舞台恐惧而躲藏。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "the hall is full of hands, not faces. Hands that will clap for every player, not judge one"  
【解析】老校工以"手而非脸"的比喻安抚她。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 "Please play for me ... Be the first"  
【解析】老校工请她为自己而演奏。B、C、D 均错。故选 A。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段 "Mr. Hong, in his dark work jacket, sitting stiffly and alone, holding an old grey cap against his chest"  
【解析】第二排那位老校工成了她的焦点观众。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】主旨/观点题  
【定位】第⑨段 "some fears do not shrink because we become fearless ... steady pair of eyes to play for"  
【解析】末段总结：恐惧的缓解来自他人温柔的同在。B、C、D 均偏离。故选 A。
