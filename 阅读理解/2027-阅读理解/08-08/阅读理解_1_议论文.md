# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Be Allowed to Choose Their Own Seats?**

① On the first day of a new term, many middle-school students stand at the classroom door and watch the same quiet scene: the head teacher has drawn a seating plan, and each name is already written on a small paper square stuck to a desk. There is no choice, no discussion, no chance to sit near your best friend. Some students accept this calmly; others complain for days. In recent years, a few schools have started to experiment: students **choose their own seats**, sometimes changing every month. Should this idea spread? In my opinion, **the answer is yes — but with some clear rules from the teacher**. Student-chosen seats can support responsibility, friendship, and learning, as long as teachers step in when things go wrong.

② The first reason is **responsibility**. When a teacher assigns every seat, a student who talks too much, or who cannot see the board, simply lives with it. Nothing is his or her decision. When students choose their own seats, they must think: "Where will I really listen? Who helps me focus, and who pulls me off task?" Making this choice — and facing the result — is, in itself, a useful lesson. Freedom without responsibility is just noise; responsibility without freedom is just obedience. The classroom is one of the safest places to practise both at the same time.

③ The second reason is **healthy friendship**. In a fixed seating plan, you spend six hours a day beside the same person for a whole year. Sometimes this is lucky; sometimes it is painful. When students can change seats now and then, they meet more classmates, hear more voices, and learn to work with many different kinds of people. In life, we rarely get to pick the colleagues around us; but in middle school, learning to sit beside many kinds of people — quiet, loud, fast, careful — may be the best preparation for the real world.

④ The third reason is **learning**. Not every student learns best in the same position. Some students are short and need to sit near the front; some wear glasses for the board; some focus better next to a calm classmate. A good seating choice can raise scores without any new textbook. Of course, a seat alone does not make a top student — effort still matters most — but removing a small disadvantage (like sitting at the back with a tall classmate in front) can make daily lessons noticeably easier.

⑤ People who are against student-chosen seats often raise two worries. One: "Best friends will sit together and chat all day." Two: "The quiet student in the corner will be left alone with nobody to sit beside." These are real problems, not small ones. But they do not prove that free seating is wrong; they prove that **free seating needs rules**.

⑥ Here are three simple rules that can fix most of these problems. **First rule:** if two friends sit together and their homework drops or their classroom behaviour becomes noisy, the teacher has the right to separate them for two weeks. This is agreed in advance, so it is not a surprise or a punishment. **Second rule:** every group of six seats must include students from at least two subject levels, so strong students and less strong students can help each other. **Third rule:** seats change every month, so no group becomes a fixed "little kingdom", and no student is stuck in the same corner forever.

⑦ The worry about the quiet student is especially important. Head teachers should make sure that in every round, **every student has a partner**. One useful method used in some schools is the "quick match": on the day of choosing, shy students write down three names they would like to sit beside. Teachers then arrange the seating so these students are not left standing alone at the end. The aim is not only a freer classroom, but a warmer one.

⑧ Opposing teachers sometimes say, "Teenagers are not mature enough to choose well." This view is too pessimistic. Teenagers often show more maturity than adults expect — when they are given responsibility. A classroom where every decision is made by teachers produces obedient students but not necessarily wise ones. A classroom that trusts students, with a gentle framework, is a small training ground for adult life.

⑨ In summary, I support letting middle-school students choose their own seats, **within a clear set of rules set by the teacher**. It teaches responsibility, builds wider friendships, and helps each student find a seat that matches his or her real needs. Schools that already practise this system report calmer classrooms, not wilder ones. A classroom, in the end, is not just a room with desks. It is a small society — and in a small society, a little trust goes a very long way.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main view on student-chosen seats?**

A. Students should never choose their own seats.  
B. Students should choose their own seats freely with no rules at all.  
C. Students should be allowed to choose their own seats, but within clear rules set by the teacher.  
D. Only teachers should choose seats, and students should never change seats.

**2. According to the passage, why does choosing their own seats help students build "responsibility"?**

A. Because students must pay a fine if they pick the wrong seat.  
B. Because students must think about where they will focus, and then face the result of their choice.  
C. Because the seats near the front have better air.  
D. Because students must write a report about every classmate.

**3. Which of the following is **NOT** one of the writer's three rules for free seating?**

A. If two friends sit together and their work falls, the teacher can separate them for two weeks.  
B. Every group of six seats should include students from at least two subject levels.  
C. Seats change every month.  
D. Students who talk in class must stand at the back of the classroom for a whole day.

**4. Why does the writer pay special attention to "the quiet student in the corner"?**

A. Because he wants to punish the quiet student.  
B. Because the writer worries a shy student may be left without a partner, and suggests teachers use methods like "quick match" to include them.  
C. Because quiet students always get the highest marks.  
D. Because quiet students should sit alone forever.

**5. What does the writer mean by "a classroom is a small society" at the end?**

A. Classrooms have tax, police and shops like a real society.  
B. A classroom is a small training ground where students can safely practise freedom and responsibility, as in adult life.  
C. A classroom has nothing in common with adult society.  
D. Only rich classrooms can be called "societies".
