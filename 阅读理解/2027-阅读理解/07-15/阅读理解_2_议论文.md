# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Read the News Every Day?**

Ten years ago, newspapers were common in our homes. Adults read them in the morning; children sometimes peeked at the cartoons. Today, paper newspapers are rare, and the news lives on phones, short-video apps, and group chats. In this noisy world, a question arises: *Should middle-school students read the news every day?* In my view, **a short, carefully chosen daily news habit is important for middle-school students, but it must be done with the right sources and the right amount**.

The first reason is **awareness**. Middle-school students live in a larger world than their classrooms. A typhoon may close a nearby harbour; a new train line may change how a cousin travels to work; a change in the traffic rule may affect how we cross the street. When students do not know these things, a teacher's simple question like "Do you know about the water-saving campaign?" may leave them silent. Reading a few news headlines a day is like opening a small window: a cool breeze of information blows in and keeps our minds connected to reality.

A second reason is **thinking skills**. News today is not always pure news; it is often mixed with opinions, emotions, and even wrong facts. A student who reads regularly learns, step by step, to ask, "Is this fact or feeling? Where does it come from? Does another source say the same?" Such questions are exactly the kind our Chinese and English teachers ask about reading passages in exams. News, when read with care, trains the same muscle — the one that says, *wait, let me think*.

Those who disagree often give two worries. First, "News makes students anxious." This worry is real. Many news stories are sad or violent, and a young mind may be over-shaken by them. But the answer is not to avoid news; it is to **choose the right news**. A simple morning scan of the national "youth news" section, or a weekly summary designed for schools, gives important facts without flooding students with heavy images. News, like food, can be served in small child-sized portions.

A second worry is **time**. "We are already too busy — where is the space for the news?" This point is fair. I would never suggest that students spend thirty minutes on the news every day. But ten minutes at breakfast, or fifteen minutes at dinner with parents, is usually possible. More importantly, it turns a silent family meal into a discussion. Students hear their parents' views; parents hear their children's questions. That conversation, repeated over many months, is **priceless**.

People sometimes say, "Why not leave the news for grown-ups? Students can read it later." I disagree. Starting late is harder. If a student has never followed news until Grade 12, the sudden arrival of world events — elections, wars, climate conferences, scientific discoveries — may feel like a foreign language. Starting small in middle school lets us grow our understanding together with our age, so that no single story ever overwhelms us.

Of course, the news habit must be **balanced**. Three simple rules help. First, pick a **serious** source: a national paper, a school news wall, or the news programme of public television. Avoid short-video clips that mix real and fake. Second, set a **time limit** — ten minutes, not one hour. Third, have a **short chat** with a family member or a friend about one story. This turns information into thinking.

To sum up, daily news reading is not an extra hobby. It is a small, steady practice that grows three important fruits: a bigger awareness of the world, a sharper way of thinking, and warmer conversations at home. A middle-school student is old enough to start. In fact, she may be at exactly the right age.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion?**

A. Students should never read the news.  
B. Students should spend one hour on the news every day.  
C. A short, carefully chosen daily news habit is important for middle-school students.  
D. News is only for grandparents.

**2. According to the writer, why does reading news train "thinking skills"?**

A. Because news is always simple.  
B. Because students learn to ask if a story is fact or feeling, and to compare sources.  
C. Because news contains many funny jokes.  
D. Because news is printed in colour.

**3. What is the writer's reply to "News makes students anxious"?**

A. We should ban all news.  
B. Choose the right source, like a youth news section or a weekly school summary; serve news in small portions.  
C. Only adults should feel anxious.  
D. Students should stop thinking.

**4. Why does the writer think dinner-time news is valuable?**

A. Because dinner is free of charge.  
B. Because it turns a silent meal into a discussion where students hear parents' views and parents hear children's questions.  
C. Because the TV is always on at dinner.  
D. Because children eat more during the news.

**5. Which of the following is NOT one of the writer's three simple rules?**

A. Pick a serious source.  
B. Set a time limit of about ten minutes.  
C. Have a short chat with a family member or a friend about one story.  
D. Read at least ten different stories each night.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】首段尾句  
【解析】短时、精选、每日。A/B/D 均偏离。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"fact or feeling... another source say the same"  
【解析】区分事实与立场，多源核对。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"choose the right news... small child-sized portions"  
【解析】选对源、控量。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"silent family meal into a discussion..."  
【解析】晚餐新闻促进家庭沟通。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节理解题（反向）  
【定位】"three simple rules"  
【解析】每晚读 10 条不在建议中。A/B/C 均为规则。故选 D。
