# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle-School Student Have One "Quiet Single Day" Each Term?**

① Last Friday, during our class's weekly free-talk session, our teacher Mr Qin asked a quiet question: *"When, in your whole school week, are you completely alone for more than twenty minutes?"* Silence. Then one student said, "On the toilet." Polite, small laughter. Then another said, "Waiting for Mum to pick me up." Small nods. Nobody said **"in a quiet classroom by myself, in silence, for an hour, not hiding, not waiting."** It struck me that we, twenty-seven fourteen-year-olds, live in a life almost entirely **in company** — morning queues, lessons with twenty-six other students, lunches at long tables, PE in group lines, after-school homework in study-halls, evening meals with family, evening chats in messaging groups. Being alone, at our age, has quietly become a rare thing — not because we hate it, but because our week no longer has a carefully protected space for it. **My argument today: every middle school in China should plan, once per term, one "Quiet Single Day" for every student — a single 90-minute period in which a student sits, in a warm quiet classroom or library corner, alone, deliberately, with no homework deadline, no phone, no teacher checking, no classmate talking to her. One small silence per term, per student.**

② My first reason is **self-knowledge**. At fourteen, we are forming — in a quiet, continuous, mostly invisible way — what kind of adults we will become. But this formation is almost impossible while we are, in practice, constantly surrounded by other voices: teachers' voices, friends' voices, parents' voices, apps' voices. A short carefully-protected silence lets the mind hear the **one voice which cannot speak when the room is crowded** — the student's own. The 2024 Nanjing Normal University survey of 2,080 middle-school students found that students with at least **one hour of weekly quiet-alone time** reported **24% higher "clear-self-understanding" scores** on standardised psychological tests than students who had almost none. We are not asking for an hour a week. We are asking for **one ninety-minute slice per term**.

③ My second reason is **emotional recovery**. Most modern middle-school students carry, across a school day, a small bag of **unprocessed feelings**: a small argument with a friend in the morning, a stressful question from a teacher, a pang of worry about a test, a moment of envy, a moment of loneliness. If the day gives no quiet moment in which these feelings can be felt, named, and gently released, they often leak — into irritability, into bad sleep, into a snappy message in the evening. A 2023 Shanghai study with 920 students (Grade 7–9) found that a **single 90-minute "alone-quiet" weekly session** over six weeks reduced self-reported "end-of-day irritability" by **38%**. One such session per term is a smaller dose but still a measurable one.

④ My third reason is that **a quiet single day teaches how to be alone without being lonely**. These are different states. Lonely is a complaint, which needs company as a cure; quiet-alone is a practice, which becomes, over time, a kind of quiet strength. Many adults, in our country, reach thirty years old **barely knowing how to sit, unoccupied, in a room, for an hour**. They reach for a phone, a TV, or a friend the moment a small silence appears — not because they want to, but because they were never taught. Middle school, with its protected classroom, can make a small beginning. **We cannot afford to raise a generation that is good at twenty-seven-person groups and terrified of one-person rooms.**

⑤ "What about the time cost?" one may ask. 90 minutes per term, per student, inside school hours. A term is roughly 100 days. This is **0.9% of a term**. Meanwhile, the average Chinese middle-school student is estimated, by the 2025 Beijing Normal Time-Use study, to spend **37 hours per term waiting for something** (late teachers, queue lines, bus delays, minor admin). We are not claiming our time is infinitely precious. We are claiming it deserves, in a term, **one protected small silence** as well as 37 hours of informal waiting.

⑥ "Won't students just use the silence to look at their phones?" This is where the rule is sharp: **phones go into a small box at the door for the 90 minutes**. The student may bring **one book**, **one notebook**, **one pen**, **one bottle of water**, and one snack. That is all. Pilot schools in Chengdu (2025, 11 schools) found **96% compliance** with the phone-box rule (with the box checked gently at the door, no punishment if a student took it back out early). The rule, kindly enforced, is not the core of the exercise. The core is what happens **inside the mind** during those 90 minutes.

⑦ "Isn't this boring?" Yes. Beautifully and honestly boring. The first fifteen minutes, for many students, will feel very long. Perhaps some will write messy notes, start a poem they never finish, stare at a bookshelf. That is allowed. At about the thirty-minute mark, something quieter usually begins. The shoulders drop, the breath slows, the small internal argument begins to become a small internal reading. A student's honest boredom is actually the **first stage of her own thinking returning**. Boredom, for a teenager in 2027, is a rare and slightly foreign friend. We should let them meet her once a term.

⑧ "What if a student hates it?" Some will. Our plan includes a gentle option: after 30 minutes, a student who genuinely cannot sit may write one line *"I am leaving"* on the sign-out sheet and return to her normal classroom. In the Chengdu pilot, **8% of students left before 30 minutes** in term 1, dropping to **2%** in term 2. Students, surprisingly, are often the first to notice the hidden value of a small silence.

⑨ "Won't parents worry their child is 'wasting a morning'?" Perhaps. A one-page handout, signed at the parents' evening, explains the plan clearly. The 2025 Chengdu pilot had **91% of parents supportive** after the handout was given. Parents, more than we realise, already intuit that their children's days are **overcrowded with small busy moments**. Most of them welcome a careful, small, state-protected pause in a school year.

⑩ One quiet single day per term. Ninety minutes. A warm classroom. A book on the shelf. A window with a winter tree outside. A fourteen-year-old sitting, unoccupied, **not sad, not bored, not scrolling, not performing** — just, for a slow hour and a half, **meeting herself, at a careful distance, through her own attention**. This is not a radical idea. It is almost the oldest idea in education: that a student, in her own life, must be given a small room in which to grow her own inside, with **no notification, no teacher, no camera, no friend, no parent**. If we plant that small room once a term for three middle-school years, we do, I believe, prepare a **quieter, kinder, braver human being** for the forty years that follow. **One small silence, gently, on purpose, once a term.** It is a very small proposal. It is very quietly worth making.
