# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The First Saturday I Bought Vegetables Alone at the Market**

① On the first Saturday of December, at 07:10 in the morning, my mother put a thin pink shopping list on the kitchen table and said, *"Yiran, the flu has finally caught me. Could you go to Xili Market alone today and buy these?"* She had written: **300 g Chinese cabbage · 2 tomatoes · 1 small cucumber · 250 g pork (shoulder cut) · 3 sticks of spring onion · 1 small piece of ginger · 1 bottle of soy sauce.** Next to the list, she had placed **one green banknote of 100 yuan**, folded once in the middle, and a small canvas bag. *"Keep the receipts,"* she said softly from the sofa, her voice hoarse, *"and don't be polite — **ask for the price twice** before you pay."* I was fourteen. I had been to the market a hundred times **with** her. I had never gone **alone**.

② Xili Market is a ten-minute walk from our building. On Saturdays it opens at seven and is, by half past, very alive. As I pushed open the heavy plastic door-curtain, the whole warm, wet, cold-bright world of a Chinese wet market came at me at once: **the smell of spring onions and fresh coriander**, **the sound of a knife hitting a wooden block**, the shining pile of tomatoes under a single yellow lamp, and a grandmother near the eggs calling to her friend two stalls away: *"Lǎo Sūn — still no cabbage? I told you, come early!"* My hands were cold. My canvas bag was empty. My list was slightly wrinkled from my pocket.

③ I decided, sensibly, to begin with the easiest item: **the Chinese cabbage**. The cabbage aunty, whose stall I have known since I was six, did not recognise me at first — probably because, without my mother, I looked smaller. I said, *"Three hundred grams, please."* She weighed it, pressed the tare button, and said, *"Two yuan one."* I was surprised. I had thought a cabbage would be more. I gave her a five-yuan note, received two coins and a one-yuan note, and put them carefully in the small paper envelope I had prepared at home. Her smile, when she finally saw my face under the hood, got a little larger. *"Your mother unwell?"* she asked, quietly. I nodded. *"Tell her to drink pear soup,"* she said.

④ At the vegetable row, I chose two tomatoes by the small rule my mother had taught me: **slightly soft to the fingers, deep red, tops green and fresh**. I picked a cucumber that was **straight and not too shiny**. I paid four yuan eighty for the three. The vegetable uncle chuckled, *"First time alone?"* I nodded. He added a small sprig of coriander to my bag and said, *"That is free. For the soup."* My ears felt warm for the first time that morning. **Kindness, I realised, grows on the edges of exchanges.**

⑤ The pork stall, however, was my real test. I had never bought pork alone. The butcher, whose right arm is the size of a small tree, was not scary at all — but the prices were a long red list on the wall, and my mother's note had just said *"250 g pork (shoulder cut)"*. I waited my turn. Then I said, as clearly as I could, *"Please, 250 grams of shoulder cut."* He nodded, lifted a clean pink piece, trimmed it, weighed it: 251 grams exactly. *"Two yuan extra, okay?"* he asked. I nodded. Total: 13.80 yuan. I paid, watched the thin paper receipt slip into my bag, and, remembering my mother's soft voice from the sofa, I said, *"May I ask — is this the shoulder, definitely?"* He laughed — not unkindly. *"Definitely. Good girl to ask twice."* Another kindness. **Twice asked, once cooked, no regret.**

⑥ Spring onions: three sticks for one yuan. Ginger: a small knobby piece for 0.50. Soy sauce: a bottle of the one my mother always buys, 11.80 yuan. I did a small sum on the back of the list, in pencil, because my mother had asked me to check: *cabbage 2.10 + vegetables 4.80 + coriander 0 + pork 13.80 + onions 1.00 + ginger 0.50 + soy 11.80 = 34.00 yuan.* From 100, that should be **66 change**. I counted. The change was 66. I breathed out slowly. **I had never before noticed that arithmetic, done right, also produces a small quiet feeling in the chest**.

⑦ Walking home through the cold, sharp December light, my canvas bag was full and, honestly, heavier than I had expected. The spring onions stuck out of the top like green feathers. A small old man I did not know, passing me, smiled and said, *"Grandma sick?"* — mistaking my age kindly. *"Mother,"* I corrected. He nodded. *"Go home quickly then. Careful of the stairs."* I decided I liked this market morning more than any weekend I could remember. I noticed, too, that the street was full of people like me — people carrying small, honest bags — and that a city is really held together, quietly, by these morning walks.

⑧ At home, I laid everything on the kitchen table. My mother counted from the sofa: list — seven items. Table — seven items. She did not get up; she only said, *"The pork looks good."* I handed her the change — 66 yuan — and the receipts. She looked at each one carefully, not because she doubted me, but, I understood later, **because looking at the small paper receipts of a child's first solo trip is itself a small ceremony**. *"Thank you, Yiran,"* she said. I went to the kitchen, washed the cabbage, peeled the ginger, and, following Dad's kitchen rules from last month, **began to cook**: pork-and-cabbage noodle soup with a little coriander and a few drops of soy, the way my grandma taught my mother, and my mother taught me.

⑨ We ate on the sofa. The pork was, truly, tender; the soup was only a little too salty. My mother, smiling through her cough, said, *"Yiran, next Saturday?"* I thought. I said, *"Yes, Mom. Next Saturday too."* Later, cleaning the bowls, I thought about the sprig of coriander the vegetable uncle had added for free, and the butcher's *"good girl to ask twice"*, and the old man on the stairs, and the cabbage aunty's *"drink pear soup"*. **A market**, I understood, **is not just a place to buy vegetables. It is a place where a whole neighbourhood reminds a fourteen-year-old, through small acts, that she is part of something very old and very warm** — the long daily kitchen of a city.

⑩ That night, before bed, I wrote four lines:

*Dec 4. Xili Market. Alone.*  
*Cabbage, tomatoes, cucumber, pork, onions, ginger, soy. All bought. Change 66 yuan. Correct.*  
*Kindness found: free coriander, butcher's smile, stranger's "careful of the stairs", aunty's pear-soup advice.*  
*I think this was today's real lesson.*

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Why did the writer go to Xili Market alone on Saturday morning?**

A. Because she wanted to play with friends.  
B. Because her school required it.  
C. Because her mother had been caught by the flu and asked her to buy a list of seven items (cabbage, tomatoes, cucumber, pork, spring onions, ginger, soy sauce), giving her a 100-yuan note and telling her to keep the receipts and to "ask for the price twice" before paying.  
D. Because she wanted to earn pocket money.

**2. What small kindness did the vegetable uncle add to her bag after she paid 4.80 yuan for tomatoes and a cucumber?**

A. He gave her more tomatoes.  
B. He gave her a free sprig of coriander, "for the soup", after teasing that it was her first time alone.  
C. He returned all her money.  
D. He gave her a candy.

**3. What did the writer do at the pork stall that made the butcher say, "Good girl to ask twice"?**

A. She bought twice the amount.  
B. She paid twice.  
C. She asked him, after the pork was weighed (251 g for 13.80 yuan), "Please — is this the shoulder, definitely?" — remembering her mother's soft advice to ask for the price / the cut twice before paying.  
D. She ordered two different meats.

**4. When the writer got home, how did her sick mother treat the receipts and change?**

A. She threw them away without looking.  
B. She ran to the kitchen to cook.  
C. She looked at each receipt carefully — not because she doubted Yiran, but, as Yiran later understood, because looking at the small paper receipts of a child's first solo trip is itself a small ceremony — and said "Thank you, Yiran."  
D. She gave the change to the cat.

**5. What did the writer mainly learn about the Xili Market in this morning?**

A. That a market is just a place for buying food quickly.  
B. That a market is not just a place to buy vegetables but a place where a whole neighbourhood — the aunty, the butcher, the uncle, a stranger on the stairs — reminds a fourteen-year-old, through small acts (free coriander, a gentle joke, a warning about the stairs, pear-soup advice), that she is part of something very old and very warm — the long daily kitchen of a city.  
C. That the prices at the market are very expensive.  
D. That she should not go to the market alone again.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段缘起  
【解析】"感冒 / 清单 / 100 元 / 问两次"直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段"free coriander"  
【解析】"添了一把香菜"直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段肉摊问答  
【解析】"definitely 是肩肉"直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑧段  
【解析】"仪式 + 谢谢"直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第⑨段总结  
【解析】"城市的长厨房"主旨。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
