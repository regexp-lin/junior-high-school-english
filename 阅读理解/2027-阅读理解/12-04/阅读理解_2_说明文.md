# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do Bees "Dance" to Tell Each Other Where the Flowers Are?**

① On a hot June afternoon in 1944, an Austrian scientist named **Karl von Frisch** was watching honeybees inside a thin glass-walled hive. He had placed a small dish of sugar-water **60 metres** east of the hive, under a walnut tree. Within a few minutes, one bee found the dish, filled her honey stomach, and flew home. What happened next was so strange that von Frisch rubbed his eyes. **Inside the hive, on a vertical comb, the returning bee began to run in a small figure-of-eight shape, wagging her abdomen sharply as she ran along the middle line.** Other bees crowded close, felt her with their antennae — and within two minutes, **fifteen of them** flew out of the hive, straight to the east, and landed on the sugar-water dish. The bee, von Frisch realised, had just **told them where the food was**. She had done it by **dancing**. For this discovery, honeybees would one day share, with humans, the 1973 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine — and turn out to be among the most surprising communicators on Earth.

② The "dance" that von Frisch watched is called, today, the **waggle dance**. It is performed only when the food source is **more than about 80 metres** from the hive. (For closer food, bees do a simpler "round dance" — they just run in a circle, and other bees go out and search nearby.) The waggle dance is a figure-of-eight with a **straight middle line**, along which the bee wiggles her body from side to side, about **13 waggles per second**. She repeats this figure five, ten, sometimes thirty times in a row. On the outside, she looks a little mad — a small golden animal jogging in a strange, tight little pattern. But every single movement, it turns out, is **carrying information**.

③ **The direction of the straight middle line codes the direction of the flowers.** Here is the most surprising part. Inside the hive, on a vertical comb, the bee cannot see the sun. But she treats **"straight up"** on the comb as **"toward the sun"** in the world outside. If the flowers are exactly toward the sun, her waggle line points straight up. If the flowers are, say, **30° to the right of the sun**, her waggle line tilts **30° to the right of vertical**. If they are directly away from the sun, she waggles **straight down**. This is a beautiful little translation from three-dimensional sunlight into two-dimensional comb. Her sisters, feeling her on the dark comb, translate back — and fly out in the right direction, **accurate to about 7–10°** over distances of hundreds of metres.

④ **The duration of the straight middle line codes the distance.** The longer the bee waggles, the farther the flowers are. On average, about **one second of waggling corresponds to about 1 kilometre** away from the hive. A tiny patch of flowers at 200 metres: about 0.2 seconds of waggle per figure. A rich patch at 3 km: three seconds. A 2019 study at the University of Sussex, which recorded more than 2,500 waggle dances with high-speed cameras, found the accuracy was remarkable: bees that followed the dance found the food source **within 30 metres** of the true spot in 84% of cases, even at 3 km distance. That is like telling a friend, by a short dance, to find a specific tree in a park **several hundred paces away**.

⑤ **The dance also tells others how good the food is.** A rich, sugary flower source makes the bee **repeat the dance more times** (sometimes a hundred figures in a row) and **wiggle more violently**. A thin, so-so source might get only three or four polite figures. Experienced watcher-bees in the hive sense this "excitement" and adjust how many of them set out. A 2022 German study found that a dance judged "high excitement" recruited **about five times** as many new foragers as a "low excitement" dance. The hive, essentially, runs a quiet, careful vote: better food, more voters, faster response.

⑥ **What about on cloudy days when bees cannot see the sun?** A 1949 experiment by von Frisch's team gave us the answer: bees can still read the **polarisation pattern** of sunlight through clouds — the invisible structure of scattered sky-light that keeps the "direction of the sun" readable even behind grey weather. As long as a **small patch of clear sky** is visible, the waggle dance still points correctly. In heavy fog, though, bees stop dancing and just rest. They are, reasonably, honest about their limits.

⑦ And what if the food is found **after sunset, when the sun has gone?** Here the bee does something almost philosophical. She remembers where the sun was **at the time she found the food**, and then **updates her dance angle continuously** as time passes, using her internal clock and her sense of how the sun moves across the sky. A 1956 study showed that a bee forced to stay in the hive for three hours after finding food would, when she finally danced, use the **correct updated sun position** — not the old one. Inside that small golden head is a quiet little astronomer.

⑧ Why did evolution invent all this? **Because honeybee food is scattered, brief, and far.** A patch of wildflowers might open for only two days, 2 km away, in one small corner of a meadow. If a single bee found it and kept the secret, the hive could only send one bee at a time — too slow for the short bloom. By **sharing the map** through dance, one bee can send **dozens** of sisters in the right direction within minutes. It is a species-level cooperative design. Humans have maps; bees have dances. The truth they both express is the same: *"go that way, for this long, it is worth it."*

⑨ **A quiet final thought.** For 80 million years before humans, bees had already invented a kind of short, careful language — with direction, distance, and enthusiasm — all packed into a small dance on a dark comb. The next time you see a bee quickly leave a clover flower, imagine her flying home and doing a small, precise dance for her sisters. Inside that one-second waggle, there is a **kilometre of sunlight**, a **patch of spring flowers**, and, quietly, **the oldest map in the world**.
