# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle School Students Still Memorise Ancient Chinese Poems?**

① Every Monday morning, before the first bell rings, you can hear the same soft sound in our classroom: Grade 8 students repeating lines from **Li Bai, Du Fu, and Su Shi**. Our language teacher, Ms. Ning, says, "Chinese children must have poems in their hearts." But some classmates quietly complain: "Why do we still memorise poems when a phone can read them in one second?" The question is worth a closer look: **should middle school students still memorise ancient poems?**

② People who **support** the practice give three strong reasons. First, a poem is a **window into our roots**. When you know the line "**床前明月光** (moonlight before my bed)", you are carrying a small piece of Tang with you. This piece of language also travels with you abroad; it quietly answers the question, "Who am I?" in a land of strangers. Second, the beauty of poems **trains language itself**. Old poems use very few words, yet they carry rich pictures, sounds, and feelings. A student who remembers "**两个黄鹂鸣翠柳**" (two yellow orioles sing on green willows) often writes cleaner, more lovely Chinese sentences in his or her own essays. Third, ancient poems are like **tiny life teachers**. Su Shi's "**不识庐山真面目，只缘身在此山中**" ("You cannot see the true face of Mount Lu, because you are already inside it") teaches us, without a lecture, to step back and look at our own problems from a distance.

③ Yet the **other side** also makes sense. Students today have a full schedule — maths, English, physics, history, PE. Memorising long poems takes time, and for some children, it leads to a **dry, painful feeling** about Chinese itself. Another worry is that **understanding** often gets lost. A student may recite a whole poem by Du Fu about a war-torn city but have no idea what the poet truly felt. Memorising without understanding, the critics say, turns a living poem into **a cold line of signs on paper**. A third voice points out that technology already lets us search any line in a second; maybe our brains should save space for **new creation** instead of old repetition.

④ My own view is a simple one: **memorise with a warm heart, not with a tired hand**. Teachers may not need to ask students to memorise 50 long poems a term. A small number — maybe **6 to 10 poems** each term — chosen because they match our age and feelings, would be enough. Before we memorise, the teacher can tell us the story behind the poet. After we memorise, we can draw pictures, act small scenes, or connect the poem to our own lives. In that way, the poem is no longer a stone on our back; it becomes a song we can hum when we walk home under a moonlit sky.

⑤ Technology can save lines; only a human heart can live them. A young person who memorises even a few old poems carries a soft torch through the long tunnel of growing up. That torch may dim sometimes, but on quiet nights, far from home, it can still show the warm colour of a thousand-year-old moon.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the main question that the article tries to answer?**

A. Who is the best poet in Chinese history?  
B. Why Ms. Ning became a Chinese teacher.  
C. Whether middle school students should still memorise ancient poems.  
D. How phones are changing modern poetry.

**2. Which is NOT mentioned as a reason to support memorising poems?**

A. Poems connect young people with Chinese cultural roots.  
B. Poems can train our language and writing skills.  
C. Poems can win high marks in any international exam.  
D. Poems offer small life lessons in a gentle way.

**3. According to paragraph ③, what is one worry about memorising too many poems?**

A. Students may recite poems without really understanding them.  
B. Poems are no longer used in daily speech.  
C. Only city students can learn them.  
D. Poems are only for university students.

**4. What does the writer suggest as a better way to memorise poems?**

A. Memorising 50 long poems each term, no matter how tired students feel.  
B. Memorising 6 – 10 poems each term together with stories and activities.  
C. Deleting all poem homework and using phones only.  
D. Asking students to write new poems instead of old ones.

**5. What does the last paragraph mainly say?**

A. Phones will soon replace ancient poems completely.  
B. Only poets should memorise poems.  
C. Ancient poems, kept in the heart, can guide us through life like a soft torch.  
D. All old poems are difficult and unhappy.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段末 "should middle school students still memorise ancient poems?"  
【解析】核心议题为是否仍需背诵古诗。A、B、D 非主题。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节否定题  
【定位】第②段三条理由：文化根脉、语言训练、生活启示  
【解析】"国际考试高分"未提。A、B、D 均为原文理由。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "Memorising without understanding..."  
【解析】担心只会背不会懂。B、C、D 与原文不符。故选 A。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】作者观点题  
【定位】第④段 "6 to 10 poems each term... stories, drawings, small scenes"  
【解析】精选数量并结合活动。A、C、D 与作者观点不符。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨推断题  
【定位】第⑤段 "a soft torch through the long tunnel of growing up"  
【解析】古诗如心中火炬照亮成长之路。A、B、D 与主旨不符。故选 C。
