# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Is the Sky Blue?**

① On a clear October afternoon, look up. The sky is a soft, deep blue. By six in the evening, it turns orange; by seven, a tired purple; by eight, almost black. Every child has asked, at some point, a very simple question: **why is the daytime sky blue?** The answer uses a little science and one beautiful idea: light is more than meets the eye.

② We usually say "sunlight is white," but in truth sunlight is made of many colours. If we let a beam of sunlight pass through a glass prism, the light spreads out and we see a small **rainbow**: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo and violet. So every ray of sunshine is secretly **seven colours travelling together**. Raindrops in a summer storm work just like that prism, which is how real rainbows appear.

③ Now here is the key fact. Each colour of light travels as a tiny wave, and the waves of different colours have different lengths. **Red waves are long; blue and violet waves are short.** Think of a long, slow ocean wave versus a short, choppy one.

④ When sunlight enters the Earth's atmosphere, it meets countless tiny particles — the molecules of nitrogen and oxygen in the air. The **short-wave** colours, especially blue, bump into these molecules and scatter in all directions, like a football hitting a wall and bouncing everywhere. The **long-wave** colours, like red, mostly pass straight through without much scattering. So when you look at the sky away from the sun, you see the blue light that has been scattered by the whole sky above you. This scattering was first fully explained by the English scientist Lord Rayleigh in the 19th century.

⑤ "But why don't we see the sky as violet?" a clever student might ask. "Violet waves are even shorter than blue." Two reasons. First, the sun gives out less violet light than blue. Second, and more importantly, our **eyes** are not equally sensitive to all colours; they are much more sensitive to blue than to violet. The sky really does contain scattered violet, but our eyes mainly notice the blue.

⑥ This same idea explains **why sunsets are red**. When the sun sits low on the horizon at evening, its light has to pass through a much thicker layer of air before reaching our eyes. The blue and violet colours are scattered away long before they reach us; only the long-wave reds and oranges make it through. That is why low, tired sunlight paints the clouds fiery orange and pink.

⑦ And this is also why the sky on the moon is **always black**, even in the middle of "day". The moon has almost no air, so there are no molecules to scatter sunlight. You see only the sun itself, bright as a lamp, against a black sky full of stars.

⑧ So the next time you look up at a clear autumn sky, remember: the blue above your head is not a paint. It is millions of tiny particles quietly bouncing a certain colour of sunlight into your eyes. The sky is, in a way, **a giant mirror of physics** — and it has been showing us this show, for free, every single afternoon of our lives.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. According to the article, what is sunlight really made of?**

A. Only the colour white.  
B. Only blue and red.  
C. Seven colours — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo and violet — travelling together.  
D. Only violet light.

**2. Which of the following is true about waves of different colours of light?**

A. Red waves are short; blue waves are long.  
B. Red waves are long; blue and violet waves are short.  
C. All colours have waves of exactly the same length.  
D. Only violet light travels in waves.

**3. Why does the daytime sky look blue, according to the article?**

A. Because the air itself is painted blue.  
B. Because blue, a short-wave colour, bumps into air molecules and scatters in all directions, while red mostly passes straight through.  
C. Because our eyes can only see blue during the day.  
D. Because the sun only sends out blue light in the morning.

**4. Why does the sky appear red or orange at sunset, not blue?**

A. Because the air becomes red at evening.  
B. Because the sun paints itself red before setting.  
C. Because low sunlight must travel through a much thicker layer of air, so blue and violet are scattered away and only the long-wave reds and oranges reach our eyes.  
D. Because evening clouds are always red.

**5. Why is the sky on the moon always black?**

A. Because the moon has no sun above it.  
B. Because the moon has almost no air, so there are no molecules to scatter sunlight — we see only the sun and stars against black space.  
C. Because the moon's sky is painted black.  
D. Because the moon is too far from the earth to receive light.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "sunlight is made of many colours ... red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo and violet"  
【解析】阳光由七色组成。A、B、D 均片面或错误。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "Red waves are long; blue and violet waves are short."  
【解析】红光波长长，蓝紫光波长短。A 颠倒、C 否定差异、D 片面。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "the short-wave colours, especially blue, bump into these molecules and scatter in all directions ... the long-wave colours, like red, mostly pass straight through"  
【解析】"瑞利散射"解释。A 字面、C 主观错误、D 与科学相反。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 "its light has to pass through a much thicker layer of air ... The blue and violet colours are scattered away long before they reach us"  
【解析】黄昏光线穿越更厚大气，蓝紫被散射，只剩红橙。A、B、D 均违反科学。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑦段 "The moon has almost no air, so there are no molecules to scatter sunlight."  
【解析】月球无大气 → 无散射 → 天空漆黑。A、C、D 皆错。故选 B。
