# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do Our Fingers Get Wrinkled in Water?**

① After a long bath or forty minutes of washing dishes, if you look closely at the tips of your fingers, you will find that they have turned into small, soft mountain ranges. The skin is covered with tiny lines and folds. Most people have noticed this change many times, but few have asked a simple question: **why does our skin, strong as it normally is, crumple in water?** For many years scientists believed the answer was simple. Today we know it is more interesting.

② The old idea was that our skin "absorbs" water like a sponge. When the outside layer soaks up water, it swells. Because this layer is glued to the layer below, it cannot stretch outward; instead, it folds, creating wrinkles. This sounds reasonable, but it does not fully explain one surprising fact: **people whose nerves in the hand are damaged do not get wrinkled fingers, even after a long bath**. If wrinkles were only the result of water soaking, damaged nerves would not matter.

③ In 1936, a doctor noticed this strange pattern: patients with nerve injuries had smooth fingers in water, while their healthy hand still wrinkled as usual. Since then, many researchers have tested the idea. Today most scientists accept a newer explanation: fingertip wrinkling is partly controlled by the **nervous system**. When the hand is in water for several minutes, tiny nerves send a signal to the small blood vessels under the skin, telling them to shrink. As blood vessels become narrower, the skin above is pulled into folds — the wrinkles we see.

④ If wrinkles are not simply caused by water, why has our body developed this automatic response? One popular answer comes from a **hand-grip** experiment. Researchers asked volunteers to pick up wet marbles with both wrinkled and smooth fingers. The result was clear: wrinkled fingers gripped the wet marbles faster and more firmly. The tiny lines, like the patterns on car tyres, let water run away more easily, which increases friction and grip. In short: wrinkles may help us hold on to wet things.

⑤ This idea fits our history. Long ago, our ancestors needed to collect food from wet riverbanks or from fishing nets. Hands that held slippery things better would help a person survive. Over many generations, bodies with this "rain-tyre" ability may have been kept by nature. Each wrinkle, then, is not a sign that our skin is weak, but a gentle reminder that our hands are still very, very old tools.

⑥ Next time your fingers turn into soft little maps after a long swim, smile. Inside those strange folds, you can read a short story about nerves, blood vessels and wet ancient riverbanks — a story kept by your own body, quietly, across thousands of years.

【答案】B

【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段
