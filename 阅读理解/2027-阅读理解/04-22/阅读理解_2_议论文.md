# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle School Students Use AI Chatbots for Homework?**

① It is 2027, and in many Chinese middle schools, students can now talk to an **AI chatbot** as easily as to a classmate. Type a maths problem, a science question, or a draft of an English essay, and in a second, a polite robot voice writes a full answer. This new tool is exciting — but it also brings a big question: **should middle school students be allowed to use AI chatbots for homework?**

② People who say **yes** point to clear benefits. First, chatbots are **patient**. They never become angry when a student asks the same question ten times. A shy child who is afraid to raise her hand in class may finally get her doubts answered at 10 p.m. Second, they are **fast**. In the time needed to flip through a thick dictionary, a chatbot can explain a word in six different ways, with examples. Third, they can offer **personalised help** — showing an easy example to a weaker student and a harder one to a quicker student at the same moment.

③ On the other hand, many teachers warn that chatbots can also become a **silent trap**. When students simply **copy** chatbot answers into their notebooks, their brains stop working. They may finish homework in five minutes, but understand almost nothing. In the exam room, where no chatbot is allowed, they are left alone with blank pages. In this way, an "easy tool" may quietly **steal** a student's ability to think.

④ There is also the problem of **mistakes**. AI chatbots sometimes write confidently about things that are not true. They may give a wrong date for a historical event, or a wrong formula for a triangle. A student who believes every answer without checking is like a traveller who trusts a broken map. Being fast is not enough; being **right** still matters, and this is a lesson a chatbot cannot fully teach.

⑤ Some people say we should simply **ban** chatbots for middle school students. Others say we should **open the door wide**. I believe the wiser answer lies in the middle: **use them, but use them thoughtfully**. A student may ask a chatbot to explain a difficult idea, to check the grammar of a paragraph, or to give three new example sentences. But the final thinking, the final writing, and the final learning must come from the student. The chatbot is a **coach**, not a **copier**.

⑥ Schools, too, have a role. They can teach "AI lessons" in which students see how chatbots are built, why they sometimes fail, and how to talk to them well. They can design homework that is hard to copy — personal interviews, classroom presentations, small group experiments, and in-class writing. In this way, AI becomes a friend of learning, not its enemy. If we are brave and careful together, tomorrow's students will not be defeated by chatbots — they will be strengthened by them.
