# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The First Time I Took the Train Alone**

① I was fourteen the first time my parents let me take the **high-speed train** by myself. My grandmother lived in a small town about two hours from our city, and every October holiday our family went to see her together. But this year my father had to work, my mother had a meeting, and my grandmother had been in hospital for a week with a bad cough. "Grandma is going home on Saturday," my mother said on Friday night, watching me carefully. "She would love to see you. Do you think you could go alone?" I said "yes" before I had time to think.

② On Saturday morning the station was **enormous**. I had been there many times with my parents, but this time nobody held my hand or my ticket. In my little backpack I had: one bottle of water, one apple, my grandmother's favourite orange-flavoured sweets, my phone with 80 % battery, my ID card, and a piece of paper on which my mother had written — in very large characters — the train number, the platform, the arrival time, and my grandmother's neighbour's phone number "in case of any emergency". I kept touching the paper in my pocket, the way a small child keeps touching a favourite toy.

③ At the security gate, my hands were **shaking** a little. A guard looked at my ID card, then at me, and smiled. "First time alone?" he asked. I nodded. "Don't worry. Just follow the numbers on the screens," he said, pointing upwards. I felt a small wave of **gratitude** towards this stranger I would never see again. Then I walked on, trying hard to look older than I was.

④ The waiting hall was full of voices and moving feet. I sat down on a metal chair and forced myself to breathe slowly. I opened my phone and read my mother's last message: *"Platform 7. Car 6, seat 12A. Don't get off at the wrong station. Call me the moment you see Grandma."* I read it three times, then closed the phone to save the battery. Next to me, a woman was feeding a baby; on my other side, an old man was carefully peeling a boiled egg. The world was going on in its ordinary way, and I was part of it — alone, but not forgotten.

⑤ The train came in **silently**, a long white line gliding beside the platform. I counted the cars — one, two, three, four, five, **six** — and climbed on. Car 6 was quieter than I had expected. I found seat 12A by the window, pushed my backpack under the seat, and sat down. A boy about my age was already in 12B, scrolling through his phone; across the aisle, a grandmother was knitting a small red sock. Nobody looked at me. For some reason, this made me feel safer than being stared at, and I smiled a tiny smile at the window.

⑥ As the train moved out of the station, the city began to slide away — shopping malls, apartment blocks, a river with grey boats, a school playground empty on Saturday. Then, suddenly, fields. Rows of corn almost ready to be cut, small ponds with white ducks, a single farmer riding a three-wheeled cart along a narrow road. Everything was racing backwards past my window at 300 kilometres an hour. I realised I had never really **watched** the countryside before; on family trips I had usually been looking at my phone or talking. Alone, with nothing to do, I saw it for the first time.

⑦ Halfway through the journey, the boy next to me dropped his pencil. It rolled under my seat. I picked it up and handed it to him. He said "thank you" so quietly that I only saw the words rather than heard them, but he also smiled. Three minutes later, a little girl across the aisle said her juice box was stuck, and her grandmother — the one knitting — couldn't open it with knitting needles in her hands. Without really thinking, I said, "I can help." I opened the juice box. The grandmother thanked me in the warm, slow voice that grandmothers everywhere seem to share, and for a moment I missed my own grandmother so much that my eyes stung.

⑧ The train announced my station. I stood up, lifted my backpack, and walked to the door with my heart beating like a drum. On the platform, among the crowd, I saw a small figure in a dark blue coat, waving with both hands. It was my grandmother, pale but standing, with the neighbour beside her just in case. I ran. When I reached her, I did not say anything smart or grown-up. I just hugged her, and she held my face in her cool hands and said, "Look at you. You came **all by yourself**." She said it as if I had crossed an ocean.

⑨ Later, walking slowly home with her, carrying her small bag, I thought about how two hours on a train had quietly changed something inside me. I still did not feel grown-up, not really. But I had packed my own bag, read my own ticket, sat by myself, helped a small stranger, and arrived exactly where I was needed. The world, I realised, was **not as big and frightening** as it had seemed at the station gate. You only had to **step onto the train** — and then, one station at a time, you became the person you were meant to be.

---

**注释：** high-speed train 高铁；platform 站台；aisle 过道；gratitude 感激；knit 编织

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Why did the narrator need to travel alone this time (Paragraph 1)?**

A. Because the narrator wanted to run away from home.  
B. Because the father had to work, the mother had a meeting, and the grandmother had been in hospital and would go home on Saturday, so the mother asked if the narrator could go alone to see her.  
C. Because the parents were on holiday overseas.  
D. Because the grandmother was moving to another country.

**2. What did the mother write on the piece of paper the narrator carried (Paragraph 2)?**

A. Only the grandmother's name.  
B. The train number, the platform, the arrival time, and the grandmother's neighbour's phone number in case of emergency.  
C. A list of homework for the weekend.  
D. Directions to a famous tourist spot.

**3. How did the narrator feel when the security guard smiled and said, "Just follow the numbers on the screens" (Paragraph 3)?**

A. Angry at the guard for asking.  
B. A small wave of gratitude towards the stranger.  
C. Completely bored.  
D. Certain that something bad would happen.

**4. What unexpected thing did the narrator do on the train (Paragraph 7)?**

A. Refused to talk to anyone.  
B. Picked up a boy's dropped pencil and later helped a little girl's grandmother open a juice box, and briefly missed his own grandmother.  
C. Fell asleep and missed the station.  
D. Argued with a passenger about the seat.

**5. What does the narrator realise at the end of the story (Paragraph 9)?**

A. That trains are dangerous and should be avoided.  
B. That the world is not as big and frightening as it had seemed at the station gate; by stepping onto the train and going one station at a time, you slowly become the person you were meant to be.  
C. That he will never travel alone again.  
D. That grandparents should not live far away.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "father had to work ... mother had a meeting ... grandmother had been in hospital ... going home on Saturday ... Do you think you could go alone?"  
【解析】家人皆无法同行，奶奶又出院，故需独自前往。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "train number, the platform, the arrival time, and my grandmother's neighbour's phone number 'in case of any emergency'"  
【解析】纸上写的信息正是 B。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】情感态度题  
【定位】第③段 "a small wave of gratitude towards this stranger"  
【解析】作者对安检员心生感激。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段 "picked it up and handed it to him ... 'I can help.' I opened the juice box ... I missed my own grandmother"  
【解析】作者主动助人，并思念奶奶。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨/观点题  
【定位】第⑨段 "not as big and frightening ... step onto the train ... one station at a time, you became the person you were meant to be"  
【解析】末段升华：独自出行促成长。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
