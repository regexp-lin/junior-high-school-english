# 阅读理解（说明文） **Chinese Paper-cutting — A Red Window Art**

① If you visit a Chinese village in winter, you may notice something bright and red dancing on every window — small flowers, fat fish, smiling children, even lucky phrases. These are not paintings. They are **paper-cuttings** (剪纸), one of the oldest folk arts in China. In 2009, Chinese paper-cutting was added to the list of **World Intangible Cultural Heritage** by UNESCO.

② The art began a very long time ago. After paper was invented in the **Eastern Han Dynasty (about AD 105)**, clever Chinese women started to cut paper into beautiful shapes. In the earliest days, these little works were used for religious ceremonies. Later, they moved onto the windows of ordinary homes to celebrate birthdays, weddings and, most of all, the **Spring Festival**.

③ The tools are surprisingly simple. A sharp pair of small scissors, a thin knife, and a piece of red paper — these are all a traditional artist needs. Red is the favourite colour, because in Chinese culture red stands for **happiness and good luck**. The paper can be folded two, four or even eight times, so one careful cut creates a perfectly balanced shape on all sides.

④ The pictures look simple, yet they carry deep meaning. A fish (鱼) sounds like the word "plenty" (余), so a paper fish means "may your life be full". A peach means long life. Two mandarin ducks mean a happy marriage. A child holding a big carp is a common New Year image, showing the family's wish for more happiness in the year ahead.

⑤ Today, paper-cutting is changing in lovely ways. Students learn it in art classes at primary and middle schools. Young designers have printed paper-cut patterns onto T-shirts, notebooks and mobile phone cases. Some artists even cut large 3D paper scenes of modern cities — a high-speed train made of paper, or a tiny forest park no bigger than your hand.

⑥ Next time you see a red paper window flower, take a closer look. Inside those tiny cuts there are thousands of years of smiles, wishes and hopes. **This small art is not only about paper; it is about keeping Chinese culture alive — one cut at a time.**
