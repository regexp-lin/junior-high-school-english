# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Schools Forbid Middle School Students from Bringing Snacks to Class?**

Many middle schools now have a simple rule: no snacks in the classroom. A few parents and students think this rule is too strict. "Why can't we have a small biscuit when we are hungry?" they ask. In my view, however, schools are **right** to forbid snacks inside the classroom, with only a few small exceptions.

First, snacks are a real disturbance to study. In a quiet afternoon class, even one student opening a plastic bag creates a long "crackle" that ten students can hear. Crunchy snacks, like chips, make noise that is hard to ignore. A single student eating happily can pull the attention of her neighbours away from the blackboard. If ten students do the same, the teacher might as well stop the class. Snacks may feel small to the eater, but their effect on 45 classmates is not small at all.

Second, snacks create health and cleanliness problems. Most popular school snacks are high in salt, sugar or oil — dry noodles, biscuits, sweet drinks. If students eat these between lessons, they are less hungry at lunch, when more healthy food is served. Over months, the habit can lead to poor nutrition. In addition, crumbs fall on desks and floors, ants appear, and cleaning workers have to do extra work every evening.

Some students answer, "But I get really hungry in the afternoon; I can't focus." This is a fair worry, but the answer is **not** to eat during class. Eating during a lesson still breaks focus. A better answer is to use the 10-minute break between classes to eat a small, clean snack in the hall or outside the classroom. Some schools now allow a fruit, a plain biscuit or a boiled egg in the corridor. This is reasonable.

Others say the rule is unfair for students who take medicine with food, or students with low blood sugar. In my view, the rule should allow these clear exceptions. A student who has written permission from a doctor and the school office should be able to eat the required food quietly at any time, including class time. Real care, not "one rule fits all", is the right answer here.

Finally, let us think about one longer-term point: the classroom is a small training ground for the habits we need in adult life. In most serious workplaces — hospitals, libraries, offices — you do not eat at your desk during a meeting. Learning to "wait fifteen minutes until the break" is a small, useful self-control that will serve students for many years. A snack in the hallway is not a hardship; it is simply the price of a focused classroom.

In short, snacks are not the enemy, but the **classroom** is not the place for them. Schools should keep the rule clear, allow medical exceptions, and use short breaks wisely. In this way, the class stays quiet, the food stays enjoyable, and everybody wins.

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the author's main opinion?**

A. Schools should allow snacks in class at all times.  
B. Schools are right to forbid snacks inside the classroom, with a few small exceptions.  
C. Snacks should be totally banned from the whole school.  
D. Snacks should be banned only for Grade 9 students.

**2. What is the first reason the author gives?**

A. Snacks are too expensive.  
B. Snacks are a real disturbance to study for 45 classmates.  
C. Snacks make students fatter.  
D. Snacks are only for children.

**3. What does paragraph 3 mainly say?**

A. Snacks make students clean the classroom.  
B. Snacks are the best source of energy in the afternoon.  
C. Snacks cause health problems (too much salt, sugar, oil, less appetite for lunch) and cleanliness problems like crumbs.  
D. Snacks cost the school too much money.

**4. How should students with medical needs be treated, according to the author?**

A. They must follow the same strict rule.  
B. They can eat loud crunchy chips in class.  
C. With a doctor's note and school office permission, they may eat the required food quietly, even during class.  
D. They should stay home during afternoon classes.

**5. Why does the author mention adult workplaces?**

A. To say students should start working at 15.  
B. To show that most serious workplaces also forbid eating at your desk during meetings, so waiting till the break is useful self-control.  
C. To say that offices have better snacks than schools.  
D. To say students should not work in hospitals.
