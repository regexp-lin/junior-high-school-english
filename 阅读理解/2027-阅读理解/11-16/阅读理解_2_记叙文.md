# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Afternoon I Made Grandma's Phone Friendlier**

① My grandmother lives on the seventh floor of an old yellow building at the corner of Xinghua Road. She is sixty-eight. Since Grandpa passed away two years ago, she has lived alone, with a pot of winter bamboo by the balcony door, a small orange cat called Taotao, and a **very old smartphone** that she and my aunt bought together in 2021. Last Saturday afternoon, after my PE test, I climbed those seven flights of stairs with a small bag of her favourite grapes, and I found her sitting on the sofa, holding the phone **slightly too close to her nose**, sighing.

② "What's wrong, Nainai?" I said, sitting down beside her. She pushed the phone into my hands. "I cannot read it any more," she said. "The letters are tiny. Even in the sun. Even with my new glasses." I looked at the screen. The font was about **12 points** — a size perfectly fine for my fourteen-year-old eyes, much too small for my grandmother's. The rows of tiny app icons on the home screen looked like a **field of small mysterious flowers**. She did not even know there was a **weather app** on the phone, and she had been asking neighbours about the rain for two years.

③ I had never been an "expert" at anything, but I suddenly felt like one. I took a deep breath and said, "Let me try. Give me thirty minutes." Nainai laughed. "If you can make this old thing easier, I will cook you two extra dumplings." So I began.

④ **Step one: font and display.** In *Settings → Display → Font Size*, I pulled the small slider from 12 points all the way up to **20 points**. Every letter on every app became clearly visible, almost like the letters in her primary-school text book. I also turned on **Bold Text**, and a new option called *Display Zoom*, which made everything — icons, photos, buttons — about 20% larger. Nainai squinted at the screen and, after a moment, her face opened like a flower at sunrise. "**I can see that!**" she whispered.

⑤ **Step two: home screen tidy-up.** On her old phone, there were 48 apps — but she only used **seven**: phone, WeChat, camera, photos, weather, bank, and a small video app where my aunt posted family clips. I moved the seven apps into a **single bright row** at the top of the home screen, each with a small hand-drawn label in her own language: "Call my family", "Message my family", "See the weather", "Our bank", "Family videos", "Take a photo", "My photo album". The other 41 apps I carefully moved into one folder at the back — not deleted, in case she ever needed them.

⑥ **Step three: the emergency button.** I asked Nainai, "If something happens and you cannot move well, what do you want to do?" She thought for a moment. "Call my son. Call my daughter. Call the community nurse, Little Mei." I programmed her **power button** to become an emergency button: press it **five times** quickly, and the phone would (a) call a chosen contact, (b) send her address by GPS to a short family list, and (c) show a bright SOS card on the screen. Then I showed her how to use it three times, slowly.

⑦ **Step four: photos.** My aunt sends Nainai more than forty family photos each week, but Nainai had no idea how to open the photo app — so the photos piled up in WeChat, unseen. I put the **photo album icon** right next to the weather icon, and set the first album to "Family 2027". Nainai's eyes grew wet when she saw a picture of my baby cousin taken last Tuesday in Shanghai. "I didn't know this was inside the phone," she said. "I thought I had to wait for the holiday to see him."

⑧ By 17:00 the sky outside had turned orange, and a soft smell of dumpling wrapping was coming from the small kitchen. Nainai had used her new phone for fifteen minutes on her own: opened the weather app, sent a voice message to my uncle, taken a slightly blurry but happy photo of Taotao the cat, and, most of all, read the word "Tuesday" on the screen without squinting. She held the phone gently, almost like a small visitor who had finally decided to stay.

⑨ Before I left, she took my hand and said, "Thank you, baobei. Other people think you are learning English and maths all day. But today you learned an older thing: you learned to be **patient with someone slower than you**." On the bus home, I remembered what my own mother often tells me: "A phone is only friendly if the person holding it is treated as a friend." I had not made Grandma's phone more modern. I had only made it **a little more like her**. That evening, over the promised two extra dumplings, I decided that once every two weeks, I would quietly visit the seventh floor of that yellow building — and make sure the little machine in Grandma's hand stayed **kind**.

---

**注释：** font size 字体大小；bold 粗体；emergency 紧急；GPS 全球定位系统；album 相册

---

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Why did Grandma sigh while holding her phone at the beginning?**

A. Because the phone was broken.  
B. Because she could not read the screen any more — the letters were tiny, even in the sun and with her new glasses.  
C. Because someone had stolen her phone.  
D. Because the phone was too loud.

**2. What did the writer do in "Step one" about font and display?**

A. She bought a new phone.  
B. She pulled the font size from 12 points to 20 points, turned on Bold Text, and turned on Display Zoom to make everything about 20% larger.  
C. She changed the colour of the phone.  
D. She turned off the screen.

**3. Why did the writer move ONLY seven apps to the top row of the home screen?**

A. Because the phone could only hold seven apps.  
B. Because Grandma only used seven apps — phone, WeChat, camera, photos, weather, bank, and a family video app — and the writer wanted to make them easy to see with hand-drawn labels, while the other 41 apps were moved into one back folder, not deleted.  
C. Because the writer forgot about the other apps.  
D. Because Grandma refused to use more than seven apps.

**4. Which of the following is TRUE about the emergency button?**

A. It calls the police immediately and silently.  
B. It needs a fingerprint to work.  
C. Pressing the power button five times quickly calls a chosen contact, sends Grandma's address by GPS to a short family list, and shows a bright SOS card on the screen.  
D. It only works at night.

**5. What does Grandma mean by "you learned to be patient with someone slower than you"?**

A. The writer learned to walk slowly.  
B. The writer learned a different kind of skill on that Saturday — not English or maths, but the patience and care to treat an older person as a friend and make technology fit her, not the other way round.  
C. The writer should stop studying.  
D. Grandma is tired of technology.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①、②段 "cannot read ... letters are tiny ... even with my new glasses"  
【解析】视觉困难直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段字体调整  
【解析】三项设置直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段主页整理  
【解析】常用 7 个 + 其他 41 个归类直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段紧急按钮设置  
【解析】三项功能直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑨段奶奶话 + 结尾段作者反思  
【解析】代际之爱与技术友好。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
