# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Night the Lights Went Out**

① It was Tuesday, 19 October, around eight at night. Our classroom was quiet. Thirty-eight of us were hunched over our physics papers, pencils moving in tiny scratches. The window was cracked open, and a cool autumn wind carried the smell of leaves from the playground. I was halfway through question 18 when everything suddenly **went black**.

② At first, there was a short silence — the kind you hear in a film just before something dramatic. Then someone whispered, "Power cut." A nervous laugh travelled through the room. Phones began to glow here and there, like small blue stars. Across the aisle, my friend Wanlin whispered, "Now what?" I had no idea. The physics paper, which had felt so heavy thirty seconds ago, suddenly seemed **far away**.

③ Our class monitor, Chen Rui, stood up calmly. "**Everyone, keep your seats,**" he said, quietly but firmly. "Don't rush to the corridor. The emergency light will come on in a minute." And indeed, a thin green glow appeared above the door, enough to show the rows of desks, but not enough to read by.

④ Ms. Lin, our physics teacher, had been marking homework at the front desk. She closed her red pen, laid it on the table, and walked slowly to the middle of the room. "Put down the phones," she said, not angry, just tired. "Phones waste the battery you will need to call your parents later." Dozens of small blue stars faded.

⑤ What happened next surprised me. In the green half-light, Ms. Lin sat on the edge of the front desk, hands folded, and began to tell a **story** — how she had been our age in 2005, how her school in a small mountain town lost power **three times a week**, and how her own physics teacher used to teach by **candlelight**. "We did not learn fewer things," she said. "We learned to **listen** more carefully, because we could not see the board." The classroom grew quiet in a new way — not the quiet of an exam, but the quiet of a family listening to grandmother after dinner.

⑥ A boy at the back, Tong, who usually liked to joke, asked seriously, "Were you afraid, Miss?" Ms. Lin laughed softly. "A little. But my classmates were there, so I was mostly **alright**." She looked around our dim faces. "Tonight you are also together. Physics can wait thirty minutes."

⑦ Someone near the window said that rain had started outside. We could hear it now — thin, gentle, tapping the roof. Wanlin took out a small chocolate bar from her pencil case and broke it into many tiny pieces. She passed them row by row. Everyone got a piece no bigger than a fingernail, but somehow it felt like more. Chen Rui quietly walked to the corridor and came back with two thermoses of hot water.

⑧ At 8:47, the lights came back on in a sudden, bright flash. For a moment, **no one moved**. Our papers were still on the desks, half-done. The green emergency light turned off. Ms. Lin stood up slowly. "Finish the last two questions," she said. "Then close the books. Go home and sleep early." She smiled, and added in a low voice, "Tonight, even the dark had something to teach."

⑨ Walking home along the wet road, I kept thinking about those thirty minutes in the dark. The physics paper, in the end, was still the same paper. But **I was a little different**. I had learned that a class can turn into a family, a teacher into a storyteller, and a power cut into a quiet little gift — if you let it.

---

**注释：** power cut 停电；emergency light 应急灯；thermos 暖水壶；candlelight 烛光；chocolate bar 巧克力条

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What were the students doing when the lights went out?**

A. Watching a film.  
B. Taking a physics test at evening self-study.  
C. Eating dinner.  
D. Running on the playground.

**2. What did the class monitor, Chen Rui, ask everyone to do?**

A. Run outside at once.  
B. Shout for help.  
C. Keep their seats and wait for the emergency light.  
D. Go to the office.

**3. Why did Ms. Lin ask the students to put their phones down?**

A. Phones are not allowed at school.  
B. She wanted to punish them.  
C. She said phones would waste the battery they might need later to call their parents.  
D. She hated bright lights.

**4. What story did Ms. Lin tell in the green half-light?**

A. She told them about her summer holiday.  
B. She told them how, as a student in 2005, her mountain-town school lost power three times a week, and her teacher taught by candlelight.  
C. She told them a ghost story.  
D. She read aloud from a textbook.

**5. What does the writer really mean in the last paragraph?**

A. Physics is the most important subject in school.  
B. Chocolate tastes better at night.  
C. An unplanned event (a power cut) can teach lessons that no textbook can — about togetherness, patience, and quiet listening.  
D. Phones should be banned forever.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "Thirty-eight of us were hunched over our physics papers ... evening self-study"  
【解析】晚自习物理测验。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "Everyone, keep your seats ... emergency light will come on"  
【解析】保持秩序原地待命。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "Phones waste the battery you will need to call your parents later."  
【解析】理由直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "in 2005 ... small mountain town ... three times a week ... taught by candlelight"  
【解析】回忆细节直引。A、C、D 无依据。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第⑨段 "a class can turn into a family ... a power cut into a quiet little gift"  
【解析】提炼事件对人的影响。A、B、D 均偏离。故选 C。
