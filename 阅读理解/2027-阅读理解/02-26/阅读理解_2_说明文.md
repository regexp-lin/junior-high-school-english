# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Bees Make Honey — A Sweet Journey**

① When you spread golden honey on a warm piece of bread, do you ever wonder how such sweet gold arrives in the jar? The answer lies in one of the busiest families in nature: the **honeybees**. To make just **one kilogram of honey**, bees visit about **four million flowers** and fly a total distance of around **150,000 kilometres** — enough to circle the Earth three times.

② The story starts in the flower. A worker bee pushes her long **tongue**, called a **proboscis** (/prəˈbɒsɪs/), deep into the flower and drinks a sweet water called **nectar**. She stores it in a special "honey stomach", different from her eating stomach. When this tiny bag is full, she flies home, carrying sometimes **almost her own weight** in nectar.

③ Back in the hive, the flying bee passes the nectar to a **house bee** from mouth to mouth. During this careful passing, **enzymes** from the bee's body mix with the nectar. These enzymes slowly change the thin sugar water into a thicker, sweeter liquid. The house bee then places the liquid into a small **wax cell** in the honeycomb.

④ The nectar is still too watery to be honey. So hundreds of bees stand around the cells and **beat their wings** quickly — like tiny electric fans — to dry the nectar. Water slowly leaves the cell, and the liquid becomes thick and golden. When it is just right, the bees cover the cell with a thin cap of wax to keep the honey safe. Inside, it can remain good for many years.

⑤ Humans have loved honey for thousands of years. Ancient paintings in Spain, drawn around **8,000 years ago**, already show people climbing rocks to collect honey. Today, honey is not only a healthy food — it also helps small wounds heal, soothes sore throats, and flavours many traditional Chinese medicines. More importantly, bees pollinate one-third of the food we eat every day, from apples to almonds.

⑥ So next time you taste a drop of honey, remember: each spoonful is the quiet gift of countless tiny wings. **Protect the flowers, protect the bees, and we protect a sweeter future for all of us.**
