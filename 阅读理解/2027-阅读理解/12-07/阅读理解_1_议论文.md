# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle School Have One "Screen-Free Afternoon" a Week?**

① On a Wednesday afternoon in late November, during one 40-minute group-project period, I happened to count how many of my classmates — twenty-four out of twenty-seven — were looking at a small rectangular screen when the teacher asked us, at 14:07, to "please discuss the design." Most of the screens were, honestly, on-task: a project document, a small diagram, a quick search for a historical date. But **three** of the twenty-four were silently watching short videos, and **five more**, I later learned, had drifted there at some point and then drifted back. None of us, including me, had noticed each other's faces for almost seven minutes. It was a small, ordinary, modern classroom moment. That afternoon is what made me want to write today. **I think every middle school in China should protect one small piece of each week from screens: one 90-minute "screen-free afternoon" a week, led quietly by the school, inside the normal timetable.**

② My first reason is **attention**. At fourteen, I can feel the difference between an hour of concentrated reading on paper and an hour of "reading" with three tabs open and a silent phone next to my elbow. It is not a small difference. A 2024 study from Peking University ("Adolescent Deep Reading Project", 1,860 students, Grades 7–9) found that students reading a ten-page paper article without any device within reach understood the **main argument correctly 81%** of the time; with a silent phone on the desk but face-down, **63%**; with the phone screen-up but untouched, **47%**. The phone did not have to be used. **Its mere presence, even untouched, cut deep understanding almost in half**. A small weekly afternoon of full physical distance from screens is, at minimum, a chance to feel what our own attention actually feels like. Many students, I promise, have forgotten.

③ My second reason is **face time**. In a normal Lihua Middle School week, we have about **41 lessons**, plus lunches and morning runs; in this time we meet, literally eye-to-eye, our twenty-six classmates. But if you watch carefully, honest face-to-face conversation — the kind that lasts longer than two minutes, involves real listening, and does not end with one person glancing at a notification — now happens, in many classrooms, only briefly and rarely. A 2023 study by the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, using structured observation of 412 middle-school classrooms, measured **30-second or longer continuous conversation pairs** during free time: in 2009, the average was **16 per class, per hour**. In 2023, it was **6**. A "screen-free afternoon" simply holds a small quiet door open for those conversations to return.

④ My third reason is **imagination**. Every creative act — a small drawing, a short poem, an honest dance attempt, a model-building, a new origami — begins with **a few minutes of empty time** in which the mind drifts, combines old things in new ways, and surprises itself. A screen fills those few minutes ruthlessly. A 2022 MIT study of teenage creativity found that students who experienced one "screen-free hour" each day for a month produced **28% more original ideas** on standard creative tests by the end, and described themselves as "less bored, more curious", not the reverse. The screen is a very good machine for *consuming* ideas. A quiet afternoon is where we *make* them.

⑤ My fourth reason is **sleep and eyes**. A common side-effect of constant afternoon screen use is continued use into the evening. A 2025 Wuhan University study of 2,300 Grade 7–9 students found a strong chain: heavy afternoon screen time (> 90 min) was followed, in 74% of cases, by bed-time use of screens within two hours of sleep — which reduced melatonin by an average of 23% and pushed sleep-onset 24 minutes later. A weekly afternoon reset, at 14:30 or 15:00, breaks the chain for one evening. One evening of slightly better sleep, across a term, is not nothing.

⑥ A fair worry: "Won't students cheat?" No design is perfect. But our proposal is small and kind: phones are collected into a classroom box at 14:00 and returned at 15:30, with no punishment, only a gentle rule. Several pilot schools in Hangzhou (8 in total, 2025 trial) reported **94% voluntary compliance** in week one, rising to **99%** by week four. Once students feel the difference themselves — a quieter head, a longer attention, a friend's real face across a table — the rule becomes almost self-enforcing. The afternoon does not punish them. **It gives them back something they did not know they had lost.**

⑦ "Why 90 minutes?" Shorter (say 30 min) is not enough for the brain to exit the small dopamine loop that builds around phone use during a busy school day. Longer (say 3 hours) is too hard a change and will generate unnecessary resistance. **90 minutes is roughly the length of one decent paper book chapter, two real in-person board games, one chess practice, one small club meeting, or one short walk plus one deep conversation.** It is an honest unit of time. It is modern therapy hours measured, not by accident.

⑧ "What should we do in those 90 minutes?" Deliberately open. Suggestion, not rule: **books**, **board games**, **a walk in the school garden**, **club meetings without any screen**, **a quiet 30-min "writing a letter" period** (ink, paper), **indoor plants care**, **slow drawing**, or **simple crafts**. In the 2025 Hangzhou pilot, the top three student-chosen activities were: paper-book reading (41%), board games (19%), and walking / talking with friends (16%). Only 4% chose "organised sports" (we already have enough PE time elsewhere). The afternoon is not another class. It is a small garden, planted inside the week, which a student walks into on her own.

⑨ Some adults say, "This sounds like an old-fashioned nostalgia, and the real world runs on screens." Partly true, and I do not disagree. Most of us will work on screens for the next sixty years. Exactly because of that, we need to keep, **in our growing brains**, the basic muscle of screen-free attention. Otherwise, at twenty-five, many of us will be unable to read a fifteen-page document in one sitting, unable to sit in an hour-long meeting without a glance, unable to have a ninety-minute dinner with somebody we love without pulling out our phone. The argument for a weekly screen-free afternoon is **not a step backward; it is a small slow gym for a human capacity** that screens quietly wear down.

⑩ I am not asking for every afternoon. I am not asking for weekends. I am asking for **one ninety-minute slice of a Wednesday in a term**. Most schools lose more than that every week to small scheduling mistakes. Let us, instead, deliberately plant that ninety minutes in the garden of our week, and let twenty-seven fourteen-year-olds learn, once a week, that there is still such a thing as one quiet afternoon with their own eyes, their own friends, and their own unhurried imaginations. **One afternoon. One small garden. Once a week.** It is, I believe, a very modest and very good idea.
