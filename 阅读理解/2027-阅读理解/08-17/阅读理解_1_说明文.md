# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Do Birds Find Their Way When They Fly South?**

① Every autumn, if you look up at the sky just before sunset, you may see long lines of birds flying in a neat "V" shape toward the south. Some of these birds, like the little Arctic **tern**, will travel more than **70,000 kilometres** in a year, from the cold far north to the far south and back. They do this without any map, any phone signal, or any teacher. How can such small creatures find the right road across half of the Earth? Scientists have been studying this question for almost a hundred years, and the answer is made up of several amazing tools.

② The first tool is the **sun**. Many **migratory birds** leave during the day, and they use the sun like a large, slowly-moving compass. They know roughly where the sun should be at each hour of the morning or afternoon. When the sun is where they expect, they know they are flying the right way. Their tiny internal clocks keep track of time, so the moving sun does not confuse them.

③ The second tool is the **stars**. Some birds fly mainly at night, when the weather is cooler and there is less wind. Scientists once placed young night-flying birds inside a planetarium — a big room where false stars are shown on the ceiling. The birds always turned toward the true south even though they were inside a closed room. This means they are born with some basic "star maps" in their brains; they do not need a teacher to show them.

④ The third tool is perhaps the most surprising: the **Earth itself acts like a giant magnet**. Birds seem to have very small **magnetic particles** in their eyes and in their beaks. These particles help them feel the Earth's magnetic field, so they always know which way is north and which way is south, even on cloudy nights when no star or sun can be seen. In this way, even a small thunderstorm in the afternoon cannot make them completely lost.

⑤ The fourth tool is **landmarks**. On long journeys, rivers, mountains, coastlines, and lakes act like road signs. A young bird flying south for the first time often follows an older one. The young bird remembers the shape of the big river, the curve of the coast, the snow-covered mountain in the middle. The next year, when the young bird travels alone, these memories guide it like a silent map.

⑥ Finally, birds have **very good eyes** and a strong sense of the weather. They can feel the wind changing hours before a storm, and they often wait on the ground until the wind is right. Some species even fly higher on clear days and lower over the sea, using the air like a skilled pilot.

⑦ Of course, human cities today are making their journey harder. Bright lights from our tall buildings can confuse night-flying birds; wide glass windows are sometimes invisible to them. In more than twenty cities around the world, people have begun to **turn off unnecessary lights for two weeks every spring and autumn** to help the birds find their way safely.

⑧ Next time you hear the quiet cries of wild geese in the October evening, remember: above our rooftops, a small living compass is working with the sun, the stars, the Earth's magnetism, and memory — all at the same time — to guide these birds across the world.

**1.**

【解析】原文明确提到北极燕鸥年飞 7 万多千米。A、C、D 均未在原文提及此数据。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "they use the sun like a ... compass ... Their tiny internal clocks keep track of time"  
【解析】原文明确白天靠太阳+生物钟。A、C、D 均无文中依据。故选 B。

【答案】A

【答案】B

【解析】磁粒子帮助感知地磁场，阴天也能辨向。A、C、D 均无文中依据。故选 B。
