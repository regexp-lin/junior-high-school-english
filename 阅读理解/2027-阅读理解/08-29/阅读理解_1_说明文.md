# 阅读理解（说明文） **The Long Journey of a Single Sheet of Paper**

① Every school day, we use dozens of sheets of paper — in exercise books, test papers, art classes, and even our lunch napkins. We take paper for granted: it is cheap, easy to tear, and thrown away without much thought. Yet paper has a very long story. When it was first invented in China more than **2,000 years ago**, by a court official named **Cai Lun**, people saw it as a small miracle. Today, even with tall paper factories and fast machines, the basic idea behind paper making is still the same as it was in Cai Lun's time.

② The true secret of paper lies in one important word: **fibre**. If you look at a piece of paper through a microscope, you will see that it is made of **thousands of tiny strings**, joined together like a thin mat. These strings are plant fibres. In ancient times, people used the **bark** of certain trees, old cloth, or the inner skin of bamboo. Today, most paper comes from **fast-growing trees** such as pine or eucalyptus, although waste paper and some cotton scraps are also used.

③ The first step in modern paper making is to turn wood into small pieces. Logs are cleaned, the bark is removed, and the wood is cut by a big machine into **wood chips** about the size of a fingernail. Small chips are easier to soften than a whole log.

④ The second step is called "**making pulp**". The wood chips are put into a huge steel tank, together with water and some **chemicals**. The mixture is then heated. Under heat and pressure, the chemicals break the wood into its tiny fibres, and the result is a wet, grey-brown "**pulp**" — a bit like very thick porridge. In some factories, the pulp is also washed many times to remove dirt and unwanted colour.

⑤ The third step is to **make the pulp whiter and cleaner**. White paper needs light-coloured fibres, so the pulp is often bleached. Today's factories try to use safer chemicals and **less water**, because paper making in the past was known to pollute rivers. Modern factories often recycle most of their water several times before letting it out.

⑥ The fourth step is the most beautiful part. The clean pulp is mixed with a lot of water and poured onto a **moving wire belt**, which is a very fine screen. As the belt moves, water drips through the tiny holes, leaving a thin, wet mat of fibres on top. Next, this wet mat passes through large **rollers** that squeeze out more water and press the fibres closer together. Finally, the mat goes through rows of **heated rollers** that dry the paper and make it smooth. In the biggest modern machines, the whole trip from pulp to finished paper takes only a few minutes.

⑦ Last comes the cutting. The long paper comes out of the machine in huge rolls, as wide as a small car. Special cutters then slice it into the sizes we see in the shop: A4, A3, small notebooks, big newspapers. Each of these has its own weight, thickness and smoothness.

⑧ Of course, paper making has one quiet problem: it uses **a lot of trees and a lot of water**. Even fast-growing pine takes six to ten years to be ready. That is why **recycling paper** is so important: one tonne of recycled paper can save about **17 trees** and a large amount of clean water. Many schools, including ours, now collect used paper each month for this reason.

⑨ So next time you take a clean sheet of paper out of your pencil case, think for one quiet moment. Behind that simple white square lies a journey — from a far-away forest, through hot tanks, across a moving wire belt, through rollers, and finally to your hand — a journey that began with Cai Lun's patient hands two thousand years ago, and that still leaves a small mark on our planet today.

**注释：** fibre 纤维；pulp 纸浆；bleach 漂白；roller 辊；recycle 回收利用

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Who is the Chinese court official mentioned as the inventor of paper more than 2,000 years ago?**

A. Confucius.  
B. Cai Lun.  
C. Li Bai.  
D. Zheng He.

**2. What is the basic building material of every sheet of paper?**

A. Pure water.  
B. Small pieces of coloured plastic.  
C. Tiny plant fibres joined together like a thin mat.  
D. Dried sand.

**3. Why are wood logs first cut into small **wood chips** before being turned into pulp?**

A. Because small chips are heavier and easier to count.  
B. Because small chips are easier to soften than a whole log.  
C. Because the chips are used directly as food for animals.  
D. Because the chips can be stored in a lunch box.

**4. What happens on the moving wire belt in the paper machine?**

A. The pulp is turned back into trees.  
B. Water drips through tiny holes in the belt, leaving a thin wet mat of fibres on top.  
C. The pulp is painted with different colours.  
D. The pulp is frozen into ice sheets.

**5. Why does the writer mention that "one tonne of recycled paper can save about 17 trees and a lot of clean water"?**

A. To make the reader feel sleepy.  
B. To show that recycling paper is an important, realistic way to protect our forests and rivers.  
C. To prove that all trees in the world will disappear next year.  
D. To encourage people to burn more wood at home.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "invented in China more than 2,000 years ago, by a court official named Cai Lun"  
【解析】原文直接写明。A、C、D 均非文中所述。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "paper ... is made of thousands of tiny strings, joined together like a thin mat. These strings are plant fibres"  
【解析】纸的构成为植物纤维编织。A、B、D 均与原文不符。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段末 "Small chips are easier to soften than a whole log"  
【解析】原文直接解释原因。A、C、D 均无依据。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 "water drips through the tiny holes, leaving a thin, wet mat of fibres on top"  
【解析】传送带上水从孔中滴下，只留下纤维薄层。A、C、D 均不合事实。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】写作意图题  
【定位】第⑧段 "recycling paper is so important: one tonne of recycled paper can save about 17 trees"  
【解析】用具体数据说明回收的重要意义。A、C、D 均与作者意图不符。故选 B。
