# 阅读理解（议论文） **Does Handwriting Still Matter in the Age of Typing?**

① A simple look inside many Grade 8 classrooms today tells us something new. Students arrive with tablets, take notes by typing, answer quizzes with a finger tap, and send homework as PDFs. At home, parents work on laptops; teachers message on phones. Pens stay in pencil cases, untouched for days. Some adults think this is modern and natural; others quietly worry. Does **handwriting** — that slow, careful act of forming letters by hand — still matter when almost every word can be typed? In my view, **yes — handwriting remains a quietly powerful skill, and middle schools should protect at least half an hour of real handwriting practice every school day.**

② The first reason is **memory**. For more than fifty years, scientists have tested whether people remember better from typed notes or handwritten ones. The results, again and again, point in the same direction. A 2025 study from Fudan University asked 300 Grade 8 students to watch the same 20-minute history lesson. Half took notes by keyboard; half by hand. One week later, those who had written by hand remembered about **28% more** facts and, more interestingly, were much better at explaining **ideas in their own words**. Why? Typing lets us catch every sentence quickly, but the hand is slower than the ear. So when we write by hand, the brain must decide: what matters? What can be shortened? This quiet, active choice is itself a kind of learning.

③ The second reason is **focus**. A keyboard is rarely alone. Notifications, tabs, and bright small lights always wait just one second away. A pen, by comparison, is quiet. It asks nothing of us except a steady hand. A classroom that spends half an hour each day writing by hand gives students a small, protected island in which their minds can slow down. Over years, this quiet island builds the rare ability to stay with one idea for longer than thirty seconds.

④ A third reason is **fine motor skills**. Handwriting is not just "writing"; it is also a complex dance of tiny finger muscles, eye-hand coordination, and careful pressure control. Studies of young children show that those who write by hand a little each day are, on average, slightly better at drawing, at threading a needle, and at using tools like screwdrivers. As we head into a world full of small, delicate tasks (surgery, art, craft, repair), the fine control of the hand is not old-fashioned at all — it is a foundation we still need.

⑤ Of course, some will argue that "typing is faster, and the world now rewards speed". This is partly true. I do not suggest we go back to writing all our homework by hand. The keyboard is an excellent tool for long reports, for editing, for sending messages across the world. The real question is not *typing or handwriting*, but **when to use each**. I suggest a simple balance: **type for editing and final drafts; write by hand for brainstorming, note-taking, and serious reading.** This balance gets the best of both worlds without pretending the world has not changed.

⑥ Another common doubt is about **neatness**. Some teachers say, "Students' handwriting today is unclear — it is hard to read." This is true, but the answer is to **practise more**, not to give up. Short, regular lessons in how to hold a pen, how to form letters carefully, and how to slow down can improve handwriting noticeably in three months. Schools in Shanghai that added 20 minutes of handwriting practice per day reported that not only did handwriting quality improve by more than 30%, but students also said they found maths problem-solving "clearer in their heads".

⑦ Some may add, "Handwriting is unfair to students with certain learning differences." This worry is fair. Like every skill, it should not be taught in a one-size-fits-all way. Students with dysgraphia or motor difficulties should be offered alternatives — a lap tablet with a touch pen, or a voice-to-text tool for long writing. Handwriting should be encouraged for everyone, but forced on no one beyond what they can reasonably do.

⑧ Finally, there is a softer but very real reason to keep handwriting alive. A handwritten note from a grandmother, a letter from a friend, a birthday card in a familiar hand — these small pieces of paper carry something a typed message does not. They are tiny portraits of the person who wrote them, shaped by the press of a tired hand on a quiet night. When a technology replaces something, we must ask not only "Is it faster?" but "Is something quiet being lost?" In the case of handwriting, the answer is yes.

⑨ Thirty minutes a day. That is all most middle schools would need to keep this steady, careful skill alive. In the noisy age of typing, those thirty minutes are not a return to the past. They are a small, daily practice of patience, memory, and care — three qualities the future will still need, on any kind of keyboard.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion?**

A. Middle schools should stop using keyboards completely.  
B. Typing is better than handwriting in every way.  
C. Handwriting is still quietly powerful; middle schools should protect at least 30 minutes of handwriting practice each school day.  
D. Only Grade 9 students need handwriting.

**2. What did the 2025 Fudan University study find?**

A. Students who took notes by hand remembered 28% more facts a week later and were better at explaining ideas in their own words.  
B. Typing helps students remember more.  
C. There was no difference between the two groups.  
D. Students who typed became more tired.

**3. How does the writer suggest balancing handwriting and typing?**

A. Never type again.  
B. Type for editing and final drafts; write by hand for brainstorming, note-taking, and serious reading.  
C. Write every long report by hand.  
D. Let students choose only one and never use the other.

**4. What did the Shanghai schools report after adding 20 minutes of daily handwriting practice?**

A. Handwriting quality fell by 30%.  
B. Handwriting quality improved by more than 30%, and students felt maths problem-solving was "clearer in their heads".  
C. Students could no longer type.  
D. Students refused to go to school.

**5. Why does the writer mention a handwritten note from a grandmother in paragraph ⑧?**

A. To show that handwritten notes are old and useless.  
B. To say that all grandmothers should learn to type.  
C. To suggest that handwriting carries something personal — tiny portraits of the writer — that a typed message does not.  
D. To criticise old people.

【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "about 28% more facts..."

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题

【答案】B

【答案】C
