# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do Geese Fly in a "V" Shape?**

① Every year in late autumn, somewhere over the fields of northern China, a small group of children will look up at the clear cold sky and see a moving **dark V** high above them — a flock of wild geese flying south for the winter. Twenty or thirty birds, perfectly spaced, one bird at the front of the V, two lines spreading backward behind her like a pair of open scissors. The sight is so common that most of us never really ask the question: **Why a V? Why not a straight line, or a neat square, or a simple cloud of birds?** The answer, it turns out, is one of the quiet small wonders of bird biology — and it is mostly about **saving energy**.

② **Finding one: flying is very, very expensive.** A Canada goose, weighing about **4 kilograms**, burns roughly **8 times more energy per minute when flying than when standing still on a lake**. On a long autumn migration — the bar-headed goose, for example, flies **5,000 kilometres** from Mongolia to India each year — every gram of energy saved matters. If the bird cannot save energy on the way, she will not arrive alive. Evolution, over millions of years, has therefore made geese very good at one thing: **finding ways to fly for less**.

③ **Finding two: every flapping wing leaves behind a small invisible stair.** When a bird's wing pushes the air down, the air does not simply disappear: it curls into two small rotating rivers of air called **wingtip vortices**, one off the end of each wing. Directly behind the wingtip, the air actually moves **upward** — a small invisible rising "stair" in the sky. If a second bird flies in exactly the right spot behind her — not directly behind, but slightly to the side — her own wings can catch this upward-moving air, and she gets a **free push upward**. Her own muscles can, for that moment, do less work. The clever V-shape is simply the **geometry that places every bird except the very first one in the upward air of the bird in front of her**.

④ **How much energy does this actually save?** In **2001**, a French research team (Weimerskirch and colleagues, at the CNRS lab in Chizé) fitted tiny heart-rate monitors to eight trained **white pelicans** flying in V-formation behind a small motorboat. The pelicans flying **behind** another bird had heart rates **11% to 14% lower**, and flapped their wings **about 7% less often**, than a pelican flying alone. A later 2014 study at the University of London, using tiny GPS-loggers on **14 northern bald ibises** migrating in V-formation, went further: the birds were so careful about timing that each wingbeat was **synchronised within 0.15 seconds** with the bird in front, placing each wing exactly inside the upward air stair. These are not accidents. These are **small living aerodynamic calculations**, made by a brain the size of a walnut, twenty times per second.

⑤ **Finding three: the leader at the front of the V has the hardest job.** The front bird gets no upward help; she flies in pure, untouched air. Her heart rate is higher. Her wings beat harder. So geese, quietly and often, **rotate the leader**. The same 2014 ibis study, using GPS trails, found that a single ibis flew at the front of the V for **only about 32% of its time in the air** before another bird moved up and took over. There is no shouted order, no cruel test. The flock, somehow, shares the hardest seat in the sky.

⑥ **Finding four: the V is also a pair of kind eyes.** Research at the University of California, Davis (2018) suggested a second quiet reason for the V: **every bird in the formation can see every other bird** out of the corner of its eye. In a straight line, only two birds can see each other clearly. In a tight square, inside birds cannot see outer birds. In a V, the angle is about **45°**, which keeps each bird visible to all the others. This matters for small steering signals — a tiny head-turn, a small dip of the wings — that warn the flock about wind, cold air, a rising mountain, or a hawk in the distance. **The V is, quietly, a form of silent group conversation.**

⑦ **Do other birds use the V?** Yes — but mostly the heavy, long-wing, long-distance migrators. **Geese, swans, pelicans, cranes, ibises** all use V-formations in some form. **Small songbirds** (sparrows, swallows) do not: their wings are too small and their vortices too weak for the saving to matter. Tiny birds usually fly in **loose, fast-changing clouds** called **murmurations**, which serve different purposes (protection from hawks, not energy saving). So the V is a **heavy bird's** invention.

⑧ **What about the young geese?** A Chinese study from the Qinghai Lake Nature Reserve (2023, 132 young bar-headed geese fitted with light trackers) found that **first-year geese make about 3 to 5 extra metres of position error** compared to adult geese — they do not catch the upward-stair as perfectly. The young goose's heart rate is correspondingly higher on her first migration. But by the following spring, after **one full round trip of 10,000 km**, the same young goose's position error is **down to adult levels**. Flying in V, scientists quietly argue, is therefore **not fully instinct** — it is **instinct plus a careful year of learning**, taught by the older birds.

⑨ **The quiet lesson.** The next time you see a long dark V moving slowly across a cold autumn sky, remember: you are not watching a neat pattern. You are watching **one of the cleverest small engineering solutions in nature** — a solution made without words, shared between twenty careful birds, updated twenty times per second, that saves perhaps **13% of their energy** on a 5,000-km journey. You are watching a small, ancient, almost-invisible piece of cooperation: every bird helping, for a few minutes, every other bird. And when the leader at the front of the V tires, somebody quietly moves up, takes her place, and lets her rest.
