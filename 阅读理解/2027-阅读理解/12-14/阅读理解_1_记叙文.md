# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Afternoon I Walked a Lost Grandmother Home**

① On Saturday afternoon, **11 December 2027, at 15:42**, I was walking back from the small corner library on Nanping Road, carrying two books in a cloth bag and eating a small baked sweet potato, when I saw, in front of Building 7 of our compound, a very small, very quiet grandmother standing alone in the cold wind. She wore a dark blue cotton coat, a thin grey scarf, and black cloth shoes with soft soles. In one hand she held a pink plastic bag with one orange inside; in the other, a small folded paper. She was not walking. She was not looking at a phone. She was simply standing there, her head slightly tilted, as if she were trying to hear a song that was no longer playing.

② I had seen old grandmothers before, many times, in our compound. But there was something quiet in the way she was standing that made my feet slow down. I came closer, carefully. "**Nainai, have you lost your way?**" I asked, in my clearest voice. She looked up at me. Her eyes were cloudy and kind, like an old cup with warm water still inside. "Little girl," she said softly, in a northern accent I did not know well, "I came out to buy an orange. I cannot find Building 3." My compound has **twenty-eight buildings**. There is no Building 3 in our compound. And yet her small paper, when I asked to see it, was handwritten, in very careful blue ink: **"南平路 37 号 院内 3 号楼 302"** — a building in a different compound, **1.4 kilometres** away, across two roads, on the other side of the park.

③ My first thought was to call my father. My second thought was to call the police. But my grandmother — her name was **Ms Chen**, she told me softly, seventy-nine — seemed tired, cold, and a little ashamed. Her hands were shaking. The orange in her pink bag was still cold. I decided, instead, to walk her home myself, slowly, and to call my father only when we arrived safely. I put my two books in her pink bag. I took her thin hand. "Nainai, I live very near you. I will walk with you. Do not be afraid." She smiled, a small, quiet smile, and said nothing, but her thin fingers closed, gratefully, around my wrist.

④ We walked together across the small inner road of our compound, past the dry winter flowerbed where two cats were sleeping in a small patch of sun, past the old security guard Uncle Li who nodded kindly without asking any questions, and out of the east gate. The cold wind pushed the back of her coat. I walked **on the wind side** so the wind reached me first. Her steps were very small — about **25 cm** each — and she had to stop every thirty metres to catch her breath. I did not hurry her. When she stopped, I stopped. When she walked, I walked. I was, for the first time in my life, the person who was not in a hurry.

⑤ At the zebra crossing on Nanping Road, the light was green for only **eighteen seconds**. We were halfway across when the green began to blink. A tall man in a black coat, on a grey electric bicycle, sped toward us from the left. I lifted my free hand firmly — not angry, only clear — and said loudly, "**Please wait; we are slow.**" He braked. He waited. When we reached the other side, he even gave us a small nod of apology. Ms Chen pressed my wrist gently. "Thank you, child," she said. "I could not have crossed alone."

⑥ Past the crossing, we walked along the small winter path through **Xili Park**. The trees were bare. A single man was flying a small red kite. A grandfather was doing slow tai-chi on the stone square, his white breath short and careful. Ms Chen looked at the kite for a long moment and said, "My husband used to fly a kite like that. He died **eight years ago**, on a December afternoon." I did not know what to say. I squeezed her hand lightly and said, "Then let's look at the kite together for one more minute, Nainai." We stood there, two small figures in the cold, watching the small red kite rise and fall in the winter wind.

⑦ At the south gate of **her** compound, a worried-looking middle-aged man was standing at the guard box, speaking quickly into his phone. When he saw us, his face changed completely. He ran forward. "**Mother!**" he cried, in a voice that was at once relieved and guilty. "I was on a work call for twenty minutes — I thought you were upstairs — I—" Ms Chen raised one thin hand to stop him. "This little girl," she said, in a voice that was surprisingly steady, "walked with me, slowly, in the cold. Please thank her properly."

⑧ Her son, whose name was **Mr Chen**, bowed slightly. His eyes were wet. He asked for my name, my school, my parents' phone. He wanted to give me a red envelope. I refused, gently but firmly. "**Please, uncle**," I said, "I walked a grandmother home. That is a very small thing. I don't need anything for that." He looked at me carefully, then bowed again. "Then may I, at least, give you one orange?" He pressed into my hand one small cold orange from his mother's pink bag, the same orange she had walked out to buy. "Tell your parents," he said quietly, "that their daughter is a kind human being."

⑨ That evening, at the dinner table, I told my father and mother the whole story, slowly. My father was silent for a long time. Then he said, "Yiran, tomorrow you and I will walk to Ms Chen's compound together, with a small box of winter pears. We will ring her bell. We will not stay long. We will simply say: we are her neighbours now." I nodded. I ate the small cold orange after dinner. It was the **sweetest** orange I have ever eaten in my life — and the sweetness, I knew quietly, was not really about the orange.

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. How did the writer first notice Ms Chen, and what tiny details made her slow down?**

A. Ms Chen was shouting for help loudly, and the writer stopped because she heard the shouting.  
B. At 15:42 on Saturday 11 December 2027, in front of Building 7 of the writer's compound, Ms Chen was standing alone in the cold wind in a dark blue cotton coat, a thin grey scarf and black cloth shoes, holding a pink plastic bag with one orange and a small folded paper — not walking, not looking at a phone, just standing with her head slightly tilted as if trying to hear a song no longer playing; there was something quiet in the way she was standing that made the writer's feet slow down.  
C. Ms Chen was riding an electric bicycle and nearly crashed.  
D. Ms Chen was sitting on the ground, crying.

**2. Why did the writer decide to walk Ms Chen home herself rather than immediately call her father or the police?**

A. Because the writer was afraid of getting into trouble.  
B. Because Ms Chen refused all help.  
C. Because Ms Chen — seventy-nine years old, named Chen — seemed tired, cold, and a little ashamed, her hands were shaking and the orange in her bag was still cold; the writer decided to walk her home slowly and call her father only after they arrived safely, putting her two books into Ms Chen's pink bag and taking her thin hand.  
D. Because the police station was closed that day.

**3. At the zebra crossing on Nanping Road, what happened and how did the writer respond?**

A. The light stayed green for an hour and no cars came.  
B. The green light lasted only 18 seconds, and when they were halfway across and the green began to blink, a tall man in a black coat on a grey electric bicycle sped toward them from the left; the writer lifted her free hand firmly — not angry, only clear — and said loudly "Please wait; we are slow", the man braked and waited, and even gave them a small nod of apology when they reached the other side.  
C. The writer ran with Ms Chen, dragging her, and they nearly fell.  
D. A police car stopped all traffic.

**4. What does Paragraph ⑥ (in the park) mainly show about the walk?**

A. The writer forced Ms Chen to walk faster.  
B. The writer ignored Ms Chen's feelings.  
C. The writer was bored and wanted to go home.  
D. The walk was not only a physical journey but also a small quiet moment of companionship — looking at the single red kite in the winter air, Ms Chen remembered that her husband had flown a kite like that before he died eight years ago on a December afternoon, and the writer, not knowing what to say, gently squeezed her hand and suggested they look at the kite together for one more minute.

**5. What is the most likely reason the writer refused Ms Chen's son's red envelope but later said that the small cold orange was the sweetest orange she had ever eaten?**

A. Because the writer hated money and loved only oranges.  
B. Because the writer wanted a bigger reward later.  
C. Because for the writer, helping a lost grandmother home was "a very small thing" that did not deserve money, but the orange — the very orange Ms Chen had walked out to buy and could not find her way home with — carried a quiet meaning of gratitude, trust and human warmth, so the sweetness she tasted was not really about the fruit; and her father's suggestion to visit Ms Chen with winter pears the next day confirmed that the kindness was mutual and belonged to a human bond, not to any payment.  
D. Because she did not know how to open a red envelope.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①②段开头  
【解析】"15:42 / 7号楼 / 一个橙子 / 小纸条 / 头微倾"直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段决定自己送  
【解析】"累冷羞愧 / 手抖 / 先送再告父亲"直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段斑马线  
【解析】"18 秒绿灯 / 电瓶车 / 举手 + 请等 / 刹车 + 致歉"直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑥段公园段  
【解析】"红风筝 / 丈夫 8 年前去世 / 一起多看一分钟"推理出陪伴与共情主旨。A、B、C 均偏离。故选 D。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】推理判断题 / 主旨大意题  
【定位】第⑧⑨段红包与橙子  
【解析】"不要红包 / 橙子承载信任 / 次日带冬梨回访"推理出"甜不在橙子"主旨。A、B、D 均偏离。故选 C。
