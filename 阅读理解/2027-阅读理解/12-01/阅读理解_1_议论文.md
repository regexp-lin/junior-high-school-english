# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle-School Student Learn Basic Sign-Language "Hello"?**

① Last Wednesday a **deaf delivery man** came into our school's front office to hand a parcel to the guard. He was about twenty-five years old, neat, careful, and smiling. He opened a small notebook and pointed to a pre-written Chinese sentence: *"Is this the right address?"* The guard, a kind man in his late fifties, blushed slightly and did not know what to do. Three students who were passing by, including me, stood still for a moment. **Not one of us knew how to say "hello" with our hands.** We wrote the answer in pencil on the same notebook, and the delivery man bowed politely and left. That small silence stayed with me for days. This is my argument: **every middle-school student should learn a short, basic "hello in sign language" course — about ten lessons, fifteen signs, one term**. Nothing more, nothing less.

② My first reason is **scale**. China has, by the 2025 census, about **27 million people with hearing loss**, of whom roughly 2 million are profoundly deaf. That is more than the entire population of some provinces. Yet almost no middle-school graduate can say "hello", "thank you", "excuse me", "I'm a student", or "please wait — I'll call a teacher" with a pair of hands. A world in which 27 million neighbours share the same city but cannot be greeted by their hearing classmates is, quietly, a smaller world than it needs to be. Ten short lessons would not close that gap. But they would leave a small door open.

③ My second reason is **simplicity**. We are not asking students to become interpreters. Fifteen signs is not a heavy load: *hello, thank you, sorry, please, slow down, water, toilet, teacher, student, help, okay, name, friend, goodbye, I love you.* In a single term, with one 35-minute lesson a week, any student can learn these. The Beijing Sign-Language Research Centre, in a 2024 report on similar trial classes in 18 junior high schools, found students **retained 11–12 of 15 signs** six months later, even without much practice. The signs are like small waves on the dock: easy to lift, easy to keep.

④ My third reason is that **signs teach something hands do not usually know**. When we talk, 60–70% of meaning travels by voice; the hand waves around vaguely in the background. In sign language, the hand carries the whole sentence. You cannot be lazy. You must **face the person**, **look at their face**, **match your expression to the meaning** of the sign. Our PSHE teacher, who quietly learned sign language on a summer course two years ago, told us last week: *"After a single month of sign class, a student's eye contact in normal Chinese conversation becomes clearly warmer."* The course teaches how to be present, not only how to be kind.

⑤ Others argue, "Most of us will rarely meet a deaf person." This is statistically wrong, but let me answer it on its own terms. You may rarely meet a deaf person at our age. But over **the next seventy years of your adult life**, how many deaf neighbours, delivery workers, shop assistants, engineers, or classmates' parents will you meet? Probably more than a few hundred. A single "hello" in sign, given warmly, is worth more to that person than ten gold stars on your report card. Learning a small skill for a few short moments across a long life is not a bad trade.

⑥ Another argument: "Sign language is different in different regions. Why bother?" Partly true. Chinese Sign Language has a national standard today, and about 85% of everyday signs are now shared across provinces. The basic fifteen we propose are **on the shared list**. Regional variation exists, but mostly for specialised words (place names, job titles). For "hello, thank you, help," you and a deaf friend in Guangzhou or Harbin will understand each other. And, more importantly, **the willingness to try** — your hands rising in a slow, careful wave — is universally understood, even by hearing people.

⑦ "But the school timetable is already full," I can hear teachers say. Our proposal is modest: **ten 35-minute lessons, once a week for one term**, shared between the Chinese department and the PSHE department. No exam, no grade on the report card. A small "hand-wave certificate" at the end, nothing more. If Grade 7 is too busy, the course can be placed in Grade 8 spring. The total cost of the course to the school: **about 6 hours of total class time per student**, plus one teacher's training in the summer. That is not nothing; it is also not very much.

⑧ The most quiet and serious worry is this: "Will a short course not encourage shallow tokenism — students who 'know fifteen signs' but still treat deaf people as curiosities?" This is fair. The answer is careful teaching. Every lesson should include a **three-minute real clip** (with permission) of a young deaf person at school, at work, at home; and one honest discussion about **how not to stare, how not to take photos, how not to turn a neighbour into a little spectacle**. The course, taught well, is not just about signs. It is about **manners in the age of many different kinds of people**. A respectful hello is already part of that.

⑨ On Tuesday, I will bring our class's handwritten short letter to the Chinese-department office. We are asking for one quiet term, ten short lessons, fifteen honest signs, one small certificate. **A school that can say "hello" gently to 10% of its neighbours it has so far been unable to greet is, very simply, a slightly better school.** The guard last Wednesday did his best; the delivery man smiled and walked out kindly. Next time, we hope, a student will step forward, lift her right hand slowly to her forehead, and sign, carefully and warmly, the one small word: **nǐ hǎo — hello**.
