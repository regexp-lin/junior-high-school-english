# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Our School Plant a Vegetable Garden for Students?**

① In the north-east corner of our school, there is a small piece of empty ground. It is about the size of two basketball courts. For years, it has grown only weeds and a few stray cats. Last month, the Students' Union proposed a bold new plan: **turn it into a student vegetable garden**, managed by Grade 7 and Grade 8 students together with biology teachers. Since then, the idea has been carefully, and sometimes warmly, debated.

② Those who **support** the garden give three clear reasons. First, a garden is a **living classroom**. Biology textbooks talk about photosynthesis, soil, and water cycles; a garden lets students **see, touch, and smell** these ideas. A tomato plant teaches more in two months than ten pages of notes. Second, gardening can quietly train **patience and responsibility**. A little tomato needs daily water and a careful eye. A student who cares for a plant for ten weeks, watching it grow from a seedling to a red fruit, will feel proud — and also tired in a good way. Third, the school canteen can use part of the harvest; the other part can be donated to **community elderly homes**, turning the garden into a small bridge between the school and the people who need a bit more care.

③ However, many careful voices also worry. Middle school students are **very busy**. Adding a garden chore may feel like another drop of water in an already full cup. A garden also needs **money and time** — seeds, tools, water pipes, and teachers who understand plants. If the garden is not well managed, it could turn into a weedy mess that makes the school look even worse. Some parents also point out that **not every child enjoys soil** or bends down easily, and forcing them to dig and weed may create unhappy memories of "garden work".

④ I think the best answer is a **carefully designed middle way**. The school does not need to turn the whole empty ground into a garden at once. Let us start with **four small beds** — 2 × 3 metres each. Each Grade 7 and Grade 8 class can adopt one bed as an **"optional" project**, open to students who truly want to try. Teachers of biology can guide the weekly work; students can keep a photo diary and share it on the school newspaper. After one term, the school can collect feedback and decide to make the garden bigger, smaller, or to try a new design.

⑤ Many things in life start on a small piece of empty ground. A middle school garden is not about becoming farmers; it is about **touching a handful of warm soil** and learning that food — like knowledge, friendship, and time — does not come from a shop, but from patience, care, and sun. Even if only a few students join in the first year, the soft green colour they bring into our corner will make the whole school breathe a little easier.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the main question the article discusses?**

A. Whether the school should hire more biology teachers.  
B. Whether the school should plant a student vegetable garden.  
C. Whether the Students' Union should change its leader.  
D. Whether the canteen food is healthy.

**2. Which of the following is NOT a reason given by supporters?**

A. A garden is a living classroom for biology ideas.  
B. Gardening trains patience and responsibility.  
C. The harvest may be shared with elderly homes.  
D. A garden can instantly raise every student's exam score.

**3. According to paragraph ③, what do the worried voices say?**

A. The students' union knows nothing about plants.  
B. Middle school students are already busy, and a garden needs money and time.  
C. There is no empty ground in any school in the city.  
D. Gardens are forbidden by the city rules.

**4. What is the "middle way" suggested by the writer?**

A. Turn the whole empty corner into a garden at once.  
B. Cancel the idea completely.  
C. Start with four small beds as an optional project and check feedback after one term.  
D. Let only Grade 9 students manage the garden.

**5. What is the writer's main message in the last paragraph?**

A. The school should train students to become farmers.  
B. Touching a little soil can teach patience, care, and the meaning of food.  
C. Empty ground should always stay empty.  
D. Only large gardens are meaningful.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段末 "turn it into a student vegetable garden"  
【解析】议题为是否建校园菜园。A、C、D 非主题。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节否定题  
【定位】第②段三条理由：活教材、耐心责任、社区分享  
【解析】"立即提升每位学生成绩"非文中理由，过度。A、B、C 都是。故选 D。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段学生繁忙 + 开销时间问题  
【解析】学生日程紧、资源需求大。A、C、D 无依据。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】作者观点题  
【定位】第④段 "start with four small beds... collect feedback"  
【解析】小规模试点+评估。A、B、D 与作者建议不符。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨推断题  
【定位】第⑤段 "touching a handful of warm soil... patience, care, and sun"  
【解析】触及泥土所启示的生活态度。A、C、D 与主旨不符。故选 B。
