# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Use AI Chat Tools for Homework?**

① In 2027, it is hard to find a middle-school student who has never opened an **AI chat tool**. You type in a question — "Write me a 100-word diary about my weekend" or "Explain this maths problem step by step" — and in two seconds a clean, friendly answer appears on the screen. Some students say AI is like a patient teacher who never gets tired. Other teachers say AI is a "smart copy machine" that will harm real learning. So, **should middle-school students be allowed to use AI chat tools when doing homework?** My answer is: yes, but only under careful rules.

② Those who support AI for homework usually give three reasons. First, AI can explain difficult ideas in different ways. If one explanation is hard, you can ask again in simpler words. Second, it is always **available**: at 10 p.m. when your teacher is asleep, AI is still there. Third, a good AI can act like a dictionary, a grammar-checker and a small coach all in one, which is helpful especially for students whose parents cannot help them in maths or English.

③ Those who are against this have equally strong reasons. They worry that some students simply copy the AI's answer and hand it in, learning almost nothing. They point out that AI sometimes gives **wrong** answers that look correct, because AI speaks in a very confident tone. Most seriously, they say that thinking is a muscle: if we let AI do all the thinking, our own minds will grow weak.

④ Both sides are describing real things. So the question is not "should AI exist?" — it already exists — but "**how should middle-school students use it?**". In my opinion, the answer lies in three simple rules.

⑤ The first rule is: **think first, ask AI second**. Before I open any AI tool, I should spend at least a few minutes trying the problem with paper and pencil. I should write down what I already understand, what I do not understand, and what my first answer is. Only after this should I turn to AI — and I should ask for an **explanation**, not for a finished answer. This keeps my own brain working.

⑥ The second rule is: **never hand in AI's words as my own**. If AI gives me an English sample diary, I should read it, understand the new phrases, close the page, and then write my own diary in my own words. My diary may be less "beautiful" than AI's, but it is really mine. Passing off another mind's work as yours, whether the mind is a classmate or an AI, is a kind of **cheating**.

⑦ The third rule is: **check AI, do not trust it blindly**. AI can confidently tell you that Qinghai Lake is in the south of China, or that a chemical reaction happens at a wrong temperature. Every answer from AI should be double-checked against a textbook, a trusted website, or a teacher. Becoming a good "checker" is itself a modern school skill.

⑧ Parents and teachers also have a job here. Instead of banning AI completely, schools can offer a short class on **how to use AI wisely** — what to ask, how to ask, and how to check. A blanket ban often just sends the tool into a "secret mode", where nobody is guiding the students at all.

⑨ So should we use AI for homework? We should. But using AI wisely is itself a new kind of homework for our generation. The clever student of 2027 is not the one who lets AI do the work, but the one who can **think with AI, check AI, and still write in his own voice**. That is the real skill for the years ahead.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main position on using AI chat tools for homework?**

A. Students should never use AI tools.  
B. Students should use AI for every single task, large or small.  
C. Students may use AI, but only under careful rules and habits.  
D. Only Grade 9 students should be allowed to use AI.

**2. According to the passage, which is **NOT** a reason given by AI supporters?**

A. AI can explain ideas in different, simpler ways.  
B. AI is available at times when teachers are asleep.  
C. AI acts like a dictionary, a grammar-checker and a coach together.  
D. AI can replace the need for any teacher at school.

**3. What do the people **against** AI homework mainly worry about?**

A. That AI is too expensive for middle-school families.  
B. That students will copy AI's answers without thinking, and that AI's confident tone can hide wrong answers.  
C. That AI will stop working for long hours.  
D. That AI will become too slow and students will get bored.

**4. Which of the following is one of the writer's "three simple rules" for using AI?**

A. Ask AI first and think later.  
B. Trust everything AI says, especially when it sounds confident.  
C. Before opening AI, try the problem yourself with paper and pencil, and then ask AI for explanation, not a finished answer.  
D. Always ask a classmate to use AI for you.

**5. What does the writer suggest schools and teachers do about AI?**

A. Completely ban AI tools and check every student's phone every day.  
B. Offer a short class on how to use AI wisely, so the tool is used in the open, not in a secret mode.  
C. Replace teachers with AI programs.  
D. Pay every student to use AI as much as possible.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段末 "yes, but only under careful rules" & 第⑨段 "using AI wisely is itself a new kind of homework"  
【解析】作者立场是谨慎、有规则地使用。A、B、D 均与立场不符。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段三条支持理由  
【解析】支持方理由包括：多种解释、随时可用、多功能小助手；并未说"替代学校老师"。A、B、C 均是文中提及的支持理由。故选 D。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "some students simply copy ... AI sometimes gives wrong answers that look correct"  
【解析】反对方主要担心照抄和 AI 自信给错。A、C、D 均无文中依据。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "think first, ask AI second ... I should turn to AI — and I should ask for an explanation, not for a finished answer"  
【解析】第一条规则即为 C 所述。A 与规则相反；B 与第⑦条检查规则相反；D 属无中生有。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑧段 "schools can offer a short class on how to use AI wisely ... A blanket ban often just sends the tool into a 'secret mode'"  
【解析】作者反对全面封禁，主张开设使用指导课。A 与作者反对"全面封禁"相反；C、D 均属无中生有。故选 B。
