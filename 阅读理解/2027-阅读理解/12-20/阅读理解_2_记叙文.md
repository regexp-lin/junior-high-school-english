# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Saturday I Returned a Lost Wallet at Nanping Subway Station**

① On Saturday afternoon, **18 December 2027, at 16:05**, I was standing on the down-escalator of **Nanping Subway Station, Exit B**, on my way home from my English weekend class, when a small, flat object slid slowly past my left shoe and came to rest between the silver steps of the escalator. I looked down. It was a **dark brown leather wallet**, perhaps eight centimetres wide, a little bit worn at the corners, caught on the very edge of a silver step so that it was about to go down into the mechanism at the bottom. In three more steps of the escalator, it would be sucked into the gears. I bent quickly — the way my mother had taught me to bend on an escalator, **knees first, not waist first** — and picked it up. My fingers trembled a little. I did not open it. I stepped off the escalator at the bottom and walked straight toward the yellow-vested staff member at the exit gate. Her name tag said **Ms Lu**. She was about thirty.

② "**Aunt Lu**," I said, holding the wallet up carefully with both hands, "I found this on the escalator. I did not open it. It was going to fall into the gears." She looked at me. She looked at the wallet. Her face became both serious and kind at the same time. She took out a small black walkie-talkie and said three careful sentences to someone. Then she led me to the **Lost-and-Found counter** on the west side of the gate. "Little girl," she said, "thank you. Come. We will open it together, calmly, with two witnesses. This is the rule."

③ A tall man in a dark blue **railway uniform**, Ms Lu's shift leader, came over. Ms Lu opened the small glass cabinet under the counter, took out a **white paper form**, and laid it flat between us. Only then did she open the wallet, very slowly, with me and the shift leader watching. Inside: one **ID card** (the photo showed a friendly-looking man, **Mr Huang Daming**, 45 years old), **three bank cards**, **¥680 in small notes** (in one hundred, fifty, and twenty yuan denominations), a **crumpled single photo** of a little girl about six years old (probably his daughter), and, tucked behind the ID, a **small folded pink paper** that looked important — the edges were thin from being re-folded many times.

④ Ms Lu wrote on the form, in careful blue ink, every item, exactly as I was watching: **1 ID card · 3 bank cards · ¥680 in cash · 1 family photo · 1 small folded pink paper** — then added, below, **"Found by Grade-8 student from Lihua Middle School, 16:05, on Exit B escalator by Yiran (age 14)."** She asked me to write, next to her handwriting, a single line in my own hand: *"I did not touch the contents of the wallet before the counter opened it."* I signed my name. I felt, quietly, the unfamiliar small dignity of a person being trusted.

⑤ The shift leader tried to call Mr Huang's number, printed on the back of his staff card. No answer — perhaps his phone was also in the lost wallet. Ms Lu then made a short announcement over the station's public speakers, in a calm clear voice: *"Passenger Huang Daming, please come to the Lost-and-Found counter near Exit B. A wallet has been handed in."* She repeated it twice. I stood on a small red rubber mat behind the counter, drinking a small paper cup of warm water that Ms Lu had gently handed me, watching the thin crowd of winter passengers move past.

⑥ **Fourteen minutes** went by. I was about to tell Ms Lu I should go home — my father would be worried by 17:00 — when, from Exit A, a tall man in a grey coat came **running** toward the counter. His face was white. His hair was slightly wet with snow. I recognised him instantly from the ID photo: it was Mr Huang. He did not stop running until he reached the counter. He placed both hands on the counter edge. He was breathing hard. He said, in a half-broken voice, *"My wallet — please — my wallet — I — oh my."*

⑦ Ms Lu asked him three careful verification questions, slowly: *"Can you tell me, sir, roughly how much cash was inside?"* He said, his eyes thinking, *"Six hundred... eighty. I think. In notes, mostly fifties."* Correct. *"Besides bank cards, was there any photo inside?"* He said, his voice breaking a little, *"One photo. My daughter Jiajia. She was six in the photo. She turned fifteen last month."* Correct. *"And is there any other paper inside that is important to you?"* He was silent for a moment. Then he said, very quietly, *"A small folded pink paper. It is — it is my late wife's handwriting. She wrote it for me before — before she left us. I carry it everywhere. Please tell me it is there."* Ms Lu opened the wallet. She showed him the small folded pink paper. His whole body, standing at the counter, suddenly trembled. He placed his forehead on his own fist for about ten seconds. He did not cry. He did not speak. I did not move.

⑧ When he lifted his face again, he looked for me. The shift leader pointed quietly. Mr Huang walked the three small steps toward me, took a careful step, bowed to me once — not a small polite bow, but a **full, slow, serious bow of the waist** — and said, in a steady voice, *"Child, thank you. Thank you for not opening the paper. Thank you for bringing the wallet to this counter in thirty seconds. Thank you for being fourteen years old and for noticing what was about to be eaten by a machine."* Then he reached into his coat pocket, took out his own phone, and asked, *"May I, at least, know the name of your school and your class, so that I may write a short, calm letter to your teacher?"* I nodded. I gave him my school and my class.

⑨ He did not offer me money, nor did he try to hand me a red envelope. He only stood beside me and Ms Lu at the counter for three more minutes, filling in the "owner" section of the white paper form. He signed it. He wrote down his phone number twice — once for the station, once for me, "only for if you, Yiran, ever need a careful friend of your family in this city." Then he walked slowly, with the wallet in both hands, toward Exit A. Before he reached the stairs, he turned once more, looked back at me, and lifted his hand in a small quiet wave. The small pink paper, I could see, was still safely inside his wallet.

⑩ At **17:02**, Ms Lu walked me to Exit B herself. She said, as we reached the last few steps up to the winter sky, *"Yiran, today you did a small thing. But a small thing, done by a fourteen-year-old in thirty seconds, can hold together a whole man's memory of his wife. Do not forget this. And do not boast about it either."* I did not know what to say. I only nodded. Above us, the winter sky was turning a deep orange. It was the most beautiful ride home I had ever had.
