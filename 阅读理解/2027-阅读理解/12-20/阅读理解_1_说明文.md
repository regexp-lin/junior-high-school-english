# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Are Winter Sunsets So Orange?**

① On a clear winter afternoon, somewhere between **16:40 and 17:20** in northern China, you may step to a west-facing window and notice, almost with small surprise, that the sun hanging low over the rooftops is not its usual noon **white-yellow**. It is **deep orange**, sometimes even a quiet **red-pink**. The sky around it glows in soft ribbons of peach, salmon, and violet. Children on the pavement below stop for a moment to look up. Adults quietly take out their phones. The whole world, for **nine or ten minutes**, seems to be painted by a careful hand. The strange thing is: the sun itself is exactly the same temperature and brightness it was at noon. The sky is the same sky. Only the **angle** has changed. This short, beautiful winter show is one of the clearest demonstrations of a small physics rule called **atmospheric scattering** — first measured carefully by the British scientist **Lord Rayleigh in 1871**.

② **Finding one: white sunlight is secretly a mixture.** Sunlight looks white to our eyes, but when it passes through a glass prism (or a raindrop), it separates into the familiar seven-colour band of the rainbow: red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet. Each colour travels as a light wave with a different **wavelength** (the distance between two wave crests). Red light has the **longest** wavelength — about **700 nanometres**. Violet light has the **shortest** — about **400 nanometres**. Blue light is close to the violet end, at about **450 nanometres**. This tiny size difference, no bigger than the width of a few molecules, turns out to matter enormously when sunlight meets the thin clear gas we call **air**.

③ **Finding two: short blue waves scatter more than long red waves.** Earth's atmosphere is about **99%** nitrogen and oxygen molecules, each one roughly **0.3 nanometres** across. When a ray of sunlight enters the atmosphere, the tiny gas molecules act like extremely small invisible bumpers. A simple physics calculation (done first by Rayleigh) shows that the **amount of scattering goes up very sharply** as wavelength goes down — precisely as **1 divided by the fourth power of the wavelength**. In plain numbers, **violet and blue light scatter roughly 10 times more strongly than red light**. So the short blue-violet part of sunlight gets bounced in every direction, while the longer red-orange part continues almost straight. This is **exactly why the daytime sky is blue**: the blue that your eye sees is scattered sunlight reaching you from every empty bit of sky, not from the sun itself.

④ **Finding three: at sunset, sunlight travels through much more air.** At noon, when the sun is directly overhead, sunlight passes through about **100 km** of effective atmosphere before reaching you. But at sunset, when the sun is nearly on the horizon, the light must travel **almost sideways through the thick lower atmosphere**, a path of around **1,200 km** — **twelve times longer**. On this long sideways journey, something quietly dramatic happens: **all the blue and green light gets scattered out of the ray** long before it reaches your eye. What remains is mostly the **yellow, orange, and red** — the long-wavelength survivors that did not scatter. By the time the sunlight lands on the west-facing window, it has been, in a sense, **filtered** by 1,200 km of air into a warm late-afternoon orange.

⑤ **Why is winter orange especially rich?** Because cold, dry winter air is **unusually clean**. Warm, humid summer air is full of tiny water droplets, pollen, and small organic dust — all of which scatter **all colours fairly evenly**, making summer sunsets a duller milky-orange. Cold winter air, by contrast, has very few of these large particles. The long sunset path is then dominated by **only Rayleigh scattering** — which is sharply favourable to reds and oranges. A 2021 Peking University atmospheric science study of **3,204 winter and summer days** in eight Chinese cities measured afternoon sky colour and found a **17% richer red-orange intensity** in clean winter sunsets than in humid summer sunsets of matched brightness. Winter, quietly, is the **clean season for sunlight**.

⑥ **What about the pink-salmon ribbons in the sky above the sun?** Those are also explained by scattering, with one extra twist. The sunset light that passes just **above** the sun — not straight at your eye, but at a small angle — scatters one more time off the dust and ice crystals high in the **upper atmosphere**, redirecting part of its warm colours down toward you. The higher, thinner ice clouds (**cirrus** clouds, made of tiny ice needles at 8–12 km altitude) act as a high kind mirror, turning part of the orange-red into softer pink. That is why the **prettiest** winter sunsets are often the ones with a thin wispy cloud layer; a completely empty sky gives only the red-orange of the sun itself, which is beautiful but simpler.

⑦ **What about the strange green flash?** On very clean, dry, flat horizons (rare over cities, more common at sea), in the last half-second before the sun disappears, an observer may see a **tiny green flash** at the very top edge of the sun. This is not a myth. It is the result of the atmosphere acting as a weak prism: the last edge of the sun bends its colours slightly, and because blue has already been scattered away, the next-shortest survivor — green — briefly peeks out on top. Jules Verne wrote a whole 1882 novel about it. A 2012 photographic study on the Chilean coast caught the green flash in **2.3%** of clean sunsets.

⑧ **Why don't winter sunrises look the same as winter sunsets?** They actually do — in physics. The same long atmospheric path and the same Rayleigh scattering happen at sunrise. But sunrise often looks **slightly paler orange** for a small human reason: in the early morning the air is often full of **fine cold water-vapour fog** that hasn't yet burned off, and this fog scatters colours a bit more evenly than clean afternoon air. That is why poets often find **winter sunsets more vivid** — not because sunset sunlight is different, but because afternoon winter air is usually cleaner than morning winter air.

⑨ **The quiet lesson.** The next time the afternoon sky in late December turns, for about ten careful minutes, that astonishing peach-orange-pink, remember: you are not watching magic. You are watching, with your own eyes, **a 1,200 km journey of sunlight through a clear cold atmosphere, in which blue and violet have been quietly bounced away and only red, orange, and yellow have survived the trip.** The physics is older than the human species; the sight is new every evening. And for ten kind minutes, as the sun dips into its horizon of bare winter trees, you are the person for whom 150 million km of travelling light made its final, softest, slowest bend.
