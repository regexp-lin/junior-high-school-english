# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Boy Who Ran with a Cardboard Medal**

On the day of the school sports meeting, the sun felt a little unfair. By ten o'clock, the running track was already hot enough to soften the bottoms of our shoes. I was there as a reporter for the school magazine, not as a runner. My job was to write one story. Just one. And it had to be "the best story of the day".

The boys' 800-metre race was the last event before lunch. Everyone expected Jiajun to win. He was tall, fast, and his long legs made him look like a pair of scissors cutting through the wind. His main rival was Wenqi, a short, thin boy in our class. Nobody expected much from Wenqi. His running shoes were old. His T-shirt had been washed so many times that you could barely read the school name on it. Still, he had trained every single evening after class for two whole months.

The race started. Jiajun took the lead at once. Wenqi stayed in the fourth place, his small legs moving very fast. "Stay calm, save energy," our teacher had always said, and Wenqi seemed to remember it. Lap two: third place. Lap three: second place. With 150 metres left, something in him changed. His eyes became very sharp, as if he had seen a door that nobody else could see. He passed Jiajun on the outside and crossed the line first.

But here is where the story becomes strange. The school had bought only three big shiny medals — gold, silver and bronze — in a hurry from the nearby shop. When the head teacher checked the drawer before the ceremony, the silver medal was missing. Somebody had borrowed it for a school poster last week and forgotten to return it. The teacher's face went red.

"Use the cardboard one," a young teacher whispered. Earlier in the morning, some junior students had painted a practice medal from an old pizza box. It was round, silver-coloured on one side, and simply lovely. A thin cotton string hung from its top.

When the ceremony began, Jiajun received the gold and waved to the crowd. Then the head teacher called, "**Second place — Wenqi.**" Everyone grew quiet when they saw the cardboard medal, painted by a child. Some students giggled. A few pointed. Jiajun's friend even called out, "A cardboard prize!"

Wenqi climbed onto the small wooden box, bowed, and took the medal. I was holding my notebook, ready to write the word "disappointment". But he did something I did not expect. He lifted the cardboard medal high above his head, looked at it carefully for a long second, then pressed it to his chest and said to the microphone, "**Thank you. I ran for two months. This is exactly what it is worth.**"

The whole ground went silent. Then somebody — I think it was a girl from Grade 7 — began to clap. The clap spread like a small wave, then a bigger one, then a sea. Jiajun stepped off his gold-medal box and shook Wenqi's hand. The head teacher wiped his eyes quickly.

That evening, I sat down to write my article. I did not write about Jiajun's speed, or the hot track, or the lost silver medal. I wrote about a boy who had turned a piece of pizza-box cardboard into something that looked, in that one shining moment, more valuable than real silver.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What was the writer's role at the sports meeting?**

A. A runner in the 800-metre race.  
B. A reporter for the school magazine.  
C. A teacher in charge of medals.  
D. A parent taking photos.

**2. How did Wenqi finally win the race?**

A. He ran in first place from the beginning.  
B. He pushed Jiajun off the track.  
C. He stayed calm, moved up gradually, and passed Jiajun with 150 metres left.  
D. He took a short-cut across the middle of the field.

**3. Why was the silver medal replaced with a cardboard one?**

A. The school had no money left.  
B. The real silver medal was lost — someone had borrowed it for a poster.  
C. The teacher wanted to punish Wenqi.  
D. Students asked for a cardboard medal.

**4. What did Wenqi do with the cardboard medal at the ceremony?**

A. He threw it on the ground.  
B. He laughed and returned it.  
C. He lifted it high, pressed it to his chest, and said it was worth what he had worked for.  
D. He gave it back to Jiajun.

**5. What does the story mainly tell us?**

A. Real medals are always better than paper ones.  
B. Running is the only way to be famous.  
C. The value of a prize depends on the effort behind it, not on the material.  
D. Reporters should always write about fast runners.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"there as a reporter for the school magazine"  
【解析】校刊记者。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"Lap two: third place. Lap three: second place... passed Jiajun"  
【解析】逐圈超越，冲刺反超。A/B/D 均错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"Somebody had borrowed it for a school poster..."  
【解析】银牌被借走未归还。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"lifted the cardboard medal high... pressed it to his chest..."  
【解析】高举、贴胸、发表感言。A/B/D 均错。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】全文  
【解析】奖牌之价在于付出。A/B/D 均偏离。故选 C。
