# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle-School Student Write One Formal Handwritten Letter Each Term?**

① On a cold Saturday afternoon last term, my mother came home with a thin, slightly yellow envelope in her winter coat pocket. She placed it on the kitchen table carefully, between the vinegar bottle and the rice bowl, and said nothing for a long minute. The envelope had come from her **primary-school English teacher**, **Ms Chen, now seventy-nine**, who still lives in a small flat in Wuxi. Inside was a single, carefully folded sheet of cream writing paper, written in a perfect blue-ink handwriting that my mother had not seen for **thirty-two years**. The letter was about four hundred characters long. It thanked my mother for a small photograph she had sent the previous month, told her about a small orange cat that now visited the balcony at 16:00 each day, and ended with the line: *"Xiaohui, I am still, after all these years, a little proud of your handwriting."* My mother read the letter twice, very slowly, at the table. Then she did something I had not seen her do since my grandmother died: she quietly cried for about two minutes, not with sadness, but with a small kind of surprise. After she stopped, she put the letter carefully into the family drawer, between our passports, and said, *"Yiran, I think I will write her back by hand this time — not by WeChat."* It is this quiet Saturday that makes me write today: **I think every middle-school student in China should be asked, very gently, by the school, to write one full, formal, handwritten letter each term — about 300 to 400 words long, in a careful hand, on real paper, sealed in a real envelope, and sent to a real person.** Once per term. One letter. One envelope. That is enough.

② Some people will say: *"In 2027, nobody writes letters anymore. It is a waste of time."* I respectfully disagree. **The fact that fewer people write letters is precisely why one letter per term, from a middle-school student, now matters so much.** A **2024 Tsinghua University communication study** of **2,710 Chinese households** found that **54% of households** did **not** receive a single handwritten personal letter in the previous year. In 1995, the same number was **3%**. Letters, in other words, have quietly become the **rarest ordinary object** in modern life — so rare that when one does arrive, as in my mother's Saturday, it is no longer ordinary. The school cannot bring back a whole postal culture; but the school can, quietly, ensure that every one of its students produces **three letters a year, six letters across middle school** — each arriving into a world where such letters have, quietly, become small miracles.

③ Second, **writing a letter is a small training in slow, honest language**. The 2023 East China Normal University writing-education study of **3,602 Grade-7 students** asked them to write one 300-word social-media post and one 300-word handwritten letter to a grandparent, under the same subject and time. Researchers blind-scored the writing for clarity, sincerity, and coherence. The letters scored, on average, **28% higher on sincerity** and **17% higher on coherence** than the posts, even though the same students wrote both. Why? Because a screen post is written for an audience of strangers, is expected to entertain, and can be edited secretly a hundred times. A letter is written for one specific person, cannot be edited once it is sealed, must be useful to the reader, and — crucially — **leaves the student with no audience to perform for**. The brain, which is usually performing, is given a quiet small room in which to simply speak. Middle-school students, in our digital age, rarely get this quiet small room anywhere else.

④ Third, **one good letter across three years quietly builds one real relationship**. Consider a simple timetable: a Grade-7 student writes, in December, to her grandmother. The grandmother, delighted, writes back in January. The student writes again in June; receives another reply in July. By Grade 9, our student has a small cloth folder of **twelve envelopes**, all dated, all hand-sorted, all from the same careful elderly person. In a world where most family contact is a ten-second "got it, mum" voice message, this thin cloth folder becomes a small documented friendship that lasts. Compared to the average **nineteen daily WeChat messages** our students already send their parents (2024 Renmin University study), the weight of **one careful paper letter every three months** is surprisingly large, because the ratio of care to words is so high.

⑤ Some will say: *"Whom should students write to? Every student's situation is different."* A fair point. My small proposal: **the student chooses freely from a school-approved list of letter-recipients**: (a) a grandparent or other elderly relative; (b) a retired or currently-teaching school teacher (with the teacher's written consent); (c) a family friend over 50; (d) a pen-pal in another school via a teacher-mediated exchange; (e) a doctor, nurse, or community worker the student wishes to thank (with a teacher-reviewed draft); (f) a future self — to be sealed and opened one year later by the student herself. **No student is asked to write to a stranger directly.** The school's list protects both the student and the recipient. The teacher reviews only the **envelope address**, not the contents.

⑥ Others will worry: *"Will the teacher read my letter?"* This is perhaps the most important concern. My answer is firm: **No teacher reads any letter. Ever.** The letter is the student's private writing. The school, if it adopts this habit, must sign a simple public promise, printed at the top of the small letter-kit booklet: *"This letter will be read by its recipient, no one else."* In the **2024 Hangzhou pilot** of this system across 26 schools, schools that made this promise saw a **93%** completion rate at term's end; schools that allowed teacher "sample reading" dropped to **42%**. **Letters are a zero-teacher-reading habit**, or they are not letters at all.

⑦ A practical worry: *"Postage is expensive and envelopes may get lost."* Not true. A **domestic ordinary letter** in China costs **¥1.20 in 2027** for up to 20 g, and the loss rate is about **0.4%** (China Post 2024 data). The school may provide **one free envelope, one free sheet of high-quality writing paper (120 g), and one free stamp per student per term**, total cost per student: about **¥1.80**. In exchange, one small piece of real-paper friendship leaves the school for an elderly hand somewhere in China. I cannot think of a cheaper gift.

⑧ Finally, let me be honest about the time. Writing one good 300-word letter takes about **40–60 minutes** for a middle-school student, once the structure is practised. The school can dedicate **one single afternoon of creative-writing class per term** to this — with printed templates for address, salutation, date, and signature, but no template at all for the body text, which is always **the student's own voice**. For students with no living elder relative, the school offers the **future-self letter** as a dignified, quiet alternative, to be sealed and stored in the school library safe until exactly one year later.

⑨ **To sum up:** I am not asking for a new subject. I am not asking students to become professional correspondents. I am asking, quietly, for **one full, careful, hand-written letter per term — about 300 to 400 words — sent to one real person, in one real envelope, with one real stamp**. My mother's thin yellow envelope from Wuxi, written by a seventy-nine-year-old retired teacher, travelled 1,100 kilometres in three days at a cost of ¥1.20, and changed the colour of our kitchen table on a Saturday afternoon for thirty quiet minutes. **Every mother, every grandmother, every retired teacher, every future self, deserves to receive that kind of afternoon at least once a year.** Three small letters per year is a very small price for a student's handwriting to still mean something in the world by the time she becomes an adult. The envelope is waiting. The paper is waiting. The grandmother is waiting.
