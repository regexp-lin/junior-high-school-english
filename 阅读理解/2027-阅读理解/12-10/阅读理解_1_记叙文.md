# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The First Time I Took a High-Speed Train to Grandma Alone**

① On Saturday morning, **December 11, 2027, at 07:32**, I stood alone in the long silver hall of our city's South Railway Station, holding an **A5 paper envelope** in my coat pocket that contained: my blue student ID, a printed train ticket for **Train G-3218, seat 07-A (window), departure 08:17**, a small folded slip with my father's mobile, and **two 100-yuan notes** in case of any emergency. I was fourteen. For the first time, I was taking the high-speed train alone to my grandma's city, **412 km north-east of here**, a **1 hour 58 minute** journey. My mother had walked me to the station gate. She had not come in. She had said, at the gate, in a very normal voice, *"Yiran, breakfast is in your bag. You know how to find the platform. I trust you."* Then she had turned and walked away, slowly, not looking back. I stood for ten seconds, watching the back of her coat become smaller, and then I took a long breath, turned, and walked carefully through the turnstile.

② The station was already very alive at 07:40 on a Saturday. I found the big blue arrival board, looked up **G-3218**, read the platform number (**Platform 6**), and walked the exactly correct distance my father had taught me on a small hand-drawn map two weeks ago: *right after the security gate, up the escalator, across the wide corridor, down the second escalator, left at the small flower-seller, to Gate 6*. I arrived at Gate 6 at **07:51**. Still 26 minutes before my train. I sat on a grey bench, opened my bag, and ate my small breakfast — one boiled egg, one steamed pumpkin bun, one small carton of warm milk. I ate slowly, the way my father had told me: **do not look nervous; do not look at your phone every 40 seconds; do not keep counting your things**. *"Sit like somebody who has done this a hundred times,"* he had said. I tried.

③ A small old lady sat down next to me at **07:58**. She had a very large cloth bag, from which the green tops of three spring onions were sticking out. She looked at me — alone, small, fourteen — and said kindly, *"Little sister, travelling alone?"* I nodded. *"To grandma's place?"* I nodded again, a little surprised. She smiled. *"You can always tell. The ones travelling to grandma sit with their bag on their lap, like a small bird they are not willing to put down."* I looked down. I was, in fact, holding my small canvas bag on my lap like that. We both laughed, a little. She said her name was Li Aunty, and that she was going eight stops, to see her own daughter. She was seventy-two. We sat quietly together after that, which was very nice, because sitting alone sometimes becomes sitting lonely, even at fourteen, even in a bright train-station hall.

④ At **08:05**, the boarding gate opened. I said a small goodbye to Li Aunty — she was in another carriage — and walked down a white-tiled ramp to the platform. The train was already there: a long, pale silver snake, warm lights inside, soft steam around the edges in the December air. My carriage was **05**, my seat **07-A**. An aunty in uniform checked my ticket and ID, smiled, and said, *"Carriage 5 is three down, little friend. Mind the small step."* I stepped up. Inside: warm. Quiet. Clean. A soft Chinese announcement in the speakers. I found seat 07-A. I placed my backpack above, kept my canvas bag on my lap. A window. The sky outside was light grey. At **08:17** exactly, the train began to move, as smoothly as if **the station itself** were gliding slowly backwards. I had never, in my life, been on a train without my parents. The world, I realised, looked not very different. I looked, probably, about the same. And yet, **something quiet inside my chest had shifted**. I was, in a small technical sense, already an older version of myself.

⑤ For the first ten minutes I tried not to look at my phone, which I had, at home, quietly decided to leave on airplane mode for the whole trip. I watched the grey winter fields slide past: bare trees, thin rivers under small broken ice, distant small villages with low chimneys smoking gently. A country boy my age, on a distant path between two fields, looked up at the train for one second, and then looked down again. We had, for that small second, seen each other and would not see each other again. *The world*, I thought, *is enormously quiet and enormously full at the same time.*

⑥ Next to me sat a very thin man of about forty, reading a thick book of short stories by **Wang Zengqi**. He did not speak. He did not check his phone. He simply read. Occasionally, he smiled at something in the book and did not look up. At some point, I took out **my own book** — a small essay collection by Feng Zikai I had borrowed from our school library — and began to read too. For almost an hour, the two of us — a fourteen-year-old and a stranger — read beside each other without a word. I remember thinking, carefully: **this silence is not cold. It is, almost, a friendship.** People who read together on trains are, in some quiet way, members of the same small tribe. When he finished his chapter, he closed the book, caught my eye, and gave a small respectful nod. I nodded back. We never, of course, spoke.

⑦ At **09:41**, the speaker softly said: *"Next station — Yangliu North. Arriving in four minutes."* My train was through at **10:15**, three stops later. I drank my small warm water, tidied my coat, put my book away. At **10:07**, I stood up, put my bag on my back, walked to the carriage end, and was ready to step out by the time the train slowed at **10:15** exactly. I stepped off with my right foot first, as my father had told me to do so I would not forget which side my bag was on. I walked out of the gate, through a short shining tunnel, into a larger grey hall.

⑧ Grandma was standing in the small group of waiting families. She had on her old red wool coat, the one she had worn in every family photograph since 1998. She saw me from twenty metres away, and her whole face lifted — the kind of lift only grandmothers can do — and I knew, without any sentence in any language, that I had, by arriving, given her the best small gift of her week. I walked, very carefully, the last twenty metres toward her. Then I ran the last five. She held me by both shoulders, looked at my face for a long moment, and said, in her soft northern voice: *"Yiran, you came alone. Grandma is so proud."*

⑨ Later that afternoon, as we peeled tangerines in her warm kitchen, I told her about Li Aunty at the station, the man with the Wang Zengqi book, the country boy between the fields, the grey rivers, the small broken ice, the smoking villages, and the exact way Mother had walked away at the gate without looking back. Grandma listened carefully. When I finished, she said, slowly: *"Yiran, a first train alone is also a first gate of a kind of growing up. **The first time you carry yourself from one city to another with your own small map** — you become, in a quiet way, a person who can be relied on, one station at a time.**"* She poured me some ginger tea, and added, smiling, *"By the way — give your mother a call. She has been worrying all morning."* I laughed. I called. Mother said, *"Good. Now eat the tangerine."* I ate the tangerine.

⑩ Before sleeping, I wrote four lines in my diary:

*Dec 11. 07:32 station. 08:17 train. 10:15 arrival.*  
*Li Aunty's bag, the Wang Zengqi reader, the boy between the fields, Grandma's red coat.*  
*Mother walked away without looking back. Grandma said, "you came alone."*  
*I think growing up is just this: carrying yourself, quietly, one station at a time.*

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. How did the writer's mother say goodbye at the station gate?**

A. She bought her breakfast and stayed until the train left.  
B. She walked her into the station and onto the platform.  
C. She said in a very normal voice, "Yiran, breakfast is in your bag. You know how to find the platform. I trust you," then turned and walked away slowly, not looking back.  
D. She gave her a big long hug and cried.

**2. What did Li Aunty tell the writer at the station?**

A. That young people should not travel alone.  
B. That the writer's train would be late.  
C. She said, kindly, "Little sister, travelling alone? To grandma's place? You can always tell. The ones travelling to grandma sit with their bag on their lap, like a small bird they are not willing to put down"; Li Aunty was going eight stops to see her own daughter and was 72.  
D. She asked the writer for help with her bag.

**3. What did the writer and the thin man reading Wang Zengqi do for almost an hour on the train?**

A. They spoke about literature in detail.  
B. They watched videos together.  
C. They sat beside each other reading without a word — a fourteen-year-old with Feng Zikai and a stranger with Wang Zengqi — and the writer thought that silence was not cold, it was almost a friendship; at the end of the stranger's chapter they exchanged a respectful nod but never spoke.  
D. They argued over a book.

**4. What did Grandma say about the writer's first train journey alone?**

A. She said the writer should not travel alone again.  
B. She said travelling is only useful if you arrive safely.  
C. She said a first train alone is also a first gate of a kind of growing up — "the first time you carry yourself from one city to another with your own small map, you become, in a quiet way, a person who can be relied on, one station at a time."  
D. She said a train is just a train.

**5. What does the writer mainly realise through this journey?**

A. Trains are dangerous and she will not take one again.  
B. That growing up, in part, is carrying yourself quietly one station at a time — a first-time 412-km solo journey under her own small map — with small encounters (Li Aunty, the Wang Zengqi reader, the country boy, a grandmother in a red coat) that together teach her she has become a slightly older, more reliable version of herself.  
C. That grandma is older than she thought.  
D. That mothers are cold and unfeeling.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段送行  
【解析】"不进站 / 我相信你 / 不回头"直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 Li Aunty  
【解析】"小鸟一样的书包 / 72 / 8 站"直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段车厢阅读  
【解析】"不言 + 小部落 + 点头"直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑨段奶奶总结  
【解析】"first gate of growing up"直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第⑩段总括  
【解析】"一次一站 + 成为可靠的自己"主旨。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
