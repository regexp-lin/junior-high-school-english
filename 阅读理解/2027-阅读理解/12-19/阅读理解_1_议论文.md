# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle School Have a "Teach-Your-Grandparent-One-Modern-Skill" Day Each Term?**

① Last Sunday afternoon, at 14:30, my **seventy-one-year-old grandmother** sat on the small sofa in our living room with her new smartphone in both hands, looking at the screen with a careful, slightly scared expression, as if the phone might bite her finger. She had, in three weeks, failed to learn on her own how to send a simple **WeChat voice message** to her sister in Chengdu, who was **75**. For forty-five patient minutes, I sat beside her, hand on her elbow, and walked her through the three small steps: *"press and hold the little microphone button, speak slowly, and release."* Her first recording was three words long: *"Yumei, I'm here."* She sent it. Twenty seconds later, her sister's reply came back — a warm tired laugh, and then, *"Jing, I can hear you. Finally."* My grandmother and I sat very still. She did not speak for about a minute. Then she said, softly, *"I have been losing her, a little each month, for a whole year. And I did not know the button was so small."* It is this quiet Sunday that makes me write today: **I think every middle school in China should plan, once per term, a "Teach-Your-Grandparent-One-Modern-Skill" Day — an afternoon on which each middle-school student sits with one elderly relative and patiently teaches them one single modern digital or practical skill.** Once per term. One skill. Forty-five minutes. That is enough.

② Some will say: *"Is this not what children already do at home, naturally?"* I respectfully disagree. The honest answer is: **most children do not.** A **2024 Renmin University family-digital study** of **3,018 urban Chinese households** found that of households containing one or more grandparents over 65 and one or more teenagers, **only 12%** reported a teenager patiently teaching a grandparent a phone skill **in the previous 12 months**; another **19%** reported the teenager teaching "angrily, in under 3 minutes, then giving up"; and a sobering **69%** reported **no teaching at all**. Grandparents, the study added, are **three times more likely** to report feeling "left behind by everyday technology" than in 2015. The natural home path, it turns out, has quietly failed. The school, without forcing anything, can gently create **the calm afternoon** on which the teaching finally happens.

③ Second, **this is a small gift to the student's own patience**. Teaching a seventy-year-old to scan a QR code, send a voice message, or buy train tickets on an app is not easy. The grandparent forgets a step; repeats a question; taps the wrong button; becomes embarrassed; tries again. Forty-five patient minutes of this teach the student something that no textbook, no exam, no tutor gives in middle school: the small discipline of **slowness**. The 2024 Tsinghua University youth-wellbeing study of **1,204 Grade-8 students** found that students who had engaged in at least **three such patient teaching sessions** in the past year scored **11.4%** higher on a standard **patience-with-strangers** questionnaire than matched students who had not. Patience cannot be taught from a book. It can, quietly, be learnt from a grandmother's slow thumb on a small microphone button.

④ Third, **this is a real, specific solution to a real, specific problem**. Our grandparents live in a quietly difficult country. In the last ten years, the daily practical world has moved almost entirely onto small rectangles of glass: paying for groceries, buying a high-speed train ticket, seeing a doctor, meeting children across the country, reading the news. A 2023 China Academy of Information study found that **32%** of Chinese adults over 65 had, in the last month, been **unable to complete one routine daily task** because they could not work a phone or app. This is not about being old. This is about a world that changed faster than a teacher's patience. A "Teach-Your-Grandparent" Day is one small afternoon a term in which the student — who learnt the skill by breathing it — gives it back, quietly, to the person who taught her how to tie her shoes twelve years ago.

⑤ Some will ask: *"Is it safe? Will the grandparent be fooled by a scam link while being taught?"* A wise concern. My small proposal: the school's **45-minute suggested curriculum** focuses on **kind, safe, everyday skills only**, chosen by parents and teachers: **(1)** how to send a voice or video message on WeChat; **(2)** how to scan a QR code for a shop or a city bus; **(3)** how to call a family member with a clear photo contact; **(4)** how to take a good-enough photo of a prescription or document; **(5)** how to set an alarm or medicine reminder; **(6)** how to spot and **refuse** five common scam patterns (the fake delivery, the fake bank, the fake hospital, the fake "lucky draw", the fake "grandson in trouble"). No student is asked to teach banking, online shopping, or investment. The **safety skill is part of the skill**: teaching refusal is as important as teaching use.

⑥ Others will say: *"What about students whose grandparents are not nearby?"* A fair point. For such students, the afternoon may be done **by a 45-minute video call**, scheduled quietly at a good afternoon hour. For students with no living elder relative, the school should gently pair the student with **a local kind elderly neighbour** from a partner community centre — never forced, always with the elder's agreement, and always with a familiar teacher present for the first ten minutes. The **2024 Nanjing pilot** of this system across 21 schools found that **97% of the elderly participants** reported afterwards that they felt "more confident with daily phone tasks"; the small community effect was real.

⑦ A deeper concern: *"Will the student lose valuable homework time?"* The school will use one afternoon per term (say, the last Saturday before winter and summer breaks). That is **four class periods, twice a year**. In exchange, each student gives one grandparent one small new skill, and keeps one small note in her family folder: *"In December 2027, I taught Nainai how to send a voice message to her sister."* Three years later, that family folder will have six notes. That is not lost time. That is, quietly, the opposite.

⑧ Finally, let me be honest about the cost. The school will spend: about **¥8 per student per term** (one small printed step-by-step booklet, one pen, one sticker for the grandparent's phone back); **one 15-minute homeroom explanation**; and **one quiet afternoon per term**. In return, the school gives, per semester, about **1,500 elderly relatives** one new everyday skill, and **1,500 students** one small lesson in patient, careful, kind teaching. Neither group gets this easily anywhere else.

⑨ **To sum up:** I am not asking children to become free tech-support workers. I am not asking parents to disappear from the room. I am asking, quietly, for **one afternoon per term, one elder relative, one booklet of safe skills, and one careful student**. My grandmother's voice message to her sister in Chengdu, on a Sunday afternoon, changed nothing about the world; it changed everything about the quiet colour of her winter. **Every grandparent in our country deserves at least six small afternoons like this across three middle-school years — and every student deserves to be the small kind teacher in them.** Forty-five patient minutes, six times. That is a small, easy, beautiful price for a family not quietly disconnecting, one button at a time.
