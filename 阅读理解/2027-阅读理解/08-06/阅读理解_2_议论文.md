# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle Schools Ban Smartphones in the Classroom?**

① On almost every busy morning, you can see it: a boy in the back row glances at his phone under the desk, a girl beside him quickly checks a new message, and the teacher, from the front, notices nothing. Across the world, middle schools are struggling with the same question: should smartphones be **banned** in the classroom? Some people say banning is too strict; a phone, they argue, is just a modern pencil case. I disagree. I believe **smartphones should be banned during class time**, because they pull attention away from learning, damage real communication between classmates, and teach a poor habit for the future.

② The first reason is simple: **attention**. Studies in many countries have shown that the human brain does not truly "multi-task"; it only switches quickly between tasks. Each switch costs time and energy. Even when a phone is face-down on the desk, knowing that a message **might** arrive keeps a part of our attention busy. Over forty minutes, these small losses add up to a big one. In several experiments, students who handed in their phones before class did better on tests than classmates who only put them in their pockets. The phone is not just a tool; it is a strong magnet for the teenage mind.

③ The second reason is **real communication**. Classrooms are one of the last places where young people still talk face-to-face for long hours with people they did not choose — teachers and classmates of different characters and backgrounds. This is where we learn to listen, disagree politely, and make a joke that lifts everyone. When phones are allowed in class, this skill is slowly weakened. During breaks, students stop chatting and look down. Friendships do not deepen; they only scroll. If a school lets phones stay in class, it is accepting this quiet loss.

④ The third reason is **long-term habits**. Middle school is where we shape habits we will carry for life. If we learn now that it is fine to check messages during every difficult 5-minute task, we will struggle later in exam halls, in universities, and in serious jobs where concentration matters. A classroom ban helps us practise the most useful modern skill: **the ability to put the phone aside and think deeply for at least one hour**. This is a gift, not a punishment.

⑤ People who oppose a ban often raise three points. First, "phones help us search for information quickly." Second, "students need phones for emergency calls from parents." Third, "a ban treats us like little children." These points deserve answers.

⑥ For **searching information**: if a teacher really needs online search in class, the school can use a cart of locked tablets, or the teacher's own device on the screen. We do not need 40 open phones to find one fact. For **emergency calls**: most schools already have a front-desk phone; parents can always reach the school office, and in serious cases, the teacher can reach the student at once. A locked phone in the bag does **not** cut the link to home; it only cuts the temptation of games and videos. For **"being treated like children"**: a rule that fits the brain's real limits is not an insult. Adult offices ban phones during meetings; adult airlines ban them during take-off. Managing distraction is a sign of maturity, not childhood.

⑦ Some suggest a "half-ban": phones off during lessons but allowed during breaks. This looks balanced, but in practice, many students start a video in one break that leads to another video, and another. The mind, still half in the last clip, drifts through the next class. A clear rule — **phones off in the bag from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m.** — is easier to follow, and kinder to the brain, than a rule that keeps asking us to start and stop.

⑧ A real ban does not mean hating technology. Students can still use computers for projects, watch a well-chosen video in class, or learn a new app at home. What we are banning is not learning; it is **a constant small interruption** that has turned many classrooms into quiet rooms of distracted faces.

⑨ So, should middle schools ban smartphones in the classroom? My answer is clear: **yes**. For the sake of our attention, our friendships, and our future adult habits, the classroom should once again be the place where we look up, listen, think, and talk. The phone is powerful, but middle school is more important. It should have, for at least six hours a day, our full quiet minds.
