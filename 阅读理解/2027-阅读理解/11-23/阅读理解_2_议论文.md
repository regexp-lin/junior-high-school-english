# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Our School Try One "No-Homework Weekend" a Month?**

① At the class meeting last Friday our form teacher asked a small but loud question: *"If our school could give you, just once a month, a weekend with no written homework, would you want it?"* The classroom went very still for a second — then exploded into a warm, careful discussion. Some people worried: "Will we fall behind?" Some people laughed with relief. After two long evenings of thinking, I have quietly settled on one opinion: **our school should try one 'no-homework weekend' a month, for at least one whole term**. I do not say this to be popular; I say it because it is honest, and the small risks are worth the big gains.

② My first reason is **tired brains do not learn well**. Middle-school students already spend about 45 hours a week in school activities — classes, short breaks, lunch, PE, and cleaning. Add 8 to 15 more hours of weekend homework, and many of us are working 60 hours, more than most adults. A 2023 report from the Ministry of Education reminded schools that "a rest that is planned is part of education, not an enemy of it." A brain given one honest, full weekend every four weeks returns on Monday **sharper, not lazier**. We will not fall behind; we will move forward with softer shoulders.

③ My second reason is **a rested weekend is not an empty weekend**. A common worry is that students will simply "play games all day." This is a fair worry, and we should answer it honestly. In the sample plan of our school's counsellor, Ms Wang, a "no-homework weekend" is paired with **three gentle invitations** (not rules): (1) **one meal** helped by the student, from shopping to washing up; (2) **one walk** of at least 3 km with a family member or a friend; (3) **one hour of reading** something not in the school timetable. No points, no exam, no report. Even if only one of the three happens, the weekend is already healthier than a normal one.

④ The third reason is that **one gap teaches the other six weekends**. When a student works without any break, he often does not know whether his weekend homework is **too much, too little, or just right**. After one "empty" weekend, he sees the contrast clearly. A student told me last week, *"I noticed that last Saturday I finished an essay in 50 minutes. Normally I drift on my phone for two hours first. The no-homework weekend showed me what my own essay speed really is."* That kind of self-knowledge is hard to buy and harder to teach. One monthly gap is a small laboratory in which each student can study his own habits.

⑤ Some argue, "Parents will still send us to extra classes. The free weekend is not really free." This is, sadly, sometimes true. But a school-wide rule can quietly change the conversation at home. If the school officially writes, "Please, on this weekend, no homework and, where possible, no cram class," most parents will respect this. A 2025 poll in our city found that **71%** of parents said they would follow a clear school "rest rule," even if their first instinct was to add more lessons. The school's voice, clear and kind, is often the anchor a family did not know it needed.

⑥ Others argue, "Weak students will fall behind more." I understand this worry — it is the most serious of all. But the plan is for **the whole class, not the top students only**. Teachers, with the no-homework weekend as a clear rest, can **shift one evening of the following week** to a short, warm **"catch-up corner"** — 40 minutes after school, voluntary, for any student who needs a quiet place to finish a small piece. The weekend is not stolen from weak students; it is **paired with a careful mid-week corner** that is often kinder than weekend homework ever was.

⑦ A fourth argument I rarely hear is about **families**. In many middle-school homes, parents and children have almost no conversation longer than five minutes on a weekday. Weekend homework turns Saturday into a second school day at the kitchen table — the parent becomes a tired deputy teacher. One no-homework weekend a month is a small national holiday inside the family. It allows the parent to be, again, simply **a parent**. This is not a small social detail; it is one of the quiet reasons many families say, in thank-you letters, that they hope the trial continues.

⑧ What are the risks? The biggest risk is **inequality**: some families know how to make a rested weekend healthy, and some do not. Our plan answers this with a simple page, printed in colour and sent home on Friday: eight ideas for the weekend, including "a thirty-minute phone-off walk," "cook one new dish together," "go to the public library at 14:00." No family is forced; but no family is left without ideas. Another risk is the worry that teachers will **"save up" homework** and give a bigger pile the next weekend. The headmaster can quietly prevent this by asking teachers to **keep total weekly homework steady** across the month, shifted, not piled.

⑨ This Monday the class representatives will put a short letter into the headmaster's postbox — 300 words, signed by every student who agrees, asking for a three-month trial. We are not asking for a day off; we are asking for **one careful, planned, measured gap** in our year. A school that knows how to plan rest will be trusted by its students more, not less. **A good school does not only teach us how to work; it also, quietly and seriously, teaches us how to stop, and how to come back.** Stop. Come back. Begin again, with a clearer head. That is the real lesson we are asking for this Monday morning.
