# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Teenagers Be Allowed to Travel Alone?**

① Every summer, a lively discussion returns to Chinese families: **should a 13- or 14-year-old be allowed to travel alone** — by train, by long-distance bus, or even by plane? Some parents proudly let their child buy a ticket and visit a cousin in another city. Other parents lock their door and say, "Not until university." As more middle school students ask for this kind of freedom, the old question becomes loud again.

② Supporters of solo travel say the world outside is an **open classroom**. When a teen must find the right platform, read a train schedule, buy a meal with his own money, and find a taxi in the rain, he learns real skills that no textbook can teach. Each decision grows into **confidence**. Many adults still remember their first long trip alone with tears and sparkle in their eyes — the day they became a little more grown-up than the day before.

③ Another reason supporters give is **trust**. Teens who feel trusted by their parents often act more responsibly. A 14-year-old who is told, "I believe you can do this," may take the trip more seriously than one who is watched all the time. Some experts say we cannot train a young person to be careful by locking him inside a safe box. **Careful adults are grown from brave little trips, not from silent rooms.**

④ However, other voices sound strong warnings. Middle school students are still **young, inexperienced, and often too kind to strangers**. Train stations and bus parks can be confusing. A smart but tired 13-year-old might get on the wrong bus, lose a wallet, or trust the wrong adult. In the worst cases, this trust can lead to serious harm. Parents who have spent their whole lives in a city can still get lost. How can we expect a child to do better?

⑤ There is also the issue of **tools and planning**. A phone battery can die. A train can be delayed by hours. A student far from home, with no adult, might suddenly face a medical problem or a weather emergency. Adults, too, have these problems, but they have more money, more experience, and more courage. A small failure on a teen's trip may become a very big memory of fear.

⑥ My own view is that the answer depends on **three factors**: the child's age and character, the difficulty of the trip, and the **preparation before it starts**. A careful, mature 15-year-old, travelling a direct high-speed train to an uncle's house in a city he knows, with a working phone and money for three days, is probably safe. A nervous 12-year-old, changing three buses to a strange town with no clear plan, is probably not.

⑦ In the end, letting a teen travel alone is not a one-word "yes" or "no". It is a **careful yes, with training, rules, and a ready hand on the phone**. Parents should not be walls; they should be coaches. Life, after all, will one day send our children out into the world — and it is better for them to take their first small trips while our love is still close enough to catch them.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段末问题  
【解析】全文讨论青少年是否可独自出行。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "real skills that no textbook can teach"  
【解析】书本学不到的技能。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "Careful adults are grown from brave little trips"  
【解析】谨慎的大人源于小小勇敢。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "wrong bus... trust the wrong adult"  
【解析】搭错车或误信他人。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨归纳题  
【定位】第⑦段 "a careful yes, with training, rules..."  
【解析】有准备地允许。故选 C。
