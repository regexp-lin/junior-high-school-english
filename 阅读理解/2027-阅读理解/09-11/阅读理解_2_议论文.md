# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Be Required to Wear School Uniforms Every Day?**

① Walk past any middle school in China at eight o'clock in the morning, and you will see almost the same scene: rivers of students in **white, blue and red tracksuit-style uniforms**, school bags on their backs, hurrying through the gate. Most of them have worn the same uniform — or a very close version of it — since Grade 1. In recent years, however, more and more middle-school students and parents have started to ask a simple question: **should middle-school students be required to wear school uniforms every single school day?** I believe the best answer is **yes, but with important improvements to the uniform itself**.

② Those who say "**no, every day is too much**" raise good points. First, many of today's uniforms are too **loose**, too **plain**, and made of **cheap cloth**. They are not comfortable in summer or in winter, and they rarely fit well. Second, girls and boys at thirteen, fourteen and fifteen begin to care about how they look. Forcing them to wear the same bag-shaped suit from Monday to Friday, they argue, kills their curiosity about style and may hurt their confidence. Third, they say uniforms do not really stop comparison among students; wealthy and less wealthy children simply compare **shoes, watches and phones** instead.

③ Those who say "**yes, every day**" answer with equally strong reasons. First, uniforms create a quiet **equality** in the classroom. When every student wears the same clothes, a child from a smaller family does not have to worry that his T-shirt is "out of date". Second, uniforms save real time: you do not have to decide what to wear at 6:30 a.m., and you do not arrive at school worried that your outfit is wrong. Third, a uniform also tells us, in a small but steady way: **we are here to learn, not to perform.**

④ I find the "yes" side more convincing, and my reasons grow from my own classroom. In Grade 7, my school ran a "free-dress week" to try the other option. By Friday, a quiet change had appeared. Some students came in very fashionable clothes; others arrived clearly **trying to match them**. One shy girl, who normally spoke often in class, hardly spoke all that week; later she admitted that she had "worried all morning about being judged". The lessons were the same; the students were not. When uniforms returned on Monday, so did her voice.

⑤ The second reason is harder to see but just as real: **uniforms protect study energy**. Middle-school years are short and full of pressure. Every small decision we remove from the morning — "what to wear", "will my friends laugh?" — gives us a little more energy for English, maths, and the long chapters of history. Some famous CEOs wear the same T-shirt every day on purpose, for exactly this reason. A uniform is not a punishment; it is a **free gift of attention** to our more important daily tasks.

⑥ However, those who complain about our current uniforms are not wrong, and a serious defender of uniforms must take these worries seriously. Most Chinese middle-school tracksuits today are honestly **not well-designed**. They are often too thick in autumn, too thin in winter, and not very flattering in any season. The answer, I believe, is not to throw away the uniform idea; it is to **make a better uniform**. Schools can, and should, work with students, parents and designers to create one or two daily versions (for warm and cold days), in well-fitting sizes, with simple, modern lines.

⑦ Some worries I would take seriously enough to turn into small rules. **One**, allow students to choose between two or three neutral **hairstyle options** if they wish. **Two**, on one day each month — for example, the **last Friday of the month** — let students wear their own clothes, with a soft guideline against very loud brands. **Three**, on special festival days (Mid-Autumn evenings, New Year's performances), students may dress in **traditional Chinese or festival wear** on campus.

⑧ Some will still ask: "Doesn't a uniform kill our individuality?" I would answer: a uniform does not kill your individuality; it saves it for more serious battles. You cannot build an original thought by matching the colour of your socks with your friends every day. You can, however, build that thought by opening a book, arguing a new idea in Chinese class, or solving an unusually hard maths question. Real individuality is never worn on the outside of the body; it is grown slowly **on the inside**, during years of honest work.

⑨ So yes, I believe middle-school students should continue to wear uniforms every school day — not the old loose tracksuits that nobody chose, but a smarter, better-fitting uniform designed **with** students, not **against** them. Keep the uniform, improve the cloth. Keep the equality, add a little freedom. Then one morning you will walk to school, and the uniform will feel less like a rule and more like a **quiet signal** you wear willingly: I am ready to learn today.

---

**注释：** tracksuit 运动服；equality 平等；flattering 显得好看的；individuality 个性；neutral 中性的

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion on daily school uniforms?**

A. Uniforms should be worn only at exam times.  
B. Uniforms should be required every school day, but with important improvements to their design and quality.  
C. Uniforms should be given up completely in favour of free clothes.  
D. Uniforms are only for Grade 7 students.

**2. Which is **NOT** a worry raised by people who say "no to uniforms every day"?**

A. Many current uniforms are too loose, plain, and made of cheap cloth.  
B. Uniforms do not stop comparison; students still compare shoes, watches and phones.  
C. Middle-school students want to show some personal style.  
D. Uniforms make all students run faster.

**3. What happened in the writer's class during a "free-dress week" in Grade 7?**

A. The whole class enjoyed the week and learning became easier.  
B. Some students worried about being judged and even a normally talkative, shy girl became very quiet, only speaking again when uniforms returned.  
C. Nothing at all changed; everyone behaved the same way.  
D. All students started dressing exactly the same by themselves.

**4. Why does the writer mention that some famous CEOs wear the same T-shirt every day on purpose?**

A. Because CEOs want to copy schoolchildren.  
B. Because this is a strange fashion trend.  
C. To support the idea that removing small daily decisions saves energy for more important work — a similar benefit to wearing a uniform.  
D. Because those CEOs are too poor to buy new clothes.

**5. Which of the following would the writer most likely support?**

A. Keeping the current loose, uncomfortable tracksuit and refusing any change.  
B. Getting rid of uniforms completely every school day.  
C. Improving uniform design with students and designers, while keeping uniforms for daily school days and allowing small exceptions like "own-clothes day" once a month.  
D. Allowing students to wear any fashion brand on any day.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段 "yes, but with important improvements to the uniform itself"  
【解析】作者支持每日穿，但强调改良校服。A、C、D 均不合。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节判断题（否定型）  
【定位】第②段列出三条反对理由  
【解析】"让学生跑得更快"从未出现。A、B、C 均为反方理由。故选 D。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "one shy girl ... hardly spoke all that week ... When uniforms returned on Monday, so did her voice"  
【解析】自由穿衣期间，原本爱发言的害羞女生明显沉默。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】写作意图/细节题  
【定位】第⑤段 "Some famous CEOs wear the same T-shirt every day on purpose, for exactly this reason. A uniform is not a punishment; it is a free gift of attention"  
【解析】作者用 CEO 的例子佐证"减少选择保存精力"与校服同理。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨/判断题  
【定位】第⑥—⑦段"make a better uniform" "last Friday of the month ... own clothes"  
【解析】作者主张改良校服并保留偶尔便装日。A、B、D 均与作者立场相悖。故选 C。
