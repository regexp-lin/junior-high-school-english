# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Be Banned from Using Short-Video Apps?**

① Walk down any school street at 4 p.m. in 2027, and you will see the same scene: students gathered in small groups, eyes locked onto the bright screen of a phone, watching one 15-second clip after another on short-video apps. Parents often complain that their children spend **three or four hours** a day on such apps. Some teachers warn that students' attention spans are now "**shorter than a goldfish's**". In recent months, a new question has become hot: **should middle-school students be completely banned from using short-video apps?** The answer is not as simple as "yes" or "no".

② Supporters of a ban point to **three real problems**. First, **time**. Short videos are **designed to be addictive**: each clip is short, sudden and entertaining, so the user never feels tired, and one hour quickly becomes four. That is one hour of homework lost, one hour of sleep lost, one hour of family talk lost. Second, **attention**. Neuroscientists warn that constantly jumping between 15-second clips can **weaken the brain's ability to focus** on a longer task — such as reading a chapter of a history book or listening to a teacher's full explanation. Third, **content**. Not every video on the app is suitable for a 13-year-old. Even with filters, dangerous jokes, harmful diet tips, and "easy-money" lies can appear on a student's screen.

③ Those against a total ban, however, answer with equal care. Short-video apps, they argue, are **not only time-wasters**. Used well, they can be a window into real life: **English vlogs** from London, **science explainers** from BBC Earth, **cooking videos** from grandmothers in Shanxi, **history cartoons** from the Palace Museum. A complete ban removes not only the bad content but also the good. Further, the ban itself may not work: students who are forbidden may simply **borrow their parents' phones** or use their friends' accounts, and the problem moves from the open living room to the **hidden corner of the bedroom**, which is harder to watch and harder to help.

④ There is also a **practical difficulty**. Who should enforce such a ban — the government? the platform company? the parent? the teacher? In practice, most national bans on specific apps have ended up either **being ignored** or being replaced by a new app with the same features. A rule that is widely ignored teaches students something worse than the content itself: that rules do not really mean anything.

⑤ My own view is that a **total ban** goes too far, but **total freedom** is clearly wrong. A **sensible middle path** would include at least three parts. First, **platforms** should be required to offer a strict **"youth mode"** — no more than **40 minutes per day**, no auto-play after 22:00, only verified educational content. The government can and should demand this. Second, **schools** can help by teaching a new, short module: "**How short-video apps make money from your attention**", so students understand the machine they are using. Third, **families** can build a simple agreement: the phone stays in the living room after dinner; homework first, videos later; no phone in the bedroom at night.

⑥ **Students themselves**, too, are not helpless. Turning off "auto-play", setting a 30-minute daily timer, unfollowing accounts that make you feel worse, and saving only 2 – 3 favourite creators — all of these are small but real tools. The goal is **not to fear the app**, but to use it **the way one uses a door, a knife or a car**: with understanding and care, aware that it can harm and help.

⑦ We live in a century in which every child will meet short videos, long videos, AI voices and many more technologies we cannot yet name. Trying to simply lock all this away from young people will not prepare them for their future. What prepares them is something slower and harder: **clear rules, honest talks, and the habit of asking, "Is this video still making me the person I want to be?"** That question, practised thirty times a day, is worth more than any national ban.

⑧ In the end, the debate is not really about one app. It is about **who controls a student's attention** — the 14-year-old brain, or the software engineers whose job is to keep that brain watching. Good education, in 2027 and after, will increasingly mean helping students **take back the remote control of their own minds**.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What question does the writer raise in the first paragraph?**

A. Whether every student should own a smartphone.  
B. Whether middle-school students should be completely banned from using short-video apps, in the light of concerns about time, attention and content.  
C. Whether long-video platforms are better than short-video ones.  
D. Whether schools should give every student a tablet.

**2. According to Paragraph 2, which is NOT listed among the "three real problems" supporters of a ban point out?**

A. Short videos are addictive and can eat up time for homework, sleep and family talk.  
B. Constantly jumping between 15-second clips may weaken the brain's ability to focus on longer tasks.  
C. Even with filters, dangerous jokes or harmful content can appear on a student's screen.  
D. Short-video apps contain no information about food and cooking.

**3. What do those AGAINST a total ban argue (Paragraph 3)?**

A. Short videos are always better than books.  
B. Short-video apps can also be a useful window into real life (language vlogs, science videos, cooking videos, history cartoons), and a total ban would also remove the good content; a ban may be ignored and push use into hidden places.  
C. Short-video apps should be used 24 hours a day.  
D. Students should be locked in their rooms while using videos.

**4. Which is part of the writer's own "sensible middle path" (Paragraph 5)?**

A. Platforms must offer a strict "youth mode" — no more than 40 minutes per day, no auto-play after 22:00, only verified educational content.  
B. Schools must ban all talk of short videos from every classroom.  
C. Parents must take away all phones forever.  
D. Students should never hear about how short-video apps make money.

**5. What is the writer's final message in Paragraphs 7–8?**

A. The government should simply ban every short-video app worldwide.  
B. The real debate is about who controls a student's attention; good education means helping students take back the remote control of their own minds, through clear rules, honest talks and the habit of asking, "Is this video still making me the person I want to be?"  
C. Short videos are harmless and students should watch as many as possible.  
D. Technology will always win, so students should give up.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】段落主旨题  
【定位】第①段 "should middle-school students be completely banned from using short-video apps?"  
【解析】首段提出核心议题。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节判断题  
【定位】第②段三点：时间、注意力、内容  
【解析】"短视频无食物信息"非文中问题，反而对立论据。A、B、C 皆被列为问题。故选 D。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "window into real life ... complete ban removes ... may simply borrow their parents' phones"  
【解析】反对方指出好内容被一同禁掉且禁令易失效。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "platforms should be required to offer a strict 'youth mode' — no more than 40 minutes per day, no auto-play after 22:00"  
【解析】平台青少年模式是作者方案的一部分。B、C、D 均被作者否定。故选 A。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨/观点题  
【定位】第⑦⑧段 "take back the remote control of their own minds ... clear rules, honest talks ..."  
【解析】作者结尾主张以教育唤起学生对注意力的主权。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
