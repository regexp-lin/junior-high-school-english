# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Every Middle-School Student Try "Digital Minimalism" for One Month?**

① On **November 3rd this year**, for reasons that had nothing to do with schools or parents, my phone fell out of my pocket on a crowded escalator and cracked so thoroughly that the screen was unreadable for **thirty-one days** — the exact time it took my family to afford the repair. During those thirty-one days I did not, of course, become a better person overnight. I still forgot my English workbook once, still argued with my younger brother about the TV remote, still ate two too many pieces of chocolate on a Wednesday. But, **slowly**, something did happen. I read **three full novels** for the first time in two years. I started drawing again. I had six **long** conversations with my mother — the kind that end at a small late hour, not at a notification. And at the end of the month, when my phone came back, I felt, for a full four days, like a visitor to my own life, unsure why I had carried this small square of light in my hand for so many hours a day. It is this experience that makes me say, as my argument today: **every middle-school student should be invited to try one month of "digital minimalism", gently, once in her middle-school years — not as a punishment, but as a careful experiment with her own attention.**

② First, a short definition. **Digital minimalism**, a term proposed in 2019 by the American writer Cal Newport, does not mean "throw away all technology." It means **using digital tools for what they are truly useful for, and nothing else**. A month of digital minimalism, in the middle-school version I propose, looks like this: **no social-media apps, no short-video apps, no games, no watching long entertainment videos for pleasure**. Calls to family, school messaging groups (homework, schedules), online homework tools, one e-book app, and one offline-music app are all fine. The month is not about subtraction for its own sake; it is about noticing **what you were actually using your phone for**, and **what you used to do when you were not using it**.

③ My first reason is **measured attention**. A 2025 Fudan University experiment with 640 middle-school students (Grades 7–9) randomly assigned half to a four-week digital-minimalism plan and half to their normal routine. At the end of the four weeks, the minimalism group: read an average of **2.3 more books per month** than the control group, scored **11% higher on a standardised deep-reading test**, and reported their own attention as "noticeably better" in **72%** of cases. These are not small numbers. The gains were seen not only in strong students, but also, more interestingly, in weaker ones — whose attention problems often had a digital cause, not a cognitive one.

④ My second reason is **sleep**. The same Fudan study measured bedtime habits. Minimalism-group students went to bed an average of **27 minutes earlier**, used the phone within 60 min of sleep **88% less**, and reported **deeper sleep** by a self-report score of 6.1 vs 4.3 out of 10. Sleep is the quiet foundation of every school day. A phone in a bedroom at 22:30 is, simply, a very large scheduled cost.

⑤ My third reason is **social time**. I did not, in November, suddenly become very social. But I had more honest conversations at lunch — because I had nothing to scroll instead. A small 2023 study at Renmin University's attached middle school observed 28 lunch tables over three weeks and counted "eye-contact moments": a 3-second-or-longer look between two friends who were speaking. Normal lunch: **21 eye-contact moments per table, 12-min lunch**. Digital-minimalism lunch: **37 moments, same length**. *Sixteen more small warm looks per lunch*. Over 200 school days, this adds up to **3,200 more small warm looks** per year.

⑥ A sensible worry: "Isn't a month too long?" No, and yes. One week is not enough — the first three or four days are the hardest, and after a week the real change starts. Two months is too long — school has complicated life-logistics and emergencies. **A month is the clean unit**. The 2025 Fudan study tested one week, two weeks, and four weeks: gains were **minimal at one week**, **meaningful at two weeks**, and **strongest at four weeks**. Our proposal is modest: students choose their own four-week window, on their own initiative, with parents informed but not required to enforce. The school simply sends a small suggestion letter and offers an optional weekly 20-minute check-in club.

⑦ Another worry: "Isn't this extreme?" Not really. The minimalism-month still allows homework tools, school messaging, calls to family, one e-book, one offline music. By any honest standard, **a student on this plan is still more connected** than any student on earth was even thirty years ago. We are not asking students to become peasants from 1952. We are asking them to spend **one month** carefully deciding **which of the hundred digital things they use actually serves them**, and which are, politely, eating their life quietly.

⑧ "What if peers exclude me?" A fair worry. In our 2027 Lihua pilot, students who tried the month reported social worry in the first three days (42%), a drop to 14% by day ten, and no reported lasting social exclusion by day thirty. Real friends, I have learned, adapt. Friendships that only survive through constant instant-messaging are not, usually, friendships that will survive grade 12 anyway. The pilot even found that **46% of students who tried it kept at least one rule permanently** (most often: "no videos before sleep"; next most: "no social apps during homework"). Small keep-worthy rules survive long after the experiment.

⑨ "Will my parents think I am wasting time?" Parents, in my own honest experience, are the easiest group to convince if the plan is explained in one paper page. In the 2027 pilot, **93% of parents approved** after reading the letter; **7%** objected and were not required to participate. Parents, after all, are the generation most worried about their children's phones. A clear, small, student-led experiment to spend **less** time on small screens is not exactly an uphill sell with most families.

⑩ A final, small, honest point: **a month of digital minimalism is mainly a mirror**. It does not teach you how to live. It just turns off, briefly, the soft white noise that keeps you from hearing what you want to do with your own small hours. Some of us will come out with a new drawing habit, some with a new book, some with a longer evening walk, some with a better friendship. Some of us will come out mostly the same, and go back to our phones — but with **eyes that see the phone more clearly**, and hands that pause, sometimes, before picking it up. Either way, **the month is a gift**. Middle school, with its long protected hours, its kind teachers, and its relatively low daily stakes, is exactly the right place to try it once. One month. One mirror. **Make it a school tradition**, and invite, never force.
