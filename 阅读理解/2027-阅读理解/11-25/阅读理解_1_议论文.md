# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Our School Set a Daily "Step Goal" for Every Student?**

① On the wall outside Lihua Middle School's small medical room, a new poster appeared last Monday. It was simple: a pair of blue running shoes, the words *"Healthy Steps 2027,"* and one sentence — *"**Shall we aim for 7,000 steps a day?**"* At the bottom, in smaller letters: *"Open for class discussion this week. Write your opinion on the green cards by Friday."* For three afternoons our class argued about this small poster. Some students were excited; some were worried. After reading my classmates' cards and thinking carefully over the weekend, I want to say clearly: **our school should set a gentle daily step goal for every student — but only if the rules are kind, private, and flexible**.

② My first argument is **our bodies simply need to move more**. A 2024 Ministry of Health report told us that middle-school students across many cities now walk, on average, about **3,900 steps a day** during school weeks — less than half the recommended 7,000 to 10,000. The reason is not mystery. We sit for 7 lessons, take short breaks between classrooms, walk to the dining hall, and go home by car or bus. A gentle daily step goal makes the invisible visible: it simply asks us to check whether our legs are being used today. That, by itself, is already a small piece of health education.

③ My second argument is that **small daily habits beat big weekend efforts**. A classmate wrote on her green card: "I run 10 km on Sunday with my dad. That is enough, isn't it?" A single Sunday run is wonderful, but our blood, our lungs, and our bones want **small, steady movement across every day**. Research in Japan in 2022 followed 1,500 teenagers for three years; those who walked about 7,000 steps a day on **five or more days a week** had fewer colds, lower resting heart rates, and better sleep than those who moved a lot on one or two days only. The lesson is clear: **rhythm beats intensity** for our age.

④ My third argument is that a step goal at school is **unusually fair and cheap**. Unlike many other health activities, it needs **no special equipment, no extra money, no booking of courts, and no unfair advantage for students with private coaches**. Even students whose families cannot afford a fitness tracker can count steps using a simple pedometer borrowed from the medical room, or the basic step app on any low-cost phone. A student without a phone can tick a paper card when she walks around the playground at lunch. The step goal is, in this sense, one of the **most equal** health ideas our school has ever tried.

⑤ Others argue, "Counting steps turns PE into a number game." I understand this. If we turn every lunch break into an anxious count, the goal has become a second exam. This is why the **three words** I believe must guide the plan are **kind, private, and flexible**. *Kind*: no red-green rankings on the wall. *Private*: only the student and a trusted teacher see the weekly number, and only if the student chooses to share. *Flexible*: a cold rainy day with 4,000 steps is still a good day; a sick week with 1,000 steps a day is completely fine. A goal that punishes you on your worst week is not a health tool — it is a small machine of shame.

⑥ Another worry is, "Students with asthma, heart problems, or mobility difficulties will feel left out or ashamed." This is the most serious worry, and it is why the plan must allow a **private alternative** — a simple "movement minutes" target (e.g. 30 gentle minutes a day, in any form a doctor allows) instead of steps, chosen together with the school nurse. No student's name ever appears on any wall. No student has to explain her target to her classmates. The goal adapts to the body, not the body to the goal. That single principle makes the plan safe.

⑦ What about **pushing weak students too hard**? Here, I think we need to be honest: the step goal should not start at 7,000. Our school's proposed plan is to start at **4,000** for Week 1, add 500 each week, and arrive at 7,000 by Week 7. That way, every student has time to build the habit. A student who only reaches 6,200 by Week 10 is not a failure; she is a small, honest winner over her own Week 1 self. The step goal is a climbing ladder, not a cliff.

⑧ Some will say the goal will pull attention from studying. My small answer: our brains, like our bodies, love steps. A 2019 University of Illinois study showed that a single 20-minute walk improved students' attention in the next classroom activity by up to **11%**. Walking is not taken from study time; it is **returned to it**, with interest. My own small proof — on the days I walk home instead of taking the bus, my English essay that evening somehow comes out cleaner.

⑨ Tomorrow, our class's six green cards will be placed together in a small brown envelope and taken to the headmaster's office by the class representative. We are not asking for a strict rule. We are asking for a **kind, private, flexible** daily step goal — 4,000 climbing softly to 7,000 — paired with alternatives for students who need them, and no public ranking at any stage. A school is, in the end, a place where small daily habits are learned. Homework and maths tests we already know; but maybe, quietly, **how to keep our bodies gently alive** deserves a small poster and a small habit too. Let the walk home be part of the curriculum. Five months from now, on a warm March afternoon, I hope we will look back and realise — we walked into spring, one quiet step at a time.
