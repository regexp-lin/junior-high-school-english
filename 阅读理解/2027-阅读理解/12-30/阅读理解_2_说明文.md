# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Is a Yawn So Easily "Caught" from Other People?**

① You are sitting quietly in a Grade-8 English class at 14:25 on a Monday afternoon. A classmate three desks in front of you **yawns softly**. Within about six seconds, **without knowing why**, your own mouth begins to open, your eyes water slightly, and a yawn of your own escapes — almost as if your classmate's yawn had **jumped across the classroom** into your body. Five of your classmates catch the same yawn in the next twenty seconds. Mr Qin, trying to talk about present perfect tense, shakes his head with a small smile. Most middle-school students have lived through this scene hundreds of times without ever asking a simple question: **why is yawning so easily passed from one person to another?** Scientists have, interestingly, been studying this since the 1950s, and although no single answer is yet 100% proven, four small facts are now widely agreed. This article explains them in simple words.

② **Fact 1 — A yawn is not really caused by too little oxygen.** For many years, the most popular idea among ordinary adults was that yawning "wakes the body up" by pulling in a big gulp of oxygen. Careful scientific experiments, however, have weakened this old idea. In a 1987 study at the University of Maryland, volunteers were asked to **breathe pure oxygen** for several minutes; their yawning rate did **not** go down. Volunteers were also asked to breathe air **rich in carbon dioxide**; their yawning rate did **not** go up. Clearly, **yawning is not mainly about oxygen levels in the blood**. What, then, is a yawn for? The current best answer is: **a yawn is a small brain-cooling action**.

③ **Fact 2 — A yawn may quietly cool the brain.** In the past ten years, Professor Andrew Gallup at Utica University has shown, in a careful set of studies, that **yawning gently drops the temperature of the brain by about 0.3 – 0.4 °C**. When you yawn, three small things happen, all at once. First, your jaw opens wide, which **stretches the blood vessels** in your cheeks and draws cool blood deeper into your head. Second, you take one deep breath of cooler outside air across the **wet inner walls** of your mouth and nose, creating a tiny cooling effect, like wind on a damp towel. Third, your throat muscles briefly press the **warm blood leaving your head** back toward the heart, making space for cooler blood coming up. Together, these three small actions lower the brain's temperature by a small but real amount — enough to **wake you up a little**, but not so much as to wake you up fully. This is why we yawn most often **before sleep, after sleep, and during boring long moments** — all times when the brain's temperature is slightly higher than is useful for clear thinking.

④ **Fact 3 — Catching a yawn is not a weakness; it is a small sign of empathy.** Here is the most interesting part. Studies on thousands of volunteers in eight countries have shown that **about 60 – 70% of healthy adults catch a yawn within a minute of seeing another person yawn**; young children under four, and certain people with very low emotional empathy scores, catch the yawn much less often. Why? Because catching a yawn, it seems, is not a breathing problem — it is an **automatic social copying action**, done by a small brain area called the **mirror system** (镜像神经系统), the same area that lets you feel a small stab of sadness when you see somebody else's knee hit a door. In other words, a middle-school student who catches a yawn easily is, statistically, more likely to be a student who **gently notices other people's feelings**. So the next time you catch five yawns in one afternoon, do not be embarrassed — you are simply a person whose mirror system is healthy and quietly awake.

⑤ **Fact 4 — You can catch a yawn from a picture, a sound, and even the word "yawn".** Careful experiments have shown that you do not need to **see** a yawning person to catch a yawn. A **silent photograph** of somebody yawning causes about 40% of viewers to yawn within one minute. A **sound recording** of a yawn — with no video at all — causes about 30% to yawn. And in one small 2003 study in Britain, merely **reading the word "yawn" several times** on a page caused 22% of readers to yawn, even alone in a silent room. (You may, quite possibly, have already yawned while reading this paragraph.) The yawn reflex, it seems, is not tied to sight alone; it is tied to the **idea** of yawning itself, wherever that idea enters the brain.

⑥ **Fact 5 — Dogs catch yawns too, especially from their owners.** If more proof were needed that yawning is an empathy-linked behaviour, pet owners have kindly given it. A 2013 study at the University of Tokyo showed that **about 45% of domestic dogs yawn within a minute of watching their owner yawn**, and the rate rises to roughly **60% when the owner is the dog's main carer**. Dogs do not catch yawns from strangers as easily. This is one small reason why many scientists now believe that catching a yawn is, at its heart, a **cross-species social signal** — a quiet way living animals say, *"I am paying attention to you, and my body is, in a small way, syncing with yours."*

⑦ So the next time a classmate three desks ahead yawns softly on a Monday afternoon and you, six seconds later, feel your own mouth open without permission, you may quietly remember four small facts: (1) your classmate's yawn is very probably a gentle brain-cooling action, not an oxygen rescue; (2) your own catch-yawn is an automatic sign of a healthy, awake mirror system; (3) the yawn probably travelled to you through your eyes, your ears, or even the idea of yawning itself; and (4) your small shared yawn with five classmates is, in quiet biological truth, one of the oldest social signals on Earth, shared even by dogs. A yawn, in short, is far more interesting than most adults believe. **The next time you catch one, give it a small thank-you — it is proof that your body is, quietly, still listening to the bodies around you.**
