# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Students Get Pocket Money for Doing Housework?**

① At almost every new term, the same scene appears in many of our homes. A middle-school student sweeps the floor for ten minutes, takes out the rubbish, and washes his bowl. Then he stretches out a hand and says, "Mum, pocket money?" Mum smiles in a tired way and looks at Dad. Dad looks up from his phone. "Should we pay the boy for washing his own bowl?" In our Politics class last week, a classmate raised this exact question, and the class was split almost evenly. I believe the honest answer is: **yes, but only for some kinds of chores, and never for all of them.**

② Those who say "**no pocket money at all**" raise a strong point. **A family is not a company.** Every member — parents, grandparents, children — uses the home, eats the meals, and sleeps in the clean rooms. Why should only the adults do all the cleaning for free, while children are paid for each small task? If we pay a child to clean his own bowl, we quietly tell him that anything unpaid is "not his job". Over time, that idea is dangerous — in a family, in a school, and later in a workplace.

③ Those who say "**yes, always pay**" also have their reasons. They argue that chores take real time and real energy. Paying for chores teaches children about **work, money and saving**, three things that school textbooks touch only lightly. A child who earns five yuan for vacuuming the floor learns, in a small but real way, that five yuan does not come from thin air. Such children are said to understand bills better, spend more carefully, and save earlier for real goals like a new book, a bus ticket home, or a small gift for Grandma.

④ Both sides carry truth, which is why I support a **middle path**. I would split housework into **two boxes**. Box A is what I call **"Basic Family Chores"** — chores a student should do **without money**, simply as a member of the family: making one's own bed, clearing one's own bowl, tidying one's own desk, and taking a small daily share of kitchen work. These are the quiet rent we pay for the roof over our heads.

⑤ Box B is what I call **"Family Project Chores"** — chores that are **clearly more than personal care**, that take longer, and that benefit the whole family: deep cleaning of the windows before spring, washing all the bed sheets, helping Dad carry home a large sack of rice, repainting a wooden bookshelf, or giving the family dog a monthly bath. For these, a small, agreed pocket money is **fair**, because the work is real, the time is long, and the benefit belongs to everyone. Here, pocket money is not a reward for being "good"; it is **real pay for real extra work**.

⑥ With this split, the worries of both sides can be answered. Children still learn that basic living tasks are **not for sale**, so the home keeps its warm, non-commercial heart. At the same time, children earn the chance to manage some real money from age twelve or thirteen, when many of them are most curious about it. That real experience is priceless: a child who has earned **thirty yuan** in a week by cleaning windows knows far more about the value of thirty yuan than a child who receives it as a birthday gift every year.

⑦ Two practical tips may make this approach work. **First**, parents and children should write the rules down together, on a piece of paper on the fridge: which chores belong to Box A and which to Box B, and what each "project chore" pays. This prevents the endless argument: "You said 10 yuan; no, I said 5." **Second**, families should **save a small part** of the money each time into a shared jar — for charity, for family trips, or for a simple dinner with grandparents. Pocket money, like work, feels different when it is not only "mine".

⑧ So the old scene at the doorway can change. When the boy stretches out his hand after washing his own bowl, Mum can smile and say, "Not for this. But remember the Saturday window project? That is yours." He will complain for one second, then nod, and learn something a price tag alone cannot teach: some work is done because we live here; some work is rewarded because we contribute beyond ourselves; and every kind of work has its own quiet dignity.

**注释：** chore 家务活；vacuum 用吸尘器打扫；non-commercial 非商业性的；dignity 尊严

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion about giving students pocket money for housework?**

A. No pocket money should ever be paid, in any situation.  
B. All kinds of housework should be paid at a fixed price.  
C. Pocket money should be paid only for some kinds of chores, but not for all.  
D. Only the oldest child in the family should receive pocket money.

**2. What is the main reason given by those who say "no pocket money at all"?**

A. Parents are usually too poor to pay their children.  
B. A family is not a company; every member should share the home's work for free.  
C. Children are always too lazy to do housework.  
D. Pocket money is banned by the government.

**3. Which of the following belongs to **"Basic Family Chores"** (Box A) according to the writer?**

A. Deep cleaning all the windows before spring.  
B. Washing all the bed sheets together.  
C. Making one's own bed and tidying one's own desk.  
D. Repainting a wooden bookshelf.

**4. According to paragraph 5, **why** is it fair to pay pocket money for Family Project Chores?**

A. Because the parents do not want to do any work.  
B. Because the work is real, takes longer, and benefits the whole family.  
C. Because children need to buy expensive games.  
D. Because the government says so.

**5. What is the main reason the writer suggests putting rules for chores on a piece of paper on the fridge?**

A. To make the fridge more beautiful.  
B. To avoid endless arguments about which chore belongs where and how much each pays.  
C. Because students cannot remember anything.  
D. So that visitors can read family secrets.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段 "yes, but only for some kinds of chores, and never for all of them"  
【解析】作者主张部分家务给零花钱。A、B、D 均极端或无据。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "A family is not a company ... every member uses the home ... Why should only the adults do all the cleaning for free"  
【解析】家非企业，家庭成员应共同承担家务。其余均非原文理由。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "making one's own bed, clearing one's own bowl, tidying one's own desk"  
【解析】A 类为与个人生活紧密相关的基础家务。A、B、D 均为 Box B。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "the work is real, the time is long, and the benefit belongs to everyone"  
【解析】B 类家务因工作量大、耗时长且惠及全家才得付酬。A、C、D 均无依据。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段 "This prevents the endless argument: 'You said 10 yuan; no, I said 5.'"  
【解析】写下规则可避免无休止争执。A、C、D 均不合文意。故选 B。
