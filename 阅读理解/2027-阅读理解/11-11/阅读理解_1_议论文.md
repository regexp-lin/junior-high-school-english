# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Basic First-Aid Be Part of Every Middle-School Timetable?**

① Last Wednesday morning, during the 09:30 break, a Grade 8 student in our school suddenly fell to the ground in the corridor. He had been laughing at a joke and, a second later, he was not breathing. Three classmates stood frozen for eight long seconds before a teacher ran past. The student is fine now; the hospital later said it was a **choking incident** caused by a sweet. But that eight-second frozen silence haunted me for the whole week. It made me ask: **Why don't we, ourselves, know what to do in those eight seconds?** My view is that **every middle school in our city should add one short, regular first-aid lesson to the timetable** — starting from Grade 7.

② The first reason is **real numbers**. According to a 2026 national report, over **120,000 people** in China die each year from **cardiac arrest** outside hospitals, and more than one third of them happen in places where young people are near — schools, parks, playgrounds, family homes. Survival after a sudden heart stop depends on **the first 4 minutes**. If a person around the patient starts chest compressions at once, survival rate can rise from below 5% to about **30% — six times higher**. Those first four minutes are almost never the minutes when a doctor is there. They are the minutes when **we are there**.

③ The second reason is **not just heart stop**. Middle-school students, at school and at home, are often the closest witnesses to small accidents: a grandmother chokes on a piece of meat at dinner; a younger brother falls from a bicycle and bleeds; a friend burns her hand on a hot cup. A 15-minute lesson on the **Heimlich manoeuvre** (chest-pushing for choking), a 10-minute lesson on **pressing a clean cloth onto a bleeding wound**, a 5-minute lesson on **cooling a burn with running water** — each one is the difference between **useful hands and useless hands**.

④ The third reason is **quiet confidence**. A student who has practised, with her own hands, on a plastic training model, does not freeze as easily when a real incident starts. A 2024 study in Shanghai found that students who had had **six first-aid lessons** were **three times more likely** to step in during a playground emergency than those who had had none. The lesson is not only in the hands — it is in the **shoulders**. After practice, the shoulders of a fourteen-year-old drop a little lower, knowing **"if something happens in front of me, I can start while waiting for the adult."**

⑤ Some people argue, "This is the doctor's job, not a school's job." Doctors are, of course, the experts. But a doctor can only arrive after a phone call. The school's job is never to replace the doctor; it is to make sure the four minutes **before the doctor arrives** are not empty minutes. Others say, "Students are too young to learn this." The American Heart Association has, since 2010, recommended hands-on CPR training **from age 12**. Many British and Australian schools begin even earlier. Our Grade 7 students — 13 years old — are no less capable than theirs.

⑥ A more serious worry is **time**. The timetable is already full; mathematics, English, physics, history — everyone wants their own minutes. My reply is: we do not need a whole subject. We need **one 40-minute lesson every two weeks**, a total of about **ten lessons a term**, taught in the PE or Health class. The cost is small; the return is large. No maths exam has ever asked a student to save a life; a quiet corridor at 09:30 once did.

⑦ A last worry is **fear of doing harm**. "What if a student hurts the patient while trying to help?" This is a fair concern; but a strong, well-known **Good-Samaritan clause** has been part of our national law since 2017 — students who help a stranger in good faith are **not legally responsible** for accidents. What is more, the **current teaching method**, called "Quality CPR + Safe Stop Points", makes it very clear when a student should stop and simply wait for an adult.

⑧ So what should be taught? A minimum plan of three skills: (1) **how to call 120** clearly (our address, the patient's condition, our name); (2) **hands-only CPR** (30 chest pushes at the speed of "staying alive", two short breaths — or chest pushes only, if uncomfortable with mouth-to-mouth); (3) **how to help a choking person** using the Heimlich manoeuvre. Add, over a year, bleeding control and burn cooling. That is all. Ten lessons in total. Ten lessons that any middle-school student can leave school with — as a quiet, lifelong gift from school to world.

⑨ My Grade 8 classmate is back at his desk. He laughs more quietly now, and eats his sweets more carefully. Next Monday, our health teacher will begin the first of ten new **First-Aid Fridays**. The whole class will stand in a circle around a plastic model of a person. We will press on his chest while counting "one and two and three..." Perhaps we will never need to press on a real chest in our lives. But if one of us does, our eight-second frozen silence will, this time, be replaced by **eight seconds of steady, trained hands**. That is why first-aid belongs on every middle-school timetable.

**1. What caused the writer to think seriously about first-aid lessons?**

**2. According to Paragraph ②, why are "the first four minutes" so important?**

A. Because they are four minutes of the PE class.

C. By ignoring the worry.  
D. By suggesting first-aid should only be taught to teachers.

**5. What does the writer mean at the end by "eight seconds of steady, trained hands"?**

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段 "corridor ... Grade 8 student fell ... eight long seconds"  
【解析】起因直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段数据句  
【解析】生存率从 5% 提升到 30%。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段末 "American Heart Association ... from age 12 ... British and Australian"  
【解析】正面反驳。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节理解题（否定）  
【定位】第⑧段三条核心技能  
【解析】A、B、C 均在文中；D 为手术，超出范围。故选 D。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑨段末与第①段"八秒呆滞"对照  
【解析】训练取代呆滞。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
