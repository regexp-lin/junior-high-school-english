# 阅读理解（议论文） **Is Learning Chinese Calligraphy Still Useful Today?**

① Across many Chinese classrooms, a quiet scene can be found on Thursday afternoons: rows of students dip **ink brushes** into small black bottles, hold thin strips of **rice paper**, and slowly write **Chinese characters** in the ancient way. Some teenagers love this calm hour. Others yawn and ask, "**Why practise calligraphy in a world of phones and printers?**" It is a fair question, and it deserves a real answer.

② People who support calligraphy lessons give several strong reasons. First, calligraphy is a **cultural bridge**. Chinese writing is one of the oldest continuous writing systems in the world, and calligraphy is its proudest art. When a child writes a single character slowly, **stroke by stroke**, he feels connected to thousands of years of poems, letters, and silent teachers. This is not a feeling any screen can easily provide.

③ Second, calligraphy is a **quiet training for the mind**. The brush cannot be moved too fast, too slow, or too hard. Every stroke requires steady breathing and patient thinking. Students who practise regularly often become **more focused** at their desks and in exams. In a time full of quick notifications and short videos, half an hour of calm writing is almost a kind of medicine for the tired young brain.

④ Third, calligraphy can build **better handwriting** in daily life. Even though most modern work happens on computers, students still write by hand in exams, notebooks, and birthday cards. A student who has practised calligraphy usually writes Chinese **more neatly and beautifully**, which helps teachers read answers quickly and saves the student precious marks.

⑤ However, the other side of the debate is not silent. Some parents say class time is very limited and should be spent on "**more useful**" subjects such as English, maths, and coding. Others argue that not every child finds joy in black ink; forcing them to practise may only create stress. A few students point out that with so many homework assignments already, one extra subject might feel like another heavy block in the schoolbag.

⑥ There are also **practical worries**. Brushes, ink, and rice paper cost money that some families can hardly spare. A careless stroke can stain the uniform or the desk, and clean-up takes time. In big, busy cities, some old calligraphy shops are closing because of high rent, making supplies harder to find.

⑦ In my view, calligraphy is not only useful today — it may even be **more important** than it was before. In a world where machines can write faster than we can think, slow skills that train patience, beauty, and cultural memory are rare and precious. A balance can be found: perhaps one shorter calligraphy class per week, with free supplies from the school, and friendly teachers who welcome small beginners. The key is not to turn calligraphy into a test, but to keep it a **quiet friend** — the kind that, many years later, grown-up students may still thank.
