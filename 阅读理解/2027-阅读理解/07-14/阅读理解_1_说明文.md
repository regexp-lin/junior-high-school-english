# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Do Bats Fly in Total Darkness?**

On a warm summer evening, if we look up at the sky as it turns from blue to purple, we may see small quick shadows flash between the trees. They are not birds — they are **bats**. Bats often hunt at night, and they can catch a tiny flying insect in almost complete darkness. How is this possible? A bat's eyes are actually not very strong. The real secret lies in its ears and mouth, and in a special skill called **echolocation**.

The word "echolocation" is made of two parts: "echo" (a sound bouncing back) and "location" (where something is). Bats use sound to "see" their world. While flying, a bat opens its mouth and sends out a series of short, high-pitched **calls**. These sounds are so high that human ears cannot hear them. When a sound wave meets an object — a tree, a wall, or a small moth — it bounces back as an echo. The bat's large ears catch this echo, and its small but fast **brain** compares the original sound with the returning echo.

From this comparison, the bat learns a surprising amount of information. **Distance** is measured by how long the echo takes to come back: longer delay means further away. **Direction** is found by comparing the two ears; if the echo reaches the right ear a tiny bit earlier, the object is on the right. **Size** and **shape** are suggested by how strong and "full" the echo sounds. Some bats can even tell whether a moth is hairy or smooth, just from the quality of the returning sound.

Why is this skill so useful? For one thing, many **insects** — a bat's main food — come out only at night. Darkness is a good time for hunting, because other predators like birds are already asleep. For another, a bat that relies on eyes would waste energy turning its head. With echolocation, the bat sends information "out" and lets the world bring information back. It is as if our eyes could throw a tiny ball of light and catch it again, rich with news from wherever it had been.

Echolocation is not only a skill for flying. Inside a dark cave, thousands of bats live together. How do they not bump into each other? First, each bat's voice has a slightly different **frequency**, like a unique fingerprint. Second, bats can also recognize "friendly" calls from the same colony. Third, if the echoes of two calls mix too much, a bat can quickly change the pitch of its own sound — like a driver honking in a different tone to avoid confusion.

People have been inspired by this small animal. The earliest **sonar** devices, used by ships during storms, work in the same way: they send a "ping" into the water and listen for echoes from the **seabed**, rocks, or fish. Modern blind people also use a simple form of echolocation: by clicking their tongue or tapping a cane, and listening to the sound pattern around them, some can walk confidently along unfamiliar streets. A skill that first evolved in a little creature of the night has now crossed into our science and even our daily life.

So, on a summer night, when you see a small dark shadow cross the lamplight, remember that it is doing something remarkable: shouting into the darkness and listening for the world to answer. A bat's world is not silent. It is full of tiny bouncing sounds, too high for us to hear, turning the night into a moving map only they can read.

【答案】B

【定位】"echo (a sound bouncing back) and location..."  
【解析】回声+定位。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题

【解析】回声时间差。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"each bat's voice... different frequency... recognize friendly calls... change the pitch"

【答案】B

【答案】B
