# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Does a Hot Drink Really Warm You on a Cold Day?**

① On a cold December morning, after fifteen careful minutes of standing in the wind at a bus stop, you walk into a small corner café, wrap your hands around a paper cup of **hot ginger tea (about 70 °C)**, and take one slow sip. Within about **ninety seconds**, a small warm wave begins to move — not only through your fingers and mouth, but oddly, into your back, your shoulders, and even the tips of your ears. You feel, genuinely, warmer all over. Most of us accept this as obvious: *"I drank something hot, so of course I am warmer."* But the physics is, quietly, a small surprise. **The tea's thermal energy is actually very small; most of the warmth you feel does not come from the tea at all. It comes from your own body, gently switched on by the tea.**

② **Finding one: the tea contains much less heat than you think.** A standard 250 ml cup of tea at 70 °C, cooling down in your stomach to body temperature (37 °C), releases roughly **35 kilocalories (kcal)** of heat into your body. For reference, your body is already burning about **60–80 kcal per hour** simply by breathing and keeping your cells alive ("basal metabolic rate"). One hot cup of tea is therefore equal, in raw heat, to about **30 minutes of extra basal warmth** — spread out over the 30 minutes it takes for the tea to be absorbed and the water to cool. That is real, but it is tiny — nowhere near enough to warm your whole 50-kilogram body meaningfully by itself. And yet you feel warmer much faster than 30 minutes. Something else is happening.

③ **Finding two: the real warming is a trick of the skin.** When the hot liquid touches the **lining of your mouth and throat**, it activates a family of tiny heat sensors called **TRPV1 receptors** — the same receptors that detect the burn of a chili pepper. The sensors instantly send a signal to your brain's **hypothalamus**, the body's small "thermostat". The hypothalamus replies, in under a second, with a broad order throughout the body: *"External heat detected — open the blood vessels near the skin — bring warm blood to the surface — you are safe now."* Blood, which had been kept mostly in your deep core during the cold, suddenly flows outward in greater quantity to your hands, feet, face, and ears. This is called **vasodilation**. Your skin, previously cold and pale, fills with warm red blood. **You feel warmer — mainly because your own warm blood has come closer to your skin nerves.** The tea itself provided maybe 15% of the warmth; your own body provided about 85%.

④ **How big is this effect?** A **2018 University of Cambridge physiology study** measured skin surface temperature in **30 healthy young adults** after standing in a 5 °C cold room for 10 minutes and then drinking a 250 ml cup of either hot (70 °C) tea, lukewarm (40 °C) water, or icy (4 °C) water. After only **90 seconds**:

| Drink | Skin temp. change (forearm) | Perceived warmth (1–10 scale) |
|---|---|---|
| **Hot tea 70 °C** | **+2.4 °C** | **+3.1** |
| **Lukewarm water 40 °C** | +0.2 °C | +0.4 |
| **Icy water 4 °C** | **+1.1 °C** | **+0.6** |

The surprising line is the last one. **Icy water also warmed the skin, by 1.1 °C on average.** Why? Because when a sudden cold signal arrived at the mouth, the hypothalamus briefly over-corrected — *"protect the core from sudden cold"* — and **temporarily increased blood flow to the skin as well**. The body, in other words, will warm your skin in response to many strong signals — including, sometimes, a cold one.

⑤ **Finding three: a hot drink actually cools your deep core very slightly.** This is the small strange fact that surprised researchers at the **University of Ottawa** in 2012. Their careful study of **9 male volunteers** on a stationary bicycle in a hot room (30 °C) showed that drinking a hot cup triggered **sweat** sooner than a cold one — and sweat evaporating from the skin cools the body **more than the hot tea itself heats it**. In a cold room, by contrast, the body almost never sweats, so this cooling effect is tiny and the warming effect of the vasodilation easily wins. That is why hot drinks feel warming only in cold environments. In a hot environment, in a perfectly strange way, a hot drink is slightly cooling.

⑥ **What about the "warm feeling" in my belly?** Part of that is real. The tea's direct 35 kcal **does** warm your stomach lining and the blood passing through it. The body's **vagus nerve** senses this gentle warming and sends a soft quieting signal — relaxing the muscles of the stomach and lowering heart rate slightly (a few beats per minute). This is the same feeling many of us describe with the slightly vague English word *cosy*. Cosiness, it turns out, is not a metaphor: it is your vagus nerve responding to a small warm wet signal in your stomach.

⑦ **Which hot drinks work best?** Any hot liquid gives the vasodilation response. But three classes of drinks add small extra effects:

| Drink | Extra effect | Science note |
|---|---|---|
| **Ginger tea** | Activates TRPV1 more strongly; makes vasodilation larger and longer | Gingerol and 6-shogaol compounds bind to TRPV1; 2020 Kyoto University study |
| **Black tea / coffee** | Provides a mild attention lift from caffeine; helps with cold-morning sluggishness | Caffeine blocks adenosine receptors in the brain |
| **Hot milk / cocoa** | Provides small amount of real calories (about 150 kcal), sustaining body warmth over 60+ minutes | Protein and fat release energy slowly |

**Herbal tisanes without gingerol** (plain chamomile, rose bud) give a smaller vasodilation boost but are still warming. **Plain hot water** works almost as well as tea, interestingly. It is the *hotness*, not the *tea-leaves*, that mainly warms the skin.

⑧ **Is it good for you to drink hot things on a cold day?** Mostly yes, with one small warning. **Chronic drinking of liquid above 65 °C** has been linked in several WHO reviews (2016, 2021) with a small increase in **oesophageal cancer risk** over decades. Most schools in China recommend drinking tea at **50–55 °C** — hot enough to feel deeply warming but not so hot that it damages the delicate cells lining the throat. The rule is simple: **a cup of tea should not make you wince when you sip.** If it does, wait two minutes.

⑨ **The quiet lesson.** Next time you come in from the cold and wrap your hands around a warm cup, remember: the cup is not doing most of the work. The cup is a small trigger. Your own body, quietly, is doing almost all of the warming — opening tens of thousands of tiny blood vessels near your skin, sending warm blood from your deep core out to your fingertips, your ears, your cheeks. **The tea is a small polite letter to your hypothalamus, and your hypothalamus, in about ninety seconds, writes a warm reply through your whole body.** Now you know. Drink the tea slowly. Your body is doing more for you than the tea is.
