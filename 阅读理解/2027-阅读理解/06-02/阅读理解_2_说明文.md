# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do Our Ears "Pop" on an Airplane?**

① The airplane is still rolling gently on the runway. Then the engines roar, the plane rises, and within one minute you feel a strange pressure inside your ears. Somebody gives a short hiccup sound; a child starts crying; your own ears feel as if they are stuffed with soft cotton. Later, when the plane lands, the feeling comes back — until suddenly, with a tiny "**pop**", your ears are comfortable again. What is really happening inside your head?

② To answer the question, we first need to know about two small rooms of air. On each side of your face, the **middle ear** is a tiny air-filled space behind the thin **eardrum**. The eardrum is like a very small, very sensitive balloon. Normally, the air on the outside and the air in the middle ear have the same **air pressure**. The eardrum then sits flat and relaxed — and you do not notice it at all.

③ Now imagine an airplane climbing quickly. The air around the plane gets thinner, so the pressure **outside** your ears falls. However, the air trapped inside the middle ear cannot change that fast. For a short time, the pressure **inside** is higher than outside. The poor eardrum is pushed outward, as if it were a small balloon blown from behind. This pushing is what feels "stuffed" or "tight".

④ The body has a clever solution: a thin tube called the **Eustachian tube**, which connects the middle ear to the back of your throat. Normally this tube is closed. When you swallow, chew, yawn or speak, the muscles around it pull it open for a split second. Air flows through — sometimes in, sometimes out — until the pressure on both sides is equal again. The sudden movement makes the eardrum snap back into place, and you hear the famous "**pop**" right beside your own ear.

⑤ During landing, everything happens in reverse. The plane falls into thicker air; now the pressure outside becomes higher than inside. The eardrum is pushed inward. Again, swallowing, yawning or chewing can open the Eustachian tube and let extra air in. That is why flight attendants often hand out small sweets before take-off and landing — chewing helps the tubes to open. Drinking a little water, or blowing air gently into your nose while pinching your nose shut (the "Valsalva move"), can also help.

⑥ Sometimes, if you have a cold, the Eustachian tube is full of mucus and cannot open easily. The pressure does not balance, and the pain can be stronger. That is why doctors may advise people with a serious cold not to fly. In most cases, however, ear "pops" are harmless — simply a sign that your body is keeping a small, healthy balance between the sky outside and the air inside you.
