# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Afternoon Grandpa and I Fixed the Broken Rocking Chair**

① On a cold Sunday afternoon, **14 December 2027, at 13:40**, my grandpa called me from the small balcony of his third-floor flat with a small wooden sigh. I went over. He was standing next to a tall, old, dark-brown **rocking chair** whose left armrest had broken off clean, lying on the floor like a quiet wooden fish. The chair had been in our family for **thirty-four years** — my grandmother had bought it for him when she was pregnant with my father, at a small carpenter's shop behind **Ping'an Road** in 1993, for **82 yuan**. Grandma had been gone for three years now. The chair had kept rocking, every evening at 19:00, in the small north corner of the balcony, under a pot of winter plum. "Yiran," Grandpa said softly, touching the broken armrest with one careful finger, "the chair is tired. But we are not going to throw it away. Today, you and I, we are going to fix it. Are you free for three hours?"

② I was fourteen. I had never used wood glue in my life. I had held a hammer exactly twice — both times as a joke, not as a tool. But I nodded. Grandpa went into his small storage room and came back carrying, in both hands, a **wooden tray** with five small things arranged on it: one **small bottle of yellow wood glue** (brand: 中华牌, half-used, bought 2019), one **thin wooden dowel** about **8 cm long**, two **small green C-clamps**, a small square of **fine sandpaper**, and a **clean damp cloth**. He also carried, under his arm, a small faded notebook. "Grandma's carpentry notes," he said, smiling. "She was, quietly, better at this than I was."

③ Before we touched the glue, Grandpa asked me to do three small things. First, "**Look**," he said, turning the broken armrest in his hand. "Before we fix, we understand." He pointed to the break. It was not a clean break — the old wood had split along its grain, leaving two thin wooden tongues that fitted into each other. He made me run my finger along the break and feel the **shape** of the damage. Second, "**Clean**." We used the damp cloth to wipe thirty-four years of small dust from the inside of the break. The cloth came back light brown. Third, "**Dry-fit**." Before any glue, we pushed the armrest back into place, without glue, to see that it still fitted exactly. It did. The chair, for **one quiet minute**, was a whole chair again. I felt, for the first time, the small pleasure of watching a broken thing become un-broken.

④ Grandpa took out Grandma's notebook. On page 14, in her small careful handwriting, was a line from 1994: **"For a clean split, glue thin, glue twice, clamp long."** He read it aloud, twice. Then, with a tiny wooden toothpick, he put a **very thin line of yellow glue** on each surface of the break. "Too much glue," he said, "is worse than too little. Glue is a kind promise; it should not shout." He waited **one full minute** for the first thin layer to become slightly tacky. Then he added a **second thin layer**. Grandma's notebook was right. The glue now shone, smooth and quiet, like a thin line of yellow winter honey.

⑤ He handed me the wooden dowel. "This is the small wooden bone," he said. He showed me a small hole we had not used yet, in the armrest, into which the dowel fitted. "The glue alone," he said, "would hold for perhaps five years. The dowel + glue will hold for **fifty**." I pushed the dowel in slowly. Grandpa pressed the armrest back into place. He adjusted it by **two millimetres** to the left. Then, very carefully, he tightened the two small green **C-clamps** — one above, one below — "just until the glue squeezes out a tiny yellow tear; no further." I watched one small drop of glue appear at the seam. He wiped it with the damp cloth. The chair looked, for the first time in an hour, quiet and normal.

⑥ "Now," Grandpa said, sitting down on a small stool, "we wait **two hours**. Glue, like tea, needs time. A fast glue is a weak glue." He made us two cups of **jasmine tea** in small white cups. We sat on the balcony, not speaking much. The winter plum above us had one tiny green bud — Grandma had planted it in 2011. At **14:45**, we listened to the small sound of the clock in the living room. At **15:40**, a grey bird landed on the railing, looked at the chair, and flew away. At **16:00**, Grandpa closed his eyes for eight minutes. I did not wake him. I watched the chair quietly recover, inside its two small green clamps.

⑦ At **16:10**, we removed the clamps slowly, one at a time. Grandpa ran his thumb along the seam. It was smooth. There was a tiny yellow line where the glue had dried, no thicker than a human hair. He handed me the fine sandpaper. "Not too hard," he said. "We are not sanding wood; we are sanding a whisper." I sanded for four minutes, very gently. The tiny yellow line disappeared. The armrest became, once again, the same warm dark brown as the rest of the chair.

⑧ Grandpa then did something I had not expected. From the bottom drawer of his small bedside table, he took out **a tiny tube of beeswax polish** in a round tin, 38 years old, bought by Grandma in 1989. He opened it carefully — the wax inside was still soft. "Grandma," he said, "used this every winter, on every wooden surface in our home. One small circle, once a year, on a December afternoon." I took a clean cloth, scooped a tiny amount, and rubbed it onto the repaired seam in small slow circles for ten minutes. The wood turned warmer under my hand. The chair smelled, suddenly, of an old honey winter.

⑨ At **16:52**, Grandpa and I together carried the rocking chair back to the small north corner of the balcony, under the winter plum. He sat down in it, very slowly, testing it with his full weight. The chair, thirty-four years old and newly fixed, held him without a sound. He rocked it once, gently, then smiled up at me. "Yiran," he said quietly, "your grandma, I think, is pleased." I did not answer, because I could not trust my voice. At **17:00**, he began his normal evening reading. I went back to our flat. I looked at my hands. There was a tiny smell of wood glue, a tiny smell of beeswax, a tiny smell of jasmine tea — three small smells of a quiet winter afternoon. For the first time in my life, I understood: **some things, when broken, are not meant to be thrown away; they are meant to teach you how to mend.**

**1. According to Paragraph ①, what exactly had happened to the rocking chair, and what is its family history?**

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段时间与家庭史  
【解析】"13:40 / 34 年 / 1993 / 82 元 / 19:00 摇 / 奶奶三年前去世"直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段三步准备  
【解析】"看形状 / 擦尘 / 干试配"直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④⑤段两遍薄胶 + 木销 + 夹具  
【解析】"薄两遍 / 一分钟 / 木销 / 2 毫米 / 夹至挤出一滴"直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】D
