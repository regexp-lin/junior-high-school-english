# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do Onions Make Us Cry?**

① Anyone who has ever stood next to a kitchen chopping-board with a small sharp knife and half a fresh onion knows the quiet, slightly funny moment very well. At first, nothing. Your eyes are fine. You start slicing. For **eight or nine seconds**, the onion simply gives off a familiar sweet-sharp smell. Then, suddenly, without anyone telling them to, your eyes fill with warm clear tears. You cannot stop them. You are not sad. The onion is not spicy on your tongue. Yet, in under ten seconds, a small ordinary vegetable has quietly forced you into a small ordinary cry. This tiny kitchen mystery has a careful chemistry answer — and the answer is actually one of the most beautiful small self-defence systems in the whole plant world.

② **Finding one: the onion does not always have the "crying chemical" inside it.** When an onion is sitting quietly in the fridge, it contains three main things relevant to our story: a sulfur-rich building block called **isoalliin**, an enzyme (a kind of biological scissors) called **alliinase**, and a very special **second enzyme** called **lachrymatory-factor synthase (LFS)**. In an intact onion, these three things are **kept apart in different tiny compartments inside the cells**. The onion is, in this state, completely safe to touch, to smell, and to hold in your hand. **Nothing happens until the cells are broken.**

③ **Finding two: the tears only start when you cut.** The moment your knife slices through the onion, you tear open thousands of tiny plant cells, and the three things — isoalliin, alliinase, and LFS — are suddenly thrown together into one small wet reaction area. Alliinase first cuts isoalliin into a smaller, angry sulfur compound. LFS, the second enzyme, then quickly rearranges it into a sharp, very light gas called **syn-propanethial-S-oxide** — the famous "onion tear gas." This gas rises from the cut surface, **drifts upward**, and reaches your eyes in roughly **2–10 seconds**. (Japanese scientists at the **House Foods laboratory, 2002**, were the first to isolate LFS and prove this was the real "tear factor" — they won an Ig Nobel Prize in **2013** for their work.)

④ **Finding three: your eye does not cry because it is hurt.** Your eye cries because it is **cleaning**. The thin sulfur gas reaches the front of the eye and dissolves in the thin layer of tears on the cornea. A small reaction forms a tiny amount of **weak sulfuric acid** — extremely small, far too weak to hurt the eye. But the **trigeminal nerve**, the large nerve that watches the front of your face for any chemical stress, is very, very sensitive, and it sends an instant signal: *"Something mildly irritating has arrived — flush it out."* The **lacrimal glands**, just above your eyes, immediately release extra tears. The tears wash the sulfur compound away. Within **30 to 60 seconds** of you stopping the cutting, the gas disperses and the tears stop. There is no injury. Your eye simply did its cleaning job.

⑤ **Why does the onion bother to do this?** The answer is quietly clever: the onion is a bulb — a seed-like underground package of food — and in the wild, hungry animals (rabbits, rats, even insects) love to dig it up and eat it. A fresh onion cannot run away. But it can, the moment it is bitten or cut, release a sharp stinging gas into the attacker's eyes, nose, and mouth. Most animals, after one attempt, leave it alone. **The onion's tear gas is not there to annoy your mother in the kitchen; it is an ancient, small, non-lethal alarm system that has kept onions alive for 6,000 years of farming and many more years before.**

⑥ **How to cut an onion without crying (three science-based tricks).** Knowing the chemistry, we can quietly cheat it:

| Trick | Why it works | How well |
|---|---|---|
| **Chill the onion for 20 minutes in the fridge before cutting** | Cold slows enzyme reactions; alliinase and LFS work more slowly at low temperature, producing less tear gas | Very helpful |
| **Cut the onion under a thin stream of cold water, or keep the chopping board wet** | Water dissolves the sulfur gas before it rises to your eyes | Helpful |
| **Use a very sharp knife; do NOT crush the onion** | A clean cut breaks fewer cells than a dull knife that crushes; fewer broken cells = less reaction | Very helpful |
| **Breathe through your mouth, keep your nose away from the cut** | Reduces the amount of gas reaching the eye-area through the air currents created by your breathing | Mild help |
| **Chewing gum while cutting** | Breathing pattern changes and saliva increases; some small home studies suggest mild reduction — no strong science yet | Possibly mild |

⑦ **What about "tearless onions"?** In **2008**, agricultural scientists in **New Zealand and Japan** used careful traditional plant breeding (not genetic modification) to produce onions with **very low LFS activity**. These "tearless onions" still have isoalliin and alliinase, so they still taste like onions — but they produce almost no syn-propanethial-S-oxide gas. They appeared in some supermarkets around 2014. Many chefs, interestingly, **prefer the crying kind**: they say the small sulfur compounds, which break down further when cooked, give the deeper sweet flavour of a well-caramelised onion. A tearless onion, in other words, is a slightly quieter onion in every sense.

⑧ **Is onion tearing bad for you?** No — and sometimes, in a small way, good. A **2021 Tel Aviv University study** of **74 adults** compared tear samples after onion cutting, after sad feelings, and from relaxed rest. The onion tears were chemically **different** from emotional tears: they contained **more antibacterial enzymes** and a **simpler salt profile**. Onion tears, it turns out, give the eye surface a small free wash — which is why some eye doctors quietly note that a kitchen full of onion work rarely harms a healthy eye. (Contact-lens wearers, of course, are a small exception: the tears may briefly fog lenses.)

⑨ **The quiet lesson.** Next time you cut an onion in a kitchen and the warm clear tears come — for no reason you can name — remember: you are not sad. You are not hurt. You are simply standing inside a **6,000-year-old quiet chemistry experiment** that a small underground bulb is performing, one last time, in its own small self-defence. Your eye is doing a small clean. Your knife is doing a small cut. And the onion, which cannot speak and cannot run, is using the only kind of voice it has — a small sulfur whisper that, for two or three seconds in a winter kitchen, turns water into a soft silver line on your cheek.
