# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Christmas Eve Granny and I Made Thirty Handmade Cards for Sunshine Elderly Home**

① On the afternoon of **Christmas Eve 2027**, my granny and I did something we had never done before. From **14:00 to 18:40** — four hours and forty minutes — we sat at the small wooden table in her north-facing kitchen and made, slowly and with quite a lot of red glue on our fingers, **thirty handmade Christmas cards** for the **thirty grandparents who live at Sunshine Elderly Home** on the other side of our compound wall. I had volunteered the idea one week before, on the Friday after the Silent-Reading Morning, when Ms Qin's small notice on the class board said, in soft pencil, *"Sunshine Elderly Home: 30 residents; no family visits expected on 24 Dec for 9 of them; small kind letters warmly welcomed."* I read "**nine of them**" three times. Nine grandparents. Christmas Eve. No visit. I went home that same evening and asked Granny if she might help me make thirty small cards — one for each of the thirty, so no grandparent would, by mistake, feel left out. Granny, who is 72 and whose hands shake a little in winter, said **yes without a second of pause**.

② At **13:45** I arrived at Granny's flat with a paper shopping bag of materials: thirty pieces of soft ivory card, thirty red paper hearts I had pre-cut the night before, a small gold pen, two old Christmas stamps from 2019 that Grandpa had kept in his biscuit tin, a pair of child-scissors with rounded ends, a bottle of white glue, and a printed list of the thirty residents' **first names only** (Ms Qin said surnames should be kept private). Granny had already set the table: a clean cotton cloth, a warm thermos of ginger tea, and a small plate of sliced pear for "tired fingers". She smiled at me and said quietly, in our local dialect, *"Slow. Thirty cards in four hours is about eight minutes a card. We have plenty of time."*

③ We began the first card at **14:00**. Granny, whose handwriting is the neat slow writing of someone who went to primary school in the 1960s, did the **inside words**. I, whose hands are steadier with glue, did the **outside cover**. The rule we made for ourselves was simple: **every card would be different**. No two cards would have the same sentence, the same heart placement, or the same little drawing. Granny said, *"These are grandparents. Grandparents can tell a copy from a real card. Do not hurry, Yiran."* For Resident 1, Grandma Chen, we wrote, *"May your winter tea be warm and your slippers never lost. — From a Grade-8 girl and her granny, 24 Dec."* For Resident 2, Grandpa Wu, we wrote, *"Wishing you one quiet afternoon this week with a book you once loved when you were young."* For Resident 3, Grandma Song, whose list note said *"loves birds"*, I drew a small sparrow on a winter branch with the gold pen.

④ At **16:10**, when we had finished the first twelve cards, Granny's right hand began to tremble a little. She put down the pen and smiled. *"Rest,"* she said gently. *"A card made with a tired hand is not a kind card."* We ate three slices of pear each, drank half a cup of warm ginger tea, and looked out her kitchen window for ten whole minutes — **not talking**, just watching the winter sparrows hop on the bare gingko tree. When we came back to the table, Granny picked up the gold pen again, and I noticed, for the first time in my thirteen years, that her writing was **slower than my mother's but kinder**. Every stroke seemed to carry a small piece of her breath.

⑤ At **17:35**, we finished Card 28 — for Grandpa Lu, whose list note said "was a primary school teacher, retired 1989." Granny wrote: *"Dear Grandpa Lu, thank you for the thirty years you gave small children. Somewhere in a city, a grown-up is still grateful to you. She simply cannot write today, so we write for her."* When Granny put down the pen, her eyes were a little wet, and she did not try to hide them. I said nothing. I slid a small red heart onto the card in the exact centre, which was, perhaps, the first silent thing I had ever done properly in my life.

⑥ At **18:40**, Card 30 was finished. We lined them up on the kitchen table in a neat **five-by-six grid** and stood back. Thirty small cards, thirty different sentences, thirty different little drawings. Granny said softly, *"Yiran, do you see? Not one of them is the best. Not one of them is the worst. They are simply thirty kindnesses, each one sent to a person whose name we barely know."* Then we put on our winter coats, took a brown basket with a red cloth over it, and walked the four hundred metres to Sunshine Elderly Home's reception desk. The kind night receptionist, Ms Ding, counted the thirty cards twice and wrote a small note back for us: *"Received 30. Will be given out at breakfast tomorrow. Thank you, quiet people."*

⑦ On the walk home in the **cold winter air**, I held Granny's left hand — the one that shakes less — and realised something I had not understood before that afternoon. **Kindness is not one big loud present.** Kindness is, more often, thirty small quiet cards, made slowly, over four hours and forty minutes, on a Christmas Eve, by a thirteen-year-old girl and her seventy-two-year-old grandmother, for thirty people whose surnames they will never learn. When I crawled into bed at **21:20**, my fingers still faintly red with glue, I thought, for the first time in my life, that **I did not want Christmas Day to come too fast tomorrow morning**. I wanted this quiet Christmas Eve to stay, just a little bit longer, in the small warm kitchen where my grandmother had, for four hours and forty minutes, taught me what slow kindness looks like.

**注释：** Sunshine Elderly Home 阳光敬老院；ivory card 米白色卡纸；glue 胶水；tremble 颤抖；receptionist 前台接待员
