# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do We Dream at Night?**

① Almost every person on Earth, including you, has spent **about six years of life dreaming** by the time she reaches 70. That is a surprising number: **six whole years of watching small silent films inside your own head**, most of which you will forget the moment your eyes open. Dreaming is so common, so quiet, and so personal that people rarely ask the simple question: **why does the brain do this every single night?** Scientists have been asking this question for more than 120 years, and while they still do not have one perfect answer, they now understand much more than they did when your grandparents were children. This short article will gently explain, in simple words, **four of the most trusted ideas** today.

② To begin, we must know that **dreaming does not happen all night**. A normal young person's sleep has about **four or five cycles** of roughly 90 minutes each. In each cycle, the brain passes through several quieter stages first, and then enters a special stage called **REM sleep** — REM stands for "Rapid Eye Movement", because during this stage your closed eyes move quickly from side to side, as if watching something. **Almost all long, vivid, story-like dreams happen during REM sleep.** A teenager gets about **100 to 120 minutes of REM sleep per night** — that is why one night usually gives two or three rememberable dreams.

③ **Idea One — "Memory sorting."** The most widely supported idea today is that dreaming helps the brain **sort out the things that happened in the day**. During the day, the brain takes in thousands of small pieces of information: the taste of lunch, a maths formula, a kind word, a sad song, a new classmate's name, a forgotten umbrella. At night, during REM sleep, the brain seems to **open many small drawers** and decide which memories go deep into long-term storage, which memories should be connected to other memories, and which small details can be quietly thrown away. This is why students who sleep well before a big test often remember more — not less — the next morning. Some scientists even say "**sleep is the second half of learning**"; the first half is study, and the second half is dreaming it into the brain.

④ **Idea Two — "Emotion washing."** A second trusted idea is that dreams help the brain **work through strong feelings** — fear, sadness, anger, worry, embarrassment. During REM sleep, a small brain area called the **amygdala** (the "feelings room") is very active, but the **stress chemicals** in the body are at their lowest level of the whole day. This unusual combination gives the brain a rare chance to **revisit a difficult feeling in a calm setting**, a bit like re-watching a scary film in bright daylight. Many people find that after a night's sleep, a quarrel with a friend feels smaller. That is not imagination — part of that gentler feeling is built, quietly, during dreams.

⑤ **Idea Three — "Problem-solving in disguise."** A third idea is that dreams help the brain **try unusual answers to real problems**. Because the "logic part" of the brain is quieter in REM sleep, the "imagination part" can **freely connect ideas that would normally never meet**. A student might dream that a maths problem is a door, a friend's face is a key, and the key opens the door. The dream sounds silly in the morning — but the brain has, in fact, **practised flexible thinking**. This is why scientists, writers, and even chemists through history have reported that **new ideas sometimes arrive during or just after a dream**.

⑥ **Idea Four — "Brain maintenance."** A fourth simpler idea is that dreaming is a side effect of **the brain's night-time cleaning work**. During sleep, tiny channels between brain cells **open wider** and allow waste products — built up during a busy day — to be gently washed away by a fluid called CSF (**cerebrospinal fluid**). Dreams may be the small stories the brain tells itself while this quiet cleaning happens in the background — a bit like humming while cleaning your room.

⑦ Do all dreams mean something deep? **Most do not.** A random dream about flying to school in a blue shoe probably means only that your brain is sorting, washing, practising, or cleaning — not predicting the future. But dreams are **never a waste of time**. They are the brain's quiet way of **tidying the day, gentling strong feelings, trying new ideas, and cleaning itself** — all at the same time. So tonight, when you turn off your small lamp and close your eyes, remember: the most useful six years of your life may happen with your eyes shut.
