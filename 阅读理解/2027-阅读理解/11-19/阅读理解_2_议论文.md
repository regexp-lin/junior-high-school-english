# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle-School Students Still Keep a Handwritten Diary in 2027?**

① At our school's "Reading Wall" outside the library, three students have, for the past four months, quietly pinned up the **first sentences** from their handwritten diaries. The sentences are short and almost shy: *"Today the maths test was easier than I had feared."* *"Lunch with Grandma. Egg drop soup. The yellow leaves outside the window."* *"I was angry on the bus this morning. By the third stop, I forgave myself."* On the same wall a typed notice has appeared, asking a sharper question: *"In 2027, with phones, voice notes, and chat groups, do we really still need to write a paper diary?"* My answer, after thinking carefully for a month, is **yes — every middle-school student should keep a small handwritten diary, even now**.

② The first reason is that **handwriting and thinking are deeply connected**. When we type quickly, our fingers move faster than our thoughts; the keyboard rewards speed. When we hold a pen, the speed slows down to the **speed of the wrist**, and our brain has time to choose. A 2019 study from Stavanger University, in Norway, showed that students who wrote by hand showed **stronger brain wave patterns** linked to memory and meaning than students who typed the same words. A diary, written by hand, is therefore not the same diary as a typed one — even if the words look the same in the end.

③ The second reason is **a quiet kind of honesty**. A typed diary, if it lives on a phone, sits next to fifty other apps. The same finger that has just scrolled through a noisy short-video feed cannot easily turn to write a quiet sentence. A paper diary, however, opens **a different room** — a small, slow, lamp-lit corner where no notification appears, no algorithm watches. In that room, a fourteen-year-old can be honest in a way she rarely is online. She can write *"I felt small today"* without worrying that anyone might "like" or screenshot it.

④ The third reason is **memory across years**. A typed diary depends on a server, an app version, a cloud account, and a phone that may be lost or sold. A paper diary depends on **a single shoebox in your wardrobe**. Many adults today still own letters from their grandparents — written in 1985, on yellowing paper, in faded blue ink. Almost no one still has the text messages they sent in 2009. The simple physical reality is: **paper outlives software**. The small paper diary you start in Grade 7 may travel with you to your own grandchildren.

⑤ Some will say, "But typing is faster, and we are very busy." This is true. The answer is not to write a long diary. A small daily note — three lines, five minutes — is enough. Many famous writers have done exactly this for decades. Even three lines a day, written by hand, are 1,000 short, honest pages by the time you reach Grade 9. That is your own quiet library, and no one else's.

⑥ Others worry, "My handwriting is ugly." This is also true, and not at all important. A diary is **not handwriting practice**; it is a private conversation with yourself. The same diary that begins in shaky Grade-7 letters slowly becomes neater by Grade 9. The diary will grow with you; you do not have to be ready before you start. Your Grade-7 self does not need beautiful pages — she needs your honest sentences.

⑦ A more serious worry is **privacy**. A handwritten diary, found by a sister or a parent, can hurt feelings. The answer is two small habits. First, keep the diary in **one fixed place** in your room, and ask your family — kindly but clearly — never to open it without your permission. In a 2024 city poll, 82% of parents said they would respect this request if asked. Second, **do not write what you would never want any human to read**. A diary should be honest, but it should not be a weapon — even against yourself.

⑧ What kind of paper diary works best at our age? Not too big — A6 or A5 is enough; a thick, stitched book with a simple cloth cover is friendlier than a fancy one with a tiny lock. The lock teaches you to be ashamed; the unlocked diary, kept in trust, teaches your family to respect your inner space. Cost should be small, around **15 to 30 yuan**; that price is **about a week of your morning milk**, and the gift to yourself lasts a year.

⑨ This Friday evening, before homework, I will spend five minutes writing one new page. Today's three lines, in advance: *"The sky was grey but kind. I forgot my pencil case in the dining hall — and someone returned it. I want to be that someone for a stranger this winter."* In 2027, with all the bright apps in our pockets, choosing the slower, quieter option is itself a small act of courage. **A handwritten diary is not a return to the past; it is a careful gift, sent forward, from the fourteen-year-old you are today to every older you who will ever exist.** Send it. Five minutes. Tonight.
