# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Saturday I Took Grandpa to the Old Barber Shop**

① Grandpa is seventy-three this year. He has lived in the same yellow building on **East Lane No. 7** for forty-two years, and once a month — always on a Saturday — he walks across the alley to **Uncle Wang's old barber shop**, three doors down from the noodle stall, to have his thin grey hair cut. Last Saturday, however, was different. On Friday evening Grandpa had slipped on the wet kitchen floor; nothing was broken, but his right knee was very stiff. So Mum, on the family group chat, asked the small question: "Who can take Grandpa to Uncle Wang's tomorrow?" Within thirty seconds, before any adult could answer, I had typed: "**I will.**"

② I am fourteen. I had walked past Uncle Wang's shop a thousand times — the small pink-and-white striped pole turning slowly in the window, the sound of small scissors snapping like crickets, the warm tea-leaf smell that floated out every time the door opened. But I had never gone in. The shop was not for children my age. It was for older men in grey jackets who sat in the same chair every month and said almost nothing.

③ At 09:30 Saturday I knocked on Grandpa's door. He was already dressed in his good navy coat — the one with the small bone buttons — and was carefully holding a **small square paper packet** in his left hand. "What's inside, Yeye?" I asked. He smiled. "Wang's favourite tea leaves. I bring him a small packet every December. This year a bit early, since you are with me." I had never seen this side of Grandpa. To his children he was the strict accountant; to his small grandson, the patient story-teller; to Uncle Wang, apparently, **a friend who never forgets a tea-leaf habit**.

④ We walked slowly. Grandpa held my left elbow with his thin warm hand. The alley smelled of fried bean cakes and a little of cold December air. I matched my step to his — long-short, long-short — like a careful, easy song. At Uncle Wang's door, the small bell rang once. Inside, three men were waiting on the wooden bench, reading old newspapers. One of them looked up, smiled, and said: "Lin Lao, today you bring a young guard." Grandpa laughed quietly. The barber — Uncle Wang himself, now sixty-eight — wiped his hands on a clean towel, walked over, took the small tea packet, and pressed it onto a high shelf above the mirror, **next to four older identical packets** from the past four Decembers.

⑤ Then Grandpa sat down on the heavy black leather chair. Uncle Wang fastened a soft white cape around his neck. He did not ask Grandpa what kind of haircut. He never had. The two old men spoke quietly while the small scissors moved: about the price of cabbage, about whether the lake near the South Gate would freeze this year, about a third old friend who had moved to Shenzhen to live with his daughter. Behind them, in the mirror, I saw a fourteen-year-old in a green coat sitting on the wooden bench with a small camera-quiet smile. I had never thought a haircut could be a kind of **slow conversation**.

⑥ Half-way through, Uncle Wang turned to me. "You hold the small comb for me, young guard?" I stood up at once. The comb was warm from his hand. He showed me, without any hurry, how to lift one section of grey hair, how to keep it just above the cape so that the cut hairs fell away cleanly. "Three things make a good haircut," he said softly, "**a sharp pair of scissors, a steady wrist, and a person you have known for a long time.** I have all three today." Grandpa, eyes closed, smiled. The whole shop felt warm and quiet at the same time, like a small room inside a longer winter.

⑦ When the haircut was finished, Uncle Wang did not take any money. Grandpa pretended to be angry — "Wang, this is the third time this year!" — and pushed a 30-yuan note onto the counter. Uncle Wang quietly slipped it into a small **red children's envelope** marked *"Lihua Children's Reading Corner"* on the side; the corner he had supported for nine years, two streets away. "Lin Lao," he said, "your money still goes to a child. Just not your own grandson." Grandpa looked at me and laughed.

⑧ On the way home, Grandpa held my elbow a little less tightly. The slight stiffness in his knee had not gone, but his shoulders were softer, his back straighter. Outside Building No. 7, before he climbed the small step, he turned and said quietly, "Today you saw something. You saw that two men who have known each other for **forty years** can sit in one small room and feel the world is enough." Then he gave me half of an orange-flavoured sweet from his coat pocket — old style, in soft white paper — and went in.

⑨ I walked home slowly. The afternoon light was the colour of warm tea. I thought about the four small tea packets on Uncle Wang's high shelf — and how, by next December, there will be five. I thought about the slow scissors and the careful comb. I thought about how Grandpa's monthly Saturday haircut is not really about the hair at all. **It is about a quiet promise two old men keep to each other across the years**, and about a small grandson who, on one cold Saturday in November, was lucky enough to walk into the room and see how grown-ups, when they have grown well, take care of each other.

**注释：** barber 理发师；alley 巷子；scissors 剪刀；cape 围布；envelope 信封

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Why did the writer take Grandpa to the barber shop?**

A. Because Grandpa wanted to buy tea leaves.  
B. Because on Friday evening Grandpa slipped on the wet kitchen floor and his right knee was stiff; Mum asked on the family group chat for someone to take Grandpa, and the writer volunteered.  
C. Because the writer wanted a haircut herself.  
D. Because the school sent the writer there.

**2. What was inside the small square paper packet Grandpa was holding?**

A. Money for the haircut.  
B. Uncle Wang's favourite tea leaves; Grandpa brings a small packet every December — and four identical older packets from the past four Decembers were already on the high shelf.  
C. A new comb.  
D. A birthday card.

**3. What three things did Uncle Wang say make a good haircut?**

A. Loud music, a fast cut, and a tip.  
B. A sharp pair of scissors, a steady wrist, and a person you have known for a long time.  
C. New scissors, new mirrors, and new soap.  
D. A clean cape, soft tea, and quiet music.

**4. What did Uncle Wang do with the 30 yuan Grandpa pushed onto the counter?**

A. He kept it for himself.  
B. He gave it back to Grandpa.  
C. He quietly slipped it into a small red children's envelope marked "Lihua Children's Reading Corner" — a corner he had supported for nine years.  
D. He used it to buy more tea leaves.

**5. What does Grandpa mean by saying "two men who have known each other for forty years can sit in one small room and feel the world is enough"?**

A. The shop is large and busy.  
B. A long, quiet friendship between two people can make daily life feel complete; the haircut is not really about the hair, but about a quiet promise two old men keep across the years.  
C. The barber shop is the centre of the world.  
D. People should not move to other cities.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段背景  
【解析】膝盖不便 + 家庭群消息直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 + 第④段 "four older identical packets"  
【解析】茶叶礼五年传统直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 Uncle Wang 三件事直引  
【解析】直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段红色信封  
【解析】公益捐赠直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑧、⑨段感悟  
【解析】友谊 + 长情之约的主旨。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
