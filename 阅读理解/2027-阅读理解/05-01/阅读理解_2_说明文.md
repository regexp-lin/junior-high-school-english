# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do Geese Fly in a V-Shape?**

① On autumn and spring mornings in many parts of China, people looking up at the sky may see a strange, neat picture: a line of birds flying across the clouds in a tidy **V-shape**. They are usually **wild geese**, travelling thousands of kilometres between their summer homes in the north and their winter homes in the south. Why do they fly in such a clear pattern? Is it just beautiful, or does the V-shape actually help them in their long journey?

② Scientists have studied this question for more than fifty years. The first important clue came from the way birds' wings make air move. When a bird flaps its wings, the air on the **tips of its wings** is pushed upward in a swirl. In a V-shape, each bird behind the leader flies a little to the **side and just behind** the wings of the bird in front. There, the air is still spinning gently upward. The follower receives a small, **free lift**, the way a small boat is carried by a bigger boat's wake.

③ How much energy does this save? In 2014, a study in the journal *Nature* used tiny GPS recorders on **bald ibises** (a kind of bird that also flies in V-shape). The scientists found that birds in good V positions used **up to 20 percent less energy** than a single bird flying alone. Over a journey of a thousand kilometres, this can be the difference between arriving with strong muscles, or falling from the sky in the middle of the sea.

④ The V-shape also helps the birds **see one another**. Unlike cars on a straight road, birds do not have bumpers or mirrors. If they flew in a straight line, they could not watch their neighbours well. In a V, every bird can see the one in front and the one behind, **adjust speed** if somebody slows, and shout to the group if there is a storm or an enemy hawk below.

⑤ Interestingly, the leader of a V is not always the same bird. Flying at the front is the hardest work — the leader meets the strongest air with no free lift from anyone. So geese often **swap leaders** every few minutes. When the front bird is tired, she drops back; another bird, stronger now, moves to the front. This kind of **teamwork** is one of the reasons wild geese can cross oceans and mountain ranges.

⑥ Human engineers have noticed this clever trick. Some studies for future **airplanes** and for **military drones** suggest that flying in groups, with careful V spacing, could save fuel, exactly as it does for birds. The next time you see a line of geese cutting across a blue sky, do not only say, "What a pretty picture!" Remember that this V is, in fact, an **ancient flying machine** — built by nature long before the first human plane rose into the air.
