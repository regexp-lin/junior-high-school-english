# 阅读理解（议论文） **Is It a Good Idea for Teenagers to Watch Short Videos Every Day?**

① Every morning, at our school gate, you can see small screens glowing in students' hands. A quick 15-second dance, a 30-second funny cat, a one-minute street food clip — and the bell rings. In China, teenagers now spend on average **more than 90 minutes a day** watching short videos. Some call them "vitamin tablets for the mind": small, sweet, easy to take. But many teachers and parents ask a harder question: **is watching short videos every day really a good thing for middle school students?**

② People who support short videos list some real **benefits**. A 90-second clip about the Three Gorges Dam can teach a student things a textbook cannot, with music, drones, and colour. Videos made by kind strangers can introduce a shy student to the **wider world**: Japanese train stations, African farms, Russian winter markets. A well-made short video is like a **window**. Compared with long TV programmes, short videos are also **free**, easy to pause, and safe to watch for a few minutes between classes.

③ Opponents, however, point to a growing pile of problems. Short videos are **designed to be easy**. Each clip is just long enough to give a quick feeling — a laugh, a surprise, a small bit of news — and just short enough to leave you wanting another. Without noticing, a student may start with "two videos" and look up an hour later. Sleep is cut. Homework is rushed. Eyes become tired. Over time, the brain may find **thick books harder to read** because it has learned to expect a new surprise every 30 seconds.

④ Another worry is content **quality**. Not every clip is a window; some are walls. Teens can easily meet videos that teach wrong grammar, show dangerous stunts, or sell fake products. Unlike a school library, no librarian checks every short video. The "trending" page often shows what is **exciting**, not what is **true**. If students do not learn how to **read short videos carefully**, they may take home pocketfuls of half-truths instead of knowledge.

⑤ In my view, the answer is not "ban everything", but **"manage it wisely"**. Short videos can be friends of learning — if the total time is **limited** (say, 30–45 minutes a day), and if the topics are **mixed**: news, science, sports, music, and English learning. Watching together with parents can also help — parents can ask simple questions like "Do you believe this?" and "Why did the maker post it?" This small habit turns passive watching into quiet training in **critical thinking**.

⑥ Schools, on their side, can teach a new kind of lesson: "**Reading short videos**", like reading books. They can show students how to check sources, how to find the original news, and how to tell a clever trick from a real fact. Short videos, like salt, can make life tasty or make a dish unfit to eat. It depends on **how much** we use, and **how carefully**. The middle school brain is young and fast; it deserves better than endless sugar, and it is strong enough to learn to choose.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段末问题  
【解析】讨论刷短视频对初中生是否有益。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段  
【解析】短视频可开阔视野。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "Sleep is cut... eyes tired... thick books harder"  
【解析】睡眠、视力、专注均受影响。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段  
【解析】内容未经审查，良莠不齐。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨归纳题  
【定位】第⑤–⑥段  
【解析】限时+混题材+共同观看+批判思维。故选 C。
