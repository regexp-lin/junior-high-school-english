# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle Schools Ban Mobile Phones on Campus?**

① A few months ago, a district in our city announced a new rule: from September, **no student** in public middle schools is allowed to use a mobile phone during school hours, and phones must be kept in a locker from 8:00 to 16:30. Some parents cheered the rule at once; other parents said it was "too rough". In our own classroom, the debate has been loud too. After listening to both sides and reading reports from several cities, I believe **a full ban is the right choice, with only a few careful exceptions**.

② Those against a full ban raise three main worries. **First**, parents want to reach their children quickly, especially in big cities where a student may take two buses home. **Second**, many classes now use the phone for quick tools — online dictionaries, note photos, short teacher polls. **Third**, they say a ban will not teach self-control; it only **hides the problem** until after 16:30, when students will use phones even more. All three points deserve honest answers.

③ On **contact with parents**, the answer is simple. Every middle school in our country already has a **public telephone** at the front desk and a **landline in each head teacher's office**. In real emergencies — a sick grandmother, a change of pick-up time — parents can reach their child **within five minutes**. The real question is not: "Can my child receive my message at any second?" It is: "Do I really need to send a non-urgent message during my child's maths lesson?" In most cases, the honest answer is no.

④ On **phones as school tools**, the picture looks useful at first. Yes, a phone can check a word. But recent research from **several Chinese provinces** has found that when the same phone is open for "useful search", it is also open for **short videos, games and chat notifications**. In one middle school, when teachers allowed phones for dictionary use during a writing lesson, more than **one third** of the students had switched to a game or a social app within **fifteen minutes**. A dictionary is lighter than a phone, and a paper one leaves no notifications.

⑤ The biggest argument, however, is about **attention**. Scientists now understand that the human brain is not very good at switching between tasks. Every quick look at a notification — even just one second — costs up to **several minutes** before the mind fully returns to the lesson. A phone in the pocket, set on silent, still quietly takes a slice of attention; the student knows it might buzz. Studies at schools that removed phones have found students reading **more books**, sleeping **slightly better**, and reporting feeling **less anxious** during the day.

⑥ On **self-control**, those against a ban are partly right: hiding the problem is not solving it. Yet the classroom is not the best place to learn control over an addictive tool. Adults, who have much more self-control than teenagers, still struggle with their phones in offices all over the world. Asking a 13-year-old to "just say no" to a colourful app during a long double-period is not training; it is a **test designed to fail**. Self-control can be taught at home, on the way to school, and in limited after-school time — not inside a lesson on ancient Chinese history.

⑦ A good ban, though, must be **thoughtful**, not harsh. I suggest four rules. **One**, phones enter school inside **locked class lockers** each morning and are returned at 16:30; nobody is strip-searched. **Two**, **emergency calls** are always possible at the head teacher's office, within five minutes. **Three**, teachers who want a short phone activity can request **school tablets** from the library for that lesson only. **Four**, after-school clubs that truly need a phone (for example, a photography club) may use it under a clear teacher's watch.

⑧ There is also a quieter benefit that no study can fully measure. Without phones, students **look at each other more**. The lunch break is not a silent sea of bowed heads under bright screens; it is a noisy, playful place where friendships grow. After a phone-free term, many students themselves report a surprising feeling: "It was lonely for two days. Then I started talking to Xiao Chen properly for the first time in months."

⑨ Middle-school years are short. Every hour spent scrolling is an hour not spent reading a novel, arguing over a football match, or daydreaming out of a window. A well-designed phone ban is not a punishment; it is a **quiet gift of time** that most adults secretly wish they still had. For a few short years, let us give students the same gift.

---

**注释：** locker 储物柜；notification 通知；addictive 容易上瘾的；strip-search 搜身

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion about banning mobile phones on middle-school campuses?**

A. A full ban is wrong in every case.  
B. A full ban is the right choice, with only a few careful exceptions.  
C. Mobile phones should be free for use at any time in class.  
D. Only Grade 9 students should be allowed to use phones.

**2. According to paragraph 3, how can parents reach their children in real emergencies at school, even if a ban is in place?**

A. Through the child's hidden phone in the school bag.  
B. By email only, with a wait of several days.  
C. Through the public phone at the front desk or the landline in the head teacher's office.  
D. They cannot reach the child at all.

**3. What did research in a middle school find about "using phones as a dictionary" in writing lessons?**

A. All students used the phone only for dictionary work.  
B. More than one third of the students switched to a game or social app within fifteen minutes.  
C. Students read more books during the lesson.  
D. Students finished their writing twenty minutes earlier.

**4. Why does the writer say asking a 13-year-old to "just say no" to a phone during a long double-period is a "test designed to fail"?**

A. Because teenagers are lazy.  
B. Because even adults, with more self-control, still struggle with phones in offices worldwide.  
C. Because teachers never check phones.  
D. Because schools are too quiet.

**5. Which is **NOT** one of the four rules the writer suggests for a "thoughtful" ban?**

A. Phones kept in locked class lockers and returned at 16:30.  
B. Emergency calls are always possible at the head teacher's office within five minutes.  
C. Teachers may use school tablets for short phone activities.  
D. Students' home phones must be taken away after school as well.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段 "a full ban is the right choice, with only a few careful exceptions"  
【解析】作者主张整体禁用并设少量例外。A、C、D 均极端或无据。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "public telephone at the front desk and a landline in each head teacher's office"  
【解析】学校前台电话与班主任座机皆可。A、B、D 均不合文意。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "more than one third of the students had switched to a game or a social app within fifteen minutes"  
【解析】15 分钟内超过三分之一同学已转到游戏或社交软件。A、C、D 均与原文不符。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑥段 "Adults, who have much more self-control than teenagers, still struggle with their phones in offices"  
【解析】连自控更强的成年人都做不到，何况少年。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节判断题（否定型）  
【定位】第⑦段四条建议  
【解析】作者从未主张没收家中电话。A、B、C 均为其建议。故选 D。
