# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Parents Be Allowed to Read Their Children's Diaries?**

① Last month, a Grade 8 girl in a nearby school found her mother sitting on her bed, **holding her diary open**. The girl cried. Her mother said, "I am your mother. I have the right to know." The girl said, "But this is my heart. It is not a report card." Within two days, the video of their argument went around the city. The question came back to many dinner tables across the country: **should parents be allowed to read their children's diaries?** I think the fair answer is **no, not without serious reason and not without asking**, and below is why.

② First, a diary is not simply a notebook. It is a **private, honest place** where a young person tries to understand himself or herself. Teenagers write down strong feelings — about their dreams, fears, anger, even small crushes — precisely because no one is watching. If a diary is secretly read, the owner no longer trusts the page. The writing becomes **polite and empty**, a performance for unseen eyes. A diary that everyone can read is no longer a diary; it is a school composition.

③ Second, our growth needs a **small room of our own**. Between the ages of twelve and sixteen, we begin to build a sense of "self" that is slightly separate from our parents. This is natural and healthy. A locked diary is one of the tiny private spaces that helps this building. When parents read the diary without permission, they do not only read words; they also knock down a wall that was quietly protecting our new self.

④ Third, it is a question of **trust**, and trust is a two-way street. Many parents, when asked why they opened a diary, say: "I was worried; I had to know." This is true love, and I do not question the worry. But if we teach children that being loved means being watched, we teach them that love is **a form of control**. Trust, on the other hand, says: "I believe you, and I will ask you when I want to know." Children who grow up trusted are more likely, not less, to talk openly with their parents in hard times.

⑤ Of course, the other side has real points too. Parents have a **duty of care**. If a teenager shows signs of serious sadness, of being bullied, or of doing something dangerous, a parent cannot simply shrug and say, "That is her diary." In such cases, the **worry is bigger than privacy**. I would agree that, under clear signs of danger, a parent may read the diary — **after first telling the child** and, if possible, with the child present.

⑥ Some might reply: "If I tell her first, she will hide the diary." Perhaps, at first. But this is the very point: the goal of a parent is **not to catch the child by surprise**, but to open a conversation. Hiding a diary more carefully is only a new problem; the old problem — the reason for sadness or danger — will still be there. Real safety comes from talking, not from spying.

⑦ There is also a practical argument. Parents who **often read diaries secretly** are often the parents who complain most loudly that their children "tell them nothing". This is not a coincidence. The child feels secretly watched and slowly closes the inner door, not only to the diary but to conversation as well. Secret reading may calm a parent for one evening; it can cost a whole year of talking.

⑧ To be fair, the responsibility is not only on parents. Children, too, should be willing to **share some parts** of their inner life: their worries, big changes, new friends, difficult choices. A diary should never become a **secret fortress** that keeps all of life away from parents. A healthy family is one where some things are private but many things are shared.

⑨ So where is the line? I would suggest three simple rules. **One**, parents should **ask before reading**, and accept a "no" unless there is a clear danger. **Two**, children should **speak up regularly** — not every sentence of the diary, but the main storms and joys. **Three**, both sides should agree that a closed book is not an enemy; it is a small garden where a young person is quietly growing up. With these rules, a diary does not divide a family — it becomes a gentle training ground for **respect, trust and honest talk**.

---

**注释：** crush 好感；compose 作文；duty of care 看护责任；fortress 堡垒；spying 偷看

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the writer's main opinion about parents reading their children's diaries?**

A. Parents should always freely read any diary without asking.  
B. Parents should never read a diary, in any situation.  
C. Parents should not read without serious reason and without asking first.  
D. Children should not be allowed to keep a diary at all.

**2. According to paragraph 2, why is a diary not simply an ordinary notebook?**

A. Because it is more expensive than other notebooks.  
B. Because it is a private place where young people try to understand themselves honestly.  
C. Because it contains only correct school homework.  
D. Because teachers check it every week.

**3. Which of the following would the writer most likely accept as a reasonable time for a parent to read a diary?**

A. When the parent is simply curious about daily school news.  
B. When the parent wants to know about a small argument with a friend.  
C. When there are clear signs of serious sadness or danger, **and after telling the child**.  
D. Anytime, because children have no right to privacy.

**4. According to paragraph 7, what long-term price may parents pay if they read diaries secretly?**

A. The children will read their parents' diaries in return.  
B. The children will buy more diaries.  
C. The children may slowly close their inner door, leading to a whole year of lost talking.  
D. The parents will forget the words they read.

**5. Which of the following is **NOT** one of the writer's "three simple rules"?**

A. Parents should ask before reading and accept "no" unless there is clear danger.  
B. Children should speak up regularly about main storms and joys.  
C. Both sides should respect a closed diary as a small garden of growth.  
D. Parents should forbid their children to keep any locked book at home.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段 "no, not without serious reason and not without asking"  
【解析】作者并非一概禁止，而是强调"非严重情况不读且须事先沟通"。A、B、D 均极端。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第②段 "a private, honest place where a young person tries to understand himself or herself"  
【解析】日记是私密、诚实的自我探索空间。A、C、D 均偏题。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "under clear signs of danger, a parent may read the diary — after first telling the child"  
【解析】作者仅在明显危险且提前告知的情形下认可阅读。A、B、D 均不符合作者立场。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段 "the child ... slowly closes the inner door ... can cost a whole year of talking"  
【解析】偷看可能让孩子关闭心门，带来长期的交流损失。A、B、D 均无依据。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节判断题（否定型）  
【定位】第⑨段三条建议  
【解析】作者从未主张禁止孩子保留日记，反而认可其为"私人花园"。A、B、C 均为三条建议。故选 D。
