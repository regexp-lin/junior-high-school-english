# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do We Yawn?**

① On a warm Monday afternoon, the teacher writes a long page on the blackboard. Suddenly, Ming in the back row **yawns**. Half a second later, Lily beside him yawns, too. Then Wang opens his mouth. Even the teacher, after a short pause, yawns quietly behind her hand. This strange chain of open mouths happens every day, in every country. But **why do we yawn**, and why is it so easy to "catch" a yawn from someone else?

② Yawning is more than being sleepy or bored. Scientists have watched people, babies, and even **unborn babies** in their mothers' bodies, and they have found that yawning starts **before birth**. A baby still in the mother's body yawns from around 12 weeks of age. Cats, dogs, chickens, and even some **fish** yawn too. Something this old and widespread in the animal world must have an important job.

③ One common idea is that yawning brings **more oxygen** into our blood. When we yawn, we open our mouths wide, and our lungs pull in a large amount of air. Some scientists used to believe tired people yawned because their blood "needed" fresh oxygen. However, newer research has questioned this. When volunteers breathed pure oxygen, they did not yawn less. So oxygen alone cannot be the whole answer.

④ A newer and more accepted idea is the **"brain-cooling" theory**. The human brain works best when its temperature is a little **cooler** than the body. When we are tired, our brain may heat up slightly. A big, deep yawn brings cool air across the mouth and nose, and at the same time stretches the muscles of the face and neck, helping **blood flow** to the brain. In this way, a yawn acts a little like a small **fan** for the tired mind.

⑤ What about "catching" a yawn? Scientists say this is **contagious yawning**, and it has a lot to do with **empathy** — the ability to share another person's feelings. People who have strong empathy often catch yawns more easily. Babies usually do not catch yawns; they begin to do so around four to five years of age, when they start to understand other people's feelings. Interestingly, this pattern matches our social growth.

⑥ Some people worry that yawning in class is rude. In fact, a few yawns are a message from your body: "I need a quick break, some fresh air, or a sip of water." Teachers who understand this may open a window or plan a short stretch. Of course, you can still **cover your mouth** politely with your hand. The next time you see a friend yawn and then yawn yourself, do not feel embarrassed. You are just showing a very old, very human way of sharing a quiet signal with another brain.

**1. What is the main idea of the article?**

A. How to teach babies to read.

**2. At what stage does yawning begin in humans?**

B. Because yawning helps only sleep.

**4. According to the "brain-cooling" theory, what does a big yawn do?**

A. It makes the brain hotter.

D. It fills the lungs with smoke.

**5. Why do babies usually NOT catch yawns from other people?**

A. Babies never see adults yawn.  
B. Babies do not have mouths.  
C. Because catching yawns is linked with empathy, which develops around age 4–5.

【答案】C

【答案】B
