# 阅读理解（说明文） **Chinese High-Speed Rail — Running Into the Future**

① In the early 1990s, many places in China could only be reached by slow trains that **rattled through the night** for 20 or 30 hours. Going from Beijing to Shanghai once felt like a two-day holiday on its own. Today, the same trip takes only about **4 hours and 30 minutes**, and the train looks like a long silver arrow. This is the story of **China's high-speed rail**, known as **CRH** (中国铁路高速).

② The first CRH line opened between **Beijing and Tianjin in 2008**, just before the Beijing Olympic Games. At that time, the total length of China's high-speed lines was under 700 kilometres. By the end of **2025**, the network had grown to **over 45,000 kilometres**, the longest in the world. You can now travel by high-speed rail from snowy Harbin in the north all the way to warm Kunming in the south.

③ What makes the trains so fast? Three main reasons. First, the **tracks** are perfectly straight and smooth, set on strong concrete beds. Second, the **wheels and bodies** are light but very tough. Third, computers quietly control the speed and the gap with the train in front, keeping everyone safe. Top trains can run at **350 km/h**, yet the coffee on your table hardly moves.

④ High-speed rail has changed everyday life. A teacher in Nanjing can travel to a workshop in Beijing and come home on the same day. Farmers in Guizhou send fresh mushrooms to Shenzhen by a special "cold-chain" high-speed train, arriving in less than ten hours. On the **Qinghai–Sichuan line**, opened in 2026, students living in small mountain towns can now reach provincial libraries in one morning, a trip that once needed a whole day of bus rides.

⑤ Chinese high-speed trains are also travelling abroad — sometimes as helpful teachers. Engineers from China have helped build high-speed lines in **Indonesia** and **Thailand**, and the Jakarta–Bandung line has cut a 3-hour road trip to about 45 minutes. China is now planning "green" trains that use less energy, make less noise, and can work in very cold deserts.

⑥ Behind every silver train there are thousands of drivers, engineers, cleaners and station workers. Behind every smooth ride, there are countless experiments, mistakes and long nights of study. Next time you feel the quiet hum of a high-speed train pulling away from the platform, remember: **you are not just travelling across the map — you are travelling through one of modern China's proudest stories.**
