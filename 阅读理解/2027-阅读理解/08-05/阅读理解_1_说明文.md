# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do We Dream at Night?**

① Last night, you may have travelled back to your old primary school, flown above a city without a plane, or had a long conversation with a grandmother who is no longer alive. In the morning, most of it was gone before you finished brushing your teeth. Dreams are strange: they feel real in the night and unreal in the day. Almost every human being dreams, yet we still cannot fully answer a simple question — **why do we dream?** Modern research in biology and psychology can now offer some careful, if partial, answers.

② First, we need to know **when** dreams happen. Sleep is not a single state; it moves in circles. Each night we go through 4 or 5 "sleep cycles", and each cycle lasts about 90 minutes. Inside a cycle, we fall first into deep sleep, and later into a stage called **REM sleep**. "REM" means "rapid eye movement": our eyelids are closed, but underneath, the eyes move quickly from side to side, and brain activity becomes almost as strong as when we are awake. **Most of our vivid dreams happen in REM sleep.** This is why, if you wake up in the last part of the night (when REM sleep is longest), you are more likely to remember your dreams.

③ Curiously, during REM sleep, the muscles of our arms and legs are almost completely turned off. Scientists believe this is a kind of **safety system**. If the body could still move, we might get up and run around while dreaming, which could be dangerous. The brain's night theatre is open, but the doors of the body are quietly locked.

④ The second question is **why the brain creates dreams at all**. There is no single answer, but three theories are widely discussed.

⑤ One theory is that dreams are **a way to sort and save memories**. During the day, we take in a huge amount of information: names, faces, words, smells, the rule of a maths question. While we sleep, the brain seems to review these pieces, mark what is important, and gently let go of what is not. In many experiments, people who slept after learning something scored better on a test than those who stayed awake. Dreams may be what this sorting looks like "from the inside".

⑥ A second theory is that dreams are **a kind of emotional repair**. Our days are full of small worries — a quarrel with a friend, a difficult question in class, a scary news story. In dreams, the brain may replay these feelings but in a safer, stranger form. Some researchers even say the brain turns down certain "fear chemicals" during REM sleep, so that we can face these feelings without panic. When morning comes, the emotion has often softened.

⑦ A third theory treats dreams as **mental rehearsal**. In some dreams, we are running from danger, solving a problem, or trying to reach a goal. These scenes may be our ancient brain "practising" — rehearsing how to deal with difficulties we might meet in real life. This is why, after a hard day of sport or study, dreams often carry pictures of the same activity.

⑧ We should also correct a few common ideas. It is **not true** that "only clever people dream", or that "dreams always predict the future". Every healthy human dreams every night, whether or not they remember. And while some dreams may feel like signs, most of the time they are **echoes of the past day**, not messages from tomorrow. If a dream gives you a fresh idea or warm feeling in the morning, enjoy it; if it frightens you, remember it is just your brain's night work.

⑨ We still cannot fully explain every dream. Why a teapot becomes a dragon; why your best friend turns into a stranger; why a dream repeats for months — these remain partly mysterious. But the direction is clear: dreams are not "nothing", and they are not magic. They are one of the many quiet ways the sleeping brain takes care of us, sorting our memories, calming our feelings, and preparing us, gently, for a new day.
