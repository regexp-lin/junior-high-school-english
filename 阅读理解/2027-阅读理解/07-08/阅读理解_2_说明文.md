# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Do Migrating Birds Find Their Way Home?**

Every autumn, when the days grow cooler, something quiet and amazing happens in the sky. Millions of birds — from tiny warblers weighing less than a pencil to large geese the size of small dogs — leave their summer homes in the north and fly thousands of kilometres south. In spring, they come back to the very same forest, the very same tree, and sometimes the very same nest. How do they find their way? For many years, scientists have asked the same question, and the answer is surprising: birds do not use one map — they use several, all at once.

The first "map" is the **sun**. Birds that fly during the day watch the sun's position and know it moves across the sky from east to west. They also have an inner clock inside their brain. By combining the time of day with the direction of the sun, the bird can work out which way is south. Cloudy days may confuse us, but birds can still find direction through a different kind of light: **polarized** light that comes through thin clouds. Our eyes cannot notice it; theirs can.

The second "map" is the **stars**. Many small songbirds fly at night, because the air is cooler and there are fewer enemies. At night they cannot use the sun. Instead, young birds learn which part of the sky turns the least — around the **North Star** in the Northern Hemisphere. This learning happens when the birds are very young, often in their first summer. Once their little brain has memorised the "quiet centre" of the turning sky, they can read the night sky as easily as we read a clock.

The third "map" is **Earth's magnetic field**. Our planet behaves like a huge soft magnet, with weak invisible lines going from south to north. Tiny bits of magnetic material inside the heads of some birds act like a natural compass. Even in a closed dark room, a bird that is ready to migrate will turn its body in a fixed direction. Scientists have also found that some birds "see" the magnetic field through a special chemical reaction in their eyes — light hitting the eye helps the bird detect direction almost as colour.

The fourth "map" is the **land itself**. Birds remember the shape of rivers, mountains, coastlines, and big cities along their route. Older birds who have travelled before often lead younger ones, teaching them how to read these landmarks. A wrong turn usually means extra hours of flying and less food, so accuracy matters.

What happens when one of these maps fails? Amazingly, birds use the others. In a famous experiment, scientists kept birds in a room with an artificial sky and changed the position of "the stars". The birds adjusted their direction to match the new sky. When the stars were removed, they followed the magnetic field. When both were wrong, they slowed down but did not give up — they waited, searched, and tried again.

The more we learn about bird migration, the clearer one fact becomes: nature is not a simple line. It is a careful, patient web of sunlight, stars, magnetism, and memory. A small warbler, with a brain smaller than your little finger, manages to read all of these at once — and come home. Perhaps the next time we feel lost, we can remember that even a bird weighing only ten grams finds its way. Then, maybe, we can too.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】sun/stars/magnetic field/land  
【解析】共四种"地图"。A/B/D 均错。故选 C。

**2.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"polarized light... Our eyes cannot notice it; theirs can"  
【解析】利用偏振光。A/B/D 均错。故选 C。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"learning happens when the birds are very young, often in their first summer"  
【解析】第一个夏天学习星象。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"magnetic material inside the heads... chemical reaction in their eyes"  
【解析】脑内磁性物+眼睛反应。B/C/D 均错。故选 A。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】全文  
【解析】多种自然导航手段协同。A/B/D 均偏离。故选 C。
