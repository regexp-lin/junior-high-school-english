# 阅读理解（说明文） **How Is Lightning Formed?**

① On a summer afternoon, the sky suddenly turns dark. A white flash cuts across the clouds, and a huge **boom** shakes the windows. Most children call it "**lightning and thunder**", and run home quickly. But where does that sudden flash come from, and why is it so dangerous? The story begins not on the ground, but **deep inside a thundercloud**.

② A thundercloud is a very tall, cold, and busy cloud. Strong air goes **up**, carrying small water drops and little pieces of ice. Other, heavier ice pieces slowly come **down**. Inside the cloud, these tiny pieces **rub against each other** millions of times a second. Just as you may feel a small shock after pulling off a sweater on a dry day, this rubbing creates what scientists call **electric charges**. The top of the cloud gathers **positive** charges, and the bottom of the cloud gathers **negative** charges.

③ When the charges become very strong, the cloud acts like a **huge battery** in the sky. The negative bottom of the cloud "pulls" positive charges upward on the ground — a church roof, a tree, even a tall person. For a short time, the air between cloud and ground becomes a **sort of switch**: the charges want to jump, but the air usually blocks them. When the push becomes too great, the air suddenly "breaks", and a bright river of electricity rushes down: **a lightning bolt**.

④ A lightning bolt is astonishingly **hot**. Its centre can reach **about 30,000 °C** — five times hotter than the surface of the sun. The heat makes the thin channel of air explode outward at great speed. This fast expansion is what our ears hear as **thunder**. Because light travels much faster than sound, we see the flash first and then, seconds later, hear the boom. If we count the seconds between them, we can roughly estimate how far away the storm is: **every three seconds** between flash and boom means about **1 km**.

⑤ Not all lightning strikes the ground. Some flashes go **from cloud to cloud**; others stay inside one big cloud. But the ones that come down to us can be dangerous. Lightning often chooses the **tallest thing** in an area — a tall tree, a metal pole, an open hill. That is why safety rules say: during a storm, do not stand under a lone tree, do not keep an umbrella with a metal tip pointing up, and try to go into a solid building or a car with a metal roof.

⑥ In 1752, the American scientist **Benjamin Franklin** used a famous kite experiment to show that lightning is a kind of electricity. From his idea came the **lightning rod**, a thin metal bar on top of tall buildings. It carries the dangerous charges safely down a wire into the ground. Today, from skyscrapers in Shanghai to village schools in Yunnan, lightning rods quietly guard many human homes. Lightning is still powerful and beautiful, but thanks to science, it is no longer a pure mystery of the sky.
