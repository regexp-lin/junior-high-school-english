# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do We Sweat on a Hot Day?**

When summer reaches its hottest week, a simple scene repeats itself in every city. A boy finishes football on the playground; his shirt is stuck to his back. A woman returns from her bus stop; her forehead shines. A dog lies under a tree with its tongue hanging out. All of us feel it — the dampness, the salty taste on our lips. We call it **sweat**. It may not be comfortable, but it is one of the cleverest **cooling** systems in the animal world. So why does our body sweat, and how does it actually cool us down?

The story begins deep inside us. The human body works best at about **37 °C**. If our **core temperature** rises even two degrees, enzymes and proteins begin to work less efficiently. To avoid this danger, a small part of the brain called the **hypothalamus** acts as the body's central **thermostat**. It reads signals from the blood: "Too hot!" Then it sends a quick message to hundreds of thousands of small glands in the skin — the **sweat glands**.

An adult has about **two to four million** sweat glands, spread over almost every part of the body. Some are larger, but most are small tubes that reach from the deep layer of skin up to its surface. When the brain commands, these tubes release a watery fluid made mostly of water, a small amount of salt, and tiny traces of other substances. This fluid pushes through a tiny hole onto the skin surface. That is the sweat we can see.

Here comes the clever part. Water does not just sit on the skin. As it changes from liquid to gas — a process called **evaporation** — it takes heat with it. Each gram of water that evaporates from the skin carries away about **580 calories** of heat. In other words, the hotter we get, the more sweat we make, and the more heat leaves our body. This process is invisible but powerful. Without it, running for ten minutes on a July afternoon could raise our core temperature to a dangerous level.

But sweat has one enemy: **humidity**. In dry air, sweat evaporates quickly, and we feel "okay hot". In wet, sticky air, sweat stays on the skin; it cannot turn into gas, so it cannot take heat away. That is why a 32 °C day in a dry city may feel cooler than a 28 °C day in a **humid** city. A simple fan can help: the moving air pushes dry air past the skin and **speeds** up evaporation.

How can we sweat safely? Three rules matter. First, drink water steadily — small cups, not a huge bottle at once. Our bodies cannot take back lost water in one gulp. Second, eat foods with a little salt and fruits with **minerals**; sweat takes salt away, and we need to refill it. Third, wear light, loose clothes that let air move. Tight clothes trap sweat and slow cooling. If someone feels sudden **dizziness**, stops sweating although it is hot, or feels a very dry skin, they may have heat stroke, and help is needed quickly.

So the next time you feel that salty drop slide down your cheek, think for a moment. Your body is doing something quite amazing — moving heat from your core, out through tiny holes, into the sky. A tiny, warm cloud, invisible to the eye, is rising from your skin. That is how your body keeps itself, and perhaps your plans for the afternoon, running on track.

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the normal working temperature of the human body?**

A. About 37 °C.  
B. About 47 °C.  
C. About 20 °C.  
D. About 100 °C.

**2. Which part of the brain tells the body it is time to sweat?**

A. The eye.  
B. The hypothalamus.  
C. The tongue.  
D. The ear.

**3. How does sweat actually cool the body?**

A. Sweat freezes on the skin and makes it cold.  
B. Sweat turns from liquid to gas, and this evaporation takes heat away from the body.  
C. Sweat pushes blood back into the heart.  
D. Sweat creates a strong wind.

**4. Why does a 28 °C humid day sometimes feel hotter than a 32 °C dry day?**

A. Because the sun is closer in humid weather.  
B. Because sweat cannot evaporate easily in wet, sticky air, so it cannot take heat away.  
C. Because humid air has more oxygen.  
D. Because humid days are always longer.

**5. Which of the following is NOT a safe way to deal with sweating?**

A. Drinking water in small cups, steadily.  
B. Eating foods with a little salt and minerals.  
C. Wearing very tight clothes that trap sweat.  
D. Wearing light, loose clothes and standing in moving air.

---

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】A  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"about 37 °C"  
【解析】约 37 度。B/C/D 均错。故选 A。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"the hypothalamus"  
【解析】下丘脑是体温中枢。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】"evaporation... takes heat with it"  
【解析】蒸发带走热量。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】因果推理题  
【定位】"humidity... sweat stays on the skin... cannot take heat away"  
【解析】潮湿空气降低蒸发效率。A/C/D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题（反向）  
【定位】"wear light, loose clothes"  
【解析】紧身衣阻碍散热。A/B/D 均正确。故选 C。
