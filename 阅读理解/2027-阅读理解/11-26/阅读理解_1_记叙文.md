# 阅读理解（记叙文） **The Morning I Walked an Old Blind Man Across an Unfamiliar Street**

① It was a cold, pale morning in late November. I had finished my early jogging around the community park and was walking home along Qing-yang Road when I saw, on the opposite side of the road, a thin old man in a navy coat standing very still at the edge of the pavement. He wore dark glasses and held a **white cane** with a red stripe near the tip. Next to his right shoe sat a small canvas bag, and on top of the bag, a folded sheet of paper. The traffic on Qing-yang Road was not heavy, but the **crossing was long** — about twelve metres — and the traffic lights were out of service, showing a silent black eye instead of a friendly green walking figure. Three or four cars had already passed him without slowing.

② I crossed carefully from my side and walked up to him, stopping about two steps away so that I would not startle him. I said quietly, *"Grandfather, good morning. I am a student from Lihua Middle School. The traffic light here is broken this morning. Would you like some help crossing the road?"* His face lifted a little — I could see his eyes moving softly behind the dark glasses — and he smiled. *"Yes, child. Thank you."* His voice was warm, a little hoarse from the cold air.

③ Then he did something I did not expect. He turned his head gently to one side and said, **"First, may I hear your voice a little more? I would like to know where you are standing."** I understood at once. I had read in our PSHE textbook that blind adults often locate another person by voice before touch. I took one small step closer and said, *"I am on your right side, about one step away. I am wearing a grey jacket and carrying a small bottle of warm water."* He nodded, satisfied. **"Please offer me your left elbow,"** he said. *"Do not hold my hand. Do not hold my cane. I will hold you lightly, and you will lead."*

④ I offered my left elbow. His hand, thin and a little cold, settled on it with a **steady, very light touch** — surprising for its calm. I thought of Mum's hand on my back in the dentist's chair two weeks ago, and realised: **a helpful grip is always lighter than you expect**. A heavy grip would have said "rescue me"; his grip said, gently, "I trust you." He also asked me something I had never considered before: *"Please tell me what we are walking into. Not only turns, but the floor."*

⑤ And so, for the next two minutes, I became a kind of **soft narrator for the road**. "Step down a small kerb in half a step — now." "Ten metres of wet cement, not slippery, but uneven in the middle." "A small patch of yellow leaves ahead — they are soft, we can walk over them." "A car is waiting for us from the left — the driver is kind, he has stopped and waved." I heard my own voice and thought: how often in a normal day do I **look but not see**? On most mornings I had walked past wet cement, yellow leaves, and a broken traffic light without ever putting any of them into words. The old man had, by one polite question, turned me from a walker into a small honest reporter.

⑥ Halfway across, he said softly, *"Thank you for describing the yellow leaves. I used to teach poetry at the city art school. I still like to know what is on the floor in November."* I laughed, surprised. "Grandfather, you are a poet?" "I was a poetry teacher, many years ago," he said. "Before my eyes stopped cooperating." Then, quietly, as if to himself: *"A good description of a yellow leaf is already half a good poem."* We reached the far kerb. I said, "Grandfather, we are there. One small step up — now." He stepped up, clean and neat, and let go of my elbow.

⑦ I did not leave at once. I asked — carefully, ready to be told "no" — "Grandfather, may I ask why you are travelling alone today?" He smiled. *"My granddaughter is in hospital with a light cold. She usually walks with me to the bus stop at 08:10. Today I am going to bring her some warm rice porridge. You walked with me the part she usually does. This is a small lucky morning for me."* He reached into the canvas bag, took out the folded sheet of paper I had seen, and pressed it gently into my hand. *"An old poem I wrote for my granddaughter, in large print, in case she cannot focus her eyes well today. Take a look before you give it to her. I trust you to deliver it to room 508 of the City No. 2 Hospital, on your way to school."*

⑧ I said yes. I said it carefully. I held the paper with both hands. On the bus to school I unfolded it at a stoplight. The poem had only four lines:

> *If you cannot see the leaves today,*
> *Listen — the wind has learned to turn them.*  
> *If you cannot walk today,*  
> *Tell someone the road, and she will carry you both across.*

⑨ At City No. 2 Hospital, room 508, a girl about eleven looked up from her pillow and smiled softly when I said, "Your grandfather sent this." She read it slowly, once, then closed her eyes. "He said you would come," she said. "He said a student would describe the yellow leaves for him." I walked to school. I was almost late, but not quite. All the way down the school corridor, I could still feel the light, trusting grip of a hand on my left elbow — **a hand that, just by asking me to look at the floor, had given me a better morning than I had earned**. I will walk a little more carefully now. I will describe the small things more honestly. **Helping someone, it turns out, is sometimes as simple as putting the world into words for them**.

**根据短文内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. Why did the writer decide to cross the street and offer help?**

A. Because the old man called out for help.  
B. Because the traffic light at a twelve-metre crossing on Qing-yang Road was not working (showing a silent black eye instead of a green walking figure), and several cars had passed the old blind man without slowing down.  
C. Because the old man was her grandfather.  
D. Because a teacher had ordered her to help.

**2. What did the old man ask the writer BEFORE he accepted her help?**

A. How old she was.  
B. Whether she could speak a little more, so he could locate where she was standing — he then asked her to offer her left elbow, not her hand, and not to hold his cane; he would hold her lightly and she would lead.  
C. How much money she had.  
D. Whether she had a phone.

**3. What important request did the old man make AS they were crossing, that the writer had "never considered before"?**

A. That she walk faster.  
B. That she stop talking.  
C. That she tell him not only about turns, but also about the floor — wet cement, kerbs, yellow leaves, and approaching cars — turning her into a soft narrator for the road.  
D. That she carry his bag.

**4. Why had the old man come out alone that morning?**

A. Because he had lost his way.  
B. Because his granddaughter, who usually walked with him to the bus stop at 08:10, was in hospital with a light cold; he was on his way to bring her warm rice porridge and a poem written in large print.  
C. Because he was going to buy food.  
D. Because he was going to work.

**5. What lesson does the writer mainly take from this morning?**

A. Helping is dangerous and tiring.  
B. Helping someone is sometimes as simple as putting the world into words for them — a hand that, just by asking her to look at the floor, had given her a better morning than she had earned.  
C. Blind people should stay home.  
D. Students should not talk to strangers.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第①段背景（红绿灯坏、十二米斑马线、车辆不让）  
【解析】直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段老人两项请求  
【解析】"声音定位"与"左臂不握手不握杖"直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**3.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④—⑤段"不仅告诉我转弯，还请告诉我地面"  
【解析】直引。A、B、D 均错。故选 C。

**4.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第⑦段老人自述  
【解析】孙女生病 + 送粥与诗直引。A、C、D 均错。故选 B。

**5.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】推理判断题  
【定位】第⑨段结尾"把世界变成言语"  
【解析】主题直引。A、C、D 均偏离。故选 B。
