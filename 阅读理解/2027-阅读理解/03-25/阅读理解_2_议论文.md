# 阅读理解（议论文） **Should Middle School Students Manage Their Own Pocket Money?**

① Every Chinese New Year, like many of my classmates, I receive **red envelopes** from aunts, uncles, and grandparents. Back in primary school, Mum took every envelope with a soft smile: "Mum will keep it for you, safe and sound." This year, in Grade 8, I asked her a bigger question at dinner: "Can I manage my own pocket money this year?" Mum did not answer at once. After two days, she and Dad called me to the living room — and the discussion at our kitchen table quickly became a **small family debate**.

② Those who **say yes** to student-managed pocket money give several thoughtful reasons. First, it teaches **real money skills early**. Making a small budget on paper is one thing; deciding whether to buy a **60-yuan paper airplane model or save for a book fair** is another. Teenagers who learn these choices at 13 make far fewer mistakes at 23. Second, managing money is a form of **self-respect**. When a student plans and saves, he or she feels trusted. That warm feeling often pushes young people to act more responsibly in other areas of life, too. Third, studies in Shanghai and Guangzhou show that students who keep a short weekly **spending diary** tend to eat healthier and buy less junk food. They are simply more aware.

③ However, parents and some teachers also have strong worries. A middle school student's **eyes are wide but judgement is still growing**. Shops and apps know this too. Pop-up game items, trendy stickers, blind-box toys — all are designed to tempt young minds. Without guidance, some students may spend everything in one week and borrow from friends, which can hurt friendships. Another point: large red-envelope money, often several thousand yuan, is too big a number to hand over at once. A 13-year-old who suddenly holds "a big stack of cash" may feel dizzy with choices, not happy.

④ Is there a middle way? I think there is. Parents can divide the pocket money into **three small drawers**: a "spending" drawer for daily needs (meals, bus, small fun), a "saving" drawer for bigger hopes (a good bike, a camp fee), and a "giving" drawer for simple charity, like buying books for rural schools. Students decide inside each drawer, parents stay quiet but watchful. Each month, the family can have a **ten-minute "money meeting"** — no scolding, only sharing what went well and what went wrong. Over time, the child's own wallet becomes a small classroom.

⑤ Money does not teach by itself; people do. Yet a teenager who never holds money will, one day, be pushed into a bigger world with only empty hands and untrained eyes. Letting us plan a small amount now is not giving in to desire — it is a **quiet vote of trust** that says, "Grow up slowly, but grow up for real."

**根据文章内容，选择最佳答案。**

**1. What is the main question the writer discusses in the article?**

A. Whether students should receive red envelopes at Chinese New Year.  
B. Whether middle school students should manage their own pocket money.  
C. How to save money for a school trip abroad.  
D. Why parents should stop giving children gifts.

**2. Which is NOT a reason for letting students manage their own money?**

A. It teaches real spending and saving skills early.  
B. It helps build self-respect and responsibility.  
C. It can guide students to eat healthier food.  
D. It allows children to spend all their money on blind-box toys.

**3. According to paragraph ③, why are some parents worried?**

A. Students know too much about money already.  
B. Teenagers can be tempted by clever shops and apps.  
C. Children never save any of their red-envelope money.  
D. Money is useless in today's digital world.

**4. What does the writer suggest as a middle way?**

A. Parents should keep all the money and give nothing.  
B. Students should spend all red-envelope money in one week.  
C. Split the money into three drawers and hold short monthly money meetings.  
D. Give the money to teachers to manage on behalf of the class.

**5. What does the underlined phrase "a quiet vote of trust" in the last paragraph mean?**

A. A secret election inside the family.  
B. A silent promise by the child never to spend money.  
C. A calm way for parents to show their children that they trust them.  
D. A strict rule made by the school about saving money.

## 答案与解析

**1.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】主旨大意题  
【定位】第①段末提问 + 全文围绕此展开  
【解析】核心为"中学生是否自行管理零花钱"。A、C、D 非主题。故选 B。

**2.**  
【答案】D  
【题型】细节否定题  
【定位】第②段三条理由：金融技能、自尊感、饮食更健康  
【解析】"让学生把钱全花在盲盒"不是支持理由。A、B、C 都是。故选 D。

**3.**  
【答案】B  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第③段 "Shops and apps know this too..."  
【解析】担心学生被商家诱惑。A、C、D 与原文不符。故选 B。

**4.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】细节理解题  
【定位】第④段 "three small drawers... ten-minute 'money meeting'"  
【解析】三抽屉+月度复盘。A、B、D 与作者建议不符。故选 C。

**5.**  
【答案】C  
【题型】词句理解题  
【定位】第⑤段 "quiet vote of trust... Grow up slowly, but grow up for real"  
【解析】意指父母以温和方式表达信任。A、B、D 均非原意。故选 C。
