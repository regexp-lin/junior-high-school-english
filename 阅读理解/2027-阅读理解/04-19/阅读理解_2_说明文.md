# 阅读理解（说明文） **Why Do Camels Have Humps?**

① When we imagine a **desert**, we often picture long lines of **camels** walking slowly across the hot sand. The most special thing about a camel, we all know, is **the hump on its back**. Some children think the hump is a big tank of water. Others believe it is just extra skin. But the real answer is more interesting, and it helps us understand why camels can survive where most animals cannot.

② First, how many humps does a camel have? There are **two main kinds**. The **Arabian camel**, found mostly in North Africa and the Middle East, has **one hump**. The **Bactrian camel**, found in Central Asia and parts of northern China, has **two humps**. Whether one or two, the hump is shaped like a soft round hill when the camel is healthy, and it can droop to one side after a hard trip with little food.

③ So what is actually inside the hump? It is **not water**. It is **fat** — a large store of fat, tightly packed together. When food is plentiful, the camel builds up this fat. When food becomes scarce, the camel's body slowly **breaks down the fat** and turns it into the energy the animal needs. Surprisingly, when fat is broken down, it also releases a little water as a side-product. So, in a clever indirect way, the hump does help the camel keep drinking — but it is still fat, not water.

④ Camels also have other amazing body tricks. Their **thick eyelashes** and **closable nostrils** protect them from blowing sand. Their **wide feet** spread on the hot ground like soft pads, so they do not sink into the sand. Their blood cells have a special shape that lets them keep moving even when the camel loses a lot of water. A fully-grown camel can drink **100 litres of water in just 10 minutes** when it finally finds a well.

⑤ Because of these body tools, camels can travel **up to 160 kilometres** without water on a cool day, carrying heavy bags of food, salt, or cloth. In ancient times, long lines of camels walked along the famous **Silk Road**, crossing the Gobi Desert, bringing tea, paper, and porcelain from China to the West, and bringing spices, glass, and music back. Without the hump, and without the camel's quiet strength, this long conversation between East and West might have been very different.

⑥ Today, trucks have replaced most camels on the modern Silk Road, but camels still help herders in desert villages in Xinjiang, Inner Mongolia, and many other places. The next time we see a camel in a picture or in real life, we should not just think of the hump as a funny shape. We should see it as a **built-in food bag**, a quiet body engineering that has kept these gentle giants and their human friends alive in the world's driest rooms for thousands of years.
